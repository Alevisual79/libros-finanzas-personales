# Anxiety and Work
## Managing Performance Pressure and Workplace Stress

**Series 2: Anxiety and Fears — Book 5 of 10**

## Prologue

By all visible measures, Maya was thriving at 36. She had been promoted twice in four years, managed a team she respected, and received consistently strong performance reviews. She also spent Sunday evenings in a state of dread that intensified through Sunday night into Monday morning; checked her email at midnight on Tuesdays to ensure nothing was going wrong with a project; rehearsed upcoming difficult conversations with colleagues for hours in the shower; and by Thursday of most weeks felt such anticipatory exhaustion about the next week that the weekend barely registered as rest. Her output was high. Her experience of that output was suffering.

Workplace anxiety is among the most prevalent and least addressed forms of anxiety in modern life. Surveys consistently find that 40-50% of working adults report significant stress or anxiety related to their work, and that work-related anxiety is the most common reason people present to occupational health services. It is also among the most normalized forms of anxiety: the driven professional who lies awake catastrophizing about quarterly targets is culturally legible as a high performer; the same person presenting to a general practitioner with identical symptoms might receive a very different response than someone whose anxiety had no identifiable "rational" external cause. The normalization of workplace anxiety conceals both its costs and the existence of effective solutions.

## Chapter 1: The Anatomy of Workplace Anxiety

Workplace anxiety is not a single phenomenon but a cluster of related experiences with distinct psychological mechanisms. Performance anxiety — the acute anxiety surrounding specific high-stakes events (presentations, reviews, crucial client meetings) — differs from chronic occupational stress (sustained, ongoing work demands that exceed resources), from evaluation anxiety (fear of being assessed negatively by supervisors or peers), and from workplace-specific manifestations of generalized anxiety disorder (using work as the organizing content for worry that would exist regardless of occupational context).

The demand-control model developed by Robert Karasek in the 1970s remains one of the most important frameworks for understanding occupational stress. The model proposes that job strain — the most harmful form of occupational stress — emerges from the combination of high demands and low control: work that places heavy demands on the worker while providing little autonomy over how those demands are met. The Whitehall studies of British civil servants, discussed in a different context in the Health and Wellness collection, found that lower-grade civil servants — who had less control over their work — showed higher rates of cardiovascular disease, depression, and anxiety than senior civil servants, despite having objectively less demanding jobs. The subjective experience of uncontrollable demands, not the objective quantity of work, was the primary driver of harm.

Jeffrey Siegrist's effort-reward imbalance model adds a complementary perspective: stress is generated not only by high demands and low control but by the perception that the effort expended is not receiving appropriate reward — whether reward is understood as salary, recognition, status, career advancement, or job security. When high effort is consistently met with insufficient reward, the imbalance produces chronic stress, anxiety, and eventually burnout. This model helps explain why objectively demanding work can be experienced as energizing when it is rewarding and as demoralizing when it is not.

## Chapter 2: Performance Anxiety

Performance anxiety — the acute anxiety response before and during high-stakes performance situations — is among the most universal occupational experiences and among those most commonly misunderstood as pathological. A moderate level of arousal before important performances is not only normal but beneficial: the Yerkes-Dodson curve (described in this series' opening book on cognitive biases) shows that arousal enhances performance up to an optimal point, beyond which further arousal impairs it. The goal in managing performance anxiety is not the elimination of arousal but its optimization.

The most significant factor determining whether performance arousal helps or hinders is its interpretation. Research by Alison Wood Brooks at Harvard Business School showed that people experiencing pre-performance anxiety who were instructed to reappraise their arousal as excitement — telling themselves "I am excited" rather than "I am anxious" — performed significantly better on subsequent tasks (singing, public speaking, mathematics) than people who were instructed to calm down. The physiological state of excitement and anxiety are nearly identical (elevated heart rate, heightened arousal, physical activation); the interpretive label determines whether that state is channeled toward approach or avoidance. "I am excited" activates an opportunity frame; "I am anxious" activates a threat frame. The reappraisal is not suppression — it does not deny the arousal — but redirection.

The physiology of performance anxiety also reveals a specific intervention: arousal that continues to escalate in the performance situation, rather than peaking and declining, indicates that the threat system is not getting the "safety signal" from initial performance that would allow it to downregulate. Techniques that specifically address physical arousal — slow breathing, progressive relaxation, mindful grounding — can interrupt this escalation loop and allow performance capacity to recover. The timing matters: these techniques work best in the minutes immediately before the performance begins, when there is still time for physiological downregulation before the critical moment.

## Chapter 3: The Perfectionism-Anxiety Connection

Perfectionism — the tendency to set excessively high standards and to evaluate one's performance critically in relation to those standards — is among the most common psychological features of workplace anxiety and among the most complex to address. Not all perfectionism is pathological: what Paul Hewitt and Gordon Flett distinguish as "adaptive perfectionism" (high standards with healthy self-evaluation) produces high achievement without the anxiety cost. "Maladaptive perfectionism" (high standards combined with harsh self-evaluation of failure and contingent self-worth) produces both high effort and chronic anxiety.

The key cognitive distortion in maladaptive perfectionism is the belief that one's worth as a person is contingent on performance — that a professional failure is not just a disappointing outcome but an indictment of fundamental value. This belief makes every task consequential in a way that extends far beyond the task itself: not meeting a project deadline is not just inconvenient, it is evidence of inadequacy as a person. The stakes of every performance are therefore elevated beyond their objective level, and the vigilance, effort, and anxiety directed at performance reflect these inflated stakes.

Cognitive restructuring targeting perfectionism focuses on decoupling performance from self-worth: recognizing that the quality of one's work and the value of one's person are different things that happen to be correlated in some professional contexts but are not identical. Carol Dweck's growth mindset research is relevant here: people who understand their abilities as fixed and definitive interpret performance failures as revelations of fundamental limits; people who understand their abilities as developable through effort interpret the same failures as information about what to practice. Perfectionism often involves a fixed-ability worldview that makes every performance a verdict rather than a data point.

## Chapter 4: Burnout and Its Relationship to Anxiety

Burnout — the state of chronic occupational stress characterized by emotional exhaustion, depersonalization (cynical detachment from one's work and colleagues), and reduced sense of personal accomplishment — is clinically and neurobiologically related to anxiety but distinct from it. Christina Maslach's three-component model, the dominant framework in burnout research, describes burnout as emerging from sustained demands exceeding resources across the dimensions of workload, control, recognition, community, fairness, and values alignment.

The relationship between anxiety and burnout is bidirectional and sequential: anxiety often precedes burnout, as the anxious overperformer expends more effort than the situation requires (driven by threat evaluation rather than genuine need), depletes resources, and eventually hits the wall of exhaustion. Burnout in turn exacerbates anxiety because the reduced cognitive and emotional resources that burnout produces make anxiety management more difficult — depleted systems are less able to regulate threat responses. The result is a deteriorating cycle that frequently ends in psychiatric sick leave, job loss, or significant physical health consequences.

Prevention of burnout requires attention to the balance between demands and resources at the organizational level (adequate staffing, clear role boundaries, genuine recognition) and at the individual level (recovery behaviors — genuine disengagement from work during non-work time, adequate sleep, social connection, physical activity). The most consistently effective individual-level burnout prevention behaviors are those that genuinely produce cognitive detachment from work during off-hours — not passive distraction (scrolling social media) but active engagement in non-work domains (sport, creative activity, social connection, nature). Segmentation strategies — firm boundary-setting between work and non-work roles — appear more protective than integration strategies for anxiety-prone individuals.

## Chapter 5: Rumination and Worry at Work

Workplace anxiety is sustained primarily by cognitive processes rather than by actual work demands — specifically by rumination (repetitive, negative thinking about past events) and worry (repetitive, negative thinking about future events). Research by Nico Frijda and others finds that the emotional consequences of stressful events persist far longer when they are ruminated upon than when they are not; the event itself generates a finite emotional response, but rumination regenerates that response indefinitely.

Worry, in particular, is the primary cognitive process maintaining generalized anxiety in occupational contexts. Worry about upcoming performance reviews, about the consequences of a project delay, about what a colleague's curt email might mean — these mental activities feel productive (surely thinking about problems helps solve them?) but research by Borkovec and colleagues found that worry rarely generates genuine problem-solving. Most worry is recursive — it circles back to the same feared outcome rather than progressing toward a solution — and what distinguishes problematic worry from constructive problem-solving is precisely this recursion versus progression.

Effective interventions for occupational rumination and worry include scheduled worry time (containing worry to a designated period and actively postponing worry thoughts outside that period), problem-solving therapy (converting unproductive worry into actionable problem-analysis when the problem is soluble), and mindfulness practices specifically targeting cognitive disengagement from work (evidence-based mindfulness training has been shown to reduce occupational rumination even when practiced outside of work hours). The common thread is creating genuine psychological separation between the person and the work-related thought content — which is harder in the era of constant connectivity and requires deliberate structural support.

## Chapter 6: Anxiety and Leadership

Leadership roles concentrate many of the anxiety-generating features of professional life: high visibility, direct responsibility for others' wellbeing, frequent high-stakes decision-making under uncertainty, regular evaluation, and the relational complexity of managing people with diverse needs and motivations. It should be unsurprising that anxiety is prevalent among leaders — and it is, though leaders are among the populations least likely to disclose it.

The relationship between anxiety and leadership effectiveness is complex and non-linear. Moderate levels of leadership anxiety may produce vigilance and thoroughness that serve the organization. High levels produce cognitive narrowing, risk aversion, avoidance of difficult conversations, and the kind of emotional unavailability that undermines psychological safety in teams. Leaders who are chronically anxious but who mask this anxiety through surface acting — performing confidence while experiencing distress — report significantly higher burnout and show lower team performance than leaders who have developed genuine anxiety management skills.

Amy Edmondson's research on psychological safety — the team climate in which members feel safe to take interpersonal risks — is directly relevant to leadership anxiety: leaders who are anxious about their own evaluation tend to respond to team members' errors and challenges with defensive criticism rather than constructive inquiry, systematically undermining the psychological safety that enables team learning and performance. The anxious leader's self-protective behavior (minimizing visible failure to maintain the appearance of competence) comes at the direct cost of team capability. This creates a perverse dynamic in which personal anxiety management becomes a leadership performance issue, not just a personal wellbeing one.

## Chapter 7: Setting Limits and Managing Demands

One of the most practically significant skills for occupational anxiety management is the ability to decline additional demands when current demands exceed capacity — what is commonly called saying no, and what organizational cultures with high performance norms have systematically discouraged. Research on perfectionism and burnout finds that the inability to set limits is among the most reliable predictors of occupational stress escalation: demands accumulate, resources deplete, and the perfectionist's catastrophic evaluation of the consequences of declining makes each new demand feel non-negotiable.

The assertion of limits requires both the practical skill of making limit-setting requests (clear, direct, non-apologetic communication of capacity constraints) and the cognitive reframing of limit-setting from a failure of commitment to a form of competence management. Leaders who model limit-setting — who explicitly acknowledge their own capacity constraints and make transparent decisions about priority — normalize this behavior in their teams and reduce the organizational pressure that drives individual-level overload.

Work-life boundary management is a skill that requires deliberate cultivation, not just permission. The structural features of contemporary work — always-on communications, remote work that removes physical separation between work and home, performance cultures that reward visibility and availability — have systematically eroded the boundaries that once provided automatic work-life separation. Recovery from work requires genuine cognitive disengagement: not just physical absence from the workplace but psychological release of work-related thought. This capacity for psychological detachment, as research by Sabine Sonnentag has shown, is among the strongest predictors of next-day energy, engagement, and work performance — suggesting that genuine recovery is not a retreat from ambition but a precondition for sustained it.

## Chapter 8: Anxiety and Remote Work

The shift to remote and hybrid work, accelerated by the COVID-19 pandemic, has had complex effects on occupational anxiety. For some workers — particularly those with social anxiety or high sensitivity to workplace interpersonal demands — remote work reduced anxiety by eliminating office politics, commuting, and the surveillance of shared space. For others — particularly those anxious about professional visibility, career advancement, or loss of social connection — remote work amplified anxiety about being forgotten, overlooked, or disconnected from the relationships that support professional identity.

The "always-on" dynamic of remote work represents a particular anxiety generator: when work is conducted from home on personal devices, the physical and temporal boundaries that once contained work are absent, and the anxiety worker finds work available for engagement 24 hours a day. Research on remote work and wellbeing consistently finds that effective boundary-setting — explicit designation of work hours, device-free recovery time, deliberate social connection with colleagues — is the primary protective factor, and that the workers most at risk in remote environments are those whose anxiety drives boundary-violating overinvestment in work availability.

The social dimension of work — relationships with colleagues, the informal communications that build trust, the sense of belonging that shared physical space provides — is also disrupted by remote work in ways that are anxiety-relevant. Research on remote team effectiveness identifies deliberate relationship investment (informal video calls, virtual social events, explicit recognition of contributions) as critical for maintaining the psychological safety that enables the difficult conversations remote teams need to have. Managers who ignore the relational dimension of remote work create anxiety-amplifying environments: workers feel isolated, uncertain about their standing, and unable to read the signals that would normally orient them in the work environment.

## Chapter 9: Seeking Help in Occupational Contexts

The reluctance to seek psychological support for workplace anxiety is deeply embedded in professional cultures that value resilience, performance, and the appearance of competence. The stigma attached to anxiety treatment in professional contexts — the fear that seeking help will be perceived as weakness, as evidence of unsuitability for demanding roles, or as a career risk — is not entirely unfounded in some organizational cultures but is typically far less severe in reality than anxious professionals anticipate.

Employee Assistance Programs (EAPs), available in most large organizations, provide confidential, free short-term psychological support specifically designed for workplace mental health challenges. Their confidentiality protections are generally robust, and their usage does not typically appear in personnel records or affect career trajectories. The short-term format (typically 6-10 sessions) is adequate for performance anxiety, occupational stress management, and skill-building approaches to anxiety, though more complex clinical presentations may require referral to longer-term support.

Occupational health services — physicians and psychologists specializing in work-related health — can provide assessment, brief intervention, and appropriate onward referral for more significant clinical presentations. The occupational health framework specifically includes adjustment of work conditions as a treatment component: temporary workload reductions, phased return after sick leave, and workplace accommodations for anxiety disorders are evidence-based approaches that occupational health services can facilitate in ways that general practitioners typically cannot.

## Chapter 10: Building a Sustainable Work Life

Maya, the thriving-but-suffering professional from our prologue, eventually described her experience to her GP in the context of a check-up for a persistent headache. The GP referred her to a psychologist who specialized in occupational stress. The first thing the psychologist asked was not about her work performance but about what she did after work. The answer was: more work, or thinking about work. The second question was what she valued in her life beyond work. The answer took six minutes and was, ultimately, about connection, creativity, and physical freedom — none of which she was regularly accessing.

The treatment was not fundamentally about reducing anxiety at work. It was about rebuilding the life outside work that would allow her to show up at work without the anxiety of someone for whom work is the only domain that counts. Anxious professionals typically have extremely narrow identity structures in which professional performance is the primary source of self-worth — which makes every professional setback personally existential. Broadening the identity — investing genuinely in relationships, physical activity, creative pursuits, community involvement — reduces the stakes of any single professional failure and expands the emotional resource base available for occupational demands.

After eight months, Maya still worked hard. She still cared about her outcomes. She no longer checked email at midnight on Tuesdays. The distinction between those two facts was, she eventually recognized, the difference between caring about work and being controlled by anxiety about work.

---

## Epilogue

Work matters. For most people, it is the largest organized commitment of their waking life, the primary arena in which their capabilities are developed and tested, and a central source of identity, purpose, and social connection. When anxiety colonizes this domain — when the place in which we find meaning becomes primarily a source of dread — the loss is profound and the costs extend far beyond productivity metrics. Taking workplace anxiety seriously — not as weakness to be hidden but as a signal to be interpreted and a problem to be addressed — is one of the highest-return investments a professional can make in their own capacity, longevity, and satisfaction.
