# Your Personality at Work
## How Who You Are Shapes How You Work — and What to Do About It

**Series 8: Personality and Behavior — Book 10 of 10**

---

## Prologue

When Elena interviewed for her current position, she had presented the most professionally confident version of herself: proactive, collaborative, decisive, comfortable with ambiguity. The presentation was accurate, in the sense that these were genuine capabilities she possessed. What she had not fully disclosed — to her employer or to herself — was the other side of her psychological profile: the need for more than two days' notice before significant changes to her work plan, the genuine difficulty with the open-plan office where the role was based, the energy cost of the prolonged client entertainment that the role required. She had been hired for her genuine strengths without either party adequately attending to the genuine fit challenges.

Two years into the role, she was performing well by every external measure and experiencing significant psychological cost by every internal one. The role was not wrong for her in any categorical sense; she could perform it. The question she had begun to ask — partly with a therapist, partly in the conversations about career development that had started to feel more urgent — was not "am I good at this job?" but "is this job good for me?" and "is there a way to design both the job and my approach to it that serves both questions better?"

The intersection of personality psychology and work is one of the most practically significant applications of personality research. The Big Five traits documented in Book 71 predict not only life outcomes in general but occupational choice, occupational performance, job satisfaction, leadership effectiveness, team functioning, and career development specifically. Understanding how your personality interacts with your work — and what that understanding implies for the choices available to you — is the subject of this concluding book in the Personality and Behavior series.

## Chapter 1: Personality and Occupational Choice

Research on personality and occupational interests converges on a consistent finding: people tend to select (and to remain in) occupations that are congruent with their personality traits, and they perform better and are more satisfied in occupations that fit their personality. John Holland's RIASEC model — which classifies both occupations and people along six types (Realistic, Investigative, Artistic, Social, Enterprising, Conventional) — provides the most extensively researched framework for person-occupation fit.

The relationship between Big Five traits and occupational interests is documented across large meta-analytic samples. Openness to Experience is associated with Artistic and Investigative interests; Extraversion is associated with Social and Enterprising interests; Conscientiousness is associated with Conventional interests; Agreeableness is associated with Social interests; and Neuroticism is negatively associated with most occupational interest dimensions. These associations are moderate in magnitude but consistent across cultures and samples.

The important practical implication is bidirectional: personality traits predict occupational interests (and therefore occupational choices), but occupational experiences also influence personality traits over time. Research by Roberts and colleagues documents that occupational experiences — specifically the degree of control, responsibility, and challenge provided by one's work — produce personality changes over decades. People who experience higher work autonomy and responsibility show increases in Conscientiousness and decreases in Neuroticism over time, while people in less demanding occupations show less personality change.

## Chapter 2: Conscientiousness and Work Performance

Conscientiousness is the personality trait with the most consistently documented relationship to work performance across occupations, cultures, and measurement approaches. Meta-analyses by Barrick and Mount, covering hundreds of studies and tens of thousands of participants, find Conscientiousness to be the single Big Five trait that predicts job performance consistently across occupational categories.

The mechanism of Conscientiousness's performance advantage is straightforward: the behavioral tendencies that characterize high Conscientiousness — thoroughness, reliability, persistence, goal-directedness, and self-discipline — are valuable in virtually all occupational contexts. The conscientious employee sets higher goals, works more consistently toward them, makes fewer errors through carelessness, and delivers on commitments more reliably than employees lower in this trait.

Importantly, Conscientiousness's performance advantage is not uniformly linear at the highest levels. Research finds evidence for a curvilinear relationship in some contexts: extremely high Conscientiousness can produce rigidity, difficulty adapting to changing circumstances, micromanagement in leadership roles, and reduced creative performance in contexts that require flexible, exploratory thinking. The optimal level of Conscientiousness for performance appears to be high but not extreme, with the caveat that the optimal level varies by occupational context.

## Chapter 3: Extraversion and Work

Extraversion's relationship to work performance is more contextually dependent than Conscientiousness's. Meta-analyses find that Extraversion predicts performance specifically in jobs that involve significant interpersonal interaction — sales, management, customer service, training, and other people-facing roles — but shows weaker or absent relationships with performance in roles that require primarily individual, technical, or analytical work.

The positive relationship between Extraversion and performance in interpersonal roles is explained by the trait's characteristic features: higher positive affect, social engagement, and dominance all facilitate the quality of social interactions that people-facing roles require. Research by Grant on introversion and leadership (described in Book 72) finds that extraverted leaders outperform introverted leaders with passive employees, while introverted leaders outperform extraverted leaders with proactive employees — a finding that illustrates both the Extraversion performance advantage in leadership and its contextual conditionality.

The relationship between Extraversion and job satisfaction is one of the most consistently documented in personality-work research: higher Extraversion is associated with higher job satisfaction across occupational contexts. The mechanism involves both the positive affect associated with Extraversion (which colors the subjective experience of work) and the interpersonal engagement that extraverted individuals find rewarding — more available in most workplace environments than introverted people typically find satisfying.

## Chapter 4: Openness and Creative Performance

Openness to Experience — the Big Five trait associated with curiosity, aesthetic sensitivity, intellectual engagement, and willingness to consider new ideas — shows its strongest relationships to work performance specifically in contexts that require creativity, innovation, and intellectual exploration.

Research on Openness and creative performance finds consistent positive associations in occupational contexts that reward novelty, innovation, and artistic expression: research and development, artistic and creative fields, academia, entrepreneurship, and strategic planning. In these contexts, the intellectual curiosity, divergent thinking capacity, and tolerance for ambiguity that characterize high Openness people provide specific performance advantages.

The relationship between Openness and occupational tenure shows an interesting pattern: high Openness individuals are more likely to change jobs and occupations, showing lower average tenure in any given position. Research finds that this higher mobility reflects both the high Openness individual's greater appetite for novelty and variety and their greater tendency to leave positions that have ceased to provide intellectual stimulation. From a career perspective, this mobility can be an advantage (exposure to diverse experiences, broader skill development) or a disadvantage (lower accumulation of position-specific expertise), depending on the occupational context.

## Chapter 5: Agreeableness, Neuroticism, and Work

Agreeableness — the trait associated with cooperativeness, empathy, trustworthiness, and concern for others — shows a more complex relationship to work performance than the other Big Five traits. While high Agreeableness is associated with better team performance and lower interpersonal conflict, it is negatively associated with individual performance in competitive contexts and with negotiation effectiveness.

Research by Graziano and colleagues on Agreeableness and cooperation finds that high Agreeableness individuals cooperate more consistently in social dilemmas, behave more prosocially in organizational contexts, and are rated as better team members by their colleagues. However, research on negotiation finds that high Agreeableness negotiators achieve lower economic outcomes than low Agreeableness negotiators, because their concern for the counterpart's wellbeing leads them to make more concessions than is economically optimal.

Neuroticism's relationship to work performance is primarily negative: meta-analyses find that higher Neuroticism is associated with lower job performance, lower job satisfaction, higher absenteeism, and higher turnover intentions across occupational contexts. The mechanisms are multiple: the emotional reactivity of high Neuroticism produces more frequent emotional disruptions to work; the worry and rumination produce cognitive interference; and the lower average positive affect reduces intrinsic motivation for work activities.

## Chapter 6: Personality and Leadership

The personality correlates of leadership effectiveness have been studied extensively, generating consistent findings about the personality profile associated with effective leadership.

Meta-analyses by Judge and colleagues find that Extraversion is the Big Five trait most strongly associated with leadership emergence (who becomes the leader) and with leader effectiveness ratings. Conscientiousness, Openness, and Agreeableness also show positive associations with leadership effectiveness. Neuroticism is negatively associated with leadership emergence and effectiveness. Together, the Big Five personality traits account for significant variance in leadership outcomes, with the highest predictions for Extraversion and Conscientiousness.

Research on the "dark triad" — the sub-clinical personality traits of narcissism, Machiavellianism, and psychopathy — documents their complex relationship to leadership. Dark triad traits are associated with leadership emergence (narcissists and Machiavellians are skilled at self-promotion and political maneuvering that facilitates leadership acquisition) but negatively associated with leadership effectiveness (particularly over the long term). The "dark triad" leader may be effective at seizing power but is associated with higher rates of unethical behavior, exploitation of followers, and organizational harm.

## Chapter 7: Person-Organization Fit

Beyond person-job fit (the match between a person's traits and the demands of a specific job), research on person-organization fit documents the importance of alignment between a person's values, personality, and the organization's culture.

Research by Chatman on person-organization fit finds that employees whose personalities and values are congruent with their organization's culture show higher job satisfaction, stronger organizational commitment, lower turnover, and better performance than employees with poor person-organization fit — even when person-job fit is controlled. The mechanism involves the reduced friction of operating in a cultural environment that reinforces rather than contradicts one's characteristic behavioral tendencies and values.

High Conscientiousness individuals thrive in organizations that are organized, structured, and performance-oriented, while finding loosely organized, improvisational cultures more stressful. High Openness individuals thrive in organizations that value innovation, learning, and experimentation, while finding rigidly conventional organizations stifling. Understanding not just the job but the organization's cultural values — and honestly assessing fit — is one of the most important and most neglected elements of career decision-making.

## Chapter 8: Personality-Informed Career Development

The practical application of personality knowledge to career development involves using personality trait information — accurately understood, not as fixed destiny but as current tendency — to make more informed choices about occupational direction, role design, professional development, and career transitions.

Career decision-making research by Holland and others documents that people tend to make better occupational choices (with higher subsequent satisfaction and performance) when they accurately understand both their own personality-interest profile and the demands and culture of the occupations they are considering. The tools of vocational psychology — interest inventories, personality assessments, informational interviewing, and work experience sampling — provide more reliable information for career decisions than the impressionistic judgments that most people rely on.

"Job crafting" — the deliberate modification of one's work within existing role constraints to increase fit with one's personality and values — is an increasingly researched approach to personality-work alignment that does not require changing positions. Research by Wrzesniewski and Dutton documents that employees who actively craft their work — reshaping task demands, relationship patterns, and the subjective meaning of their work — show higher job satisfaction, stronger work identity, and better performance than those who accept their job as fixed. Personality-informed job crafting allows high Openness individuals to increase the intellectual novelty of their work, introverts to manage the social demands of their role, and high Conscientiousness individuals to structure their work in ways that serve their characteristic strengths.

## Chapter 9: Elena's Redesign

Elena's career conversation eventually produced a redesign that neither required changing jobs nor accepted the current fit challenges as permanent. The redesign was small in scope and significant in impact: she negotiated two days per week of remote work (reducing the open-plan exposure); she reduced her client entertainment obligations by identifying specific events she genuinely valued and declining others; and she modified her project intake process to include explicit buffer time for significant plan changes.

None of these changes made the fundamental fit challenges disappear — the role continued to require more sustained social engagement than was optimal for her, in an organizational culture that valued extraverted expression more than she found natural. What the changes did was shift the ratio from unmanageable to manageable: she was still spending energy on dimensions of the role that were costly for her, but not at levels that were producing the cumulative depletion that had been her experience for two years.

The more significant conversation — about whether this role trajectory was the right long-term career direction, or whether a different organizational culture or role type would provide a better underlying fit — was one she was still having, with herself and with her therapist and mentor. The redesign had bought her time and reduced her distress; the larger question about long-term fit remained genuinely open.

## Chapter 10: The Authentic Work Life

The concept of an "authentic work life" — one in which the person one is at work is not fundamentally different from the person one is elsewhere, and in which the work's demands and the person's genuine characteristics are reasonably aligned — is both an aspiration and a constraint. It is an aspiration because many people spend significant portions of their working lives performing a professionally constructed version of themselves that is genuinely exhausting to maintain. It is a constraint because work environments have their own requirements that cannot always be accommodated to individual personality.

The practical goal is not perfect fit — which is rarely available — but sufficient fit: work environments that allow one's genuine strengths to be engaged, that do not require the sustained suppression of fundamental characteristics, and that are compatible with the kind of life one is trying to build. Personality knowledge contributes to this goal by providing more accurate self-knowledge (understanding one's actual traits rather than idealized self-perceptions), more realistic assessment of environmental demands (understanding what different roles, organizations, and career paths actually require), and more effective advocacy for the conditions one genuinely needs.

---

## Epilogue

Personality does not determine career outcomes — but it shapes the environments in which one will most effectively perform, the occupational contexts that will be most naturally sustaining, the roles in which one's genuine strengths will be most available, and the work cultures in which the psychological cost of professional engagement will be most manageable. Knowing this, specifically and accurately, is among the most practically valuable applications of personality knowledge available. The authentic work life is built from that knowledge, applied with both ambition and realistic self-acceptance to the specific circumstances of a specific career.
