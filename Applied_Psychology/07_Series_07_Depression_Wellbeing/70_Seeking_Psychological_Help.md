# Seeking Psychological Help
## How to Find the Right Support and Make the Most of It

**Series 7: Depression and Mental Wellbeing — Book 10 of 10**

---

## Prologue

The decision to seek psychological help is, for many people, one of the most difficult decisions they will make — not because the need is unclear but because the barriers are substantial. The barriers are multiple: stigma (the fear of being judged for needing help); uncertainty (not knowing what kind of help is available or appropriate); practical obstacles (cost, access, time, knowing where to start); and the specific features of the conditions that make help-seeking most necessary (depression reduces motivation and initiative; anxiety produces avoidance; shame reduces the willingness to disclose).

Understanding what psychological help actually looks like, how to find it, what to expect from it, how to evaluate it, and how to make the most of it is not information that most people have before they need it. The consequence is that many people either delay help-seeking until conditions are significantly worse than they needed to be, or engage with ineffective help without understanding how to identify this or seek something better.

Jonas had been considering therapy for eight months before he made the first appointment. The decision itself had required more psychological work than he had anticipated: working through the belief that needing help meant something was fundamentally wrong with him rather than something was temporarily hard; finding out what therapy actually involved (he had been surprised to discover it was not primarily about lying on a couch describing his childhood); and navigating the practical obstacle of finding someone accepting new patients, in network, and available at a time he could actually attend.

## Chapter 1: When to Seek Help

The question of when psychological distress requires professional help — as opposed to the passage of time, the support of personal relationships, and the normal processes of self-regulation — is one that many people navigate without adequate guidance.

Research on help-seeking delay in mental health conditions documents the striking gap between onset of symptoms and first treatment contact: for depression, the median delay is approximately two years in the US; for anxiety disorders, the median delay is up to a decade for some conditions. This delay reflects the stigma, uncertainty, and structural barriers described above, but it also reflects a genuine difficulty in knowing when distress has crossed the threshold from normal and self-resolving to clinically significant and requiring professional attention.

The indicators that professional help is warranted include: persistence (symptoms that have been present for more than a few weeks without improvement); functional impairment (symptoms that are significantly affecting work, relationships, physical health, or daily functioning); safety concerns (any thoughts of self-harm, suicide, or harm to others); subjective distress (experiencing levels of distress that feel genuinely unmanageable); and treatment failure (distress that has not responded to reasonable self-care efforts including exercise, sleep improvement, social support, and time).

## Chapter 2: Types of Professional Help

The landscape of professional psychological help is more diverse and more differentiated than most people appreciate, and understanding the differences between types of providers and types of treatment is important for making informed decisions about help-seeking.

Psychiatrists are medical doctors (MD or DO) who have completed residency training in psychiatry. Their primary scope is biological: assessment and treatment of mental health conditions using medications. In many jurisdictions, psychiatrists have reduced their involvement in psychotherapy due to the relative scarcity of psychiatrist time and the comparable availability of psychotherapy from non-medical providers. In complex cases (severe depression, psychotic disorders, bipolar disorder, conditions requiring careful medication management) psychiatrist involvement is often essential.

Psychologists (typically PhD or PsyD) have doctoral-level training in psychological assessment and treatment. Clinical psychologists are specifically trained in the evidence-based psychological therapies (CBT, DBT, IPT, ACT, and others) and are the highest-credentialed providers of psychotherapy. In the US, most psychologists cannot prescribe medications (with the exception of military psychologists and psychologists in some states with prescribing privileges); they work in collaboration with prescribing providers when medication is indicated.

Licensed counselors, social workers (LCSW), and marriage and family therapists (MFT) have master's-level training and provide psychotherapy across a range of presentations. The evidence base for these providers is comparable to that for doctoral-level providers in most presentation areas. Access to master's-level providers is typically greater than to doctoral-level providers, and the cost is typically lower.

## Chapter 3: Types of Therapy — A Practical Guide

The diversity of therapeutic approaches can be overwhelming to navigate from the outside. A practical guide to the most evidence-supported approaches, and the presentations for which each is most specifically indicated, is useful context for the help-seeking process.

Cognitive Behavioral Therapy (CBT) is the most extensively researched of the psychotherapies, with the broadest evidence base across presentations. CBT is structured, goal-directed, and relatively time-limited (typically 12-20 sessions). It is well-suited for depression, anxiety disorders, OCD, insomnia, eating disorders, and many other conditions where cognitive and behavioral patterns are central maintaining factors. CBT requires active engagement between sessions — homework is a genuine component, not an optional add-on — and may not be the right fit for people who prefer less structured approaches.

Dialectical Behavior Therapy (DBT), developed by Marsha Linehan for borderline personality disorder and subsequently extended to other presentations, emphasizes four skill modules (mindfulness, distress tolerance, emotion regulation, interpersonal effectiveness) and combines individual therapy with skills group. DBT has the strongest evidence base for borderline personality disorder and significant evidence for eating disorders, self-harm, and chronic suicidality.

Eye Movement Desensitization and Reprocessing (EMDR), Prolonged Exposure (PE), and Cognitive Processing Therapy (CPT) are the trauma-specific treatments with the strongest evidence for PTSD. Acceptance and Commitment Therapy (ACT) emphasizes psychological flexibility and valued living over symptom reduction. Interpersonal Psychotherapy (IPT) targets interpersonal factors in depression and has specific evidence for postpartum depression, adolescent depression, and grief presentations.

## Chapter 4: Finding a Therapist — Practically

The practical process of finding a therapist is one of the most significant barriers to help-seeking and deserves specific, concrete guidance that is often unavailable to people navigating the process for the first time.

Starting points for finding a therapist include: referrals from a primary care physician or psychiatrist; referrals from trusted personal contacts (therapist recommendations from people who have had positive experiences with specific providers are often the highest-quality referrals); the therapist locators on professional association websites (the American Psychological Association's locator, the Psychology Today therapist directory, the SAMHSA locator for substance use treatment); and the employee assistance program (EAP) that many employers provide, which typically includes several free or low-cost sessions with a licensed therapist.

Practical criteria for selecting a therapist include: credentials and training (verifying that the therapist has appropriate licensure and training in evidence-based approaches relevant to the presenting concern); approach fit (whether the therapeutic approach is compatible with one's preferences and the nature of one's presenting concern); practical availability (whether the therapist has openings at compatible times and is accepting new patients); and insurance and cost (whether the therapist is in-network, what the out-of-pocket cost will be, and whether sliding-scale fees are available).

## Chapter 5: The Therapeutic Alliance

The therapeutic relationship — the quality of the working relationship between therapist and client — is one of the most consistently documented predictors of therapy outcome across all therapeutic approaches. Research by Michael Lambert and colleagues, synthesizing decades of psychotherapy outcome research, finds that common factors shared across therapeutic approaches — particularly the therapeutic alliance — account for approximately 30% of therapy outcome variance, compared to approximately 15% for specific technique effects.

The therapeutic alliance consists of three components identified by Bordin's influential model: agreement on goals (therapist and client agree on what therapy is working toward); agreement on tasks (therapist and client agree on the methods being used to reach those goals); and the bond (the quality of trust, warmth, and mutual regard between therapist and client). Research finds that alliance quality assessed in the early sessions is one of the strongest predictors of therapy outcome.

The implication for the client is important: the quality of the therapeutic relationship is not merely a pleasant feature of therapy but a clinically significant determinant of outcome. Feeling genuinely heard, respected, and collaborative in the therapeutic relationship is not a secondary consideration to the technical approach — it is a primary consideration. If the therapeutic relationship does not feel genuine and collaborative after three to four sessions, this is an important data point about fit rather than an indication that therapy doesn't work.

## Chapter 6: Getting the Most from Therapy

Research on what predicts good outcomes in therapy — beyond the therapeutic relationship and the specific treatment approach — documents several factors that clients can actively influence.

Preparation is associated with better outcomes: clients who come to sessions with a sense of what they want to work on, who can articulate the specific difficulties they are experiencing and their contexts, and who have reflected on what might be useful to explore produce more productive sessions than clients who come without preparation. Even a brief pre-session review of what has been happening since the last session and what feels most important to address improves session productivity.

Engagement between sessions — completing homework assignments, practicing skills introduced in sessions, noticing and recording relevant experiences — is consistently associated with better outcomes in CBT and related approaches. The skills practiced in therapy develop through practice, and the practice between sessions is where the majority of the skill-building occurs. Research on homework compliance in CBT finds that patients who complete homework more consistently show significantly better outcomes than those who do not.

Honest disclosure — telling the therapist about relevant thoughts, feelings, and experiences, including material that feels embarrassing or difficult to share — is essential for effective therapy. Research by Clara Hill and others documents that clients frequently hide material from their therapists, particularly material related to the therapeutic relationship itself (negative feelings about the therapist, doubts about the therapy, dissatisfaction with how sessions are going). Developing the capacity to disclose this material directly is itself a therapeutic achievement and is associated with better outcomes.

## Chapter 7: Medication and Therapy — Navigating the Decision

One of the most common and most practically significant decisions in help-seeking is whether to pursue medication, therapy, or both. The research on this decision has been described in Book 62; what is addressed here are the practical and psychological dimensions of the decision.

Medication is generally recommended as a first-line treatment (alongside therapy) for moderate-to-severe depression, for presentations with significant biological dimensions (melancholic features, significant neurovegetative symptoms, bipolar depression), and for conditions with a strong evidence base for pharmacological treatment (OCD responds particularly well to SSRIs; panic disorder responds well to both SSRIs and short-term benzodiazepines in specific circumstances). The research does not support medication as a preferred first-line treatment for mild depression or for the anxiety and stress-related conditions that predominate in primary care.

The stigma associated with psychiatric medication remains a significant barrier to adherence for many people. Research on medication adherence in depression finds that approximately 50% of patients discontinue antidepressant medication within the first three months of treatment, before clinical benefit has typically developed, often due to side effects in the early weeks and the misattribution of early side effects as evidence that the medication is making things worse. Understanding the typical trajectory of antidepressant response (early side effects that typically resolve in 2-4 weeks; gradual clinical benefit beginning at 4-6 weeks) reduces premature discontinuation significantly.

## Chapter 8: When Therapy Isn't Working

Knowing when therapy is not working and what to do about it is important information that is rarely provided to people seeking therapy. The research on therapy non-response documents that approximately 40-50% of people who begin a standard course of therapy do not achieve their treatment goals, and that early response trajectory (improvement or lack thereof in the first four to eight sessions) is a significant predictor of ultimate outcome.

A useful framework for evaluating whether therapy is working involves: regular assessment of symptom levels (many evidence-based therapists use standardized questionnaires such as the PHQ-9 or GAD-7 to track symptoms over time); honest evaluation of whether the therapeutic relationship feels genuine and collaborative; assessment of whether the focus of therapy seems relevant to the presenting concerns; and the presence of some change — in understanding, in behavior, in symptoms, or in the quality of one's experience — even if the change is modest.

If therapy does not appear to be working after an adequate trial (typically 8-12 sessions with a competent therapist using an evidence-based approach), the appropriate next steps include: discussing the lack of progress directly with the current therapist (this conversation is itself therapeutically valuable and may identify modifiable factors); seeking a consultation with a different provider for a second opinion; considering whether an alternative treatment approach might be more appropriate; and considering evaluation for medication if medication has not previously been tried.

## Chapter 9: Jonas's Journey

Jonas's first therapy appointment was different from what he had expected. There was no couch; he sat across from his therapist in what looked like an ordinary office. The first session was primarily assessment — the therapist asking about the history of his difficulties, their context, how they affected his daily life, what he had already tried. It was more like a structured conversation than anything else, and what surprised him most was how clearly the therapist seemed to understand his situation before the end of the first session — more clearly, in some respects, than he had himself.

The first few weeks were uncomfortable in a different way than he had expected. Not uncomfortable because the therapist was unkind — she was consistently warm and direct. Uncomfortable because the work of examining his patterns, which he had been avoiding for several years, was genuinely effortful. The homework felt like homework. The improvement, when it came, was gradual rather than sudden: less a transformation than a slow, measurable shift in his average level of distress and his average capacity to meet the days as they arrived.

What he valued most, months in, was something he had not anticipated valuing: the specific experience of being consistently, genuinely heard by someone whose attention was entirely on him, with no social reciprocity required. His friendships required reciprocity — he could share difficulty with his closest friends, but they shared difficulty back, and the mutual support of friendship was both valuable and limiting. Therapy had no reciprocity requirement. It was specifically designed to provide the kind of sustained, non-judgmental attention to his inner life that he had not previously had access to.

## Chapter 10: Help as Strength

The cultural association of help-seeking with weakness — the idea that needing psychological help signals inadequacy or failure — is one of the most damaging misconceptions in the broader mental health landscape. The research on help-seeking and outcomes demonstrates the opposite: people who seek help appropriately and use it effectively show better outcomes than people who do not, not because they are weaker but because they have accessed a resource that produces genuine change.

The willingness to seek help when it is needed is an expression of the same values that the psychological research consistently identifies as central to wellbeing: the realistic assessment of one's situation; the belief that one's actions (including the action of seeking help) can influence one's outcomes; the courage to be seen in difficulty; and the relational engagement that psychological health requires. Help-seeking, understood correctly, is not a concession to weakness — it is one of the most sophisticated and most evidence-supported choices available to a person who is suffering and wants to be well.

The series on Depression and Mental Wellbeing that concludes with this book has aimed to provide a comprehensive, research-grounded account of what depression is, how it is treated, how it can be prevented, and how wellbeing is built and sustained across a life. The most important single contribution that this knowledge can make is to reduce the gap between the experience of psychological difficulty and the decision to seek help for it. That gap costs real suffering, real time, and real wellbeing. Closing it — through knowledge, through reduced stigma, and through the specific practical information provided here — is the most direct application of everything this series has described.

## Epilogue

Seeking psychological help is not the end of the story of one's struggle with mental health; it is often the beginning of the most productive chapter of that story. The help available is genuine, the evidence base for its efficacy is substantial, and the experience of receiving it — though rarely easy — is often one of the most valuable experiences available to a person who has been struggling. The barriers to seeking it are real and deserve acknowledgment; they are not, however, reasons to delay. The cost of delay is paid in suffering; the benefit of engagement is paid in wellbeing. The calculus, for most people and most presentations, strongly favors the engagement.
