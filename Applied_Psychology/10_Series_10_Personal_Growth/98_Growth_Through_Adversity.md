## Prologue

The idea that suffering can produce growth — that hardship, loss, and trauma are not only costs to be survived but experiences that sometimes produce genuine positive change — is among the oldest and most widely held intuitions in human culture. Every wisdom tradition has its version: Nietzsche's "that which does not kill me makes me stronger," the Stoic advocacy of voluntary hardship, the Buddhist teaching on suffering as the path to enlightenment, the Christian theology of redemptive suffering. That such different traditions converged on the same basic claim suggests they were responding to something genuinely observable in human experience.

Research psychology has engaged this question empirically, and its findings are more precise and more qualified than the popular formulations suggest. Adversity does sometimes produce growth. It also sometimes produces lasting damage. The difference between these outcomes is substantially predicted by specific psychological and social factors that the research has identified. Understanding which factors determine whether adversity produces growth or harm — and what can be done to influence those factors — is one of the most practically important contributions of the scientific psychology of resilience.

## Chapter 1: What Adversity Does

Research on the psychological effects of major adversity — serious illness, loss of loved ones, trauma, significant life disruption — documents a range of outcomes that are more varied and more time-sensitive than the popular binary of "recovery" versus "not recovery" captures.

George Bonanno's research on resilience to loss and trauma has identified four distinct trajectories that people show following major adverse events: chronic dysfunction (sustained high distress and functional impairment); delayed reaction (initial apparent resilience followed by later emergence of distress); recovery (initial distress followed by gradual return to prior functioning); and resilience (minimal disruption to functioning throughout the post-adversity period). Research finds that the resilience trajectory is more common than the mental health literature had previously assumed — approximately 35-65% of people exposed to severe adverse events show the resilience trajectory.

Bereavement research by Bonanno and others documents that the "grief work hypothesis" — the belief that healthy processing of grief requires a period of intense emotional processing and that failure to show such distress reflects pathological avoidance — is not supported by the evidence. People who show minimal distress following significant loss are not suppressing or avoiding their grief; many of them are genuinely and adaptively resilient, and their initial positive outcome predicts positive long-term outcomes.

## Chapter 2: Post-Traumatic Growth

Richard Tedeschi and Lawrence Calhoun coined the term "post-traumatic growth" (PTG) to describe the positive psychological changes that some people experience as a result of their struggles with highly challenging life circumstances. Research on PTG documents five primary domains of growth: personal strength ("I discovered that I'm stronger than I thought I was"); new possibilities ("new opportunities are available to me"); relating to others ("I have a greater sense of closeness with others"); appreciation of life ("I have a greater appreciation for the value of my own life"); and spiritual change ("I have a better understanding of spiritual matters").

Research finds that PTG is reported by 30-70% of people who have experienced major adversity, with rates varying substantially by the type of adversity, its severity, and the population studied. PTG does not occur because the adversity was positive — it occurs because the struggle with the adversity's consequences sometimes produces changes that would not have occurred without that struggle. The growth is not a silver lining of the adversity itself but a product of the meaning-making and psychological work that the adversity makes necessary.

The relationship between PTG and actual psychological wellbeing is more complex than the PTG reports alone suggest: research by Zoellner and Maercker distinguishes between "constructive PTG" (genuine positive change accompanied by improved function) and "illusory PTG" (positive self-reports that do not reflect genuine functional improvement, possibly representing defensive self-enhancement). The distinction is important for understanding what the PTG literature actually shows.

## Chapter 3: Resilience and Its Components

Research on resilience — the capacity to maintain or rapidly restore functioning following significant adversity — has identified specific factors that predict resilient outcomes, across the domains of individual psychology, social relationships, and community support.

Individual factors consistently associated with resilience include: positive emotionality (the capacity to experience positive emotions even in the context of adversity); flexible thinking (the ability to shift perspective and generate alternative interpretations of difficult situations); efficacy beliefs (confidence in one's capacity to manage challenges); meaning-making (the ability to find meaning in or through the adverse experience); and acceptance (the willingness to acknowledge reality as it is, without excessive struggle against what cannot be changed).

Research by Norman Garmezy, Emmy Werner, and others on longitudinal studies of children exposed to significant adversity — poverty, parental mental illness, community violence — found that the primary individual protective factor was not the absence of risk but the presence of at least one stable, caring relationship with a competent adult. The single protective relationship — grandparent, teacher, community member — was more predictive of resilient outcomes than any combination of individual psychological factors, highlighting the fundamentally relational basis of resilience.

## Chapter 4: Meaning-Making and Growth

The specific mechanism that most consistently distinguishes adversity that produces growth from adversity that produces only damage is meaning-making: the process by which people make sense of their experience in ways that integrate it into their understanding of themselves and their world.

Crystal Park's meaning-making model proposes that adverse events produce distress when they violate the person's "global meaning" — their fundamental assumptions about how the world works, what they deserve, and what makes life worth living. Recovery from adversity involves either assimilating the event into the existing global meaning framework (finding a way to understand how this experience fits what one already believed about the world) or accommodating the global meaning framework to incorporate the new experience (modifying one's worldview to encompass the reality that the event has revealed). The depth and quality of this meaning-making process substantially determines whether adversity produces growth or chronic distress.

Research by Janoff-Bulman on "shattered assumptions" documents that the most severe adverse events are those that challenge or destroy the person's most fundamental assumptions — that the world is benevolent, that the world is meaningful, and that the self is worthy. Recovery from these events requires not only processing the event but rebuilding the assumption framework that the event has destroyed — a deep psychological process that takes time and typically requires social support.

## Chapter 5: Social Support in Adversity

Research on social support and adversity recovery consistently documents that the quality and responsiveness of social support is among the strongest predictors of both resilience and growth following adversity. The research also documents that the provision of social support is more complex than simple availability — support that is poorly calibrated to the person's needs can be unhelpful or harmful.

Research on "perceived social support" — the subjective sense that support is available and will be provided if needed — finds that it is more consistently associated with positive outcomes than "received social support" (the actual amount of help received). The mechanism: perceived support buffers the stress response (reducing the physiological and psychological impact of adversity) even when the support is not actually mobilized, apparently because the knowledge of its availability reduces the experienced threat of adversity.

Research on "responsive" versus "non-responsive" support documents specific features of support that are consistently helpful: validation of the person's emotional experience (acknowledgment that their reaction is understandable); practical assistance that matches the person's actual needs; and respect for the person's autonomy in managing the adversity rather than taking over or directing the person's response. Non-responsive support — dismissing the person's emotional experience, providing unsolicited advice, or providing practical help that was not wanted — consistently produces poorer outcomes than well-calibrated responsive support.

## Chapter 6: The Serenity Prayer as Psychology

The Serenity Prayer — "God grant me the serenity to accept the things I cannot change, the courage to change the things I can, and the wisdom to know the difference" — captures one of the most practically important distinctions in adversity psychology: the distinction between controllable and uncontrollable aspects of one's situation, and the differential value of acceptance versus action in response to each.

Research on control and adversity documents that the allocation of coping effort between acceptance (for uncontrollable aspects) and problem-solving (for controllable aspects) is one of the most reliable predictors of adaptive functioning in adversity. Research by Susan Folkman and Richard Lazarus on stress and coping identifies "primary appraisal" (assessing whether the situation is threatening) and "secondary appraisal" (assessing what can be done about it) as the key cognitive processes that determine coping strategy selection. Misallocation — applying active problem-solving to uncontrollable events, or passive acceptance to controllable ones — consistently produces worse outcomes than matched allocation.

Research on ACT (Acceptance and Commitment Therapy) in adversity contexts finds that the explicit practice of acceptance — deliberately acknowledging and sitting with aspects of adversity that cannot be changed rather than struggling against them — is associated with lower distress and better functional outcomes than equivalent struggle for change where change is not possible. The research does not endorse passive resignation; it endorses the accurate discrimination between what is and is not within one's capacity to change, and the appropriate application of acceptance where change is not available.

## Chapter 7: Hardiness and Posttraumatic Growth

Salvatore Maddi's research on "psychological hardiness" — a personality constellation of commitment, control, and challenge — provides a framework for understanding individual differences in resilience that has received significant empirical support.

Commitment is the tendency to remain engaged with the challenges of life rather than withdrawing into alienation; control is the belief that one has the capacity to influence the circumstances of one's life rather than being powerless; and challenge is the interpretation of stressful change as a normal feature of life that provides opportunities for growth rather than a threatening catastrophe. Research on hardiness documents that higher hardiness predicts lower distress, better health outcomes, and higher performance under stressful conditions, apparently by shaping the appraisal of adversity in ways that increase coping efficacy.

Research on the development of hardiness — whether it can be increased through deliberate practice — finds modest evidence for specific intervention programs developed by Maddi and colleagues that combine cognitive reappraisal training (developing the challenge orientation), self-knowledge work (developing the commitment orientation), and efficacy building (developing the control orientation). The interventions show positive effects on hardiness-related outcomes in military, corporate, and community settings, suggesting that hardiness is not simply fixed temperament but a developable set of orientations.

## Chapter 8: Narrative and Adversity

Research on narrative and adversity — the role of storytelling in the processing and integration of adverse experience — documents that the stories people tell about their adverse experiences are among the most powerful determinants of their psychological outcomes.

James Pennebaker's research on expressive writing (discussed in Book 59) finds that writing about traumatic experiences — specifically, writing that involves both emotional processing and cognitive meaning-making (narrative construction of why the event happened and what it means) — produces significant improvements in physical and psychological health outcomes compared to writing about trivial topics. The health benefits are mediated by the degree to which the writing produces genuine insight and meaning-making rather than simply emotional discharge.

Research on narrative coherence in trauma processing finds that trauma memories are often characterized by fragmentation — the inability to construct a coherent narrative of what happened, when, and what it meant — and that the development of narrative coherence (a clear, integrated account that places the event in temporal and causal context) is one of the specific processes that trauma therapy facilitates. Narrative therapies explicitly focus on this meaning-making process, helping clients construct life narratives in which adverse experiences are integrated rather than encapsulated as incomprehensible intrusions.

## Chapter 9: What the Research Cannot Say

Intellectual honesty requires acknowledging what the research on adversity and growth cannot say. It cannot say that all adversity produces growth, or that greater adversity produces greater growth, or that the growth produced is worth the suffering that enabled it. It cannot say that people should welcome adversity, or that failure to grow from adversity represents personal failure.

Research by Joseph and Linley on the range of adversity outcomes finds that adversity more often produces no growth, or produces both growth and distress in the same person simultaneously, than it produces pure growth. The person who has grown from a cancer diagnosis also suffers from cancer; these are not mutually exclusive and the growth does not redeem the suffering or make the disease worth having. Post-traumatic growth is a finding about what sometimes happens despite adversity, not what happens because of it.

The research is also honest that some adversity produces lasting damage rather than growth — particularly severe, chronic adversity that overwhelms the meaning-making and social support resources that growth requires. Acknowledging this is not pessimism; it is the accuracy that prevents the harmful implication that people who have not grown from their adversity have somehow failed.

## Chapter 10: Toward a Psychology of Flourishing Through Adversity

The research on adversity and growth supports a psychology of flourishing through adversity — not in spite of adversity, or after adversity, but through it: the recognition that the deepest human development sometimes occurs precisely at the intersection of genuine challenge and genuine engagement, where the demand exceeds one's current capacity and must either be retreated from or grown toward.

This is not advocacy for unnecessary hardship. It is the recognition that some degree of adversity is inevitable, that the psychological orientation toward adversity significantly determines its outcome, and that the specific factors that support growth through adversity — social support, meaning-making, acceptance of what cannot be changed, challenge orientation toward what can — are developable through specific practices and deliberate cultivation. The person who has these resources is not protected from adversity; they are better equipped to find in it the growth that adversity sometimes, genuinely, contains.

---

## Epilogue

Adversity is not optional. What is at least partly optional is the orientation with which one meets it: the meanings one constructs, the support one accepts, the discrimination between what can and cannot be changed, and the willingness to allow difficulty to reveal capacity and connection that comfort does not require. The research on growth through adversity does not make hard things easier; it does make them more legible — and legibility is the beginning of navigation.
