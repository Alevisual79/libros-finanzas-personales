## Prologue

The man who would become one of the most influential figures in positive psychology, Martin Seligman, has described a moment in his garden in 1998 when his five-year-old daughter Nikki — annoyed at being ignored while he weeded — told him that she had decided to stop whining on her fifth birthday, and asked him why, if she could do that, he couldn't stop being grumpy. The exchange struck Seligman with force: the discipline of psychology, he recognized in that moment, had focused almost exclusively on the repair of deficiency. It had almost nothing to say about the development of what was already good.

The Values in Action (VIA) Classification of Character Strengths — developed by Seligman and Christopher Peterson in collaboration with a team of scholars across psychology, philosophy, and history — was the first systematic attempt to do for human virtue what the Diagnostic and Statistical Manual had done for human pathology: create a comprehensive, theoretically grounded, empirically testable taxonomy of the positive features of human character. Published in 2004, the VIA classification identified 24 character strengths organized under six virtues, provided assessment tools for measuring individual strength profiles, and generated a research program on the relationship between character strengths and wellbeing that has now produced thousands of studies.

## Chapter 1: What Character Strengths Are

Character strengths, as defined in the VIA framework, are positive personality traits that are morally valued, cross-culturally recognized, developmentally malleable, and associated with beneficial outcomes for the individual and for others. Peterson and Seligman distinguished character strengths from talents (natural abilities like athletic skill or musical aptitude that are not morally evaluated) and from interests (preferences that may or may not involve character) by their dual criteria of moral valuation and cross-cultural recognition.

The 24 VIA strengths are: Creativity, Curiosity, Judgment/Critical Thinking, Love of Learning, Perspective, Bravery, Perseverance, Honesty, Zest, Love, Kindness, Social Intelligence, Teamwork, Fairness, Leadership, Forgiveness, Humility, Prudence, Self-Regulation, Appreciation of Beauty and Excellence, Gratitude, Hope, Humor, and Spirituality. These are organized under six virtues: Wisdom (creativity through perspective), Courage (bravery through zest), Humanity (love through social intelligence), Justice (teamwork through leadership), Temperance (forgiveness through self-regulation), and Transcendence (appreciation through spirituality).

Research on the cross-cultural replicability of the VIA framework finds consistent recognition of the 24 strengths across cultures, supporting the claim that the identified strengths represent universal human virtues rather than culturally specific values. Rank-ordering of strengths — which strengths are most highly valued — shows more cultural variation, with different cultures prioritizing different strengths.

## Chapter 2: Identifying Signature Strengths

The VIA Survey — a 240-item questionnaire available free at www.viacharacter.org — provides a profile of an individual's 24 strengths ranked from highest to lowest. The top strengths in the profile — typically the top 4-7 — are designated "signature strengths" and are the focus of most practical applications of the VIA framework.

Research on the measurement properties of the VIA Survey finds adequate to good reliability and moderate evidence for factorial validity of the six-virtue structure. The survey has been completed by millions of people globally and provides the most extensively used measure of character strengths in both research and applied settings.

Research on the signature strength concept documents that signature strengths have specific features that distinguish them from the other strengths in a person's profile: they are experienced as authentic ("this is really me"), energizing (using them produces engagement and vitality rather than depletion), and natural (using them feels effortless rather than forced). Research by Peterson and Seligman suggests that signature strengths are the traits most reliably associated with the person's psychological wellbeing, most reliably expressed across situations, and most productively developed.

## Chapter 3: Strengths and Wellbeing

The most extensively researched application of the VIA framework is the relationship between character strengths engagement and subjective wellbeing. Research by Seligman, Park, Rashid, and Uhl finds consistent positive associations between signature strength use and multiple wellbeing indicators: positive affect, life satisfaction, engagement, meaning, and positive relationships.

Research on the specific strengths most consistently associated with wellbeing finds that the "transcendence" strengths — gratitude, hope, zest, and humor — are among the strongest predictors of life satisfaction across studies and samples. Strengths in the "humanity" cluster — love and social intelligence — are also consistently among the highest wellbeing predictors, consistent with the broader research on social connection and happiness documented in Book 88.

Research on the mechanisms connecting strength use to wellbeing finds several pathways: strength engagement produces positive affect (because people feel energized when using their strengths); strength engagement supports self-determination (it involves autonomous expression of genuine values and capacities, satisfying the autonomy and competence needs identified in SDT); and strength engagement improves performance (producing the mastery experiences that support self-efficacy and wellbeing).

## Chapter 4: Strengths in Work

Research on character strengths in the workplace documents significant relationships between strength use and work engagement, job satisfaction, and performance — and has generated practical applications in organizational development, leadership, and team design.

Research by Linley, Nielsen, Gillett, and Biswas-Diener finds that employees who report using their strengths more at work show higher engagement, higher performance, and higher job satisfaction than those who do not. Research on the development of strengths-based management — in which managers identify and develop employees' strengths rather than focusing primarily on correcting weaknesses — finds positive effects on engagement and performance in organizational studies.

The Gallup Organization's research on employee engagement — tracking millions of employees across thousands of organizations — identified "at work, I have the opportunity to do what I do best every day" as one of twelve items that most reliably predicted performance, retention, and profitability. This item — which operationalizes the strengths-use opportunity concept — was among the strongest predictors in the battery, supporting the practical importance of strengths-based work environments for organizational outcomes.

## Chapter 5: Strengths and Relationships

Research on character strengths in intimate relationships documents that partner similarity in top strengths is associated with higher relationship satisfaction, and that deliberate expression of gratitude and love strengths is associated with higher partner wellbeing and relationship quality.

Research by Vella-Brodrick and colleagues on strengths and relationships finds that people who recognize and appreciate their partners' strengths — as opposed to focusing primarily on their weaknesses — report higher relationship satisfaction and show more constructive conflict behavior. The "strengths-spotting" practice — deliberately attending to and acknowledging the character strengths expressed by significant others — has been developed as a relationship intervention with initial positive evidence.

Research on parenting and character strengths development finds that parents who use strengths-based language with their children (acknowledging and labeling children's expressed strengths rather than focusing primarily on their deficits or rule violations) produce children with higher self-efficacy, higher prosocial behavior, and higher resilience. The parenting practice operationalizes the same growth mindset principle (attending to capacity and effort rather than only to performance outcomes) in the specific language of character strengths.

## Chapter 6: The "Overuse" of Strengths

One of the most practically important and most frequently overlooked aspects of the VIA framework is the finding that character strengths can be overused — expressed at too high an intensity for the specific situation — with negative consequences for the person and for others.

Research by Niemiec on the overuse and underuse of character strengths documents characteristic patterns: bravery overused becomes recklessness; perseverance overused becomes rigidity and stubbornness; honesty overused becomes bluntness and disregard for others' emotional needs; self-regulation overused becomes rigidity and inhibition. Each strength, expressed in excess, produces the characteristic difficulties that research on personality extreme scores has documented.

The concept of "strengths balance" — using strengths at the appropriate level for the specific situation — provides a more nuanced framework for strength development than simple strength amplification. Research finds that the development of "meta-cognitive awareness" of one's own strength deployment — the capacity to notice when a strength is being over- or under-expressed and to modulate accordingly — is the most sophisticated form of strength development available and is associated with higher interpersonal effectiveness.

## Chapter 7: Developing Character Strengths

Research on the malleability of character strengths documents that strengths are developable — not fixed at birth or in childhood but genuinely modifiable through deliberate practice and appropriate experience. This finding is practically important: it means that the identification of signature strengths is not simply descriptive but actionable.

Research on strengths-based intervention programs — which typically involve assessing one's VIA strength profile, identifying one's top signature strengths, and deliberately planning to use these strengths in new ways across different domains of life — finds significant and sustained improvements in wellbeing outcomes. Seligman and colleagues' research on the "use your signature strengths in a new way" intervention finds sustained wellbeing improvements at six-month follow-up, making it one of the more durable positive psychology interventions in the research literature.

Research on the development of lower-ranked strengths — those not in the signature strength profile — documents that deliberate practice can develop virtually any of the 24 strengths, though typically with more effort and lower intrinsic motivation than the development of signature strengths. The practical implication: developing strengths that are already at the top of one's profile is easier and more rewarding than developing those at the bottom, and the most effective growth strategy typically involves the amplification and creative application of existing signature strengths rather than the labor of developing absent ones.

## Chapter 8: Character Strengths in Difficult Times

Research on character strengths and resilience documents that specific strengths — particularly bravery, perseverance, hope, self-regulation, and forgiveness — are especially associated with resilient responses to adversity. Research on post-traumatic growth (discussed in Book 92) finds that the growth that follows adversity is characterized by the development or deepening of several character strengths, particularly gratitude, appreciation of beauty, love, and spirituality.

Research by Tedeschi and McNally on resilience and character strengths in military personnel finds that pre-existing character strengths profile significantly predicts resilience outcomes after combat exposure — not through any single strength but through the combination of strengths available in the individual's profile. Research on strength-based approaches to adversity — helping people identify and deploy their existing strengths in response to difficult circumstances rather than only addressing their deficits and symptoms — finds positive effects on resilience and recovery outcomes.

Research on the recovery from depression documents that the engagement of character strengths — even when depression makes them feel less accessible — is among the most effective behavioral activation strategies for improving mood. Research by Rashid and Seligman on positive psychotherapy (which incorporates character strengths work as a core component alongside the standard therapeutic approaches) finds outcomes comparable to cognitive-behavioral therapy for mild to moderate depression, with the additional benefit of improvements in meaning and engagement that standard depression treatment does not typically produce.

## Chapter 9: Strengths-Based Living

The practical application of character strengths research to daily life — strengths-based living — involves three inter-related practices: awareness (identifying one's signature strengths and recognizing when they are being expressed); appreciation (valuing one's strengths and the strengths of others); and application (deliberately deploying signature strengths in new contexts, situations, and ways).

Research on brief strengths-based interventions with healthy populations finds consistent positive effects on wellbeing that are sustained over months — a durability that many brief positive psychology interventions fail to achieve. The mechanism: strengths awareness and application creates new positive feedback loops (using strengths produces positive affect and competence experiences, which reinforce further strengths use) that are self-sustaining in ways that one-time insight experiences are not.

The workplace application — identifying one's signature strengths and finding or creating ways to express them more fully in one's work — is the job crafting approach described in Book 80 applied through the specific lens of character strengths. Research on strengths-based job crafting finds significant positive effects on work engagement and job satisfaction, with particularly strong effects for workers in roles where their current job design provides little natural opportunity for signature strength expression.

## Chapter 10: The Virtuous Person

The VIA framework is ultimately a contemporary scientific treatment of an ancient philosophical question: what does it mean to be a good person, and how does one become one? The empirical findings of the VIA research program have produced an account that is both more specific and more accessible than most philosophical treatments, and that connects the moral question to the wellbeing question in a way that confirms what moral philosophers have argued for millennia: living virtuously and living well are not in tension but are complementary.

The research on character strengths provides an answer to the question of personal growth that transcends the deficit-repair model that has dominated psychology: personal growth is not only the elimination of pathology and the correction of deficiency but the development and expression of what is genuinely best in oneself. Understanding and investing in one's character strengths is, the research suggests, both the most authentic and the most effective path to the flourishing that genuine personal growth aims at.

---

## Epilogue

Character strengths are not decorative features of personality but functional capacities that produce genuine good — for the person who expresses them and for the people and communities that benefit from their expression. Knowing your strengths specifically and accurately, and finding ways to use them more fully and more creatively across the domains of your life, is among the most directly actionable prescriptions that psychology has produced. It is also one of the few prescriptions that is genuinely enjoyable to follow.
