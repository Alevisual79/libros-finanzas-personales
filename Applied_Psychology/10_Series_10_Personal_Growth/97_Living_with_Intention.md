## Prologue

There is a meaningful difference between a life that happens to a person and a life that a person designs. The distinction is not about control — both lives contain vast quantities of uncontrolled, unforeseeable, and unchosen events. It is about the orientation toward those events, the degree to which responses to them are deliberate rather than reflexive, and the degree to which the overall direction of the life reflects genuine choice rather than accumulated default.

Research in psychology documents both the difficulty and the importance of this distinction. On the difficulty side: research on automatic behavior, social influence, unconscious motivation, and cognitive bias consistently documents how much of human behavior is driven by processes that operate outside deliberate awareness and choice. On the importance side: research on autonomy, authenticity, and self-determination consistently documents that the experience of one's life as genuinely chosen — rather than merely happened-to — is one of the strongest predictors of psychological wellbeing and life satisfaction.

Living with intention is the practice of bringing deliberate awareness and deliberate choice to a larger proportion of one's behavior and one's life direction — not the impossible goal of making everything conscious, but the achievable goal of making the things that matter most more genuinely chosen. This book examines the psychology of intentional living: what it is, what produces it, what prevents it, and what the research shows about its relationship to the quality of life.

## Chapter 1: The Default Life

Research on behavioral automaticity documents that approximately 40-50% of daily behaviors occur through habitual rather than deliberate processes — a proportion that increases significantly in stable, familiar environments. The "default life" — the life that occurs when most behavior is habitual and most important choices are made through reactive rather than deliberate processes — is not necessarily a bad life, but it is not a chosen one.

Research on the costs of default living is primarily found in the end-of-life regret literature: the consistent finding that people's most significant regrets involve things they failed to do — authentic choices they failed to make, directions they failed to pursue — rather than things they did. The Bronnie Ware research on the regrets of the dying — which identified "I wish I'd had the courage to live a life true to myself, not the life others expected of me" as the most common deathbed regret — reflects the lived experience of default living in which others' expectations and situational pressures consistently overcame genuine personal direction.

Research on what distinguishes default from intentional behavior identifies awareness as the necessary condition: the capacity to notice that a choice is occurring, to recognize what one is actually choosing, and to engage deliberate evaluation of whether the automatic response serves one's genuine priorities. The first practice of intentional living is the cultivation of this awareness — the capacity to observe one's own behavior and its drivers with sufficient clarity to identify when genuine choice is available.

## Chapter 2: Values-Driven Behavior

The research on values-driven behavior — behavior motivated by one's genuine values rather than by situational pressures, others' expectations, or automatic habit — consistently finds it to be the most sustainable, most satisfying, and most productive form of motivation for long-term goal pursuit.

Research by Deci and Ryan on intrinsic motivation and autonomous regulation (discussed throughout this collection) documents that behavior experienced as genuinely expressive of one's own values — what SDT calls "integrated regulation" — shows higher persistence, higher quality, and greater association with wellbeing than behavior experienced as externally pressured or internally obligatory. The person who pursues a goal because it genuinely matters to them, rather than because they feel they should or because others expect it, is not only more likely to achieve the goal but more likely to experience the pursuit as meaningful.

Research on "ego-dystonic" versus "ego-syntonic" goals — goals that are or are not consistent with one's self-concept and values — finds that ego-dystonic goal pursuit is associated with significantly more psychological cost (anxiety, conflict, ambivalence) than ego-syntonic pursuit, even when the objective difficulty of the goals is comparable. Choosing goals that are genuinely consistent with one's values — taking the time to distinguish between what one actually wants and what one feels one should want — is not merely an aesthetic preference but a significant determinant of the wellbeing of the pursuit.

## Chapter 3: Mindfulness as Intentional Living

The practice of mindfulness — deliberate, non-judgmental attention to present-moment experience — is the most extensively researched psychological practice for developing the awareness that intentional living requires.

Research on the relationship between trait mindfulness (the habitual disposition toward present-moment awareness) and behavioral outcomes documents that higher trait mindfulness is associated with more consistent alignment between values and behavior, more deliberate (rather than reactive) responding to challenging situations, and higher sense of authenticity in behavior. Research by Brown and Ryan using the Mindful Attention Awareness Scale finds that higher trait mindfulness is associated with higher self-determination, higher wellbeing, and lower discrepancy between implicit and explicit self-concept measures — suggesting that mindfulness increases the correspondence between what people consciously value and how they actually behave.

Research on the mechanism of mindfulness effects documents that mindfulness training reduces the automaticity of habitual responses by increasing the brief moment of awareness between stimulus and response that allows deliberate choice to occur. Victor Frankl's observation — "Between stimulus and response there is a space. In that space is our power to choose our response. In our response lies our growth and our freedom" — describes the psychological function that mindfulness practice is documented to develop.

## Chapter 4: The Intention-Action Gap

One of the most consistently documented findings in behavioral science is the gap between intention and action — the reliable and substantial difference between what people intend to do and what they actually do. Understanding the psychology of this gap is essential for intentional living.

Research documents that intention alone is a weak predictor of behavior, accounting for approximately 30% of the variance in behavioral outcomes in meta-analytic reviews. The majority of the variance — the actions that do not follow from intentions — reflects the competing influences of habit, situational pressure, mood, fatigue, and the failure of intention to translate into specific behavior plans at the moment of decision.

Research by Gollwitzer on implementation intentions (discussed in Books 56 and 78) provides the most extensively validated intervention for closing the intention-action gap: the formation of specific "if-then" plans that link target behaviors to specific situational triggers. "When X occurs, I will do Y" plans increase the probability of intended behavior significantly over general goal intentions alone, apparently by pre-committing the automatic response to the specified trigger and thereby removing the need for deliberate decision at the moment of action.

## Chapter 5: Designing Your Day

The most practical expression of intentional living is the deliberate design of daily life — the explicit allocation of time, attention, and energy to the activities that reflect one's genuine priorities, rather than allowing these resources to be claimed by default by urgent demands and habit.

Research by Cal Newport and others on the "time blocking" approach to daily planning — pre-scheduling specific blocks of time for specific types of work and activity, rather than maintaining an undifferentiated open schedule — finds significant improvements in task completion, satisfaction, and alignment between time use and stated priorities. The time blocking approach operationalizes intentional living at the daily level: by deciding in advance how time will be used, it protects important activities from the moment-by-moment displacement by urgent demands that default scheduling produces.

Research on "evening review" and "morning review" practices — brief daily reflection on the previous day's alignment between values and behavior, and deliberate intention-setting for the upcoming day — documents improvements in values-behavior alignment and goal progress in practitioner samples. Research on the relationship between planned versus reactive behavior and subjective wellbeing finds that people who plan their day in advance report higher productivity and higher satisfaction than those who respond reactively to whatever presents itself.

## Chapter 6: The Examined Relationship

Intentional living extends from individual behavior to the design of relationships — the deliberate cultivation of the qualities, patterns, and commitments that produce the kind of relationships one genuinely wants rather than the relationships that default social dynamics produce.

Research on relationship quality and intentionality documents that couples who deliberately discuss and design their relationship — who explicitly address their values, their expectations, their needs, and the patterns they want to establish — report higher satisfaction and higher resilience to relationship stressors than those who allow relationship patterns to develop without deliberate attention. The Gottman Institute's "love maps" practice — in which partners deliberately learn and maintain knowledge of each other's inner world — operationalizes intentional relationship design in the domain of intimate knowledge.

Research on friendship and deliberate cultivation documents that the friendships that prove most sustaining and most meaningful over time are typically those that involved some degree of deliberate investment — initiating contact, creating shared experiences, expressing appreciation, and maintaining connection through the transitions and separations that erode default friendships. Research by Roberts and Dunbar on friendship maintenance finds that friendships that are not actively maintained decline in quality at predictable rates, and that the intention to maintain must be translated into specific actions at sufficient frequency.

## Chapter 7: Career Design

The application of intentional living to career development — what Newport has called "career capital theory" and what Wrzesniewski and Dutton have studied as "job crafting" — represents one of the most practically consequential domains of intentional design.

Research by Wrzesniewski on the relationship between job orientation and career development finds that people who actively craft their work — who deliberately modify the tasks, relationships, and perceived purpose of their role to increase its alignment with their skills and values — show higher job satisfaction, higher work identity, and better performance than those who accept their job as fixed. The job crafting research documents that intentional career design does not require changing positions; it can occur within existing roles through the deliberate shaping of how those roles are enacted.

Research on "career experiments" — small, deliberate explorations of potential career directions that provide experiential data about fit, interest, and capability — finds that people who systematically explore career directions through concrete experience make better career decisions than those who rely only on prediction and reflection. The career experiment approach operationalizes intentional career design by converting the abstract question "what career should I pursue?" into the concrete, testable question "what does this specific type of work actually feel like when I do it?"

## Chapter 8: Environmental Design as Intentional Living

The design of one's physical and social environment — the deliberate shaping of the contexts in which one lives and works to support one's intentions — is one of the most reliable and most under-utilized tools of intentional living.

Research on environmental design and behavior (discussed extensively in Book 76) documents that the physical environment is one of the most powerful determinants of behavior, often more powerful than deliberate intention. Designing one's environment to make desired behaviors easy (placing exercise equipment visibly, keeping healthy food accessible, eliminating digital distraction from the workspace) and undesired behaviors difficult (removing temptations from the environment, creating friction between impulse and action) is a form of intentional living that operates through environmental design rather than through in-the-moment willpower.

Research on "social environment design" — the deliberate cultivation of a social network whose members support one's intentions and share one's values — finds that the social environment is one of the strongest predictors of behavior, particularly for challenging behavior change goals. Jim Rohn's observation — "you are the average of the five people you spend the most time with" — is supported by the social contagion research documented in Book 87: the values, behaviors, and ambitions of close others systematically influence one's own through normative influence, emotional contagion, and reciprocal reinforcement.

## Chapter 9: The Intentional Life in Practice

The intentional life is not a perfectly designed, fully optimized existence in which every moment is deliberate and every behavior is consciously chosen. It is a life in which the things that matter most are more reliably protected, more consistently enacted, and more genuinely aligned with one's values than the default — a life in which the proportion of genuinely chosen behavior is higher than it would be without deliberate attention.

Research on the practice of intentional living suggests several specific habits that support it consistently: a morning practice that includes brief reflection on the day's priorities before the day's reactive demands begin; a weekly review that examines the previous week's time use and plans the next week with genuine priorities rather than only reactive scheduling; periodic values clarification that updates one's explicit understanding of what genuinely matters and how current choices align with it; and regular consultation of the long-term commitments and directions that short-term urgency pressure tends to displace.

None of these practices is time-consuming. All of them require the consistent prioritization of brief reflection against the constant pull of immediate demands — a small but significant form of discipline that the research on intentional living documents as consequential for the quality of the life it shapes.

## Chapter 10: The Chosen Life

The research on intentional living converges on a finding that psychological science and philosophical tradition share: the experience of one's life as genuinely chosen — as reflecting one's actual values and priorities rather than the accumulated residue of others' expectations and situational pressures — is one of the most significant contributors to psychological wellbeing and life satisfaction.

This is not an argument that all choice produces wellbeing, or that the deliberate life is always a better life. The research on autonomy documents that self-determination is beneficial when the self being expressed is genuinely one's own — when the choices reflect genuine values rather than reactive impulse or socially absorbed preference. Intentional living requires, as its prerequisite, the self-knowledge that makes genuine values-based choice possible. Without that prerequisite, intention is merely the deliberate execution of values that have not been examined.

With it, the intention to live deliberately transforms from a philosophical aspiration to a practical program: the specific, evidence-supported practices of daily design, values clarification, environmental shaping, and relationship cultivation that together constitute the life more genuinely one's own.

---

## Epilogue

Living with intention does not produce a perfect life. It produces a more genuinely one's own life — a life in which the gap between what one values and how one actually lives is narrower, in which the most important choices are made more deliberately and with more awareness of what is actually being chosen. That narrowing, the research shows, is among the most reliable sources of the satisfaction and meaning that constitute a life well lived.
