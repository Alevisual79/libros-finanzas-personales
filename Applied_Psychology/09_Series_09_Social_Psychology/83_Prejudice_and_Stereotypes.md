# Prejudice and Stereotypes
## The Psychology of Bias and What We Can Actually Do About It

**Series 9: Social Psychology and Influence — Book 3 of 10**

## Prologue

When Patricia Devine published her landmark 1989 paper on prejudice and automatic versus controlled processing, she made a claim that was both scientifically rigorous and personally important: stereotypes are automatically activated in the minds of virtually everyone who grows up in a culture that contains them, regardless of those people's conscious attitudes toward the groups the stereotypes describe. The research she conducted showed that both high-prejudice and low-prejudice individuals showed automatic activation of racial stereotypes when subliminally primed with racial cues. What distinguished low-prejudice individuals was not the absence of stereotype activation but the presence of controlled processes that overrode and suppressed the automatic response.

The implications were uncomfortable: even people who genuinely believed in equality and were genuinely committed to non-discriminatory behavior were carrying and activating stereotypes they did not endorse. The goal of reducing prejudice and discrimination could not rest on the elimination of automatic stereotype activation — that appeared to be a product of cultural learning that could not simply be unlearned. It had to rest on the development and deployment of the controlled processes that override the automatic response.

This is the framework — automatic activation, controlled response — that has organized three decades of subsequent prejudice and bias research, and that provides the most accurate and most practically useful account of what prejudice is, where it comes from, and what can actually be done to reduce its effects.

## Chapter 1: What Stereotypes Are and Where They Come From

Stereotypes are cognitive schemas — organized clusters of knowledge, expectations, and associations — about the characteristics of social groups. They are formed through the same learning processes that form all cognitive schemas: repeated exposure to group-attribute associations, through direct experience, cultural transmission, media representation, and statistical information.

Research on the accuracy of stereotypes — a contested but important question — finds that stereotypes are not uniformly inaccurate. Lee Jussim's research documents that many demographic stereotypes accurately reflect real statistical differences between groups in the specific attributes they describe, even while overgeneralizing (applying to individuals the statistical properties of groups) and while failing to account for the social processes (discrimination, structural disadvantage) that have produced the statistical differences.

The "kernel of truth" in stereotypes does not justify using them for individual-level predictions, which is where stereotyping produces its most direct harm. Group-level statistical regularities, even accurate ones, make relatively poor predictions about individuals because within-group variance is typically much larger than between-group variance for most measured attributes. The woman who is told, based on gender stereotypes, that she is likely to be less technically competent than her male colleagues is being subjected to a probabilistic inference that is not only unfair but also typically inaccurate for any specific individual.

## Chapter 2: Implicit Bias

The development of the Implicit Association Test (IAT) by Anthony Greenwald and Mahzarin Banaji in the 1990s provided a method for measuring the strength of automatic associations between concepts (e.g., Black/White faces and positive/negative evaluations) that bypasses the conscious control that can suppress explicit prejudice expression.

The IAT measures the differential ease (reaction time) of categorizing paired concepts: if "Black faces" and "bad words" are categorized together more easily than "Black faces" and "good words," the test-taker is said to show implicit bias toward associating Black faces with negativity. IAT results consistently show widespread implicit bias across samples, including in people who score low on explicit prejudice measures — a finding that appears consistent with Devine's dual-process model.

The IAT has been enormously influential but also controversially contested. Research on the predictive validity of the IAT — whether IAT scores predict discriminatory behavior — has produced mixed findings, with some meta-analyses finding significant but modest predictive relationships and others finding weak or inconsistent ones. A comprehensive meta-analysis by Oswald and colleagues found that IAT scores explained only a small proportion of variance in discriminatory behavior, raising questions about the IAT's utility as an individual-level bias predictor.

The current scientific consensus is nuanced: implicit bias as measured by the IAT and related measures is a real phenomenon that reflects genuine differences in automatic associations; the translation of these automatic associations into discriminatory behavior is substantially moderated by situational factors, motivation, and cognitive control resources; and the predictive validity of implicit bias measures for specific discriminatory behavior in specific situations is modest.

## Chapter 3: Stereotype Threat

Claude Steele and Joshua Aronson's research on stereotype threat — the concern that one's behavior may confirm a negative stereotype about one's group — has produced one of the most extensively replicated and most practically significant findings in social psychology.

Steele and Aronson's original 1995 experiments documented that Black college students performed significantly worse on a difficult verbal test when it was presented as a test of intellectual ability (activating stereotype threat about Black intellectual ability) than when it was presented as a problem-solving task unrelated to ability. White students' performance was unaffected by the framing condition. The performance difference occurred despite controlling for SAT scores — the stereotype threat produced a deficit beyond what could be attributed to prior academic preparation.

Subsequent research documented stereotype threat effects across a wide range of groups and domains: women in mathematics (activating the gender-math stereotype); White and Asian men in athletics (when outperformed by Black competitors and when this was made salient); older adults on memory tasks; and many other group-domain combinations. The research suggests that stereotype threat is a near-universal human experience — anyone who belongs to a group about which negative stereotypes exist in domains they care about can experience it.

## Chapter 4: The Origins of Prejudice

The psychological origins of prejudice — the negative attitudes toward social groups that stereotype threat, discrimination, and intergroup hostility reflect — have been studied through multiple theoretical frameworks that together illuminate different aspects of a complex phenomenon.

Realistic conflict theory (Sherif) proposes that intergroup hostility develops from genuine competition over scarce resources. Sherif's Robbers Cave experiment documented that randomly assigned groups developed strong hostility toward each other through competitive activities, and that hostility was reduced by the introduction of superordinate goals that required intergroup cooperation. Research supports the role of resource competition in intergroup conflict, but also documents that prejudice often exists in the absence of actual resource competition.

Social identity theory (Tajfel and Turner) proposes that prejudice reflects the need to maintain a positive social identity through favorable comparisons between one's in-group and out-groups. The minimal group paradigm research documented that mere categorization into groups produces in-group favoritism and out-group derogation, even in the absence of realistic conflict or prior relationship — suggesting that the need for positive social identity is a sufficient condition for the development of intergroup bias.

Authoritarian personality theory (Adorno and colleagues) and, more recently, social dominance theory (Pratto and Sidanius) and right-wing authoritarianism (Altemeyer) propose that prejudice reflects personality-level tendencies toward in-group conformity, hostility toward out-groups, and acceptance of social hierarchies — tendencies that develop through specific socialization experiences and that predict prejudice across target groups.

## Chapter 5: Reducing Prejudice — What Works

The history of prejudice reduction interventions is longer than the history of research on their effectiveness, and the gap between popular approaches and evidence-supported approaches is significant.

Contact theory — the hypothesis that positive intergroup contact reduces prejudice — is the most extensively researched prejudice reduction approach. Gordon Allport's original formulation (1954) proposed that contact reduces prejudice when it occurs under conditions of equal status, cooperative interdependence, institutional support, and the potential for friendship formation. A meta-analysis by Pettigrew and Tropp covering over 500 studies and over 250,000 participants found significant positive effects of contact on prejudice, confirming the basic hypothesis, though the four conditions did not all emerge as necessary moderators.

Perspective-taking interventions — activities that require participants to mentally adopt the perspective of an out-group member — have shown mixed but generally positive effects on prejudice measures. Research by Galinsky and Moskowitz finds that perspective-taking reduces automatic stereotype activation and increases the accessibility of out-group members' perspectives. The effect is more consistent on explicit prejudice measures than on implicit measures, suggesting that perspective-taking changes conscious attitudes more reliably than automatic associations.

Individuation — attending to the specific characteristics of individual group members rather than to group stereotypes — is among the most reliably effective short-term strategies for reducing discriminatory responses toward specific individuals. Research documents that when sufficient individuating information is available and the motivation to process it exists, group stereotypes are used less and individual information is weighted more in person evaluation.

## Chapter 6: Structural Versus Individual-Level Interventions

One of the most significant debates in contemporary prejudice research concerns the relative effectiveness of individual-level interventions (bias training, perspective-taking, stereotype suppression) versus structural interventions (policy changes, institutional accountability mechanisms, representation changes) for reducing discrimination.

Research on implicit bias training — widely deployed in organizations following research on implicit bias — finds disappointing evidence of effectiveness for changing discriminatory behavior. Meta-analyses consistently find that implicit bias training produces modest changes in measured implicit bias but limited evidence that these changes translate into reduced discriminatory behavior in real-world contexts. The gap between measured bias change and behavioral discrimination change suggests that the mechanisms linking implicit bias to behavior are not effectively addressed by training alone.

Research on structural interventions — changes to organizational policies, accountability mechanisms, and representation — finds more consistent evidence for reducing discrimination in specific contexts. Research by Kalev, Dobbin, and Kelly finds that organizations that implement formal diversity programs (affirmative action plans, diversity managers, diversity task forces) show more consistent demographic diversification than those that rely on voluntary diversity training. The structural accountability mechanisms produce behavioral change through mechanisms (accountability, monitoring, explicit goals) that do not depend on the changing of internal attitudes.

## Chapter 7: Microaggressions and Their Effects

The concept of microaggressions — brief, everyday exchanges that send denigrating messages to members of marginalized groups, whether or not they are intended to — has become both widely used and widely contested in contemporary discourse. The psychological research on their effects is instructive.

Research by Sue, Capodilupo, and colleagues on the racial microaggression taxonomy identifies several categories: microinsults (communications that convey rudeness or insensitivity), microinvalidations (communications that negate or nullify the experiences of people of color), and environmental microaggressions (systematic devaluing of certain groups in larger environments). The defining feature is that microaggressions are typically ambiguous: the recipient cannot be certain whether the communication reflects prejudice or innocent unfamiliarity, and this ambiguity itself is psychologically costly.

Research on the cumulative effects of microaggression exposure documents significant psychological costs, including increased anxiety, depression, rumination, and the cognitive burden of constant vigilance and uncertainty. The ambiguity of microaggressions makes them particularly costly: the recipient must simultaneously evaluate the communication's meaning, decide whether to respond, weigh the costs of responding against the costs of not responding, and continue functioning in the interaction — a sustained cognitive and emotional burden that is invisible to observers who do not experience it.

## Chapter 8: Prejudice Across Groups

The research literature on prejudice has historically focused primarily on racial prejudice in the United States, but a broader comparative perspective reveals that the psychological mechanisms documented in this research operate across group-attribute combinations worldwide.

Sexism — prejudice based on gender — is documented in research that distinguishes between hostile sexism (overtly negative attitudes toward women, particularly those who violate gender norms) and benevolent sexism (subjectively positive attitudes toward women that nonetheless restrict women's roles and autonomy). Research by Glick and Fiske documents that both forms of sexism are associated with discriminatory outcomes, with benevolent sexism presenting particular challenges because its apparently positive valence can obscure its limiting function.

Age discrimination (ageism) is documented as one of the most prevalent and most socially tolerated forms of prejudice in contemporary Western contexts. Research by Levy and colleagues finds that both positive age stereotypes ("old people are wise") and negative age stereotypes ("old people are incompetent") contribute to the same basic categorical treatment that reduces older adults' access to resources and opportunities.

## Chapter 9: The Individual's Role

The research on prejudice and stereotyping does not support either the position that individuals bear no responsibility for their biases (because they are automatic and culturally installed) or the position that individuals can simply choose not to be biased (because automatic activation occurs below the level of deliberate control). It supports a more nuanced position: individuals are not responsible for automatic stereotype activation, but they are responsible for the controlled processes they deploy in response.

Devine's research framework — that the critical variable for prejudiced versus non-prejudiced behavior is not the presence or absence of automatic activation but the presence or absence of effective controlled processes for overriding automatic responses — places the locus of individual responsibility at the right point. The person who notices that they are applying group stereotypes to an individual evaluation, who deliberately seeks individuating information, and who specifically checks their automatic response against more deliberate analysis is doing what the research supports as effective individual-level bias reduction.

The "prejudice habit-breaking" intervention developed by Devine and colleagues — which teaches people the automatic-controlled distinction, provides specific strategies for implementing controlled processing, and explicitly links this project to personal values of equality and fairness — has shown significant effects on implicit bias over time in participants who are both internally motivated to reduce prejudice and informed of the specific strategies for doing so.

## Chapter 10: An Honest Account

An honest account of prejudice and stereotyping resists both the dismissal of bias as a residual problem affecting only explicitly prejudiced people and the totalizing claim that bias makes genuine progress toward equality impossible. The research supports a more complex and ultimately more hopeful picture: prejudice and stereotyping are deeply embedded in human cognition and culture, they produce genuine harm, and they are also genuinely addressable through the combination of individual commitment, effective strategies, and structural changes that reduce the institutional reproduction of bias.

The complexity of the research — on what works and what doesn't, on the role of automatic versus controlled processes, on structural versus individual interventions — is not an obstacle to action; it is the empirical foundation for action that is actually likely to work, as opposed to action that feels morally satisfying but produces less change than it consumes in effort and good intentions.

---

## Epilogue

Stereotypes and prejudice are not products of bad people. They are products of human cognitive systems learning in cultural environments that contain group-attribute associations, activated by mechanisms that operate below the level of deliberate control, and maintained by social structures that continue to reproduce the unequal conditions that originally produced the stereotypes. Addressing them requires understanding all of these levels — the cognitive, the cultural, the social, and the structural — and deploying interventions calibrated to each.
