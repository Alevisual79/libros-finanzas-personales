## Why We Help Each Other — and When We Don't

### Prologue
In 2004, a genetic epidemiologist named David Sloan Wilson proposed that human beings are, in the most literal and precise sense, "the ultrasocial species" — the only species, among all the social species on Earth, that has built extensive cooperation with genetically unrelated strangers. Ants cooperate, but with genetic relatives. Chimpanzees cooperate, but within small groups of individuals with extensive prior relationship. Humans cooperate with millions of strangers who share no genetic relationship and no prior acquaintance, in institutional structures (markets, states, religions, universities) that span generations and continents.

This extraordinary capacity for large-scale cooperation with strangers is the source of virtually every distinctively human achievement: science, literature, medicine, law, music, architecture, agriculture. It is also fragile, contested, and unevenly distributed. The same species that built the International Space Station has twice fought World Wars. Understanding cooperation — what produces it, what undermines it, what the research shows about altruism and its limits — is one of the most important problems in social science.

## Chapter 1: The Problem of Cooperation

The problem of cooperation — why individually rational actors would help others at cost to themselves — is one of the oldest in social science and has been formalized in the "prisoner's dilemma" game that has served as the model system for cooperation research for more than sixty years.

In the prisoner's dilemma, two players must simultaneously choose to cooperate or defect. If both cooperate, both receive a moderate payoff. If both defect, both receive a poor payoff. If one cooperates while the other defects, the defector receives the highest payoff and the cooperator receives the lowest. The individually rational strategy is always to defect, regardless of what the other player does — but mutual defection produces worse outcomes for both players than mutual cooperation. The game formalizes a fundamental social tension: individual rational interest can undermine collective welfare.

The extensive research on cooperation in prisoner's dilemma games has produced several robust findings. In one-shot games with strangers, defection is common. In repeated games where players know they will interact again, cooperation is significantly more common — the repeated interaction allows the development of reputation and the implementation of reciprocal strategies (cooperating conditional on the other's cooperation history) that make cooperation individually rational. Robert Axelrod's computer tournaments, which ran prisoner's dilemma simulations to identify the most effective strategy, found that "tit-for-tat" (cooperating on the first move, then copying the other player's previous move) was the most successful strategy among all submitted programs — simple, cooperative, retaliatory against defection, and forgiving after punishment.

## Chapter 2: Evolutionary Roots of Cooperation

The evolutionary origins of human cooperation have been the subject of extensive theoretical and empirical investigation, with several mechanisms identified as potentially sufficient to explain altruistic behavior within a Darwinian framework.

Kin selection (Hamilton's inclusive fitness theory) proposes that altruism toward genetic relatives can evolve when the benefit to the relative, weighted by relatedness, exceeds the cost to the altruist. Hamilton's rule (rB > C, where r is relatedness, B is benefit, and C is cost) predicts and explains the observation across species that helping rates correlate with genetic relatedness. The famous quip attributed to Haldane — "I'd lay down my life for two brothers or eight cousins" — captures the genetic logic.

Reciprocal altruism (Trivers) proposes that altruism toward unrelated individuals can evolve when altruism is reciprocated — when individuals help those who have helped them and withhold help from those who have not. The mechanism requires repeated interaction, sufficient memory to track interaction history, and sufficient population stability for individuals to interact repeatedly. Research on reciprocal altruism in humans finds extensive evidence of the anticipated patterns: people help more in long-term relationships, reputation for cooperation is highly valuable, and defection from cooperative agreements produces social punishment.

Group selection (Wilson and Sober) proposes that natural selection operates not only on individuals but on groups, and that groups with more cooperating members outcompete groups with more defecting members. The multilevel selection debate has been one of the most contested in evolutionary biology, with recent research by Wilson and others providing more rigorous empirical support than was previously available.

## Chapter 3: Altruism in Humans

Across these evolutionary mechanisms, the fundamental question for psychology is whether humans exhibit genuine altruism — behavior that benefits others at net cost to the actor, motivated by concern for others' welfare — or whether apparent altruism is ultimately reducible to selfish motivations (reputation management, emotional relief, reciprocity expectations).

Daniel Batson's extensive experimental research program on the "empathy-altruism hypothesis" provides the most rigorous evidence in psychology for genuine altruism. Batson's paradigm involves inducing empathy for a specific target person in need (through experimental instructions to imagine the target's perspective), and examining whether the resulting helping behavior is driven by genuine concern for the target's welfare or by the actor's desire to reduce their own empathic distress. Research by Batson and colleagues finds that empathy-induced helping persists even when alternative (less costly) ways of reducing personal distress are available — evidence that the helping is genuinely motivated by concern for the other's welfare rather than by self-interest in distress reduction.

Research on "warm glow" altruism — the pleasant feelings generated by helping that might appear to provide a selfish motivation — is consistent with genuine altruism rather than contradicting it: the fact that helping produces positive affect does not imply that positive affect was the goal. Research on the neural correlates of charitable giving finds that similar reward circuits activate during voluntary charitable giving as during the receipt of monetary rewards — a finding sometimes interpreted as evidence of "selfish" altruism, but more accurately interpreted as evidence that genuine prosocial motivation is wired into the brain's reward systems.

## Chapter 4: The Social Psychology of Helping

The research on helping behavior in everyday social contexts focuses not on the evolutionary roots of altruism but on the situational and motivational factors that determine whether people help in specific situations.

Piliavin, Dovidio, Gaertner, and Clark's arousal/cost-benefit model of helping proposes that the decision to help in an emergency is determined by two factors: the arousal produced by witnessing someone in need (which motivates action) and the cost-benefit analysis of helping versus not helping (which determines whether action takes the form of direct assistance, redirection, or leaving the scene). Research supports this model: people are more likely to help when the cost of helping is low, when the benefit of helping is high, and when the arousal produced by witnessing the need is high.

The factors that predict helping in everyday (non-emergency) contexts include: empathy toward the person in need; perception of the person as deserving help; the requester's similarity to the helper; the helper's current positive mood (research consistently finds that positive mood increases helping, through both cognitive and motivational mechanisms); and the social norm for helping in the specific context.

## Chapter 5: The Bystander Effect Revisited

The bystander effect — discussed in Book 87 as an example of others' presence inhibiting behavior — deserves specific attention in the context of altruism research, as it represents one of the most practically significant findings about the gap between people's genuine prosocial motivation and their actual helping behavior.

Research by Levine and colleagues on "group identification" and the bystander effect finds that the diffusion of responsibility effect is substantially moderated by group identity: when bystanders perceive the person in need as a member of their in-group, or when the bystanders perceive themselves as members of a shared group, helping rates are significantly higher than in conditions where the stranger is an out-group member or where no group identity is salient. The bystander effect appears to be partly a failure of identification with the victim rather than a pure numerical effect.

Research by Manning, Levine, and Collins reviewing the original Genovese case found that the "38 witnesses" narrative that formed the cultural context for Darley and Latané's research was substantially inaccurate — many of the reported witnesses were uncertain about what they were hearing, many did call police, and the actual dynamics of the event were more complex than the newspaper accounts suggested. The bystander effect as an experimental laboratory phenomenon is robustly replicated; the specific Genovese narrative that inspired it was significantly mythologized.

## Chapter 6: Cooperation in Organizations

Research on cooperation within organizations — teams, firms, professional groups — documents that the same basic mechanisms operating in laboratory games and in face-to-face social interaction operate in organizational contexts, moderated by the specific institutional and cultural features of the organizational environment.

Research on prosocial motivation in work contexts by Adam Grant and colleagues documents that the experience of one's work as benefiting others (task significance) significantly increases both effort and persistence, above and beyond the intrinsic interest of the work itself. The mechanism involves prosocial motivation — genuine concern for beneficiaries — as a supplementary motivational source that is particularly stable and persistent because it does not depend on the task's inherent interest fluctuating.

Research on cooperation in teams finds that cooperation is significantly predicted by group trust (the expectation that other members will cooperate and will not exploit one's cooperation); psychological safety (the shared belief that the team is a safe space for interpersonal risk-taking, including help-seeking); and identity fusion (the degree to which team members experience group membership as part of their self-concept). All three of these factors are influenced by leader behavior, making leadership among the most powerful determinants of team cooperation.

## Chapter 7: Cooperation in Communities

Research on cooperation in communities — neighborhoods, towns, civil society organizations, social movements — extends the organizational findings to larger and more diffuse social units where repeated interaction is more variable and institutional enforcement of cooperation is less reliable.

Robert Putnam's research on "social capital" — the networks, norms, and trust that enable cooperation in communities — documents that higher social capital communities show better governance outcomes, better health outcomes, higher educational achievement, and stronger economic performance than lower social capital communities. The research finds that "generalized trust" — trust in strangers rather than only in known individuals — is one of the most important forms of social capital for enabling large-scale cooperation.

Research on commons governance by Elinor Ostrom (the first woman to win the Nobel Prize in Economics) documented that communities can sustain cooperative management of shared resources (fisheries, forests, irrigation systems) without the "tragedy of the commons" predicted by theoretical models, when they develop specific institutional features: clear boundaries for who has resource access; rules adapted to local conditions; collective choice arrangements; monitoring; graduated sanctions; conflict resolution mechanisms; and external recognition of self-governance rights. Ostrom's research provides the most empirically grounded account available of the conditions under which large-scale cooperation is sustainable.

## Chapter 8: Limits of Altruism — In-Group Favoritism

The most significant limit on human altruism is the consistent preference for in-group over out-group members that the social identity research (discussed throughout this series) has documented across cultures and group types. Research finds that helping rates, trust, cooperation, and charitable giving all decline significantly as social distance from the target increases, and that this decline follows the contours of group membership.

Research by Levine and Crowther on helping rates for injured soccer fans found that fans of a specific team helped a fellow fan significantly more than they helped a non-fan stranger, and helped an injured rival fan at the same low rates as a complete stranger — demonstrating that the boundary of helping follows the boundary of group membership rather than geographic proximity or objective need.

Research on the determinants of charitable giving documents that proximity (geographic and social) to the recipient, identifiability of the recipient (a single identified individual versus a statistical victim), and the recipient's perceived in-group membership are among the strongest predictors of donation amounts. The "singularity effect" — donations to a single identified individual are significantly larger than to an equivalent statistical case — reflects a specific cognitive and emotional feature of human prosocial motivation: we respond to the particular rather than the statistical.

## Chapter 9: Expanding the Circle

The history of moral progress can be read as the gradual expansion of the "moral circle" — the set of beings whose welfare is given weight in moral deliberation — from immediate kin to tribe to nation to humanity to, increasingly, other species. Research by Peter Singer and others on the psychology of "effective altruism" — the attempt to determine and do the most good possible with available resources — documents both the potential and the psychological barriers to this expansion.

Research on "psychic numbing" by Paul Slovic and colleagues finds that people's emotional response to mass suffering does not scale proportionately with the number of victims — the suffering of a large number of people may produce less emotional response than the suffering of a single identified individual. This "collapse of compassion" has practical implications for charitable giving and political action on large-scale humanitarian crises.

Research on the effectiveness of different appeals in expanding prosocial behavior finds that concrete, specific representations of need (individual stories, local examples) consistently outperform abstract statistical representations in mobilizing help, despite the fact that the abstract representations typically describe greater total need. The implication — that effective communication about large-scale human need should be personal and concrete rather than statistical — is among the most important practical findings in altruism research.

## Chapter 10: The Cooperative Society

The research on cooperation and altruism converges on a finding that is both familiar and profound: the quality of human social life depends fundamentally on the degree to which individuals and institutions sustain cooperative rather than competitive orientations toward each other. The conditions that sustain cooperation — trust, accountability, reciprocity, shared identity, meaningful contact — are not self-generating; they require deliberate cultivation through the design of institutions, communities, and relationships.

The contribution of individual behavior to this collective project is not negligible: research consistently finds that individual acts of trust, cooperation, and generosity have positive effects on others' subsequent behavior through the contagion and reciprocity mechanisms documented throughout this book. The person who acts generously in a social dilemma, who cooperates when defection would be rational, who extends trust to strangers — that person is doing more than expressing their values; they are contributing to the social conditions that make cooperation sustainable for everyone.

---

## Epilogue

Cooperation is not the default condition of human social life; it is an achievement that depends on specific conditions, deliberate institutions, and the cultivation of the motivational systems — empathy, generalized trust, prosocial identity — that sustain cooperative behavior when short-term self-interest points in another direction. Understanding these conditions and these systems is among the most important contributions that psychology can make to the project of sustaining the cooperative society that human flourishing depends on.
