### Prologue
Gordon Allport's classic definition of social psychology — the scientific study of how the actual, imagined, or implied presence of other people affects an individual's thoughts, feelings, and behavior — contains a phrase that deserves more attention than it typically receives: "imagined or implied presence." We are not merely affected by the people who are physically present with us; we are affected by the people we imagine are watching us, by the internalized standards of social groups we belong to, and by the presence of others implied by the social contexts we inhabit even when no one specific is actually there.

Understanding the effect of others on your behavior is, in a significant sense, the master key to social psychology. Nearly every phenomenon in the field — conformity, obedience, helping, aggression, performance, prejudice — involves some version of how others' presence (actual, imagined, or implied) shapes individual behavior. This book takes the question as its organizing frame and examines the range of ways in which others shape us: some obvious, some subtle, some that we would prefer to believe we are immune to.

## Chapter 1: Social Facilitation and Inhibition

Norman Triplett's 1898 study of cyclists — who rode faster when racing against others than when racing alone against the clock — is often cited as the first social psychology experiment. Triplett's finding that the presence of others improves performance was partially right but incompletely specified: subsequent research documented that the presence of others sometimes impairs performance rather than improving it.

Robert Zajonc's 1965 theoretical synthesis resolved the apparent contradiction: the presence of others produces "arousal" (a general increase in drive or activation) that increases the probability of the performer's "dominant response" — the response most likely given the individual's current skill level. For well-learned behaviors (dominant response = correct response), arousal improves performance; for novel or poorly learned behaviors (dominant response = incorrect response), arousal impairs performance. The expert musician performs better under audience observation; the beginning musician performs worse.

Research on the mechanisms of social facilitation has expanded Zajonc's original arousal account to include evaluation apprehension (concern about being judged by observers) and distraction (the competing attentional demands of monitoring the audience while performing). Research by Guerin and colleagues using non-evaluating observers (who were present but clearly not evaluating the performer) found that mere presence alone can produce some facilitation effects, while evaluation apprehension amplifies the effect substantially.

## Chapter 2: Social Comparison

Leon Festinger's social comparison theory — the proposition that humans have a fundamental drive to evaluate their own opinions and abilities, and in the absence of objective standards, they do so by comparing themselves with others — has generated one of the most extensive research literatures in social psychology and has significant implications for wellbeing, motivation, and self-evaluation.

Research on social comparison processes documents that people engage in both upward comparison (comparing with those who are better-off on the dimension of comparison) and downward comparison (comparing with those who are worse-off), with different motivational effects. Upward comparison tends to produce one of two responses: inspiration (when the comparison target is seen as similar and achievable) or discouragement (when the comparison target is seen as remote and unachievable). Downward comparison tends to produce temporarily enhanced self-evaluation and wellbeing, at the potential cost of motivation for improvement.

Research on social media and social comparison finds that the curated self-presentations typical of social media platforms systematically bias social comparison toward upward comparison with idealized images of others' lives, happiness, and appearance. Research by Vogel, Rose, Roberts, and Eckles finds that passive social media use (browsing others' profiles without active posting) is associated with lower self-evaluations and reduced wellbeing, with the effect mediated by unfavorable social comparison.

## Chapter 3: Norms and Social Reality

Muzafer Sherif's autokinetic effect studies — in which participants' estimates of the apparent movement of a stationary point of light (an ambiguous stimulus with no objective referent) converged on a shared norm when made in groups, and then showed individual persistence of this norm even when tested alone — demonstrated one of the most fundamental effects of others on perception and judgment: the construction of social reality.

Sherif's finding illustrates that in ambiguous situations where objective standards are unavailable, social reality is constructed through interaction with others, and once constructed, persists as an internal standard that continues to influence individual judgment independent of the social context that produced it. This "reference norm" effect provides the mechanism for the cultural transmission of perceptual and evaluative standards across generations: what we take to be direct perception is partly a social construction that began in interaction and has been internalized as automatic.

Research on descriptive norms — what most people in a relevant group actually do — finds powerful effects on behavior across a wide range of domains. The norm emerges from social observation and social communication, and once established, it functions as a behavioral constraint for group members regardless of whether actual enforcement occurs. Research by Cialdini and colleagues documents that norm-based messages (informing people about what the majority of similar others actually do) are among the most effective behavior-change interventions available, often more effective than appeals to individual consequences.

## Chapter 4: The Mere Presence Effect and Audience Effects

Beyond social facilitation and social comparison, research on the "mere presence" of others documents a range of behavioral effects that occur even without specific feedback, evaluation, or communication from the others present.

Research on "audience effects" in consumer behavior finds that the social composition of the consumption context significantly influences product choices, consumption amounts, and perceived quality. People eat more when dining with groups than when dining alone; they consume more alcohol in social settings; they choose more conformist options when they believe their choices will be observed. These effects occur without any direct social pressure — simply the presence and observation of others changes what people do and how much they do of it.

Research on "social eating" finds that meal size scales with the size of the dining group in a remarkably consistent way: people eat approximately 35% more when dining with one other person than when alone, and progressively more with larger groups. The mechanism involves both disinhibition (social meals are treated as occasions for relaxed dietary standards) and social facilitation of eating (the act of eating by companions provides a social cue that triggers and maintains eating behavior).

## Chapter 5: Helping and the Presence of Others

One of the most counterintuitive effects of others' presence on behavior is its inhibiting effect on helping. The bystander effect — documented by Darley and Latané following the 1964 Kitty Genovese murder, in which numerous witnesses reportedly did not intervene — is among the most extensively replicated findings in social psychology.

Laboratory research by Darley and Latané documents that the probability of any individual helping in an emergency decreases as the number of apparent bystanders increases. In one study, a confederate experienced a simulated seizure over an intercom: 85% of participants who believed they were the only bystander sought help within six minutes; only 31% of participants in groups of five did so. The presence of others, far from increasing the likelihood of help through greater resources, dramatically reduced it.

The mechanisms are dual. Diffusion of responsibility: when others are present, the sense of personal responsibility for helping is divided across bystanders, reducing each individual's felt obligation to act. Pluralistic ignorance: in ambiguous emergencies, bystanders look to each other for cues about whether the situation requires intervention; each bystander, observing others' non-reaction, interprets the situation as non-emergency, producing a cascade of inaction based on a shared misinterpretation.

## Chapter 6: Social Contagion

The spread of behaviors, emotions, and beliefs through social networks — social contagion — is among the most significant and most studied effects of others on individual behavior. Research by Christakis and Fowler on social network data from the Framingham Heart Study documents that obesity, smoking, happiness, loneliness, and other behaviors and states spread through social networks in patterns consistent with contagion models.

Emotional contagion — the automatic mimicry and synchronization of emotional expressions, vocalizations, and postures, which produces corresponding emotional experiences — is one of the most fundamental mechanisms of social influence. Research by Hatfield, Cacioppo, and Rapson documents that people automatically mimic others' emotional expressions and that this mimicry produces afferent feedback (from facial muscles to brain) that generates the corresponding emotional state in the mimicker. The "ripple effect" of mood in organizations — documented by Sy, Côté, and Saavedra — finds that group mood states, propagated through emotional contagion from leaders and influential members, significantly affect group performance.

Research on "social proof" and social norms (discussed in Books 81 and 83) documents the contagion of beliefs and behavioral standards through social networks. When obesity spreads through networks in ways that are not explained by shared environmental factors (because the effect is found for network connections that are geographically distant), the most plausible mechanism is the normative influence of network members' behavior on the behaviors of those connected to them.

## Chapter 7: Deindividuation

When individuals lose their sense of individual identity within a group context — through anonymity, submersion in group identity, or the reduction of self-awareness that large, aroused groups can produce — their behavior often shifts in ways consistent with "deindividuation": reduced self-monitoring, increased responsiveness to immediate situational cues, reduced constraint by internalized standards.

Research by Zimbardo and others on deindividuation finds that anonymized participants (wearing hoods, in groups) administer more electric shocks than identifiable participants in obedience paradigms, and engage in more aggressive behavior in other contexts. The mechanism involves both reduced concern about external evaluation (due to anonymity) and reduced self-awareness (due to group submersion), both of which reduce the internal monitoring that constrains normally inhibited behavior.

Research on online behavior and anonymity extends the deindividuation research to digital contexts: online anonymity is associated with increased hostile, aggressive, and norm-violating behavior ("trolling," harassment, disinhibited sexual expression) in ways that the deindividuation research predicted and the research on the "online disinhibition effect" documents.

## Chapter 8: The Looking-Glass Self

Charles Cooley's concept of the "looking-glass self" — the self-concept formed through imagining how one appears to others and internalizing that imagined evaluation — captures a fundamental truth about the social construction of identity: who we believe we are is substantially determined by who we believe others see us as being.

Research on the formation of self-concept in childhood documents that children's self-evaluations in academic, athletic, and social domains are substantially shaped by the evaluations they perceive from significant others (parents, teachers, peers), and that inaccurate feedback from these sources produces inaccurate self-concepts that persist and influence behavior long after the original feedback has been forgotten. The "expectation effect" — teachers' expectations for students' performance influencing actual performance (Rosenthal and Jacobson's "Pygmalion in the Classroom") — operates partly through this mechanism: high-expectation teachers interact differently with students (more warmly, more challengingly, with more positive feedback), which produces a different self-concept in those students that then drives the expected performance.

Research on the persistence of others' evaluations in self-concept formation suggests that the social construction of the self is not merely a process of early childhood — it continues throughout adulthood in every social context in which one receives and internalizes others' evaluations. The people whose views of us we internalize most powerfully are those whose evaluations matter most to us, which makes intimate relationships the most consequential sources of self-concept formation in adult life.

## Chapter 9: Resisting Social Influence

The recognition that others systematically influence our behavior — often without our awareness and often in directions we would not choose if we were aware — raises the important question of how to maintain genuine autonomy in a social world that is constantly shaping our behavior.

Research on resistance to social influence identifies several strategies with consistent empirical support. Forewarning — being informed in advance that a persuasive attempt will occur — significantly reduces persuasion effectiveness by activating counterarguing and critical evaluation before the persuasive message is encountered. Research by Petty and Cacioppo finds that forewarned participants produce significantly more counterarguments and show less attitude change than unwarned participants in response to the same persuasive message.

Building minority identity and independent thinking skills — practiced through deliberate engagement with diverse perspectives, through the cultivation of clear personal values, and through the development of awareness of automatic social influence processes — is associated with more genuinely independent judgment and more resilience to social pressure. Research on "autonomous individuals" — those who show both high awareness of social influence and clear internal standards — finds they show both higher resistance to inappropriate conformity and higher responsiveness to genuinely informative social cues.

## Chapter 10: Social Beings

The research on social influence — the effect of actual, imagined, and implied others on behavior — does not support either complete social determinism (the view that behavior is entirely a product of social influence and there is no genuine individual agency) or complete individual autonomy (the view that behavior is primarily a product of individual choice unaffected by social context). It supports the more complex and more accurate view that humans are fundamentally social beings whose behavior is constituted in social interaction, whose self-concept is constructed through social feedback, and whose behavioral choices are systematically shaped by social context in ways that exceed conscious awareness — while also being capable of genuine reflection, deliberate choice, and resistance to social influence when they choose to exercise these capacities.

The honest self-knowledge that enables genuine autonomy includes knowledge of one's specific susceptibilities to social influence — the comparison processes, the norms, the authority figures, the emotional contagion mechanisms that most powerfully shape one's own behavior in one's own specific contexts. That knowledge is not a guarantee of freedom from social influence; it is the beginning of genuine choice about when to accept and when to resist it.

---

## Epilogue

Others are not merely present in our social lives; they are constitutive of our behavior, our self-concepts, and our judgments about reality. The research on social influence reveals the depth of this truth and provides the most accurate available map of the specific mechanisms through which this constitution occurs. Reading that map — with honesty about one's own susceptibilities — is the most reliable path to the kind of informed autonomy that social beings can actually achieve.
