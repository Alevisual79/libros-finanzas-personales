## How Power Changes People — and What This Means for All of Us

### Prologue
The philosopher Acton's aphorism — "power tends to corrupt, and absolute power corrupts absolutely" — is among the most quoted observations in political philosophy and one of the least carefully examined in its psychological claims. Does power actually corrupt? If so, through what mechanisms? And is the corruption the property of specific individuals, or is it a predictable consequence of the psychological effects of power on virtually anyone who experiences it?

The scientific study of power — led by researchers including Dacher Keltner, Michael Inesi, Ana Guinote, and Adam Galinsky — has produced findings that are both sobering and practically important. Power does change people, in systematic and largely predictable ways. It changes their cognition, their emotion, their behavior toward others, and their sense of moral constraint. These changes are not primarily products of the personalities of those who seek power; they are, in significant measure, products of what power does to the minds of those who hold it.

This book examines the psychology of power: what power is and how it is acquired, what it does to the people who hold it, how the powerless experience and respond to power, and what the research implies for the design of institutions and the cultivation of personal responsibility among those with social influence.

## Chapter 1: What Power Is Psychologically

Power is typically defined in social psychology as the relative ability to control outcomes — one's own and others'. The most influential theoretical treatment, proposed by French and Raven, identifies five bases of social power: legitimate power (authority conferred by role or position); reward power (ability to provide valued outcomes); coercive power (ability to impose negative outcomes); expert power (knowledge or expertise); and referent power (identification or attraction to the power holder). These bases are not mutually exclusive and typically operate in combination.

Research by Keltner, Gruenfeld, and Anderson proposes the "approach-inhibition theory of power" as the organizing framework for understanding power's psychological effects. The theory proposes that power activates an approach motivational state (associated with positive affect, action orientation, reward attention, and reduced sensitivity to threat and punishment) while powerlessness activates an inhibition motivational state (associated with negative affect, behavioral constraint, attention to threats, and sensitivity to others' evaluations). These motivational effects produce the systematic behavioral differences between high- and low-power individuals documented throughout the research literature.

## Chapter 2: Power and Cognition

Research on power and cognition documents a consistent pattern: power increases cognitive abstraction, reduces attention to specific details of others and situations, and increases focus on one's own internal states and preferences.

Research by Gruenfeld and colleagues on power and abstract construal finds that high-power individuals think more abstractly, focusing on the central, goal-relevant features of situations while ignoring peripheral details. This abstraction can facilitate strategic thinking and big-picture planning while simultaneously reducing attention to specific situational constraints, other people's specific circumstances, and the concrete consequences of decisions for individuals rather than categories.

The "power-as-license" effect documented by Lammers and Stapel finds that power produces a reduced tendency to conform to existing rules and norms — not through deliberate decision to violate them, but through a reduced sense of constraint that makes rule-following feel less automatically required. Research finds that high-power individuals are more likely to morally disengage — to convince themselves that rules that apply to others do not apply to them — with significant ethical consequences.

## Chapter 3: Power and Empathy

The empathy-reducing effects of power are among the most consistently documented and most practically significant in the power research literature. Research by Keltner and colleagues documents that high-power individuals show reduced ability to take others' perspectives, reduced accuracy in reading others' emotional states, and reduced sensitivity to others' experiences and needs.

Research by Galinsky, Magee, Inesi, and Gruenfeld on perspective-taking and power finds that power is associated with reduced accuracy in estimating others' beliefs, preferences, and emotional states. The reduced perspective-taking appears to result from the reduced need to attend carefully to others that power confers: the powerful can rely more on their own preferences and judgments because they need others' cooperation less than the powerless need the powerful's cooperation.

The empathy-power relationship creates a paradox for effective leadership: the individuals who achieve high positions are often those who are most socially skilled and attentive to others in lower-power positions; having achieved those positions, the power they hold then systematically reduces the empathic accuracy that contributed to their success. Research by Hogeveen and colleagues finds that even brief laboratory manipulations of power produce measurable reductions in "motor resonance" — the neurological mirroring of others' actions that underlies empathy — suggesting that the empathy reduction of power is rapid and automatic.

## Chapter 4: Power and Ethics

The relationship between power and ethical behavior is one of the most studied and most disturbing in the power literature. Research across organizational behavior, social psychology, and political science consistently documents that power is associated with increased rates of unethical behavior, moral disengagement, and self-serving reasoning.

Keltner's research program on power and disinhibition documents that high-power individuals are more likely to engage in impulsive, unconstrained behavior of multiple types — including sexual harassment, aggressive behavior, and rule violation — reflecting the reduced behavioral inhibition that the approach motivational state associated with power produces. The research documents not only that power holders behave badly more often but that they respond to the bad behavior of others with greater moral condemnation than to their own equivalent behavior — a moral hypocrisy that reflects the power-as-license phenomenon.

Research on the "moral licensing" effect — the tendency to behave less ethically after establishing one's moral credentials — is particularly pronounced in high-power individuals. Power holders who have recently behaved ethically (or who see themselves as generally ethical people) show increased willingness to engage in subsequent self-serving or unethical behavior, as if the prior good behavior has created a moral "credit" that can be spent on self-interested actions.

## Chapter 5: The Powerless and Their Psychology

The psychology of low power — the experience of those in subordinate, dependent, or marginalized positions — is equally important for understanding power dynamics and has received less research attention than the psychology of power holders.

Research on the behavioral inhibition associated with low power finds that powerless individuals are more careful, more attentive to social cues, more sensitive to others' evaluations, and more constrained in their behavior than their high-power counterparts. These features reflect both the functional adaptations to low-power positions (where careful attention to powerful others is genuinely necessary for wellbeing) and the psychological costs of chronic inhibition (which are associated with reduced wellbeing, increased anxiety, and reduced sense of personal agency).

Research on the "learned helplessness" effects of chronic powerlessness — extended by Seligman's work from animal studies to human contexts — documents that people who repeatedly experience outcomes as uncontrollable show cognitive, motivational, and emotional deficits that generalize beyond the specific context where control was lost. Employees in chronically low-power organizational positions, residents in chronically under-resourced communities, and individuals in abusive relationships all show forms of behavioral inhibition and motivational deficit that reflect the psychological effects of chronic powerlessness rather than personality deficiencies.

## Chapter 6: Power and Corruption — The Evidence

The claim that power corrupts is supported by substantial experimental evidence, but the specific mechanisms and conditions are more precise than the aphorism suggests. Power does not corrupt through simple moral degradation; it corrupts through specific psychological mechanisms that produce predictable behavior changes under predictable conditions.

Research by Côté, Hideg, and van Kleef documents that power holders' increased goal pursuit and approach motivation, combined with their reduced attention to others' emotional states, produces increased willingness to use others instrumentally for goal achievement. This "using people as means" effect is not accompanied by conscious malevolence; power holders who engage in this behavior typically do not experience themselves as exploiting others but simply as pursuing goals without the level of sensitivity to others' experiences that would constrain that pursuit.

The conditions that moderate the power-corruption relationship are important: research finds that power is more corrupting for individuals who are primarily self-interested before power acquisition, while for individuals with other-oriented or prosocial values, power sometimes amplifies prosocial behavior rather than reducing it. This suggests that the character of the person who acquires power matters, even if power has systematic effects that can override even good character.

## Chapter 7: Accountability and Its Effects

Research on the moderating role of accountability — the expectation that one will be required to justify one's decisions and actions to others — provides one of the most practically important findings in the power-ethics literature. Power holders who are accountable to others whose respect they value show significantly reduced moral disengagement, reduced ethical violations, and increased attention to others' perspectives.

Research by Tetlock and colleagues on the "accountability effect" in judgment and decision-making finds that accountability to knowledgeable others (who are critical and do not simply want to hear the decision maker's preferred conclusion) significantly improves decision quality, reduces overconfidence, and reduces self-serving bias. The accountability effect is specific to accountability to critical, knowledgeable others before the decision (pre-decisional accountability); post-decisional accountability, or accountability to audiences who simply want confirmation, does not produce the same benefits.

Structural accountability mechanisms — separation of powers, independent oversight, required justification processes, and transparency requirements — are the institutional translations of the accountability research. The most effective institutions for limiting the corruption effects of power are those that build accountability into the structure rather than relying on the character of power holders to self-regulate, because the research documents that power systematically reduces the self-regulation that character-based accountability requires.

## Chapter 8: Powerlessness and Resistance

The psychology of resistance to power — the ways in which people in subordinate positions challenge, undermine, and modify the exercise of power over them — has been studied primarily through the lens of social movement and collective action research.

Research by Jost and colleagues on "system justification" — the tendency of even disadvantaged group members to defend and legitimate the social systems that disadvantage them — documents one of the most counterintuitive findings in the power literature: people in low-power positions often ideologically support the systems that maintain their low power. The mechanism: system justification provides psychological benefits (reduced anxiety, increased sense of order and fairness) that partially compensate for the material and social costs of low power.

The conditions that predict resistance rather than system justification include: high group identification (seeing oneself as a member of a disadvantaged group rather than an individual in an unfortunate situation); belief in the illegitimacy and changeability of the power arrangement; and social support from fellow group members who validate the injustice of the situation. Research on social movements documents that the transition from individual grievance to collective action requires all three of these conditions.

## Chapter 9: Power in Everyday Relationships

The psychology of power is not confined to organizational and political hierarchies. The same mechanisms that operate in institutional power relationships operate in the power dynamics of everyday relationships: between partners, parents and children, friends, and colleagues.

Research on power in intimate relationships finds that the partner with more relative power (typically measured by alternatives to the relationship, resource contribution, and emotional investment) shows behavioral patterns consistent with the power research: more action-orientation, less sensitivity to the partner's emotional states, and more focus on personal preferences in joint decision-making. The research does not imply that power asymmetry in intimate relationships is inevitably harmful; it documents the psychological effects of the asymmetry and the factors that moderate its impact on relationship quality.

Research by Simpson, Farrell, Oriña, and Rothman on power and relationship accommodation finds that the less powerful partner in relationships shows greater accommodation — greater behavioral change in response to the other's preferences and needs — than the more powerful partner. This asymmetry in accommodation is one of the mechanisms by which power asymmetry reproduces itself over time within relationships: the less powerful partner's accommodation reinforces the more powerful partner's sense that their preferences naturally prevail.

## Chapter 10: Using Power Well

The research on power does not support the conclusion that power is inherently corrupting or that people with power are inherently worse than those without it. It supports the conclusion that power has systematic psychological effects that produce predictable behavior changes — and that these behavior changes can be counteracted through deliberate effort, structural accountability, and the cultivation of specific dispositions.

The dispositions that moderate the negative effects of power include: habitual perspective-taking (deliberately and regularly taking others' points of view even when power makes this less automatic); a strong sense of responsibility to those over whom power is held (a stewardship orientation rather than an entitlement orientation); accountability to internal standards rather than primarily to external enforcement; and genuine awareness of the psychological effects of power, including the recognition that one is not exempt from them.

The design of institutions matters as much as the character of individuals in those institutions: the research consistently documents that structural accountability, transparency, independent oversight, and norms of justification protect against power corruption more reliably than the good intentions of individual power holders. Both individual character and institutional design are necessary; neither is sufficient alone.

---

## Epilogue

Power is among the most significant variables in human social life, and its psychological effects are among the most important findings in social psychology. Understanding these effects — with specificity and honesty, without either cynicism (assuming all power holders are corrupt) or naivety (assuming good intentions protect against power's psychological effects) — is the beginning of the kind of informed engagement with power that genuinely protects individuals, relationships, and institutions from its most predictable costs.
