### Prologue
In the months before the January 28, 1986 Challenger launch, engineers at Morton Thiokol and at NASA had raised concerns about the O-ring seals that would fail catastrophically at the temperature of that January morning. The concerns were technically specific, documented, and serious. They were also, in the decision meeting the night before the launch, overridden. The engineers who raised them were told that their concerns were not adequately supported by data; the presentation of their safety objections was flawed; and the decision proceeded. Seven astronauts died.

The Presidential Commission that investigated the Challenger disaster documented a decision-making process that exhibited virtually every symptom of groupthink: the illusion of invulnerability that a long string of successful launches had produced; the rationalization that the O-ring concerns did not constitute sufficient evidence for delay; the pressure on dissenters to prove safety, rather than requiring management to prove safety; and the organizational culture that made speaking up against launch decisions professionally and socially costly. The decision was not made by bad people. It was made by a group of people operating in a decision-making environment that systematically suppressed the accurate information that was available.

## Chapter 1: Groupthink Defined

Irving Janis coined the term "groupthink" in 1972 to describe a deterioration of mental efficiency, reality testing, and moral judgment that results from in-group pressures. His analysis was based on comparative case studies of foreign policy decision disasters (Bay of Pigs, Pearl Harbor, Korea, Vietnam) and successes (Cuban Missile Crisis, Marshall Plan), and identified a cluster of symptoms that characterized the decision processes of the failing cases.

Janis's eight symptoms of groupthink: illusion of invulnerability (excessive optimism that encourages extreme risk-taking); collective rationalization (members discount warnings that might lead to reconsideration); belief in inherent morality (members believe in the moral rightness of their cause and ignore ethical consequences); stereotyped views of out-groups (enemies are too weak, evil, or stupid to be a serious threat); pressure on dissenters (members who question assumptions are pressured to conform); self-censorship (members avoid deviating from the apparent group consensus); illusion of unanimity (the majority view is assumed to be unanimous because dissenters remain silent); and self-appointed mindguards (members protect the group from information that challenges assumptions).

Research on the prevalence and mechanisms of groupthink has both supported and complicated Janis's original account. Some of the boundary conditions he specified (high cohesion as a necessary condition) have not been consistently supported; other features of the groupthink syndrome (particularly the suppression of dissent and the illusion of unanimity) have shown more consistent empirical support. The current scientific consensus treats groupthink not as a single syndrome with a specific set of necessary antecedents but as a family of related decision quality problems that share the feature of insufficient critical evaluation.

## Chapter 2: The Conditions That Produce Groupthink

Research on the antecedents of groupthink identifies several conditions that increase the likelihood of poor group decision-making, even when the individual members are intelligent, knowledgeable, and well-intentioned.

High group cohesion — the degree to which members feel emotionally attached to the group and motivated to maintain group harmony — is the antecedent Janis most emphasized, but research finds it produces poor decisions specifically when it is combined with other conditions rather than in isolation. Highly cohesive groups can make excellent decisions when they have the appropriate structural features (diverse perspectives, clear norms for critical evaluation, psychological safety for dissent).

Directive leadership is a more consistently documented antecedent than cohesion. Research finds that leaders who express their preferred conclusions early in the deliberation, who appear resistant to contrary views, and who create social cost for disagreement systematically reduce the quality of information sharing in groups. The leader who says "I think X — what do you all think?" has effectively determined the outcome before deliberation begins, because members are unlikely to contradict the leader's expressed preference.

Insulation from external perspectives — groups that make decisions without access to outside information, outside experts, or external review — show significantly lower decision quality than groups that incorporate external perspectives. The insulated group relies on the shared assumptions and shared information of its members, which are exactly the features most likely to be wrong in precisely the same ways.

## Chapter 3: Why Smart People Succumb

One of the most troubling features of groupthink — and one that Janis explicitly noted — is that it affects highly intelligent, well-educated, genuinely competent people making decisions in domains where they have substantial expertise. The Kennedy administration officials who approved the Bay of Pigs invasion included some of the most sophisticated foreign policy analysts in the United States at the time. Their groupthink failure was not a failure of individual intelligence or expertise; it was a failure of the group decision process.

Research on the relationship between individual intelligence and susceptibility to group influence finds that higher intelligence is not protective against groupthink — and in some contexts may even increase susceptibility. Research by Stanovich and colleagues on "myside bias" (the tendency to evaluate evidence through the lens of one's prior conclusions) finds that more intelligent people are more skilled at generating arguments for positions they already hold, which can make them more effective defenders of group consensus rather than more effective critical evaluators of it.

The social dynamics that produce groupthink are also more powerful in groups of high-status, high-commitment members than in groups of lower-investment members. The more someone values their membership in a group, the more the social costs of dissent threaten something important, and the more powerful the motivation to self-censor to maintain group harmony.

## Chapter 4: The Challenger and Columbia Cases

The Challenger disaster and the Columbia disaster (2003) provide instructive comparison cases that document the persistence of groupthink-like processes in NASA's decision-making culture despite the extensive analysis of the Challenger case that occurred after the 1986 disaster.

The Columbia Accident Investigation Board documented that the organizational culture of NASA had not substantially changed between 1986 and 2003: the same pressures that had produced the Challenger decision (schedule pressure, normalization of deviance, suppression of safety dissent, inadequate investigation of known risks) were documented in the Columbia decision-making process. Engineers who raised concerns about foam strike damage to Columbia's thermal protection system encountered an organizational culture that had normalized the dismissal of such concerns and that provided no effective escalation path for safety objections that management had not welcomed.

The Columbia case demonstrates that groupthink is not primarily about personality or individual failure but about organizational structures and cultures that systematically suppress the critical evaluation that good decisions require. The same organizational culture can produce the same decision failures repeatedly, with different individuals in the same roles, because the failure is structural rather than personal.

## Chapter 5: Interventions That Work

Research on groupthink prevention has identified structural interventions that significantly improve group decision quality by changing the conditions under which deliberation occurs rather than by appealing to the group's motivation to decide well.

The formal devil's advocate — a role that is officially assigned and that carries explicit permission to challenge the group's emerging consensus — is among the most extensively researched group process interventions. Research by Janis, Nemeth, and others finds that devil's advocates improve decision quality by eliciting more information, generating more alternatives, and producing more thorough examination of the assumptions underlying the preferred option. The key finding: the devil's advocate must be a genuine advocate (genuinely challenging the consensus) rather than a nominal one (going through the motions of challenge without actually believing or expressing genuine concerns).

The "pre-mortem" technique — proposed by Gary Klein and researched by Deborah Mitchell and colleagues — involves asking group members to imagine that the decision they have just made has been implemented and has failed catastrophically, and to generate all the reasons this might have happened. Research finds that the pre-mortem produces significantly more identification of potential failure modes than standard risk assessment, because the hypothetical failure framing liberates members from the political cost of raising concerns before the decision is made.

Multi-team review — having major decisions reviewed by groups that were not involved in the original deliberation — provides the external perspective that reduces the insulation from outside views. Research on multi-team review processes in medical, military, and organizational contexts finds significant improvements in decision quality attributable to the external review component.

## Chapter 6: Groupthink in Everyday Contexts

Groupthink is not exclusively a phenomenon of high-stakes governmental or organizational decision-making. The same psychological mechanisms operate in everyday group contexts: in family decisions, in team meetings, in community organizations, and in any group context where the social pressure to maintain harmony exceeds the willingness to examine assumptions critically.

The family meeting that produces agreement on a course of action that multiple members privately doubt but none raises objections to; the project team that commits to a timeline that multiple members believe is unrealistic but none explicitly challenges; the community organization that adopts a policy that several members find problematic but whose objections are never voiced — all exhibit the essential features of groupthink: private doubt, self-censored expression, and the production of a consensus that does not reflect the genuine assessments of the group's members.

Research on family decision-making by Galambos and colleagues finds that family decisions about major life events (health care, financial decisions, housing) frequently show groupthink-like dynamics: powerful family members' preferences become the outcome of apparently collective deliberation, minority views are suppressed by the social dynamics of family loyalty and the desire to avoid conflict, and the decision process produces less consideration of alternatives than would be expected from the collective intelligence available in the group.

## Chapter 7: Groupthink and Leadership

The role of leadership in producing and preventing groupthink is among the most consistently documented findings in the groupthink literature. Leaders who express their preferred conclusions, who resist challenge, and who create social costs for disagreement are the most consistent antecedents of groupthink in organizations. Leaders who withhold their own preferences, who explicitly invite dissent, who model genuine curiosity about contrary views, and who create social safety for speaking up are the most consistent preventive factors.

Research by Avolio and Bass on transformational leadership — leadership that inspires, motivates, and intellectually stimulates followers — finds that transformational leaders are significantly associated with better group decision quality than transactional leaders, partly through the intellectual stimulation dimension of transformational leadership that creates expectations of critical analysis rather than compliance.

Amy Edmondson's research on leader behavior and psychological safety (discussed in Book 65) is directly applicable to groupthink prevention: leaders who model asking questions, acknowledging uncertainty, and genuinely welcoming contrary views create team environments with higher psychological safety that show significantly better information sharing, including the sharing of information that challenges the group's current direction.

## Chapter 8: Groupthink in Political and Social Contexts

The analysis of groupthink has been extended from the organizational and small-group contexts of the original research to political and social phenomena at larger scales. Research on political polarization, ideological echo chambers, and collective belief in conspiracy theories has applied groupthink mechanisms to explain how groups maintain internally consistent but objectively inaccurate beliefs.

Research on political echo chambers — informational environments in which people receive primarily news and opinion that confirms their existing political beliefs — documents that exposure to politically homogeneous information networks produces the same epistemic consequences as groupthink in organizational settings: increased certainty in the positions already held, decreased exposure to contrary evidence, and decreased critical evaluation of the assumptions underlying those positions.

Research on conspiracy belief in groups documents that socially isolated groups sharing misinformation ("closed" information environments) show similar processes of reality-testing deterioration, rationalization, and self-censorship of doubt that Janis identified in small decision-making groups. The scale is larger and the mechanism is diffuse rather than concentrated in a single deliberation, but the essential features — suppression of disconfirming evidence, social pressure toward belief conformity, illusion of consensus — are recognizable from the small-group research.

## Chapter 9: The Culture of Critical Inquiry

The most durable prevention of groupthink is not any specific technique or intervention but the development of a culture of critical inquiry — a shared set of norms, practices, and expectations in which the evaluation of assumptions is understood as a sign of commitment rather than a sign of disloyalty.

Research on high-reliability organizations — nuclear power plants, aircraft carriers, air traffic control systems — that operate in high-stakes environments with remarkably low error rates documents that these organizations share a specific cultural feature: the expectation of persistent, active doubt. HRO research by Weick, Sutcliffe, and Obstfeld identifies "preoccupation with failure" (active search for potential failures even in successful operations), "reluctance to simplify" (resistance to simplifying assumptions that reduce complexity), and "sensitivity to operations" (attention to conditions at the operational level rather than relying on abstractions) as cultural features that prevent the normalization of deviance that groupthink produces.

Building this culture requires sustained, visible leadership commitment over time; it cannot be installed through a single training program or intervention. The organizations that have most successfully built cultures of critical inquiry have done so through years of consistent leader modeling, structural reinforcement, and the deliberate celebration of mistake identification rather than the suppression of it.

## Chapter 10: Thinking for Yourself in a Group

The individual's contribution to groupthink prevention is the cultivation of the specific habit of mind that groupthink suppresses: the willingness to notice, articulate, and pursue one's genuine assessment of a situation even when it diverges from the group's emerging consensus.

This is not a call to contrarianism — the systematic disagreement with majority views regardless of their merits. It is a call to the kind of intellectual honesty that a genuine commitment to accuracy requires: genuinely asking whether one's agreement with the group reflects independent evaluation of the available information or reflects the social pressure to conform; genuinely asking whether one's silence reflects the absence of a contrary view or reflects the social cost of voicing one; genuinely asking whether the group's confidence is warranted by the evidence or is an illusion of certainty produced by self-censorship of doubt.

This kind of internal honesty — which the research on groupthink consistently identifies as the cognitive prerequisite for the dissent that prevents groupthink — is a practice that can be developed. It begins with noticing, as accurately as one can, whether what one says in groups reflects what one thinks.

---

## Epilogue

Groupthink has produced some of the most costly decision failures in human history. It has done so not by assembling malicious people or stupid people but by assembling ordinary people in decision environments that systematically suppressed the critical evaluation that good decisions require. Understanding how it works — the conditions that produce it, the symptoms that signal it, and the interventions that prevent it — is one of the most practically important applications of social psychology available to anyone who participates in collective decisions.
