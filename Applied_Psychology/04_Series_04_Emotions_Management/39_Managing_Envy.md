## Prologue

Clara and Sofia had been close friends since graduate school. When Sofia's first novel was published to significant critical attention, Clara discovered something in herself she hadn't known was there: a persistent, uncomfortable response to her friend's success that went beyond simple joy and contained, alongside genuine pleasure for Sofia, a quality she recognized with shame as envy. She wanted what Sofia had — not instead of her, not without wanting Sofia to have it, but alongside the genuine happiness. The envy was uncomfortable precisely because it coexisted with real love for her friend and real joy at her success. Clara had always thought of envy as a simple, ugly emotion that clean-hearted people didn't feel. What she was discovering was considerably more complicated.

Envy — the painful emotion that arises when another person has something one desires and lacks — is among the most universally experienced and most universally denied human emotions. Its denial is partly cultural: envy is regarded as one of the less admirable emotional experiences, morally coded as a failing in most religious and ethical traditions (it is one of the seven deadly sins in Christian tradition). Its denial is also partly psychological: the combination of desire, comparison, and resentment that envy involves is genuinely uncomfortable and triggers the same shame-and-hiding dynamic described in Book 35. Understanding envy — its function, its forms, its relationship to self-esteem and social comparison, and what can be done with it productively — is an important component of emotional literacy for anyone who operates in competitive or comparison-rich environments.

## Chapter 1: What Envy Is

Richard Smith's foundational research on envy provides the most careful psychological analysis of the emotion and its structure. Envy arises at the intersection of three factors: a comparison with another person who has something one desires (the social comparison component); the perception that this comparison is unfavorable — the other person has something one lacks (the inferiority component); and the resulting pain, which may be accompanied by hostility toward the envied person (the hostile component that distinguishes envy from simple desire).

The critical distinction between envy and jealousy is one of the most persistently confused in popular usage. Jealousy involves three parties: oneself, a valued relationship, and a third party who threatens that relationship. Jealousy is the fear of losing something one has to a rival. Envy involves two parties: oneself and another person who has something one desires. Envy is the pain of not having something that another has. The popular use of "jealous" to describe envy ("I'm so jealous of your promotion") reflects this confusion — the experience described is typically envy (wanting what the other has) rather than jealousy (fearing the loss of something one has to the other).

Smith's research distinguishes two forms of envy with substantially different behavioral and psychological profiles. Malicious envy — the form most associated with the cultural image of envy as vicious — involves the hostile motivation to bring the envied person down: to undermine their advantage, diminish their achievement, or cause them to lose what they have. Benign envy involves the motivation to rise to the level of the envied person: to acquire, achieve, or develop what they have. Research finds that benign envy can function as a motivating emotion, while malicious envy is associated with hostile behavior, social disruption, and deterioration of social relationships.

## Chapter 2: The Evolutionary Function of Envy

Like all emotions, envy has evolutionary roots that make sense in the context of the ancestral environments in which humans evolved. Social animals living in groups with hierarchical resource distributions need some mechanism for monitoring their relative position in the hierarchy and motivating behavior that maintains or improves it. Envy provides this monitoring function: it is triggered by the perception that another group member has resources, status, or advantages that place them above one in the social hierarchy, and it motivates behavior that either acquires those advantages (benign envy) or reduces the other's advantage (malicious envy).

This evolutionary function explains several of the features of envy that make it psychologically distinctive. Envy is most powerfully triggered by people who are similar to oneself and in one's relevant comparison group: we envy peers more than celebrities, colleagues more than strangers, people in our field more than people in different fields. A person who is very different from us or very distant in circumstance does not trigger the intense comparison that generates envy, because they are not in the same reference group as us for hierarchical monitoring purposes.

The same evolutionary logic explains why envy's targets are achievements and attributes that are valued in the relevant social group: envy tracks what matters for social standing in one's current context. In achievement-oriented professional environments, it tracks professional recognition; in appearance-oriented social contexts, it tracks beauty and attractiveness; in parenting contexts, it tracks children's achievements; in wealth-displaying contexts, it tracks material possessions. Envy, from this perspective, is not a moral failing but an evolved social monitoring system that has been activated by the comparison-rich, status-visible environments of contemporary social life in ways that exceed its usefulness.

## Chapter 3: Envy and Social Comparison

The relationship between envy and social comparison is direct: envy is the emotional product of unfavorable upward social comparison in a domain relevant to one's self-concept. Social comparison theory (Festinger, 1954) predicts that comparison with similar others who outperform one will produce the self-evaluative threat that generates envy; research by Smith and colleagues confirms this prediction and specifies the conditions that amplify it.

Relevance is perhaps the most important moderator of envy's intensity. The same unfavorable comparison produces different emotional responses depending on whether the comparison domain is central or peripheral to one's self-concept. The professional musician who is outperformed by a colleague in musical achievement experiences intense envy; outperformed by a friend in cooking, experiences merely mild admiration. The domain relevance principle means that the situations most likely to generate intense envy are precisely those most connected to one's core identity and values — making high-achieving, identity-invested individuals particularly vulnerable to comparison-driven envy in their areas of greatest commitment.

Social media has created environments of unprecedented comparison density: the individual who previously compared with a small social circle now has access to a curated presentation of the achievements, possessions, and experiences of hundreds or thousands of people, systematically filtered to include their most favorable presentations. Research on social media use and envy finds consistent positive associations between passive social media consumption and envy, and between envy and reduced wellbeing. The Instagram internal research documents mentioned in Book 24 specifically identified envy as a primary mechanism through which the platform's use produced negative psychological effects.

## Chapter 4: Schadenfreude — Envy's Shadow

Closely related to envy, and its shadow in many psychological accounts, is schadenfreude — pleasure at the misfortune of others. Research by Smith and colleagues finds that envy and schadenfreude are functionally linked: the intensity of schadenfreude experienced at an envied person's misfortune is predicted by the intensity of envy previously experienced toward them. The person one most enviably regards is the person one most experiences satisfaction when they fail.

The relationship is psychologically coherent from the perspective of social comparison theory: the envied person's advantage was the source of the unfavorable comparison and the pain of envy; their misfortune removes the advantage and the comparison threat, producing the relief that is experienced as pleasure. This does not make schadenfreude psychologically comfortable — most people experience it alongside guilt, because the pleasure at another's suffering conflicts with their self-image as empathic and compassionate. But its prevalence is documented across cultures, and its connection to envy is one of the most consistent findings in the psychology of social emotions.

The psychological understanding of schadenfreude is practically useful not as a permission for enjoying others' misfortunes but as a diagnostic signal. The experience of schadenfreude at a specific person's setback is reliable evidence that one was envying them — which, in turn, is evidence that they possess something one desires. This diagnostic use of schadenfreude follows the general principle, articulated throughout this series, of treating difficult emotions as information rather than as moral verdicts.

## Chapter 5: Envy in Organizations and Social Groups

Envy in organizational contexts produces some of the most practically significant and most costly of its effects. Research on envy in the workplace finds that it is associated with reduced organizational citizenship behavior (helping colleagues, going beyond the job description), increased counterproductive work behavior (sabotage, spreading rumors, withholding cooperation), and reduced information sharing. The organizational colleague who is envied may be subtly undermined — in ways that are invisible to management but obvious to peers — in the service of reducing the advantage that triggered the envy.

The competitive organizational cultures that many organizations deliberately create — in the belief that competition motivates performance — may produce envy as a systematic byproduct, with significant organizational costs. Research by Tomas Chamorro-Premuzic and colleagues on organizational performance cultures finds that the benefits of competitive culture (higher individual performance on simple tasks) are frequently outweighed by the costs (reduced cooperation, increased envy, reduced psychological safety, higher turnover). The assumption that competition is the primary driver of high performance turns out to be empirically questionable when the research examines the full range of its effects.

Friendship groups provide particularly complex contexts for envy because they combine close comparison (friends are among the most similar and relevant comparators) with relationship bonds that envy threatens. The friend whose success generates envy presents the dual challenge of a comparison that triggers envy and a relationship that one values enough to want to protect from the hostility that envy's malicious form can produce. This tension between envy and affection is what Clara experienced with Sofia — not as a pathology but as a normal feature of close relationships between comparably ambitious people.

## Chapter 6: Transforming Envy Into Information

The most practically effective approach to envy is not its suppression — which maintains it while adding the secondary suffering of shame about having it — but its use as diagnostic information. The experience of envy is a reliable signal about desire: when one envies another person's achievement, relationship, lifestyle, or characteristic, one is discovering what one values and wants in one's own life. This diagnostic function is the most practically useful thing envy provides.

The reflection that envy invites — "what, specifically, do I desire about what this person has, and what would it mean for my own life?" — converts envy from a passive, comparison-generated suffering into an active discovery process about one's own values and aspirations. The aspiring writer who envies a published colleague has discovered something about their own aspirations; the executive who envies a colleague's autonomy has discovered something about what they value in work; the parent who envies another family's closeness has discovered something about what they want in their own family relationships.

This diagnostic use does not eliminate the painful quality of envy — the discovery of desire does not reduce the pain of lacking what one desires. But it redirects the energy that envy generates from the malicious impulse to undermine the envied person to the constructive pursuit of what one has discovered one actually wants. The shift from "I resent what she has" to "I want what she has, and here is what that means for what I'm going to do" is the transformation of envy from a corrosive to a generative emotion.

## Chapter 7: Admiration as an Alternative

Admiration — genuine recognition of and pleasure in another's achievement, characteristic, or quality — is the social emotion most closely related to benign envy and represents the most constructive available alternative to malicious envy. Admiration involves upward comparison (the admired person has something valuable) but without the pain of inferiority: the admiring person's own position is not threatened by the admired person's achievement.

Research on admiration finds that it is associated with the motivation to improve oneself and to emulate the admired person — similar to the motivation associated with benign envy but without the painful quality. The shift from envy to admiration is not always psychologically available — when the comparison domain is central to one's self-concept, the pain of inferiority is difficult to simply set aside — but the deliberate cultivation of an admiring stance toward comparable others is both psychologically healthier and more likely to produce the self-improvement motivation that benign envy provides.

The specific practice of asking "what, specifically, do I admire about this achievement?" — rather than stopping at the general painful awareness of another's advantage — produces a concrete analysis that is simultaneously more respectful of the admired person and more useful to oneself. The specific qualities one identifies as admirable become the specific qualities one can set as development targets.

## Chapter 8: Gratitude and the Limitation of Comparison

Gratitude — the recognition and appreciation of what one has and what one has received — is the cognitive alternative to comparison-generated envy at the most fundamental level. Where envy is organized around the gap between what one has and what another has, gratitude is organized around what one actually has. These two orientations are literally opposite: envy attends to absence, gratitude attends to presence.

The wellbeing benefits of gratitude practices — documented extensively in the positive psychology literature — operate partly through this mechanism: the deliberate redirection of attention from comparison-generated awareness of relative position to appreciation of actual circumstances. This redirection is not the denial of one's genuine desires or the resignation to inadequate circumstances — it is the practice of experiencing the genuine value of what is present rather than allowing comparison to render it invisible.

For Clara, the movement toward a more constructive relationship with her envy of Sofia's literary success eventually involved both the diagnostic function (recognizing that she wanted to write seriously herself, something she had been deferring for years) and gratitude for aspects of her own life that the comparison with Sofia had been temporarily rendering invisible. The envy, at its most useful, had pointed her toward her own work. The gratitude had reminded her of what was already present.

## Chapter 9: Envy and the Social Environment

Not all envy is primarily a product of individual psychology — the social environment in which one lives and works either amplifies or dampens the comparison processes that generate envy. Environments that emphasize social ranking, public visibility of individual achievement, and competitive comparison produce more envy than environments that emphasize collaboration, shared achievement, and individual growth without constant comparison.

The practical management of envy therefore includes both individual practices (developing the diagnostic and admiration-shifting approaches described above) and environmental management: deliberately limiting exposure to comparison-intensive environments, platforms, and social contexts that reliably generate envy without corresponding benefit; and choosing or building environments that generate the collaborative, growth-oriented social comparison that inspires rather than the competitive, ranking-oriented comparison that depletes.

Research on comparison fasting — deliberate periods of abstaining from social media and comparison-rich contexts — finds significant benefits for wellbeing and envy reduction, particularly for individuals who have developed habitual patterns of passive, unfocused social media consumption that generates comparison without the connection or information that more active social media use can provide.

## Chapter 10: Envy as a Teacher

Clara eventually finished a short story — the first creative writing she had completed in seven years. It was, she thought, not as good as anything Sofia had published. It also existed, which her writing had not existed for seven years while she had deferred it. The envy had not disappeared, but it had become useful rather than merely painful: a reliable signal about what she wanted that she had previously been too busy (or too afraid) to attend to.

The mature relationship with envy is neither its suppression nor its endorsement but its intelligent reception. Suppression leaves the desire unacknowledged and the malicious hostility available for expression in other forms. Endorsement — accepting envy's hostile impulse at face value — damages relationships and the self of the person who acts on it. Intelligent reception — acknowledging the envy, attending to what it is signaling about desire, redirecting its energy toward constructive pursuit, and practicing the admiration that can gradually replace its hostile component — transforms envy from a relational liability into a personal teacher.

---

## Epilogue

Envy is information about desire, dressed in the uncomfortable clothing of comparison and resentment. The desire is real; the resentment is a strategy that doesn't serve the desire. Beneath every envy is the recognition of something valued, something missing, something toward which effort could be directed. That recognition — separated from the hostility and held with the same honest attention one would give any other important information about oneself — is envy's contribution to the examined life.
