## Prologue

For six years after the assault, Amara maintained that she was fine. She had not told many people what had happened. She had gone back to work within days. She was functional, organized, and by all visible measures, coping. She had also, since the assault, lived in a body that felt like a foreign country: hyperalert in any enclosed space, unable to be touched without tensing, cycling between profound exhaustion and inexplicable arousal that had no cognitive correlate. She had chronic back pain that three physiotherapists had been unable to explain. She startled at sounds that preceded any cognitive recognition of what she had heard. She had become, without intending to or understanding why, a person who lived from the neck up — managing the world with her mind while her body ran its own, parallel, alarmed program.

Trauma lives in the body. This is not a metaphor — it is a neurobiological reality that has become one of the most important insights in contemporary trauma treatment. Bessel van der Kolk's landmark 2014 book "The Body Keeps the Score" summarized decades of clinical and research evidence for the somatic encoding of traumatic experience and brought this understanding to a public audience in ways that have fundamentally changed how both clinicians and trauma survivors understand what happens to the body in the aftermath of trauma. This book builds on that foundation, examining the specific ways in which trauma is encoded, expressed, and released through the body — and the somatic approaches to trauma healing that address these bodily dimensions directly.

## Chapter 1: Why the Body Keeps the Score

The neurobiological argument for why trauma is encoded somatically begins with the architecture of the stress response. When a traumatic experience occurs, the body's threat response system activates the fight, flight, or freeze response through the amygdala and the autonomic nervous system before the cortex has time to process and contextualize what is happening. The physiological response — the cardiovascular arousal, the muscular preparation, the hormonal cascade — occurs in the body immediately, while the cortex is still catching up.

Under non-traumatic circumstances, the stress response is followed by resolution: the threat passes, the body returns to baseline, and the experience is consolidated into memory along with the resolution. Under traumatic circumstances — when the threat overwhelms the person's capacity to respond or when the resolution does not occur (as in chronic trauma, inescapable trauma, or trauma that produces the freeze response) — the physiological stress response remains unresolved. The body is left in a state of incomplete completion: the energy of the threat response mobilization has been neither discharged (through effective fight or flight) nor resolved through genuine safety. Peter Levine's research on somatic trauma, developed in his "Somatic Experiencing" approach, proposes that this incomplete biological completion is the core mechanism of trauma's somatic persistence.

The implication is significant: the primary record of traumatic experience may be the body's physiological state rather than the cortical narrative. The person who cannot access verbal memories of childhood trauma may nonetheless carry the trauma in the body's chronic arousal patterns, the startle response, the inability to feel safe, and the somatic symptoms that persist without apparent cause. Approaching trauma through the body — working with these somatic patterns directly — may reach aspects of the traumatic experience that purely cognitive or narrative approaches cannot access.

## Chapter 2: Somatic Markers of Trauma

Trauma's somatic expression takes recognizable forms that, once one knows what to look for, are often more legible than the verbal narrative. These somatic markers include: chronic muscular tension in specific areas of the body (particularly the neck, shoulders, jaw, abdomen, and pelvic floor — areas where tension is held in response to threat); a chronically elevated or chronically depressed baseline autonomic arousal (the hyperarousal of sympathetic dominance or the hypoarousal of dorsal vagal shutdown); altered interoceptive processing (difficulty accurately perceiving internal bodily states, including hunger, satiation, pain, pleasure, and the somatic signals of emotion); and chronic pain syndromes without identified physical cause.

The relationship between trauma and chronic pain is among the most clinically important aspects of somatic trauma expression. Research on fibromyalgia, irritable bowel syndrome, chronic pelvic pain, and unexplained chronic back pain finds significantly elevated rates of trauma history — particularly childhood trauma — in people with these conditions, beyond what would be expected by chance. The specific mechanisms through which trauma produces chronic pain are not fully elucidated but include: central sensitization (the lowering of pain detection thresholds through sustained pain signal processing), altered HPA axis function affecting pain modulation, the somatic expression of unprocessed emotional experience, and the body's chronic holding of defensive tension in areas associated with the traumatic experience.

The body's autonomic state — its degree of sympathetic activation versus parasympathetic activation — is a moment-to-moment index of felt safety. Research using heart rate variability (HRV) as a physiological measure of vagal tone (parasympathetic nervous system activity) finds that trauma survivors show lower baseline HRV — less vagal regulation — than non-traumatized individuals, consistent with the chronic sympathetic dominance that trauma produces. Low HRV is associated with impaired emotional regulation, higher stress reactivity, and poorer physical health outcomes.

## Chapter 3: The Freeze Response and Its Aftermath

The freeze response — the dorsal vagal shutdown described in Polyvagal Theory — deserves particular attention as a somatic trauma response because it is the least understood and most stigmatized of the three trauma responses (fight, flight, freeze). The person who "freezes" during a traumatic experience — who is unable to move, speak, or act — is often the person most likely to experience self-blame following the trauma ("why didn't I do something?") and least likely to have their response recognized as the involuntary biological response it actually is.

The dorsal vagal shutdown occurs when the threat detection system assesses that active defense is not possible — when the threat is too overwhelming for fight or flight to be available. In these circumstances, the evolutionarily ancient dorsal vagal system produces a physiological state of immobility, reduced metabolic rate, and dissociation that served, in the ancestral environment of mammalian prey animals, as a "playing dead" strategy that could reduce predator interest and buy time for escape opportunities. In human trauma, this same response occurs when the threat overwhelms the person's sense of capacity for active defense.

The aftermath of freeze response includes the physiological state of incomplete mobilization: the threat-response energy that was mobilized but not discharged through active defense remains in the system, producing the chronic hyperarousal, somatic tension, and sensory hypersensitivity of unresolved trauma. Somatic approaches to trauma treatment specifically address this incomplete discharge — working with the body's physiological state to support the completion of the interrupted defensive responses through safe, titrated, somatic work.

## Chapter 4: Dissociation — Mind Leaving Body

Dissociation — the disruption of the normally integrated experience of consciousness, memory, identity, and perception — is the psychological mechanism that allows the mind to leave the body during overwhelming experience. Dissociation exists on a continuum from the mild (highway hypnosis, daydreaming, missing portions of a familiar routine) to the severe (depersonalization disorder, dissociative identity disorder). In the context of trauma, dissociation serves the adaptive function of allowing the person to survive an overwhelming experience by partially removing from conscious awareness the full impact of what is occurring.

The somatic experience of dissociation is distinctive and recognizable: depersonalization (the feeling of being detached from one's body, watching oneself from outside, feeling unreal as a person); derealization (the feeling that the environment is unreal, foggy, or dreamlike); and the numbing of physical sensation that accompanies these states. Many trauma survivors describe a long-standing pattern of being unable to feel their body below a certain threshold — neither pain, pleasure, tension, nor relaxation registers normally, because the dissociative disconnection from somatic experience has become chronic.

The treatment of somatic dissociation — reconnecting with the body's experience in a safe, titrated way — is one of the most important and most delicate aspects of somatic trauma work. The body was left because it was not safe to be in the body; returning to the body requires the gradual development of the felt sense of safety that makes embodiment possible without the overwhelming experience that produced the disconnection. This return is typically done in small doses, with careful attention to the window of tolerance — the range of arousal in which the body can be engaged without either overwhelming or numbing the person.

## Chapter 5: Somatic Trauma Therapies

The recognition of trauma's somatic dimension has generated a family of treatment approaches specifically designed to address the body's role in trauma storage and recovery. These approaches vary in their theoretical foundations and specific techniques, but share the common element of working with body-level experience — sensation, movement, posture, breath, and autonomic state — as the primary therapeutic medium.

Somatic Experiencing (SE), developed by Peter Levine, is the most widely practiced of the somatic trauma approaches and has the strongest research base. SE works with the concept of the "felt sense" (a term borrowed from philosopher Eugene Gendlin) — the whole-body, pre-verbal sense of a situation that is more holistic than any specific sensation or emotion. SE facilitates the tracking of somatic sensations as they arise and change in relation to traumatic material, supporting the pendulation (alternating between trauma-related and resource-related experience) and titration (working with small pieces of traumatic material rather than flooding) that allow the incomplete biological responses to complete safely.

Sensorimotor Psychotherapy, developed by Pat Ogden and Janina Fisher, integrates somatic awareness with the processing of traumatic memories and the repair of disrupted attachment patterns. It specifically addresses "trauma-related procedural learning" — the automatic body-level responses (postures, movement patterns, defensive preparations) that trauma has conditioned — by helping the person notice these somatic patterns and complete the interrupted defensive movements in a safe, supported context.

EMDR's bilateral stimulation component has been proposed to produce its effects partly through somatic mechanisms: the bilateral stimulation may activate a reflexive dual-attention state that enables simultaneous access to the traumatic material and its somatic components while maintaining the dual awareness (past memory versus present safety) that prevents re-traumatization during processing.

## Chapter 6: The Window of Tolerance

The "window of tolerance" — a concept developed by Daniel Siegel and applied to trauma treatment by Pat Ogden and others — describes the optimal zone of arousal within which the nervous system can process experience without being overwhelmed (hyper-aroused) or shut down (hypo-aroused). Within the window of tolerance, the person can engage with difficult material — including traumatic memories and their somatic correlates — with sufficient regulation to process and integrate the experience. Outside the window — in either the hyperarousal of emotional flooding or the hypoarousal of dissociation and shutdown — processing is unavailable because the system is in survival mode.

The window of tolerance varies across individuals and across moments: it is wider when a person is well-rested, physically healthy, emotionally resourced, and in a safe relational context, and narrower when they are depleted, stressed, or in an activating environment. In trauma survivors, the window is often significantly narrowed: a relatively small stimulus can move the person out of their window into hyperarousal or hypoarousal, and they may spend much of their daily life outside the window in one direction or the other.

Trauma treatment works partly by widening the window of tolerance: developing the nervous system's capacity for greater arousal range before losing regulation capacity. This widening occurs through the accumulation of experiences of navigating into distress and returning to regulation — each successful return expanding the range of distress the person can tolerate while remaining within their window. Somatic and mindfulness-based practices specifically develop interoceptive awareness and the capacity to tolerate somatic experience, which directly expands the window of tolerance.

## Chapter 7: Breath as Gateway

The breath is the one physiological process that is both automatic (maintained without conscious attention) and voluntarily controllable, making it a uniquely accessible intervention point for nervous system regulation. Research on respiratory physiology documents that the pattern of breathing directly influences the autonomic nervous system: slow, deep breathing with an extended exhalation activates the parasympathetic system through the respiratory sinus arrhythmia mechanism; rapid, shallow breathing with a short exhalation activates the sympathetic system.

The breath is also a primary site of trauma's somatic expression. Many trauma survivors habitually breathe shallowly, with chronic chest tightening, brief exhalations, and restricted expansion — a breathing pattern consistent with chronic low-grade defensive readiness. This restricted breathing pattern maintains sympathetic activation and reduces interoceptive access to the body's experience. Trauma-informed breath work — practices that gradually expand the range and depth of breathing while maintaining safety and regulation — works with this relationship between breath and nervous system state.

Research on breath-focused practices for trauma and PTSD has grown substantially in recent years. Yoga Nidra (a body-scan and breath-focused practice), coherent breathing (breathing at approximately 5-6 breaths per minute to maximize HRV), and box breathing (4-4-4-4 counts) have all shown efficacy in research studies for reducing PTSD symptoms and improving autonomic regulation. These practices are accessible, low-cost, and compatible with other trauma treatments.

## Chapter 8: Movement, Yoga, and Embodiment

The body's recovery from trauma requires more than verbal or cognitive processing — it requires the somatic experience of safety, agency, and aliveness in the body that was lost through traumatic experience. Movement practices offer one of the most direct paths to this recovery: they engage the body directly, develop interoceptive awareness, restore the sense of physical competence and agency, and — particularly in practices done in community — provide the social co-regulation that supports nervous system calming.

Research on yoga for PTSD, building on the clinical work of van der Kolk and colleagues at the Trauma Center in Brookline, Massachusetts, has found significant reductions in PTSD symptom severity following trauma-sensitive yoga programs. Trauma-sensitive yoga differs from standard yoga in its emphasis on invitational rather than directive language (participants are invited rather than told to take positions), the focus on interoceptive experience rather than external form, the cultivation of choice and agency in each moment, and the explicit avoidance of hands-on adjustments that could trigger trauma responses.

Research on Trauma Releasing Exercises (TRE), developed by David Berceli, finds efficacy for a specific set of exercises designed to invoke the tremoring mechanism — the natural physiological discharge of threat-response energy through voluntary trembling — that is observed in other mammals following threat encounters and is proposed to discharge the incomplete defensive responses left by trauma. While the research base for TRE is less developed than for yoga and SE, preliminary evidence and the theoretical consistency with Levine's somatic completion model support its place in the trauma treatment toolkit.

## Chapter 9: Amara's Return to Her Body

Amara's therapy involved, from its earliest stages, a different quality of attention to her body than she had been accustomed to. Her therapist did not immediately ask her to talk about what had happened. Instead, she asked her to notice: what does her body feel like right now, in this chair, in this room? What does she notice in her chest? Her abdomen? Her shoulders?

The noticing was difficult and, initially, produced mostly blankness — the numbness that chronic disconnection from bodily experience produces. But gradually, with patient attention and the therapist's modeling of curious, non-alarmed observation, something began to become available: tension in her shoulders she had not known was constant. Shallow breathing she had not known was habitual. A slight bracing in her abdomen that she had been carrying for six years without noticing. These observations were not catastrophic; they were just information. Her body was doing things, and she could observe them, and they did not overwhelm her.

From this foundation — the ability to notice somatic experience without being flooded by it — the processing work became possible. Not narrative processing first, but somatic: tracking how her body responded when the traumatic material was approached, supporting the completion of the incomplete defensive responses that her nervous system had been holding, and gradually, over months, allowing the body that had been carrying the trauma to do something different with what it had been holding. The chronic back pain decreased. The startle response moderated. She began, gradually, to inhabit herself again.

## Chapter 10: The Body Recovering

The body's recovery from trauma is gradual, non-linear, and often surprising. People in somatic trauma work frequently describe moments of unexpected release — tears that arise from body work without being connected to any conscious memory, shaking that completes itself without the person having done anything other than allow it, the sudden dropping of a tension that had been present for so long it was unrecognized as tension. These moments are not regressions or breakdowns — they are the body's natural completion processes finally being given the space to occur.

The goal of somatic trauma work is not a body without arousal or sensation — it is a body with regulated, responsive arousal and accurate sensation: a body that can activate in response to genuine threat and return to baseline when the threat has passed; that can feel pleasure, warmth, connection, and aliveness alongside the dysregulated states that trauma has produced; and that the person inhabits fully rather than managing from a distance. That full inhabitation — the experience of being genuinely at home in one's own body — is one of the most profound gifts that somatic trauma recovery makes possible.

## Epilogue

The body is not the enemy. It has been keeping record, in the only language available to it, of everything that has required it to survive. Its alarm systems, its tensions, its restricted breath and its chronic vigilance are not failures — they are the faithful records of what it has lived through. Healing, in the somatic sense, is not the erasure of these records but their revision: not forgetting what happened, but teaching the body that what happened is past, and that the present moment, with its actual conditions, is a different place than the one the body has been preparing for ever since.
