## How Wounds Shape the Way We Connect

### Prologue
By the time James came to couples therapy, he and his partner had been together for seven years and had developed what both of them, separately, described as a specific, exhausting pattern. Something would happen — something his partner did or didn't do — and James would disconnect, withdraw, and become distant in a way that was simultaneously impenetrable and deeply alarming to the partner, who would pursue and attempt to reconnect, which would cause James to withdraw further. Neither of them understood what was happening. From James's perspective, he was protecting himself from something he couldn't quite name. From his partner's perspective, the person she loved kept disappearing at the moments when she most needed him. What their couples therapist eventually helped them see was that what was happening between them was, in important ways, older than their relationship — it was James's nervous system replaying the most important thing it had ever learned about what happened when people got too close.

Trauma shapes relationships. This is among the most consistently documented findings in attachment and trauma research, and among the most important for the partners, family members, and close friends of trauma survivors to understand. The hypervigilance, the emotional numbing, the difficulty trusting, the sudden and inexplicable responses to situations that seem minor — these are not personality failures or relationship limitations. They are the relational consequences of experiences that taught the nervous system specific lessons about what relationships are like. Understanding these consequences — their origins, their mechanics, and what they require of both partners — is the foundation of a relationship that can survive trauma's relational effects and sometimes become, itself, part of the healing.

## Chapter 1: Attachment Trauma and Adult Relationships

The internal working models developed in early attachment relationships — the expectations about what relationships are like, whether others can be relied on, and whether one is worthy of care — are carried into adult relationships in ways that profoundly shape their dynamics. Research by Mary Main, Phillip Shaver, and others on the Adult Attachment Interview and adult attachment styles documents that early attachment experiences leave lasting templates that are specifically activated in adult intimate relationships.

For people whose early attachment relationships were characterized by harm, inconsistency, emotional unavailability, or terror, the adult intimate relationship — which is, by definition, a context of dependency and vulnerability — activates the same nervous system responses that the early attachment relationships established. The person with an anxious attachment style (who learned in childhood that caregivers were inconsistently available) brings into adult relationships the hypervigilance for signs of abandonment, the reassurance-seeking, and the intense emotional reactivity to perceived distance that the early environment made adaptive. The person with an avoidant attachment style (who learned that caregivers were consistently unavailable) brings into adult relationships the learned suppression of attachment needs and the defensive self-sufficiency that minimized the pain of repeated unresponsiveness.

The person with disorganized attachment — who learned that the caregiver was both the source of safety and the source of danger — brings into adult relationships the paradoxical pattern of approach-avoidance: simultaneously wanting and fearing closeness, simultaneously seeking and sabotaging connection. Research by Judith Crowell and colleagues on adult romantic relationships finds that attachment style significantly predicts relationship quality, conflict behavior, and relationship stability — with disorganized attachment producing the most relationship difficulty.

## Chapter 2: Hypervigilance in Relationships

Hypervigilance — the chronic, heightened scanning for threat that is one of PTSD's core features — has specific relational consequences that are among trauma's most disruptive effects on intimate relationships. The trauma-hypervigilant person monitors their partner's behavior, affect, and tone with constant, largely unconscious attention for signs of threat: of imminent criticism, of incipient abandonment, of the approach of harm. This monitoring is exhausting for the hypervigilant person and often experienced as surveillance or distrust by the partner who is being monitored.

The hypervigilance is not willfully produced and cannot be willfully suspended. It is the nervous system's automatic threat-monitoring function, sensitized by prior experience to detect the early warning signs of harm that was once genuinely present in relationship. What makes hypervigilance specifically disruptive in intimate relationships is that the early warning signs it is detecting are often ambiguous (partner's silence, change in tone, distracted attention) and may be interpreted as threat on the basis of prior experience rather than current reality. The pattern of misinterpreting normal partner behavior as threatening, and responding with defensive or withdrawal behavior that the partner experiences as inexplicable, is one of the most common relationship dynamics that trauma hypervigilance produces.

Research on couples where one partner has PTSD finds significantly reduced relationship satisfaction and higher conflict for both partners, with the hypervigilance-driven misinterpretation of partner behavior as one of the strongest predictors of this impairment. Helping the hypervigilant partner develop the capacity to pause, orient to the present context, and check the current threat signal against current reality — rather than automatically responding to the trauma-conditioned interpretation — is a key target of trauma-informed couples work.

## Chapter 3: Emotional Numbing and Intimacy

The emotional numbing that is a feature of PTSD's negative alterations in mood and cognition — the difficulty experiencing positive emotions, the detachment or estrangement from others, the restricted range of affect — is among trauma's most directly damaging effects on intimate relationships. Intimacy requires the capacity to experience and communicate emotional states, to respond emotionally to one's partner, and to allow oneself to be known in one's full emotional reality. Emotional numbing reduces or eliminates these capacities.

Partners of trauma survivors who are emotionally numb often describe feeling that they are in relationship with someone who is present physically but absent emotionally — that the connection they need and want from the relationship is inaccessible. Research on secondary traumatic stress finds that partners of people with PTSD show elevated rates of their own PTSD symptoms, partly through vicarious traumatization (exposure to the partner's traumatic material) and partly through the chronic relational stress of living with a partner whose emotional availability is significantly impaired.

The numbing is not a choice, and it is not the same as not caring. Many trauma survivors describe a genuine awareness of their emotional inaccessibility and a genuine wish to be emotionally present with partners they love, alongside the inability to bridge the gap between the wish and the actual emotional experience. This gap — between wanting to be emotionally available and being unable to — is itself a source of grief and shame for many trauma survivors in relationships.

## Chapter 4: Triggers in Relationship Contexts

Many of the most disruptive moments in relationships affected by trauma occur when the trauma survivor is triggered — when a stimulus in the current relationship activates the trauma-related response in ways that are sudden, unexpected, and often bewildering to both partners. The trigger may be a tone of voice that resembles the abuser's; a specific phrase; a situation that resembles a traumatic situation from the past; or even a positive situation — intimacy, vulnerability, being cared for — that the nervous system associates with trauma.

The experience of being triggered is typically not consciously connected to its source: the person does not think "this reminds me of that traumatic event"; they simply find themselves suddenly in a state of heightened arousal, fear, anger, or disconnection that has no apparent relationship to what is currently happening. This disconnection between the triggered state and its actual source is one of the most confusing features of trauma's relational effects — for the triggered person, who may be as confused as their partner about what just happened, and for the partner, who is experiencing a response that seems grossly disproportionate to the current situation.

Couples work with trauma-related triggers involves several components: psychoeducation (helping both partners understand what triggers are and how they work); identification (mapping the specific triggers that are most active in the relationship); signaling (developing communication about when triggering is occurring, so the partner can adjust their response rather than escalating into the negative interaction cycle that triggered states often produce); and trauma processing (addressing the underlying traumatic material that produces the triggers, so that the triggers themselves reduce over time).

## Chapter 5: Secondary Traumatic Stress

Living with, loving, or providing care for a trauma survivor can produce secondary traumatic stress — the development of trauma-related symptoms in the person who is close to the survivor but did not directly experience the original trauma. Secondary traumatic stress was first documented in therapists who worked with trauma survivors and has since been documented in partners, family members, children, and social workers of trauma survivors.

The mechanisms of secondary traumatic stress include: direct exposure to the survivor's traumatic material (hearing accounts of trauma, witnessing flashbacks or dissociative episodes); vicarious traumatization (developing a changed worldview and sense of safety through sustained exposure to another's traumatic experience); and the cumulative stress of the relational dynamics that trauma produces — the hypervigilance, the numbing, the triggers, the secondary traumatic stress responses that compound over time.

Research on partners of combat veterans with PTSD by Galovski and Lyons finds that partners show elevated rates of PTSD, depression, and anxiety relative to comparison populations, and that the severity of the partner's secondary traumatic stress is predicted by both the severity of the veteran's PTSD and the quality of the couple's communication about the veteran's trauma. This finding suggests that supporting the partner's ability to communicate about the impact of trauma on the relationship — a component of trauma-informed couples therapy — is important for the partner's wellbeing as well as the relationship's.

## Chapter 6: Trauma-Informed Couples Therapy

Several evidence-based couples therapy approaches have been adapted or developed specifically for couples affected by trauma. The most extensively researched is the integration of Emotionally Focused Therapy (EFT) with trauma-informed principles, developed by Kathryn Rheem and others and described in research by Johnson and colleagues.

EFT, which focuses on the identification and transformation of negative interactional cycles and the development of secure attachment between partners, is particularly well-suited to trauma-affected couples because it directly addresses the attachment disruptions that trauma produces. EFT's primary intervention target is the "negative cycle" — the repetitive interactional pattern in which each partner's trauma-driven behavior (hypervigilance, withdrawal, triggering) elicits the other's complementary trauma-driven behavior, producing an escalating cycle that both partners experience as the relationship failing rather than as two trauma-affected nervous systems colliding.

The trauma-informed adaptation of EFT adds specific attention to trauma education (helping both partners understand trauma's relational effects), the identification and communication of triggers, modifications for working with hyperarousal and dissociation in session, and coordination with individual trauma treatment alongside couples work. Research on EFT for couples affected by trauma finds significant improvements in relationship satisfaction and significant reductions in PTSD symptoms — a finding consistent with the theoretical account of relationship as a healing context for trauma.

## Chapter 7: The Relationship as a Healing Context

One of the most significant findings in the attachment and trauma literature is that secure, reliable, attuned relationship is itself a healing experience for trauma — particularly for relational trauma (trauma that originated in relationship with other people). The therapist who provides the consistent, attuned, responsive presence of secure attachment offers a "corrective relational experience" — an encounter with relationship that disconfirms the expectations set by traumatic relational experience and gradually revises the internal working model toward greater security.

The intimate partnership, with its sustained presence and its emotional significance, is potentially an even more powerful corrective relational experience than the therapeutic relationship — if it is characterized by the qualities that enable this healing. Research by Karen Kafetsios and colleagues on the healing potential of adult attachment relationships finds that secure romantic relationships can produce genuine movement toward earned security in partners who entered with insecure attachment histories, through the accumulation of experiences of responsive, reliable care.

The partner who understands trauma's relational effects — who can offer the consistent, non-reactive presence that the trauma-hypervigilant partner needs; who can remain regulated when their partner is triggered rather than escalating; who can tolerate the distance that trauma numbing produces without interpreting it as rejection — provides exactly the kind of sustained corrective experience that healing from relational trauma requires. This is a significant demand on the non-traumatized partner, and it requires both knowledge of what trauma does and adequate support for the partner's own wellbeing.

## Chapter 8: What Partners of Trauma Survivors Need

The relational demands of loving someone with significant trauma are real and require specific support. Partners of trauma survivors frequently describe: feeling blamed for responses that are actually trauma-triggered; feeling shut out by the numbing that they cannot access; feeling confused about what will trigger the trauma response and therefore walking on eggshells; and feeling their own needs for emotional connection going unmet in ways that produce their own distress and sometimes their own secondary traumatic stress.

Validation of these experiences is important: the partner's difficulties are real, the impact on their wellbeing is real, and their needs for support are legitimate. The framing in which everything is organized around the trauma survivor's needs, with the partner expected to provide unlimited understanding and accommodation without support of their own, is both unfair and ultimately unsustainable for the relationship.

The practical supports that are most helpful for partners of trauma survivors include: education about trauma and its relational effects; clear communication from the survivor about their trauma and its triggers, when they are able to provide it; access to their own therapeutic support; the development of specific communication strategies for when triggering or numbing is occurring; and explicit acknowledgment from the survivor that the partner's needs and experiences matter alongside the survivor's.

## Chapter 9: James and His Partner

James's couples therapy lasted fourteen months and produced changes that neither of them had expected when they began. The primary change was not the elimination of James's triggered withdrawal — that continued, though less frequently and with less intensity — but the development of a shared language for what was happening and a different choreography around it. When James felt himself withdrawing, he learned to name it briefly rather than simply disappearing. When his partner felt the familiar alarm of his disconnection, she learned to anchor herself in her own experience rather than pursuing him into the withdrawal. And when they came back together after the triggering had passed, they had more to say to each other about what had happened than they had when it simply occurred and was pretended not to have.

The relationship became, over those months, a place where James's history was not just accommodated but understood and, in a limited but genuine way, addressed. His partner could not repair what had happened to James; she could provide the consistent, available presence that made the present moment different from the past. The difference was not dramatic — it was a series of small, reliable moments of attunement that, accumulated across months and years, revised the nervous system's expectation of what being close to another person was like.

## Chapter 10: Toward Relational Healing

Trauma's relational effects are not permanent, and relationships are not merely the context in which trauma's effects are managed — they are, potentially, the context in which trauma heals. The secure, attuned, responsive relationship offers what the traumatic relationship failed to provide: the experience of genuine safety in another's presence, of needs being received and responded to, and of being known without being harmed. This experience, accumulated over time, is one of the most powerful healing interventions available — not a substitute for professional trauma treatment, but a complement to it that may in some ways go deeper.

The relationship that is informed about trauma — that understands what it does, what it produces, and what it needs — is better positioned to provide this healing context than the relationship that is simply well-intentioned. Knowledge, compassion, and adequate support for both partners create the conditions in which the relationship can be, as Sue Johnson describes, "a safe haven and secure base" — which is ultimately what human beings have always most needed from one another.

---

## Epilogue

Trauma is not only a wound to be healed in private. It is a disruption to the social fabric of the self — to the capacity for connection, trust, and the shared vulnerability that intimacy requires. Healing it, therefore, requires the mending of that social fabric, which is the work that genuine relationships, at their best, do. The relationship that understands trauma and responds to it with sustained patience and knowledge is not merely a context for recovery; it is a significant part of the recovery itself.
