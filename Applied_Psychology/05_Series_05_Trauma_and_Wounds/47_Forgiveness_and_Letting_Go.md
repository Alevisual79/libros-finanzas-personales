# Forgiveness and Letting Go
## Releasing the Resentment That Imprisons You

**Series 5: Trauma and Wounds — Book 7 of 10**

---

## Prologue

For eleven years, Paulo carried what had been done to him like ballast in his chest — a sustained, specific weight that his therapist eventually named as resentment. The person who had done the harm was no longer in his life, had never apologized, and, as far as Paulo knew, had never experienced any consequence proportionate to the harm they had caused. He thought about them more than he thought about most of the living people in his present life. He did not want to forgive them. Forgiveness, as he understood it, would mean something like: it's okay that this happened, or: you are absolved, or: I am giving you a gift you don't deserve. None of these felt acceptable. What he didn't know yet was that forgiveness, as the research understands it, means none of these things.

The psychology of forgiveness is one of the most significant and most practically important — and most frequently misunderstood — areas in contemporary applied psychology. The popular conception of forgiveness conflates it with condoning, forgetting, reconciling, and excusing in ways that make it feel unacceptable to people who have experienced genuine harm. The psychological and religious research on forgiveness reveals a different picture: forgiveness is a decision to relinquish resentment as an act of self-interest, entirely separable from whether one condones the harm, whether one maintains the relationship, or whether the harmer deserves to be forgiven. The research on its effects — physical, psychological, and relational — is among the strongest in positive psychology. And its primary beneficiary is the person who forgives, not the person who is forgiven.

## Chapter 1: What Forgiveness Is — and Is Not

The most important conceptual clarification in the psychology of forgiveness is what it is not. Forgiveness is not condoning (accepting or excusing the harmful behavior). It is not forgetting (obliterating the memory of what was done). It is not reconciling (resuming or repairing the relationship with the person who caused the harm). It is not the elimination of all negative feeling toward the person (one may still feel anger or sadness when thinking about what was done). And it is not a gift to the person who harmed you — it is a decision made for oneself.

Robert Enright's Forgiveness Process Model — the most extensively researched psychological framework for forgiveness — defines forgiveness as "a willingness to abandon one's right to resentment, negative judgment, and indifferent behavior toward one who unjustly injured us, while fostering the undeserved qualities of compassion, generosity, and even love toward him or her." This definition makes explicit that forgiveness involves the acknowledgment of genuine harm (not minimization), the recognition that resentment is a legitimate right (not the premature abandonment of appropriate anger), and a deliberate choice to relinquish that right for one's own benefit.

The distinction between forgiveness and reconciliation is particularly important to establish clearly. One can forgive the parent who abused them without resuming or repairing the relationship. One can forgive the partner who was dishonest without continuing the relationship. One can forgive an aggressor without ever seeing them again. The internal process of forgiveness — the decision to relinquish resentment — is entirely independent of the external decisions about the relationship. This distinction is crucial because the conflation of forgiveness with reconciliation is the primary reason many people who could benefit from forgiveness refuse to consider it: they fear that forgiving means accepting the person back into their life.

## Chapter 2: The Physiology of Resentment

Resentment is not merely a psychological state — it is a sustained physiological activation that has measurable effects on the body. Research on the physiology of unforgiveness finds that holding resentment is associated with chronic activation of the sympathetic nervous system, elevated cortisol, higher blood pressure and cardiovascular reactivity, impaired immune function, and increased inflammatory markers. The person who ruminates regularly on a past harm is, physiologically, repeatedly re-experiencing the threat and arousal of the original harm — the body does not distinguish between the memory of harm and the harm itself.

Research by Everett Worthington, Charlotte van Oyen Witvliet, and colleagues using laboratory paradigms that induce rumination about specific past harms versus forgiveness-focused processing found significant differences in physiological arousal profiles: rumination on unforgiven harm produced sustained increases in heart rate, blood pressure, and cortisol; forgiveness-focused imagery produced comparably reduced or reversed these physiological responses. The implication is physiologically direct: forgiveness reduces chronic sympathetic activation, and resentment maintains it.

The research on unforgiveness and health outcomes is consistent with this physiological account. A series of studies by Loren Toussaint and colleagues found that higher trait forgiveness (the tendency to forgive across situations) was associated with lower mortality in nationally representative samples, controlling for demographics, health behaviors, and baseline health status. The effect sizes are comparable to those of other major health behavior predictors — forgiveness appears to be a genuine health variable, not merely a morally relevant one.

## Chapter 3: The Research on Forgiveness Interventions

The evidence base for forgiveness interventions has developed substantially since the 1990s and now includes well-designed randomized controlled trials across diverse populations. Enright's Forgiveness Process Model and Worthington's REACH model are the two most extensively researched intervention frameworks.

Enright's Process Model proposes four phases: Uncovering (examining and acknowledging the harm, the anger, and the effects of the harm); Decision (making an informed choice to pursue forgiveness as a strategy for one's own healing, understanding that it is not condoning); Work (actively working to develop empathy for the person who caused the harm, understanding their background and limitations without excusing the specific harm); and Deepening (finding meaning in the experience, experiencing reduced resentment, and discovering positive changes). Research trials of Enright's model find significant reductions in anxiety, depression, and unforgiveness, with effects maintained at follow-up.

Worthington's REACH model — an acronym for Recall the hurt, Empathize with the one who hurt you, offer the Altruistic gift of forgiveness, Commit publicly to forgive, Hold onto forgiveness when doubt arises — has been tested in a series of group-format intervention studies finding significant effects on forgiveness, wellbeing, and health-related outcomes. A notable feature of REACH is its explicit framing of forgiveness as an "altruistic gift" — something one gives for moral reasons even when the recipient may not deserve it — which appeals to people for whom the self-interest framing of forgiveness feels insufficient.

## Chapter 4: The Empathy Challenge

Most forgiveness frameworks include the development of empathy for the person who caused the harm as a component of the forgiveness process — a component that is often the most challenging and most misunderstood element. Empathy in forgiveness is not the validation of the harm, the condoning of the behavior, or the denial of its wrongness. It is the attempt to understand the other person's experience, history, and limitations in ways that situate their harmful behavior in a human context — while fully acknowledging the harm they caused.

The developmental perspective on the person who caused harm is one of the most useful orientations for this work. Most people who cause significant harm to others were themselves harmed, often significantly, in ways that produced the psychological conditions that enabled the harm they later caused: the abusive parent who was abused, the person with narcissistic patterns who developed them in response to early relational trauma, the person who was dishonest because honesty felt unbearably dangerous in their formative experiences. This developmental context does not excuse the harm; it humanizes the person who caused it in ways that make them less of a uniquely malevolent monster and more of a damaged human being whose damage caused collateral harm.

The challenge with empathy in forgiveness is the risk that it can feel like minimization to people who have experienced severe harm. "Empathizing" with someone who deliberately harmed you feels like a betrayal of the harm you experienced. The distinction between empathy (understanding) and justification (acceptance) is crucial here: one can understand another person's developmental history without concluding that their behavior was acceptable or that they should not be held accountable. Empathy in forgiveness is a tool for releasing resentment, not a verdict on the harmfulness of what was done.

## Chapter 5: Self-Forgiveness

Self-forgiveness — the release of self-directed resentment, shame, and condemnation for one's own past actions — is a distinct and somewhat underresearched dimension of the forgiveness literature. Research by Mark Leary, June Price Tangney, and others distinguishes self-forgiveness from other self-directed responses to failure: genuine self-forgiveness is not the same as minimizing or excusing (which avoids taking responsibility for the harm) or self-punishment (which continues beyond any productive function) or positive self-assessment (which ignores the harm done).

Genuine self-forgiveness follows the same structure as forgiveness of others: it requires acknowledging the harm done (not minimizing it), taking genuine responsibility for it (not externalizing blame), experiencing appropriate remorse and engaging in repair where possible, and then — without indefinite continuation of self-punishment — releasing the resentment and condemnation directed at oneself. Research by Leary and colleagues finds that genuine self-forgiveness is associated with greater motivation to repair harm and avoid repetition — not with complacency or moral disengagement, which is the common fear about self-forgiveness.

The religious and philosophical dimensions of self-forgiveness are particularly relevant: many religious traditions emphasize the possibility of genuine forgiveness of oneself as part of broader spiritual practice, and the research on religion/spirituality and forgiveness finds that religious affiliation and practice are among the strongest predictors of forgiveness capacity. The mechanisms appear to include the provision of a framework within which forgiveness makes sense as a value, social support from religious community, and specific practices (prayer, meditation, ritual) that facilitate the forgiveness process.

## Chapter 6: When Forgiveness Is Difficult or Impossible

Forgiveness research and frameworks developed primarily in the context of ordinary interpersonal harms may not straightforwardly translate to the most severe forms of harm — war crimes, genocide, sexual violence, murder. The question of whether forgiveness is appropriate, possible, or desirable in these contexts is both a genuine psychological question and a moral one that deserves serious treatment rather than formulaic answer.

Research and clinical accounts of forgiveness in the context of severe harm — including published accounts of forgiveness by survivors of mass violence in South Africa's Truth and Reconciliation Commission process and by survivors of severe individual trauma — document that forgiveness can occur in these contexts, but that it typically requires more time, more support, and a different process than forgiveness for ordinary harms. It also frequently involves significant ambivalence and partial forgiveness: the person who has been severely harmed may find that they can forgive certain aspects of what was done while remaining unable to forgive others, and this partial forgiveness is genuine and valuable.

The position of not forgiving is also a legitimate outcome in some circumstances — particularly when the harm was severe, when the perpetrator has shown no accountability or remorse, or when the person genuinely does not yet have the psychological resources to pursue forgiveness. The pressure to forgive — from family, religious community, or therapeutic context — before a person is ready is itself potentially harmful, and the psychological literature on forgiveness is clear that forgiveness imposed from the outside rather than chosen from within is not genuine forgiveness and does not produce the psychological benefits that genuine forgiveness produces.

## Chapter 7: Letting Go Without Forgiving

The concept of "letting go" — while related to forgiveness — is distinct and may be more accessible for people who find themselves unable to forgive but are seeking to reduce the burden of resentment. Letting go, in its psychological sense, involves the deliberate reduction of rumination and the re-investment of psychological resources that resentment has been consuming — not through the process of forgiving, but through a shift in relationship to the resentment that reduces its dominance without requiring the full forgiveness process.

Acceptance-based approaches — particularly the Buddhist concept of non-attachment and ACT's acceptance framework — provide useful tools for this less-than-forgiveness letting go. The Buddhist recognition that suffering is produced by the clinging to things being other than they are is directly applicable to resentment: the resentment about a past harm clinging to the wish that it had not happened. Radical acceptance of what was — not endorsing it, not pretending it was acceptable, but releasing the struggle against the unchangeable fact of its having occurred — reduces the sustained physiological activation of resentment without requiring the development of empathy for the perpetrator that forgiveness frameworks typically include.

## Chapter 8: Forgiveness in Trauma Recovery

The intersection of forgiveness work with trauma recovery requires careful navigation. The recommendation to "forgive and move on" that trauma survivors often receive from well-meaning others — frequently premature, often pressured, and not sufficiently specific about what forgiveness actually involves — can be harmful when it bypasses the necessary processing of the trauma and the appropriate mobilization of anger that is part of trauma recovery.

The sequencing of forgiveness work in the context of trauma typically suggests that forgiveness is most productive after the trauma has been adequately processed through evidence-based trauma treatment: after the full experience of the harm has been acknowledged, the anger about it mobilized and expressed appropriately, and the primary traumatic symptoms reduced to a level that allows the cognitive and emotional work of forgiveness to proceed without being overwhelmed by intrusion and hyperarousal. Attempting forgiveness before this groundwork is in place may produce superficial "forgiveness" that bypasses rather than processes the trauma.

When forgiveness work follows adequate trauma processing, it can provide significant additional healing: the shift from ongoing resentment to the release of forgiveness reduces the sustained physiological arousal that resentment produces, enables a different relationship with the memory of the harm, and frees psychological resources previously consumed by the maintenance of unforgiveness for investment in the present life.

## Chapter 9: Paulo's Decision

Paulo did not decide to forgive in a single moment of clarity. The decision developed gradually, over months of therapy, as the difference between what forgiveness would actually mean and what he had feared it meant became clearer. He was not going to forgive the harm — the harm had been real and wrong and his anger about it had been legitimate and was still partly present. He was going to forgive the person — not the specific acts, but the damaged human being who had committed them — as an act of releasing the resentment that had been taking more of him than the original harm had.

The release, when it came, was not dramatic. It was a quieting: the person who had lived in his chest as a sustained source of activation gradually became, over weeks and months, a memory he could think about without the full physiological arousal of the resentment returning. They had not become unimportant; what had happened had not become acceptable. What had changed was his relationship to what had happened — from ongoing wound to past event, located in the past rather than continuously present.

## Chapter 10: The Freedom of Release

Resentment, sustained, is a form of ongoing relationship with the person who harmed you — a relationship organized around the harm, maintained through rumination, and consuming psychological resources that might otherwise be available for living. The paradox of resentment is that it punishes primarily the person who carries it, not the person it is directed at. In Paulo's metaphor: "I had been holding a hot coal, waiting to throw it at someone who had already left the room."

Forgiveness — the deliberate relinquishment of resentment as an act of self-interest — is not the claim that what was done was acceptable. It is the recognition that carrying the resentment indefinitely costs more than it is worth, and that what was done does not deserve the ongoing resources of one's psychological life. The freedom that follows is not the absence of the memory or the complete elimination of the harm's effects — those remain. It is the reduction of the resentment's ongoing claim on the present, and the availability of psychological resources for the life that continues beyond the harm.

---

## Epilogue

Forgiveness is not owed to anyone who has harmed you. It is a gift — to yourself. The resentment you have earned may be entirely justified; the anger about the harm may be entirely appropriate. None of this means that carrying it indefinitely serves you, or that releasing it diminishes the harm's reality, or that the person who harmed you deserves anything from the act of your forgiveness. You may choose to forgive or not to forgive; the choice belongs entirely to you. If you do choose it, what you will find on the other side is not the validation of what was done but the freedom that releasing the resentment makes available for the life you are still living.
