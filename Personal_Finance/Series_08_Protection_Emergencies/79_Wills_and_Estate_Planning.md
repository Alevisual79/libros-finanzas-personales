### Prologue
Isabel's mother died without a will.

This fact — a seemingly simple oversight — produced 14 months of legal complexity, family tension, and administrative burden that cost more than $12,000 in attorney fees, court costs, and delays before her mother's estate was finally settled.

Her mother's assets weren't complicated: a modest checking account, a savings account, a small life insurance policy, and personal property. But without a will specifying how these should be distributed, the state's intestacy laws controlled the outcome — and the outcome didn't match what her mother had wanted. The life insurance policy beneficiary designation hadn't been updated in 20 years and still named her mother's first husband (divorced for 15 years) as primary beneficiary.

Isabel resolved not to repeat this experience for her own daughter. Estate planning, she learned, isn't just a document — it's a gift to the people you leave behind.

## Chapter 1: Why Most People Don't Have Estate Plans

More than half of American adults don't have a will. The reasons are consistent:
- "I don't have enough assets to need one"
- "I'll do it eventually — there's no rush"
- "It's expensive and complicated"
- "Thinking about death is uncomfortable"

None of these objections survives examination.

**"Not enough assets":** Even modest assets — a car, a bank account, a retirement account, personal property — create administrative complexity at death without proper documentation. And most people who say they don't have enough assets now will have significantly more in 10-20 years when they're still using the same logic.

**"Eventually":** Death arrives on its schedule, not yours. The person who didn't "get around to it" at 35 often doesn't get around to it at 45, 55, or 65 — until the opportunity passes entirely.

**"Expensive and complicated":** Basic estate planning documents (will, healthcare directive, durable power of attorney) cost $300-$800 at most estate planning attorneys. Online tools (Trust & Will, LegalZoom) reduce this to $100-$300 for straightforward situations. Not cheap, but not prohibitive.

**"Thinking about death is uncomfortable":** This is the real reason. Estate planning forces acknowledgment of mortality. The discomfort is real; the cost of not planning is higher.

## Chapter 2: What Happens Without a Will — Intestate Succession

When someone dies without a will, they die "intestate." The state's intestacy laws determine how their assets are distributed — and the outcome often doesn't match what the deceased would have chosen.

Intestacy succession rules vary by state but generally follow a hierarchy:
- Spouse gets a share (often half or everything if no children)
- Children split the remainder (or everything if no spouse)
- If no spouse or children: parents, then siblings, then more distant relatives

Common intestacy surprises:
- **Unmarried partner:** A long-term partner receives nothing under intestacy unless specifically named in a will. Many states recognize no inheritance rights for unmarried partners.
- **Step-children:** Legally adopted step-children inherit; non-adopted step-children typically do not under intestacy.
- **Estranged relatives:** An estranged parent or sibling may inherit alongside people the deceased was close to.
- **Minor children:** Assets left to minor children are managed by a court-appointed conservator until age 18 — not necessarily the person the deceased would have chosen.

## Chapter 3: The Core Estate Planning Documents

A complete basic estate plan requires four documents:

**Will:** Specifies who receives your assets, names an executor (the person who manages the estate settlement process), and if you have minor children, names a guardian. Without a will, courts make guardian decisions — not you.

**Durable Power of Attorney (DPOA):** Names someone to make financial decisions on your behalf if you are incapacitated (but still alive). Without a DPOA, your family may need to go to court to establish guardianship or conservatorship to manage your finances while you're incapacitated.

**Healthcare Power of Attorney / Healthcare Proxy:** Names someone to make medical decisions on your behalf if you cannot make them yourself. Different from the financial DPOA; a separate document in most states.

**Advance Healthcare Directive / Living Will:** Specifies your wishes for end-of-life medical care: do you want life-sustaining treatment if there is no reasonable expectation of recovery? Do you consent to organ donation? Relieves your family from making these decisions under devastating emotional circumstances.

All four documents work together. Dying with only a will but without healthcare directives leaves critical decision-making gaps during potential incapacity.

## Chapter 4: Beneficiary Designations — The Most Important Documents You're Not Thinking About

Here is a critical fact that many people don't understand: beneficiary designations on retirement accounts and life insurance policies override your will.

If your IRA says "beneficiary: former spouse" and your will says "everything to my current spouse," your IRA goes to the former spouse. Every time. The beneficiary designation is a contract between you and the financial institution that supersedes estate law.

**Where beneficiary designations apply:**
- 401(k), 403(b), and other employer retirement plans
- IRAs (traditional, Roth, SEP, SIMPLE)
- Life insurance policies
- Annuities
- Some bank accounts (POD — payable on death designations)
- Some brokerage accounts (TOD — transfer on death designations)

**Review checklist:**
- Check every account with a beneficiary designation annually or after any major life event
- Name a primary AND contingent beneficiary (if the primary predeceases you, the contingent receives)
- For minor children as beneficiaries: consult an estate attorney about trust structures; leaving significant assets directly to minors triggers court oversight
- For retirement accounts: understand the tax implications for non-spouse beneficiaries (the SECURE Act changed inherited IRA rules significantly in 2020)

Isabel discovered three outdated beneficiary designations during her estate planning review: her life insurance policy still listed her ex-husband, her old employer 401(k) (rolled to an IRA) had no contingent beneficiary, and her bank account had no POD designation. All three were corrected in 45 minutes of administrative work.

## Chapter 5: Trusts — When a Will Isn't Enough

For many people, a will is sufficient estate planning. For others — particularly those with larger estates, minor children, or assets in multiple states — a trust provides advantages that a will cannot.

**Revocable living trust:** A legal structure that holds your assets during your life and directs their distribution after your death — outside of probate. The trust is "revocable" because you can modify or revoke it during your lifetime.

**Advantages:**
- **Avoids probate:** Assets in a trust transfer to beneficiaries without going through the court-supervised probate process. Probate is public, slow (months to years), and costly.
- **Multi-state property:** Real estate in multiple states would require probate in each state without a trust. Trust assets pass without multi-state probate.
- **Privacy:** Wills become public court records during probate. Trusts remain private.
- **Incapacity management:** The trust continues during your incapacity without court intervention; the successor trustee takes over.

**Disadvantages:**
- More expensive to establish ($1,500-$5,000 vs. $300-$800 for a will)
- Requires "funding" — assets must be transferred into the trust; an unfunded trust at death doesn't provide the probate benefits

**Who benefits from a trust:**
- People with real estate in multiple states
- People with estates above their state's probate threshold (typically $50,000-$200,000)
- People who value privacy
- People with minor children beneficiaries or beneficiaries with special needs

## Chapter 6: The Probate Process

Probate is the court-supervised process of validating a will and distributing an estate. For estates that go through it, probate:
- Takes 6 months to 2+ years depending on the state and estate complexity
- Costs 3-7% of the estate value in attorney fees and court costs
- Creates a public court record of the estate's assets and beneficiaries
- Can create family conflict when contested

Probate avoidance is a primary motivation for trust planning, but it's not the only option:
- **Beneficiary designations (TOD, POD):** Assets with payable-on-death or transfer-on-death designations pass outside probate
- **Joint ownership:** Property held in joint tenancy with right of survivorship passes to the surviving owner outside probate
- **Small estate procedures:** Most states have simplified probate or small estate affidavit procedures for estates below a threshold (typically $25,000-$100,000)

A will-based estate plan with careful use of beneficiary designations and joint ownership may avoid most probate for a modest estate without requiring a trust.

## Chapter 7: Tax-Efficient Estate Transfer

For most Americans, federal estate taxes are not a concern: the federal estate tax exemption in 2024 is approximately $13.6 million per individual ($27.2 million per married couple). Only estates above this threshold pay federal estate tax.

However:
- **State estate taxes:** Several states have their own estate taxes with much lower exemptions ($1-$3 million in some states). Massachusetts, Oregon, and Washington have estate taxes beginning at $1-$2 million. Residents of these states with significant wealth may need estate tax planning.
- **Gift tax:** Individuals can give up to $18,000 per recipient per year (2024 annual gift tax exclusion) without gift tax consequences. Married couples can give $36,000/year to each recipient. Gifts above this amount reduce the lifetime estate/gift tax exemption.
- **Basis step-up:** Assets inherited at death receive a "step-up" in cost basis to the value at the date of death. This eliminates the capital gains tax on appreciation that occurred during the deceased's lifetime — a significant tax benefit for inherited appreciated assets.
- **Roth IRA conversion before death:** Roth IRA assets pass to heirs income-tax-free (under the 10-year distribution rule post-SECURE Act). Converting traditional IRA to Roth during your lifetime — paying the tax now — can leave heirs a significantly larger after-tax inheritance.

## Chapter 8: Planning for Minor Children

Parents of minor children have the most urgent estate planning need. Two documents are critical:

**Guardianship designation in your will:** Without a named guardian, a court decides who raises your children. A court may not choose the person you would have chosen. The will designates a guardian — and should also name a backup.

**Guardianship considerations:**
- Same values and parenting philosophy?
- Financial capacity and stability?
- Geographic accessibility to children's existing life?
- Age and health?
- Willingness? (Ask before naming someone)

**Testamentary trust for minor children:** Rather than leaving assets directly to minor children (which triggers court conservatorship), a will can establish a trust that holds assets for children until they reach a specified age (18, 21, 25). The trustee manages the assets and distributes them for specified purposes (education, health, welfare) until the age is reached.

The trustee and the guardian can be different people — and often should be. Financial management and parenting are different skills.

## Chapter 9: Elena's Estate Plan at 33

Elena's estate plan, as a 33-year-old without children or marriage:
- **Will:** Leave everything to her sister (primary) and parents (contingent)
- **Durable power of attorney:** Name her sister as agent
- **Healthcare power of attorney:** Name her sister as agent
- **Advance directive:** Complete document specifying her wishes for end-of-life care and organ donation
- **Beneficiary designations:** Update Roth IRA and 401(k) to match will intentions; add TOD designation to brokerage account

Total cost: $450 at a local estate planning attorney for the four documents plus the half-day of her time for the attorney meeting and beneficiary updates.

"I don't need estate planning at 33" — actually, she needed it most when she was young and healthy. Estate planning, like insurance, is hardest to get when you need it most (illness, accident). Get it when you're well and healthy and it's easiest.

## Chapter 10: Keeping Your Estate Plan Current

Estate planning documents are not written once and forgotten. They require periodic review and updating.

**Review triggers:**
- Marriage or divorce
- Birth or adoption of a child
- Death of a named executor, guardian, trustee, or beneficiary
- Significant change in assets or wealth
- Move to a different state (estate laws vary significantly)
- Change in your wishes about beneficiaries or guardianship
- Federal or state law changes affecting estate taxes

**Minimum review frequency:** Every 5 years, even if no triggering event occurred. Values change. People change. Laws change.

**Document storage:** Keep original documents in a secure but accessible location. Tell your executor and healthcare proxy where documents are kept. Digital copies are useful for reference but may not be sufficient for legal purposes — originals matter for most estate documents.

**Immediate exercise:** For every financial account and insurance policy you own, look up the current beneficiary designation. Make a list. Are any outdated? Do all have both primary and contingent beneficiaries? This 30-minute exercise surfaces the most common and most damaging estate planning gap.

## Epilogue

Estate planning is not about death. It is about caring enough for the people you love to protect them from unnecessary legal complexity, family conflict, and preventable financial loss.

The documents aren't hard to create. The attorney meeting is two hours of your life. The beneficiary updates are 30 minutes at a computer. The cost is several hundred dollars.

The alternative — no plan — costs your family months of grief compounded by administrative chaos.

Do it. Now, while you can.
