# Life Insurance
## The Coverage That Protects the People Who Depend on You

**Series 8: Protection & Emergencies — Book 5 of 10**

---

## Prologue

Life insurance is the only financial product whose benefit is specifically designed for someone who won't be there to use it. You buy it not for yourself but for the people who depend on your income and presence — and who would face financial hardship if that income disappeared.

For people without dependents, or with sufficient assets to protect dependents without insurance, life insurance may be unnecessary. For people with dependents and insufficient assets to protect them, life insurance is arguably the most urgent financial protection available.

Elena, unmarried and without children, has no dependents. She has a small term policy that would cover her student loans — she cosigned with her parents — but doesn't need substantial coverage.

Ruben, also unmarried, has no policy. His only debt is car-related; his parents are financially independent. His situation is appropriate for someone without dependents.

Isabel carries $750,000 in term coverage. Her death would significantly impact her two adult children who still receive her financial support, and her death would eliminate her mortgage repayment. The insurance addresses both.

---

## Chapter 1: Who Needs Life Insurance

Life insurance exists to replace income and resources that dependents would lose if you died. The question "do I need life insurance?" reduces to: who depends on my income and resources, and would they face financial hardship without them?

**Clear need:**
- Parents of minor children (especially the primary earner)
- Partners whose income supports shared mortgage or living expenses
- People with financially dependent elderly parents
- People with co-signed debt that would become a family member's obligation

**Possible need:**
- Parents whose adult children still receive financial support
- People with business partners who would face buyout needs at death
- People with income-producing businesses that need continuity protection

**Generally no need:**
- Single people with no dependents and no co-signed debt
- People whose assets are sufficient to protect dependents without insurance
- Children (life insurance for children is almost always unnecessary and often a high-cost sales product)

## Chapter 2: Term vs. Permanent Life Insurance

The debate between term and permanent (whole life, universal life) insurance is one of the most significant financial product decisions people make.

**Term life insurance:**
- Coverage for a specified period (10, 20, or 30 years)
- Pure death benefit — no cash value accumulation
- Substantially lower premiums than permanent coverage for the same death benefit
- Appropriate for the most common use case: replacing income during the years when dependents are financially dependent

**Permanent life insurance (whole life, universal life):**
- Coverage for the entire life (no expiration)
- Includes a cash value component that grows over time
- Premiums are substantially higher than term for the same death benefit
- The cash value can be borrowed against or surrendered

**The general financial planning consensus:** For most people, term life insurance is the appropriate choice. The lower premium allows the savings difference to be invested in actual investment accounts — which typically outperform the return embedded in whole life policies. "Buy term and invest the difference" is one of the most consistent pieces of advice across independent financial planners.

Permanent insurance is appropriate in specific situations: very high net worth estates with estate tax concerns, certain business succession planning scenarios, and situations where lifelong coverage is genuinely needed. For most middle-income people, these situations don't apply.

## Chapter 3: How Much Coverage Do You Need?

Several approaches exist for calculating appropriate life insurance coverage:

**Income replacement method:** 10-12× current annual income. The logic: invested at 5-7%, the death benefit replaces the income it was generating.

**DIME method:** Debt + Income replacement + Mortgage + Education costs. Sum of all financial obligations the surviving family would need to cover.

**Needs analysis:** The most thorough approach. Calculate the full financial needs of survivors over the relevant period, subtract existing assets, and the gap is the coverage needed.

A practical approximation for most families: enough to pay off mortgage + other debt + 10-12× annual income for each dependent year remaining.

Isabel's calculation:
- Mortgage: $180,000
- Other debt: $0
- Income × 10: $780,000 (salary of $78,000)
- Education potential support for adult children: $30,000
- Minus existing assets: -$240,000
- Coverage needed: approximately $750,000

She carries exactly $750,000 in coverage.

## Chapter 4: Term Life Insurance Mechanics

Term life insurance is straightforward but has details that matter:

**Policy term selection:**
- 10-year term: appropriate if dependents will be financially independent within 10 years (children are almost teenagers, mortgage payoff is near)
- 20-year term: the most common choice for parents of young children or new mortgagors
- 30-year term: appropriate for young parents with very young children, or large mortgage commitments

**Renewability:** Some term policies are guaranteed renewable at expiration, though at substantially higher premiums. It's usually better to buy the longer initial term than to plan on renewal.

**Premium levels:** Term life premiums are "level" — the same each year for the policy term. This predictability makes budgeting straightforward.

**Underwriting:** Premiums depend on age, health, smoking status, and coverage amount. Applying when young and healthy produces the lowest lifetime cost. Common health conditions (controlled blood pressure, cholesterol) typically affect premiums modestly. Serious conditions can make coverage unavailable or very expensive.

## Chapter 5: Getting Quotes and Buying a Policy

Term life insurance has become commoditized and comparatively easy to purchase.

**Online comparison:** Sites like Policygenius, Term4Sale, and SelectQuote provide multi-company quotes in minutes. The quotes are accurate and the purchase process is fully online.

**Medical exam requirement:** Most policies above $500,000 (and many above $250,000) require a medical exam — blood pressure, blood draw, basic physical. This is scheduled through the insurer and typically completed at home by a traveling nurse.

**No-exam policies:** Available up to approximately $3,000,000 with some insurers for healthy applicants. Slightly higher premiums in exchange for skipping the exam. Appropriate for people who prefer the convenience.

**Application to approval timeline:** Typically 2-6 weeks for fully underwritten policies. No-exam policies can be approved in 24-48 hours.

**Key comparison factors:** Price is important but not the only factor. The insurer's financial strength (A.M. Best rating of A- or better is recommended), the exclusions and policy terms, and the customer service record all matter.

## Chapter 6: Policy Ownership and Beneficiary Designations

Who owns the policy and who is named as beneficiary are decisions that affect how the death benefit is paid and to whom.

**Ownership:** The policy owner controls the policy — can cancel it, change beneficiaries, take loans against cash value. Usually this is the insured person; sometimes it is a trust (for estate planning purposes).

**Beneficiaries:** Primary and contingent (backup) beneficiaries should be named. Avoid naming "estate" as beneficiary — it subjects the death benefit to probate. Name individuals or trusts specifically.

**Updating beneficiaries:** Life changes (marriage, divorce, death of a named beneficiary) require updating beneficiary designations. Life insurance that goes to an ex-spouse because a designation was never updated after divorce is a common and devastating error.

**Minor children as beneficiaries:** Insurance companies don't pay directly to minors. If you want to provide for minor children, name a trust or a trusted adult as beneficiary with explicit instructions, or use a custodial arrangement.

**Immediate exercise:** If you have life insurance, look up your current beneficiary designations (usually available through the insurer's website or by calling). Verify they are current and correctly named.

## Chapter 7: Group Life Insurance Through Employers

Most employers offer group life insurance at low or no cost — typically one or two times annual salary. This is a valuable benefit but usually insufficient as a primary life insurance strategy.

Issues with relying on group coverage:
- It ends when employment ends
- One to two times salary is almost always insufficient for families with dependents and mortgages
- You may not be able to convert it to individual coverage at affordable rates when you leave

Supplemental group life insurance (available through many employers at group rates) is worth considering, but the group rate advantage diminishes at higher coverage levels, and you lose the coverage if you change employers.

The best practice: buy an individual term policy (portable, coverage you control) and use employer group coverage as supplemental.

## Chapter 8: Life Insurance for the Self-Employed

For self-employed individuals without employer group coverage, individual term life insurance is the only option — and it is more important because there's no employer backup.

Ruben's situation: as a freelancer, he has no employer coverage. When he marries (or otherwise has dependents), an individual term policy will be essential. In anticipation of this life stage, he's keeping his health profile favorable for underwriting.

For business owners, life insurance also serves business succession purposes:
- **Key person insurance:** The business owns a policy on a critical person (owner or key employee); the death benefit provides the business with capital to survive the loss
- **Buy-sell agreement funding:** Partners buy insurance on each other; the death benefit funds the buyout of the deceased partner's interest by the surviving partners

These are more complex structures worth discussing with an attorney and financial planner.

## Chapter 9: When to Review and Adjust Coverage

Life insurance needs change with life. Regular review ensures coverage remains appropriate.

Trigger events for coverage review:
- Marriage or partnership (likely increased need)
- Birth or adoption of children (increased need)
- Purchase of home with mortgage (increased need)
- Significant income increase (may need more coverage)
- Children reaching financial independence (coverage need decreasing)
- Paying off mortgage (coverage need decreasing)
- Accumulation of significant investment portfolio (coverage need decreasing as assets grow)

The goal of life insurance is not permanent — it is to bridge the period between current financial vulnerability and self-insured financial resilience. As assets grow and dependents become independent, the coverage need decreases. Most people who achieve financial independence find that life insurance is no longer necessary.

## Chapter 10: The Life Insurance Nobody Talks About

Life insurance is one of those financial decisions that's easy to avoid because it requires confronting mortality. The cultural discomfort with death extends to the financial planning around it.

The people who are protected by life insurance are not the policyholders — they're the people left behind. The parent who defers buying a policy "until after the holidays" is not protecting themselves; they're leaving their children unprotected in the event of an unlikely but catastrophic outcome.

The purchase of a life insurance policy is a clear act of financial care for the people who matter to you. It costs relatively little (a healthy 35-year-old can buy $500,000 in 20-year term coverage for $20-$30/month). The protection it provides is enormous.

---

## Epilogue

Buy the coverage that your dependents would need if you weren't there. Not more, not less. Review it periodically. Name the right beneficiaries and keep them current.

That's life insurance done right.
