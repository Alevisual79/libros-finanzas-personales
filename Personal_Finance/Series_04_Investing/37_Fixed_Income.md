### Prologue
Isabel, at 52, had an all-equity investment portfolio until her financial advisor introduced the concept of bond allocation. "You're getting closer to the years when you'll actually need this money," the advisor said. "Stocks can drop 40% right when you need to withdraw. Bonds provide a buffer."

Isabel pushed back: "But bonds earn less. I'm behind on savings. I need the growth."

The advisor's response reframed the question: "Bonds aren't there to earn. They're there to still be there when stocks aren't. It's insurance, not growth."

Fixed income — bonds and Treasury securities — occupies a different role in a portfolio than equities. Understanding that role, how bonds work mechanically, and when to include them changes the portfolio conversation from pure growth optimization to risk management. For younger investors, bonds are often unnecessary or minimal. For investors approaching retirement, they become critical.

## Chapter 1: What Bonds Are

A bond is a debt instrument. When you buy a bond, you are lending money to the issuer — a government, corporation, or municipality — in exchange for:
- Regular interest payments (the coupon) at a specified rate
- Return of the principal (face value) at a specified future date (maturity)

Example: You buy a $1,000 US Treasury bond with a 5% coupon and 10-year maturity. You receive $50/year ($25 every six months) for 10 years. At year 10, you receive your $1,000 back. Total received: $1,500 on a $1,000 investment.

Bonds are "fixed income" because the cash flows are fixed at issuance: the coupon rate doesn't change, and the maturity value is predetermined.

## Chapter 2: Bond Types

**US Treasury Bonds:** Issued by the US federal government. Backed by the full faith and credit of the US government — considered the safest bonds in the world. Types:
- T-Bills (maturity < 1 year): No coupon; sold at discount to face value
- T-Notes (2-10 year maturity): Coupon-bearing
- T-Bonds (10-30 year maturity): Longer-duration coupon bonds
- I-Bonds: Inflation-indexed savings bonds; yield adjusted for CPI semi-annually; purchase limit $10,000/year/person

**TIPS (Treasury Inflation-Protected Securities):** Principal adjusts with inflation; real return is guaranteed. Useful inflation hedge.

**Municipal Bonds ("Munis"):** Issued by state and local governments. Interest is exempt from federal income tax (and often state tax for residents). Best for high-income investors in high tax brackets.

**Corporate Bonds:** Issued by companies. Higher yield than Treasuries to compensate for credit risk (the possibility the company can't repay). Investment-grade corporate bonds (rated BBB- or higher) have lower default risk; high-yield ("junk") bonds carry higher default risk and higher yields.

**Agency Bonds:** Issued by government-sponsored enterprises (Fannie Mae, Freddie Mac, Ginnie Mae). Slightly higher yield than Treasuries with minimal additional risk.

## Chapter 3: The Relationship Between Interest Rates and Bond Prices

The most counterintuitive aspect of bonds: when interest rates rise, existing bond prices fall. When rates fall, existing bond prices rise.

The mechanism: if you own a bond paying 3% coupon and new bonds are issued at 5%, your 3% bond becomes less desirable. To sell it, you must reduce the price. The market price adjusts until the yield of your older, lower-coupon bond matches the current market yield.

**Duration:** A bond's sensitivity to interest rate changes is measured by duration. Approximately: a bond with 10-year duration will fall approximately 10% in price if interest rates rise 1%.

**Implication for investors:**
- Short-duration bonds (1-3 years): low interest rate sensitivity, lower yield
- Long-duration bonds (20-30 years): high interest rate sensitivity, higher yield

For conservative bond investors and retirees: shorter-duration bonds provide income with less price volatility. For investors expecting rates to fall: long-duration bonds provide more price appreciation potential.

The 2022 bond market collapse (the worst calendar year for bonds in US history) occurred because the Federal Reserve raised rates dramatically. Bond investors who held long-duration bonds saw prices fall 20-30%. The lesson reinforced for portfolio construction: bond duration matters, not just the presence of bonds.

## Chapter 4: The Role of Bonds in a Portfolio

Bonds serve specific portfolio functions:

**Stability:** Bonds are generally less volatile than stocks. During stock market crashes, bonds (especially Treasuries) often hold value or appreciate as investors flee to safety.

**Income:** Regular coupon payments provide cash flow that can fund retirement expenses without requiring equity sales.

**Rebalancing opportunity:** When stocks fall and bonds hold, rebalancing (selling bonds, buying stocks) systematically buys equities at lower prices — the mechanized version of "buy the dip."

**Sequence-of-returns protection:** Having bonds in retirement means you can fund expenses from bond income/sales during equity downturns, without selling stocks at depressed prices.

The 2022 exception (bonds fell alongside stocks) was unusual — driven by the unusual combination of high inflation and rising rates. The historical correlation between stocks and bonds (negative or low) was temporarily reversed. This is why "bonds and stocks both falling" is possible but historically uncommon.

## Chapter 5: Bond Funds vs. Individual Bonds

**Bond mutual funds and ETFs (e.g., BND, AGG):**
- Diversified: own thousands of bonds
- Continuously managed: maturing bonds are replaced with new bonds
- No maturity date: principal fluctuates with interest rate changes
- Liquid: buy and sell at any time
- Good for most investors — the simplest way to hold bond exposure

**Individual bonds (held to maturity):**
- You know exactly when and how much you'll receive
- If rates rise, the market value falls — but if you hold to maturity, you receive par value
- Interest rate changes don't affect your outcome if you don't sell
- Requires more capital to achieve diversification across bond issuers

**Bond ladders:** Holding individual bonds with staggered maturities (e.g., bonds maturing in 1, 2, 3, 4, 5 years). As each bond matures, reinvest in a new long-maturity bond. This provides predictable income and reduces interest rate timing risk.

For most investors without significant bond expertise or very large portfolios: bond ETFs (BND, VBTLX) are simpler and provide adequate diversification.

## Chapter 6: I-Bonds — The Inflation Protector

I-Bonds (Series I Savings Bonds) are issued directly by the US Treasury and have characteristics that make them unique:

- **Yield:** Composed of a fixed rate (set at issuance) plus an inflation adjustment (updated every six months based on CPI). The inflation adjustment means the real return is approximately the fixed rate.
- **Purchase limit:** $10,000/year per person (plus $5,000 in paper bonds with a tax refund)
- **Tax deferral:** Interest can be deferred until redemption (unlike bond funds where interest is taxable annually)
- **Early redemption penalty:** Bonds held less than 5 years lose 3 months of interest upon early redemption; bonds held 1+ year can be redeemed
- **Risk:** No credit risk (US government backs them); no market risk (price doesn't fluctuate)

During high-inflation periods (2021-2022), I-Bond yields reached 7-9%, making them unusually attractive. In normal inflation environments, yields are lower but still competitive with HYSA rates.

Best use: short-to-medium term savings that need inflation protection and for which the purchase limit is sufficient. Not a substitute for a full bond allocation in a large portfolio.

## Chapter 7: Municipal Bonds for High-Income Investors

For investors in high marginal tax brackets, municipal bonds offer a tax-equivalent yield that often exceeds taxable bonds after tax.

Tax-equivalent yield calculation: Muni yield ÷ (1 - marginal tax rate) = taxable equivalent yield.

A muni bond yielding 3.5% in a 37% marginal bracket:
- Tax-equivalent yield: 3.5% ÷ (1 - 0.37) = 5.56%
- A taxable bond would need to yield 5.56% to match the after-tax return

For investors in the 22-24% brackets, the tax advantage of munis is less compelling. For investors in the 32%+ brackets, munis often provide superior after-tax yield to comparable-quality taxable bonds.

**Immediate exercise:** If you're nearing or in retirement, open your brokerage's bond section and look at current 5-year Treasury yield vs. 5-year TIPS yield. The difference between them is the market's implied 5-year inflation expectation (the "break-even inflation rate"). This tells you what inflation the market is pricing in — useful context for the inflation/bond discussion.

## Chapter 8: How Much Bond Allocation?

The conventional formula: 110 minus your age = the percentage in stocks; the rest in bonds.
- At 30: 80% stocks, 20% bonds
- At 50: 60% stocks, 40% bonds
- At 70: 40% stocks, 60% bonds

This formula is a useful starting point, not a rule. It should be adjusted for:
- **Risk tolerance:** If a 30% portfolio decline would cause you to sell, you need more bonds than the formula suggests
- **Time horizon:** Early retirees (65 with a 30-year horizon) may maintain more equity exposure than the formula suggests
- **Other income sources:** A pension or Social Security that covers most retirement expenses reduces the need for a conservative portfolio
- **Spending flexibility:** Someone who can reduce spending by 20% during a market decline needs less bond buffer than someone with fixed expenses

Isabel at 52, 15 years from retirement, with no pension: approximately 30% bonds, 70% stocks. More conservative than a 30-year-old but not as conservative as the formula suggests, because she has a long enough retirement horizon to need growth.

## Chapter 9: The Bond Ladder in Retirement

The bond ladder is a practical strategy for funding retirement withdrawals without selling equities during market downturns.

Bucket approach (see also Book 54):
- **Bucket 1 (years 1-2):** Cash/HYSA
- **Bucket 2 (years 3-10):** Bond ladder: bonds maturing each year, providing predictable annual income
- **Bucket 3 (years 10+):** Equities — 10+ years to recover from any market decline

The bond ladder in Bucket 2 means you have 7-8 years of predictable income without needing to sell equities. During that 7-8 years, equities in Bucket 3 can recover from any market decline before they're needed.

This structure addresses sequence-of-returns risk — the primary financial risk in the early years of retirement — by ensuring no forced equity sales during market downturns.

## Chapter 10: The Portfolio With and Without Bonds

To illustrate the bond function: two portfolios during the 2008-2009 financial crisis.

**100% Equity Portfolio:** Peak to trough decline of approximately 50% (2007-2009). Needed approximately 4 years to recover to previous highs.

**60/40 Portfolio (60% stocks, 40% bonds):** Peak to trough decline of approximately 30%. Recovered to previous highs in approximately 2 years.

The bond allocation didn't prevent the loss — it moderated it. For an investor who needed to fund expenses during those two years, the moderated loss meant selling fewer shares at depressed prices. The long-term impact of not being forced to sell at the bottom is significant.

For a 30-year-old with no retirement withdrawals planned for 35 years: this protection has limited value because the time horizon allows full recovery. For a 65-year-old who needs to fund expenses now: the protection is critical.

## Epilogue

Bonds are not growth instruments. They are stability and income instruments. Their role in a portfolio depends on when you'll need the money and how much volatility your specific situation can absorb.

The closer you are to needing your portfolio, the more bonds belong in it. The further away, the less necessary they are.

Understand what bonds do. Use them for what they do.
