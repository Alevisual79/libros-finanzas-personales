## Prologue

The United States is one of only two countries in the world (the other is Eritrea) that taxes its citizens on worldwide income regardless of where they live. An American who moves to Portugal, Japan, or Argentina and earns income there must still file US tax returns — and potentially pay US taxes — even if they haven't set foot in the US for years.

This citizenship-based taxation system makes American expatriate tax planning one of the most complex topics in personal finance. Most Americans moving abroad discover this reality too late, having assumed that leaving the US meant leaving US taxes behind.

This book is for Americans who live abroad, plan to live abroad, or earn income from foreign sources — and who need to understand how US tax law treats them.

---

## Chapter 1: The Fundamental Rule — Worldwide Income Taxation

Every US citizen and permanent resident (green card holder), regardless of where they live, must:
- File a US federal income tax return annually (if income exceeds filing thresholds)
- Report all worldwide income (wages earned in Germany, interest in a Swiss bank, rental income from a Tokyo apartment — all reportable)
- Report foreign financial accounts and assets (FBAR, FATCA — discussed below)

This applies even if you've lived abroad for 30 years and have never paid US taxes in your life because foreign taxes covered your liability. The obligation to file does not disappear with residency.

Many Americans abroad don't file because they don't know they're required to. The IRS has been increasingly aggressive in enforcing compliance among Americans abroad, and the penalties for non-compliance are severe.

## Chapter 2: The Foreign Earned Income Exclusion (FEIE)

The primary protection for Americans working abroad: the Foreign Earned Income Exclusion (Form 2555) allows qualifying Americans to exclude a significant portion of foreign-earned income from US taxable income.

**2024 exclusion amount:** $126,500 per qualifying person.

**Who qualifies:**
You must meet either the "bona fide residence test" or the "physical presence test":

*Bona fide residence test:* You are a bona fide resident of a foreign country for an uninterrupted period covering an entire tax year. "Bona fide residence" is a facts-and-circumstances determination.

*Physical presence test:* You are physically present in a foreign country (or countries) for at least 330 full days during any 12-month period. This is more mechanical — days in the US count against your 330-day requirement.

**What is "foreign earned income":**
Income from personal services performed in a foreign country while a qualifying individual: wages, salaries, professional fees, and other compensation for services. Does NOT include:
- Passive investment income (dividends, interest, capital gains)
- Pensions or Social Security
- Remote work for a US employer (debated — depends on whether services are performed on US soil)

## Chapter 3: The Foreign Tax Credit

For income not covered by the FEIE (passive income, income above the exclusion amount), the Foreign Tax Credit (Form 1116) allows a dollar-for-dollar credit against US tax liability for foreign income taxes paid.

**How it works:**
- You earn $200,000 in Germany (above the $126,500 FEIE)
- Germany withholds taxes equivalent to 35% of the excess ($73,500 × 0.35 = $25,725 in German taxes)
- The FTC provides a credit against your US tax liability for the foreign taxes paid
- If your US tax on the excess would have been $25,725, the FTC eliminates the liability

**The FTC vs. FEIE choice:**
Some expatriates benefit from using the FTC instead of (or in addition to) the FEIE. The optimal strategy depends on:
- Your total income level
- The foreign tax rates in your country of residence (high-tax countries often result in FTC being more advantageous)
- Whether your income will grow (FEIE may limit future social security benefits)
- Whether you have passive foreign income

The FEIE/FTC interaction is complex; for expat income planning, a CPA specializing in expatriate taxation is highly recommended.

## Chapter 4: FBAR and FATCA — Foreign Account Reporting

Beyond income taxes, Americans with foreign financial accounts and assets face significant reporting requirements.

**FBAR (FinCEN Form 114):**
Required if the aggregate value of all foreign financial accounts exceeds $10,000 at any point during the year. Filed with FinCEN (not the IRS), due April 15 (extended to October 15 automatically).

*What must be reported:* Foreign bank accounts, brokerage accounts, foreign mutual funds, foreign-held retirement accounts.

*Penalties:* Non-willful violations: $10,000 per year per account. Willful violations: the greater of $100,000 or 50% of the account value per year. Criminal penalties in egregious cases.

**FATCA (Foreign Account Tax Compliance Act, Form 8938):**
Filed with the federal income tax return. Reports specified foreign financial assets above thresholds:
- Living abroad: $200,000 at year-end or $300,000 at any point during the year (single); double for married filing jointly
- Living in the US: $50,000 at year-end or $75,000 at any point (single)

*Penalties:* $10,000 for failure to file, up to $50,000 if failure continues after IRS notification.

The key: FBAR and FATCA are in addition to income tax reporting, not instead of. And the reporting thresholds are low enough that almost any American with a foreign bank account or investment account must file.

## Chapter 5: State Taxes for Expats

Federal taxes are only part of the expatriate tax picture. Many states continue to tax residents (or former residents) who haven't properly established domicile elsewhere.

**States with aggressive residency rules:**
California, New York, and Virginia have particularly aggressive approaches to determining whether former residents still owe state income tax. California specifically looks at where you intend to eventually return — maintaining ties to California (driver's license, registered vehicles, family home, business relationships) can result in continued California tax liability even while living abroad.

**Establishing domicile in a new location:**
Before expatriating, formally sever domicile from your current state:
- Cancel California (or other high-tax state) driver's license
- Register voter registration at a new address
- Update mailing address to a foreign address or a state with no income tax (South Dakota, Nevada, Wyoming, Texas are popular for expats)
- Close state-specific accounts and memberships
- File a final state return indicating change of domicile

## Chapter 6: The Foreign Housing Exclusion or Deduction

In addition to the FEIE for earned income, qualifying expats may be able to exclude or deduct foreign housing expenses.

**Foreign Housing Exclusion (for employees):**
Excludes a portion of employer-provided housing costs or housing allowances. The exclusion is calculated as: actual housing expenses minus a base amount (approximately 16% of the FEIE amount). Maximum exclusion varies by location.

**Foreign Housing Deduction (for self-employed):**
Self-employed expats can deduct foreign housing costs, but it reduces (rather than excludes from) the self-employment tax calculation.

**Qualifying housing expenses:** Rent, utilities (not telephone), insurance on household goods, and similar expenses. Does not include lavish or extravagant housing costs.

## Chapter 7: Social Security and Medicare for Expats

**Social Security:** If you work abroad for a foreign employer, you typically don't pay US Social Security taxes (you pay the local equivalent). However, your Social Security benefit record isn't accumulating during those years. For long-term expats, this can significantly reduce eventual Social Security benefits.

**Totalization agreements:** The US has totalization agreements with approximately 30 countries, preventing double Social Security taxation. Under these agreements, you contribute to either the US or the foreign system, not both.

**Self-employed expats:** Self-employment tax (Social Security and Medicare) generally applies to self-employed US citizens worldwide, even if exempt from income tax through the FEIE. This is one significant disadvantage of the FEIE for self-employed expats: it excludes income from income tax but does not exempt from self-employment tax.

## Chapter 8: Renouncing US Citizenship

Some long-term expats choose to renounce US citizenship to permanently escape the US worldwide tax net. This is a serious, irrevocable decision with significant financial implications.

**The exit tax (Section 877A):** "Covered expatriates" (those who meet certain net worth or tax liability thresholds) are subject to a mark-to-market exit tax: all worldwide assets are treated as if sold on the day before renunciation at fair market value. Capital gains above the exclusion ($866,000 in 2024, indexed for inflation) are taxed at regular rates.

**Covered expatriate thresholds:**
- Average annual net income tax liability exceeds $201,000 over the 5 years preceding renunciation
- Net worth exceeds $2 million
- Not in full compliance with tax obligations for the 5 preceding years

**Administrative requirements:**
- Renunciation is done at a US embassy or consulate
- Renunciation fee: $2,350
- Must be in full tax compliance at renunciation
- May lose the right to ever live or work in the US without a visa

For most expats, the complexity and permanence of renunciation outweigh the benefits. The combination of FEIE, FTC, and proper planning often eliminates most or all US tax liability for Americans in typical expat situations.

## Chapter 9: Retirement Accounts for Expats

US retirement accounts (401k, IRA) don't disappear when you move abroad — but accessing them from abroad has implications.

**Contributions while abroad:**
- If your foreign income is fully excluded under the FEIE, you have no US earned income and cannot contribute to a traditional or Roth IRA (which requires taxable compensation equal to or greater than the contribution)
- If you have income above the FEIE (or choose not to use the FEIE for this reason), you can contribute to an IRA

**Distributions from abroad:**
- Distributions from traditional IRAs and 401(k)s are taxed as ordinary income
- Whether your country of residence taxes these distributions depends on your country's tax treaty with the US
- Tax treaties vary significantly: some exempt IRA/401(k) distributions from local taxation; others don't

**Foreign pension plans:**
- Contributions to foreign pension plans are generally not deductible for US tax purposes
- Distributions may or may not be taxable depending on tax treaties and account structure
- PFIC (Passive Foreign Investment Company) rules apply to foreign mutual funds inside pension accounts in some cases — creating significant complexity

## Chapter 10: Getting Professional Help

Expatriate taxation is among the most complex areas of US tax law. The intersection of domestic tax rules, foreign tax systems, tax treaties, FBAR/FATCA reporting, and state taxes creates situations where DIY tax preparation is genuinely risky.

**Finding qualified expat tax professionals:**

*CPAs and enrolled agents specializing in expatriate taxation:*
- AICPA Global Tax Resources
- Greenback Expat Tax Services (US-based firm specializing in expat returns)
- Taxes for Expats (online, focused on Americans abroad)
- Local CPA firm in your country of residence with US tax knowledge

**Cost:** Expat tax returns typically cost $500-$2,000 depending on complexity. FBAR and FATCA compliance are typically included or add modest additional cost.

**Free resources:**
- IRS Publication 54 (Tax Guide for US Citizens and Resident Aliens Abroad)
- IRS FBAR resources at fincen.gov

**The most common expat tax mistakes:**
- Not filing US returns while abroad (most common)
- Not filing FBARs despite required thresholds (second most common)
- Incorrectly calculating the physical presence test
- Failing to properly claim the FEIE after return to the US creates complications

**Immediate exercise:** If you are currently living abroad or planning to do so, verify that you are filing US tax returns and FBARs. If you've been non-compliant, the IRS offers streamlined compliance procedures (Streamlined Foreign Offshore Procedures) for non-willful non-compliance that significantly reduce penalties.

## Epilogue

US citizenship abroad comes with a tax obligation that follows you anywhere in the world. The obligation is real; so are the tools to manage it. The FEIE eliminates most income tax for most expats; the FTC handles the rest. FBAR and FATCA compliance is non-negotiable.

Get a qualified expat tax professional in your first year abroad. The cost is small relative to the penalty exposure of non-compliance — and relative to the tax savings from proper planning.
