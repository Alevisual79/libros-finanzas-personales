## How to Keep More of What Your Portfolio Earns

### Prologue
Two investors, side by side, both earn 8% gross returns over 30 years.

Investor A pays no attention to the tax implications of their investment decisions. They hold assets in the wrong accounts, trade frequently, and realize short-term gains unnecessarily.

Investor B structures investments with tax efficiency in mind: the right assets in the right accounts, low turnover, qualified dividends over ordinary income, strategic tax-loss harvesting.

Same gross return. Dramatically different net returns after taxes.

Tax efficiency in investing doesn't require exotic strategies or aggressive planning. It requires understanding a few core principles and applying them consistently. The effect compounds over decades into outcomes that differ by hundreds of thousands of dollars.

## Chapter 1: How Investment Income Is Taxed

Different types of investment income are taxed at different rates. Understanding the distinctions is the foundation of tax-efficient investing.

**Long-term capital gains:** Gains on assets held more than 12 months. Taxed at preferential rates: 0% (income below $47,025 for singles, $94,050 for married filing jointly in 2024), 15% (most taxpayers), or 20% (highest earners).

**Short-term capital gains:** Gains on assets held 12 months or less. Taxed as ordinary income — up to 37% for high earners.

**Qualified dividends:** Dividends from US corporations and qualifying foreign corporations held for a required holding period. Taxed at long-term capital gains rates (0-20%).

**Ordinary dividends:** Dividends that don't meet the qualified standard. Taxed as ordinary income.

**Interest income:** Interest from bonds, CDs, savings accounts. Taxed as ordinary income. Exception: municipal bond interest is generally exempt from federal income tax.

**The key implication:** Holding investments for more than 12 months before selling converts short-term gains (up to 37%) to long-term gains (0-20%) — potentially eliminating more than half the tax liability on the same income.

## Chapter 2: Asset Location — The Right Assets in the Right Accounts

Asset location is the strategy of placing different investments in tax-advantaged vs. taxable accounts to minimize total tax across the portfolio.

**Tax-advantaged accounts (traditional 401k, IRA):**
- Contributions reduce current-year income taxes
- Growth deferred until withdrawal
- Withdrawals taxed as ordinary income
- Best for: assets that generate ordinary income (bonds, REITs, high-dividend stocks), high-turnover active funds, assets expected to generate significant ordinary dividends

**Roth accounts (Roth 401k, Roth IRA):**
- Contributions with after-tax dollars (no current deduction)
- Growth and qualified withdrawals completely tax-free
- Best for: highest-expected-return assets (small-cap value, individual stocks with high growth potential), assets you'll hold longest before needing

**Taxable brokerage accounts:**
- No contribution limits
- Contributions with after-tax dollars
- Capital gains and dividends taxed annually (as income is realized)
- Best for: tax-efficient assets (index funds with low turnover and qualified dividends), municipal bonds

**Asset location strategy in practice:**

| Asset Type | Best Account |
|---|---|
| Bond index funds | Traditional IRA/401(k) |
| REITs | Traditional IRA/401(k) |
| High-dividend funds | Traditional IRA/401(k) |
| US total market index funds | Taxable or Roth |
| International index funds | Taxable (for foreign tax credit) |
| Small-cap value funds | Roth IRA (long holding period) |
| Tax-exempt municipal bonds | Taxable (already tax-efficient) |

## Chapter 3: Tax-Loss Harvesting

Tax-loss harvesting is the practice of selling investments at a loss to offset capital gains and reduce current-year tax liability.

**How it works:**
- You own Fund A (now down 20%)
- You sell Fund A, realizing a capital loss
- You immediately buy Fund B (similar but not "substantially identical") to maintain market exposure
- The realized loss offsets capital gains (or up to $3,000 of ordinary income per year)
- If losses exceed gains, the excess $3,000 deduction carries forward to future years indefinitely

**The wash-sale rule:** The IRS prohibits claiming a loss if you buy the "substantially identical" security within 30 days before or after the sale. You must wait 31 days before buying back the sold security. Buying a similar-but-different fund maintains market exposure without violating the wash-sale rule.

**Example:** Elena's taxable brokerage account holds:
- VTI (US total market) — down $4,000 from purchase
- VXUS (international) — up $6,000 since purchase

She could sell VTI, realizing a $4,000 loss, and simultaneously buy ITOT (essentially the same index, different fund) to maintain exposure. The $4,000 loss offsets $4,000 of the $6,000 VXUS gain if she sells VXUS in the same year. Tax savings at 15% long-term capital gains rate: $600.

**Tax-loss harvesting is most valuable when:**
- You have significant realized gains in the same tax year
- You're in a higher marginal bracket (higher gains rates)
- The investments have significant unrealized losses available
- You have a long time horizon (the harvested loss is reinvested and grows)

## Chapter 4: The Power of Low-Turnover Investing

High-turnover investments (active funds that frequently buy and sell securities) generate capital gains distributions annually, even if you haven't sold your fund shares. These distributions are taxable to you in a taxable account.

In 2022 and 2023, many investors in actively managed funds received substantial capital gains distributions even in years when the fund declined in value — a "phantom income" problem where you pay tax on gains that benefited other investors who sold during the year.

**The index fund tax advantage in taxable accounts:**
- Index funds rarely sell securities (only when the index composition changes)
- Low turnover = minimal capital gains distributions
- Investor controls when gains are realized (by choosing when to sell)
- Total return index fund vs. active fund comparison: after-tax advantage of index fund increases with the investor's tax bracket and time horizon

**The Vanguard example:** Over a recent 10-year period, Vanguard's S&P 500 index fund distributed approximately $0 in capital gains distributions. Many actively managed funds distributing 3-10% of NAV annually in gains distributions — creating tax bills for taxable account holders even without selling.

## Chapter 5: The Qualified Dividend Advantage

Dividends are not all equal for tax purposes. Qualified dividends (from most US corporations) are taxed at capital gains rates (0-20%). Ordinary dividends are taxed at ordinary income rates (up to 37%).

For investors in the 22%+ marginal bracket, the difference between qualified and ordinary dividend treatment is 2-17 percentage points — meaningful on significant dividend income.

**Qualified dividends come from:**
- Most dividends from US corporations
- Dividends from certain qualified foreign corporations
- Holding requirement: must hold the stock for more than 60 days during the 121-day period around the ex-dividend date

**Ordinary dividends come from:**
- REITs (usually non-qualified)
- MLPs (master limited partnerships)
- Money market funds
- Some foreign corporations
- Short-duration holdings

**Asset location application:** Because REIT dividends are typically ordinary (non-qualified), REITs belong in tax-advantaged accounts where the dividend tax treatment doesn't matter. In a taxable account, REIT dividends face ordinary income rates — inefficient.

## Chapter 6: Municipal Bonds for High-Income Investors

Municipal bond interest is exempt from federal income tax (and usually from state taxes for residents). For investors in high marginal brackets, the after-tax yield of municipal bonds can exceed the after-tax yield of comparable taxable bonds.

See also Book 37 (Fixed Income) for detailed discussion. The tax efficiency angle:

**Tax-equivalent yield formula:**
Municipal yield ÷ (1 - marginal tax rate) = taxable equivalent yield

A muni yielding 3.8% for a taxpayer in the 35% federal + 9.3% California state bracket:
Combined rate: approximately 40% (net of the federal deduction for state taxes)
Tax-equivalent yield: 3.8% ÷ (1 - 0.40) = 6.33%

A taxable bond would need to yield 6.33% to match the after-tax return — significantly higher than a comparable-quality taxable bond would offer.

**Who benefits:** Investors in the 32%+ marginal bracket with significant fixed income allocation in taxable accounts. Not relevant for investments inside tax-advantaged accounts (where the tax-free treatment doesn't add incremental benefit).

## Chapter 7: Roth Conversion Optimization

Strategic Roth IRA conversions — converting traditional IRA/401(k) assets to Roth — can significantly reduce lifetime tax burden for the right investor.

**The opportunity:** Traditional IRA withdrawals in retirement are taxed as ordinary income. If you're in a lower tax bracket today than you will be in retirement (or if tax rates are expected to rise), converting now locks in the lower rate.

**When Roth conversions make sense:**
- In years with unusually low income (career transition, sabbatical, early retirement before Social Security)
- In years when deductions create unused low-bracket room
- Before age 73 when Required Minimum Distributions (RMDs) force larger withdrawals
- If your heirs will be in a higher bracket than you (they pay taxes on inherited traditional IRA distributions)

**Isabel's Roth conversion strategy:**
At 52, with a salary of $90,000, she's in the 22% bracket. Her traditional IRA balance: $240,000. She projects being in the 24% or 28% bracket in retirement due to pension-like income and portfolio withdrawals.

Converting $20,000/year to Roth while still in the 22% bracket allows her to pre-pay the tax at 22% on funds that would otherwise be taxed at 24-28% in retirement.

Estimated lifetime tax savings from systematic 22% conversions: $12,000-$18,000 over 10 years.

## Chapter 8: Tax-Efficient Withdrawal Sequencing

In retirement, the order in which you withdraw from different account types significantly affects lifetime tax.

**The general sequence:**
1. Required Minimum Distributions (from traditional IRA/401k — mandatory from age 73)
2. Taxable account assets (to let tax-advantaged accounts continue growing)
3. Traditional IRA/401k (ordinary income on each dollar)
4. Roth IRA (tax-free; preserve as long as possible — no RMDs during owner's lifetime)

**The optimization layer:** Within this general sequence, managing each year's taxable income to stay in a target tax bracket allows strategic conversions, capital gains harvesting at 0%, and minimization of Medicare premium surcharges (IRMAA).

**Immediate exercise:** Log in to every investment account you own. For each, write down: the account type, the tax treatment of withdrawals, and the primary investment holdings. This inventory is the foundation for identifying whether your current asset location is tax-optimal.

## Chapter 9: Donor-Advised Funds and Charitable Giving

For investors who give to charity, donor-advised funds (DAFs) enable significantly more tax-efficient charitable giving.

**Standard charitable giving:** Cash donation → charitable deduction (if itemizing).

**DAF charitable giving with appreciated securities:**
- Donate appreciated stock or fund shares to the DAF instead of selling
- Receive a charitable deduction for the full market value (not the original cost basis)
- No capital gains tax on the appreciation
- Grant from the DAF to the charity (at any time, in any amount)

**Example:**
- You own stock purchased at $10,000, now worth $25,000 (gain: $15,000)
- If you sell and donate cash: pay 15% capital gains tax ($2,250), donate $22,750
- If you donate shares to DAF: donate $25,000 (no capital gains tax), deduct $25,000

The DAF approach effectively gives the charity more money (the full $25,000 vs. $22,750 after taxes) while also eliminating your capital gains tax.

**Bunching:** Concentrating multiple years of charitable contributions into a single year (donating 3-5 years of contributions to the DAF in one year) allows itemizing in that year and claiming the standard deduction in other years — maximizing total deductions.

## Chapter 10: The Tax-Efficient Portfolio in Practice

Integrating these principles, a tax-efficient investment approach for an investor with both taxable and tax-advantaged accounts:

**Tax-advantaged accounts (traditional 401k + IRA):**
- Total bond market index fund (BND)
- US REIT index fund (VNQ)
- Active funds or higher-turnover allocations

**Roth IRA:**
- Small-cap value fund (VBR or AVUV) — highest long-term expected return; tax-free growth
- International small-cap (VSS) — same rationale

**Taxable brokerage:**
- US total market index fund (VTI) — low turnover, qualified dividends, full control of gain timing
- International index fund (VXUS) — for foreign tax credit
- Municipal bonds if in high bracket
- Tax-loss harvesting opportunities as available

**The ongoing practice:**
- Annual account review: is asset location still optimal as balances change?
- Rebalancing: prefer rebalancing within tax-advantaged accounts (no tax cost); use new contributions to maintain allocation in taxable accounts
- Year-end: review for tax-loss harvesting opportunities, Roth conversion opportunities, and capital gain management

## Epilogue

Tax efficiency in investing is not complex. It requires understanding how different income types are taxed, placing the right assets in the right accounts, minimizing unnecessary turnover and short-term gains, and taking advantage of available tools (tax-loss harvesting, Roth conversions, qualified giving vehicles).

The investor who applies these principles consistently doesn't need to earn higher returns. They keep more of the returns they earn — which, compounded over decades, produces dramatically different outcomes.
