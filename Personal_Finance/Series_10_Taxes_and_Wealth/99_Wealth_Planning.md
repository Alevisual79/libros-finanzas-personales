## Managing and Preserving What You've Built

### Prologue
At a certain threshold — approximately $500,000 in net worth, or earlier depending on circumstances — financial planning transitions from accumulation to management. The questions shift:

From: "How do I save more? How do I invest better?"

To: "How do I protect what I've built? How do I ensure it goes where I want? How do I minimize the tax drag on what I've accumulated?"

Wealth planning is the set of disciplines concerned with managing accumulated wealth: tax efficiency at higher asset levels, estate planning optimization, insurance adequacy, charitable strategy, and the generational transfer of wealth.

This is not exotic. It is what anyone with meaningful accumulated assets — $250,000, $500,000, $1 million — should be thinking about intentionally rather than by default.

## Chapter 1: The Wealth Threshold Concept

"Wealth planning" sounds like a concern for the ultra-rich. It is not. The disciplines become relevant at much lower thresholds than most people assume.

**$100,000-$250,000 net worth:**
- Estate planning basics (will, beneficiary designations, healthcare directive)
- Umbrella insurance
- Asset protection (LLC for business owners, retirement account maximization)
- Tax-efficient investment structure (asset location)

**$250,000-$750,000 net worth:**
- All of the above, plus:
- Roth conversion strategy
- Tax-loss harvesting
- Charitable giving strategy (DAF consideration)
- Long-term care insurance evaluation
- Review of estate plan complexity

**$750,000-$2,000,000 net worth:**
- All of the above, plus:
- Trust planning (revocable living trust at minimum)
- More sophisticated tax planning with CPA
- Insurance coverage review (is umbrella limit still adequate?)
- Investment advisor consideration (fee-only fiduciary)
- Beneficiary review with attention to tax implications for heirs

**$2,000,000+ net worth:**
- All of the above, plus:
- Estate tax planning (state estate taxes become relevant below federal threshold)
- Advanced charitable strategies (charitable remainder trusts, private foundations)
- Generation-skipping trust consideration
- Family governance discussions

## Chapter 2: Net Worth Tracking and the Financial Dashboard

Managing wealth requires knowing what you have, where it is, and how it's changing.

**The personal balance sheet:**
- Assets: all accounts, properties, vehicles, business interests, receivables
- Liabilities: all debts (mortgage, loans, credit cards, other)
- Net worth: assets minus liabilities

**What to track:**
- All investment accounts (current value, contribution history, allocation)
- Retirement accounts (balance, employer match status, beneficiary designations)
- Real estate (estimated market value, mortgage balance, equity)
- Business interests (estimated value)
- All debt (balance, interest rate, minimum payment, payoff timeline)
- Insurance policies (type, coverage amount, premium, beneficiary)

**Tools:**
- Empower (formerly Personal Capital): free aggregation of all financial accounts; automatic net worth tracking; investment analysis
- Monarch Money: comprehensive personal finance dashboard
- Spreadsheet: complete control; requires manual updates; works for lower-tech preference

**Review frequency:** Net worth tracking quarterly; complete financial dashboard review annually. The annual review should include: progress toward goals, allocation drift, insurance adequacy, estate plan currency.

## Chapter 3: Working With Financial Professionals

At higher wealth levels, the DIY approach has an increasing opportunity cost. The tax savings, investment decisions, and estate planning optimizations that a team of professionals provides can significantly exceed their cost.

**The financial professional team for significant wealth:**

**Fee-only fiduciary financial advisor:** Paid by you (flat fee, hourly, or percentage of assets), not by commissions. Legally required to act in your interest. Manages investment portfolio, coordinates planning, integrates tax and estate considerations.
- NAPFA.org: Network of fee-only advisors
- Garrett Planning Network: hourly-rate fee-only advisors
- AUM fees: typically 0.5-1.0% of assets under management

**CPA specializing in high-income/high-net-worth:**
- Annual tax planning and preparation
- Entity structure advice
- Roth conversion and distribution strategy
- Qualified opportunity zone and other investment tax strategy

**Estate planning attorney:**
- Trust drafting and planning
- Beneficiary structure optimization
- Charitable giving strategy
- Business succession planning

**The team integration:** These professionals should communicate with each other. Tax strategies affect investment decisions; investment strategies affect estate plans; estate plans affect tax planning. The most sophisticated wealth management happens when the CPA, advisor, and attorney coordinate rather than working in silos.

## Chapter 4: Investment Portfolio Management at Higher Levels

At $500,000+, investment portfolio management becomes more sophisticated.

**Beyond the three-fund portfolio:** The three-fund portfolio remains appropriate and excellent for most investors regardless of wealth. But higher levels open additional tools:

**Direct indexing:** Owning the individual stocks of an index rather than the index fund itself. Enables:
- Tax-loss harvesting on individual securities (not just the whole fund)
- ESG customization (exclude specific sectors or companies)
- Better tax management for investors with appreciated legacy positions

Available through Schwab Personalized Indexing, Vanguard Digital Advisor, Fidelity Managed FidFolios. Minimums typically $100,000-$500,000.

**Alternative investments:** Higher minimums allow access to:
- Private equity (minimum investments of $50,000-$250,000)
- Private credit
- Real estate limited partnerships
- Hedge funds (accredited investor requirement: $1 million net worth or $200,000 annual income)

These alternatives are often illiquid, complex, and expensive. Most high-net-worth investors are better served by a well-structured portfolio of public market index funds than by complex alternative investments that often underperform after fees.

**Concentrated position management:** Many investors accumulate large concentrated positions in a single stock (employer equity, IPO, inheritance). Managing this concentration (without triggering a massive capital gains tax bill) requires strategies including:
- Gradual diversification (selling annually up to a tax-efficient threshold)
- Charitable donation of appreciated shares
- Exchange funds (pooling with other concentrated holders; tax-deferred; 7+ year lockup)
- Collared options (limit downside while deferring the gain)
- Opportunity zone investment (defer and potentially reduce gain)

## Chapter 5: Tax Planning at Higher Income Levels

**Alternative Minimum Tax (AMT):** A parallel tax calculation that eliminates certain deductions and preferences. High-income earners may be subject to AMT. The Tax Cuts and Jobs Act of 2017 significantly reduced AMT exposure for most taxpayers, but it remains relevant for some situations (large capital gains, ISOs — incentive stock options).

**Net Investment Income Tax (NIIT):** A 3.8% surtax on net investment income (interest, dividends, capital gains, rental income, royalties) for taxpayers with AGI above $200,000 (single) / $250,000 (married). Effectively adds 3.8% to investment income tax rates for higher earners.

**Qualified Opportunity Zone (QOZ) investment:** Deferral and potential reduction of capital gains by investing in designated economic development zones. Defers the original gain until 2026 (or when the QOZ investment is sold, whichever is earlier); provides complete exemption from gain on the QOZ investment itself if held 10+ years.

**Tax bracket management:** High-income earners benefit from year-to-year income management — deferring income to lower-income years, accelerating deductions to higher-income years, and strategically timing Roth conversions, charitable contributions, and asset sales.

## Chapter 6: Long-Term Care Planning

Long-term care (LTC) — assistance with activities of daily living (bathing, dressing, eating, mobility) due to chronic illness, disability, or cognitive impairment — is a significant financial risk for people over 65.

**Statistics:**
- Approximately 70% of people over 65 will need some form of long-term care
- Average duration: 2-3 years; 20% will need 5+ years of care
- Average annual cost of assisted living: $54,000 (2023)
- Average annual cost of nursing home (private room): $108,000
- Medicare covers limited LTC (short-term skilled nursing after hospitalization); Medicaid covers LTC only after spending down most assets to poverty-level

**Planning options:**

Traditional LTC insurance: standalone policies that cover LTC expenses. Premiums have increased substantially; policies are harder to obtain for people over 65. Best purchased at 50-60 before health changes make obtaining coverage difficult or impossible.

Hybrid life/LTC products: life insurance policies with LTC riders. Premiums are guaranteed; if LTC is needed, benefits cover costs; if not used for LTC, benefits pass to heirs. More predictable than traditional LTC.

Self-insurance: having sufficient assets that LTC costs can be covered from the portfolio. Requires approximately $300,000-$500,000 in accessible, non-retirement assets to absorb potential LTC costs without depleting the portfolio to poverty.

**Isabel's LTC planning:** At 52, she purchased a hybrid life/LTC policy at $2,400/year. The death benefit is $150,000; the LTC benefit is $200,000+ depending on how long benefits are needed. She views this as protecting her daughter's inheritance from potential LTC expenses.

## Chapter 7: Charitable Wealth Planning

For people who want to give significantly to charity, several strategies maximize the impact and tax efficiency of giving.

**Donor-Advised Fund (DAF):** Covered in Book 95. The most accessible charitable wealth tool. Contribute appreciated securities; receive the deduction; grant from the fund on your schedule.

**Charitable Remainder Trust (CRT):** Transfer assets to a trust; receive an income stream for life or a term of years; remainder goes to charity at death. Provides: charitable deduction (partial), capital gains avoidance on appreciated assets contributed, income stream.

**Charitable Lead Annuity Trust (CLAT):** The reverse of a CRT: charity receives income payments for a term of years; assets then pass to heirs. Useful for reducing estate tax while benefiting charity during life.

**Private Foundation:** For major donors ($1 million+), a private foundation enables family-controlled charitable grantmaking. Significant administrative requirements; mandatory 5% annual distribution of assets; public disclosure of grants.

**Qualified Charitable Distribution (QCD):** For IRA owners over 70½, direct transfers from IRA to qualified charity (up to $105,000/year in 2024) are excluded from taxable income. This satisfies the RMD without creating taxable income — particularly valuable for people who don't need the RMD income and have charitable intent.

## Chapter 8: Business Succession and Exit Planning

For business owners, the business itself is often the largest asset in their wealth portfolio. Planning for its eventual transition (sale, merger, family transfer, employee ownership) is wealth planning.

**Valuation:** Know approximately what your business is worth before planning its exit. Business valuation depends on earnings, industry multiples, business-specific factors. A CPA or business valuator can provide a formal valuation.

**Exit options:**
- Third-party sale: most common; typically the highest financial return
- Management buyout: sale to existing management team
- Employee Stock Ownership Plan (ESOP): sale to employee-owned trust; tax advantages; complex
- Family transfer: to children or other family members; estate/gift tax implications
- Merger: combining with another business; may provide synergies

**Tax considerations for business sale:**
- Structure matters enormously: asset sale vs. stock sale has different tax implications for buyer and seller
- Installment sale: receiving purchase price over multiple years spreads income recognition
- Qualified Small Business Stock (Section 1202): gains from sale of qualified C-Corp stock held more than 5 years may be partially or fully excluded from federal capital gains tax

**Immediate exercise:** If you own a business, calculate a rough estimate of its value using a common multiple for your industry (often 2-5× annual EBITDA for service businesses; higher for software). Does this match your expectations? How does the business value compare to your other assets?

## Chapter 9: Generational Wealth Transfer

Passing wealth to the next generation (or to charities) in a tax-efficient, thoughtful way is the final dimension of wealth planning.

**Annual gifting:** The $18,000 annual gift tax exclusion (2024, indexed for inflation) allows transfers to any number of recipients each year without gift tax implications. A couple can give $36,000/person/year. Over 10 years, a couple with three adult children can transfer $1,080,000 gift-tax-free.

**529 superfunding:** 5-year gift tax election allows funding a 529 college savings plan with 5 years' worth of annual exclusion gifts at once ($90,000 per person, $180,000 per couple) without gift tax. After 5 years, the donor can resume annual contributions.

**Trusts for generational transfer:**
- Irrevocable Life Insurance Trust (ILIT): holds life insurance outside the taxable estate; proceeds pass to heirs tax-free
- Generation-Skipping Trust (GST): transfers wealth to grandchildren and later generations while minimizing estate tax at each generation
- Spousal Lifetime Access Trust (SLAT): removes assets from taxable estate while maintaining indirect access through the spouse

**The inheritance conversation:** Before sophisticated transfer structures, the most important step is having explicit conversations with your family about your values, your intentions, and your expectations for inherited wealth. Structuring transfers without these conversations often produces unintended consequences.

## Chapter 10: The Integrated Wealth Plan

Wealth planning is not a single decision — it is an ongoing system that integrates investment management, tax planning, estate planning, risk management, and charitable strategy.

**Annual review checklist:**
- Net worth statement: updated
- Investment allocation: still appropriate?
- Tax plan for current year: optimized?
- Estate documents: current, beneficiaries correct?
- Insurance: coverage limits still adequate for current net worth?
- Charitable strategy: is current approach optimal?
- Professional team: coordinated and aware of recent changes?

**The five-year financial plan:** Beyond annual reviews, a five-year financial plan — where do I want to be in 5 years, and what decisions do I need to make to get there? — provides a longer-horizon framework for wealth management decisions.

Isabel's five-year plan at 52:
- Goal: retire at 67 with $1.2M portfolio and mortgage-free home
- Required actions: maximize 403(b) contributions for 15 years, pay extra $300/month on mortgage, purchase hybrid LTC policy, complete estate plan update
- Five-year milestone ($57): $350,000 portfolio, 9 years of mortgage payments remaining, LTC policy in place

Having the plan makes each year's financial decisions coherent rather than reactive.

## Epilogue

Wealth built over decades deserves intentional management. The emergency fund that took two years to build, the investment account that grew for 20 years, the business equity that represents a career of work — these are worth protecting, optimizing, and intentionally directing.

Wealth planning is not complicated. It is consistent attention to a set of disciplines that, applied over time, produce significantly better outcomes than the default.
