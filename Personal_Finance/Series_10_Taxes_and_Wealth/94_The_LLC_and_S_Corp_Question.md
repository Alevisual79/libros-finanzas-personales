## Prologue

By year three of freelancing, Ruben's CPA asked him a question he hadn't considered: "Are you still operating as a sole proprietor?"

He was. It had always felt like too much complexity to change.

"At your income level," the CPA explained, "operating as an S-Corp would save you approximately $4,000-$5,000 per year in self-employment taxes. It requires more paperwork and a small ongoing cost, but the tax savings are significantly larger."

This was not a theory. It was arithmetic. And the arithmetic, Ruben discovered, applied to most self-employed people above approximately $60,000 in net income.

Business entity structure is one of the highest-leverage tax decisions available to self-employed individuals and small business owners. Most make it by default (staying as a sole proprietor because it's the path of least resistance), not by deliberate analysis.

---

## Chapter 1: The Entity Options

**Sole Proprietorship:** The default state for self-employed individuals who haven't chosen a different structure. No registration required. Income and expenses reported on Schedule C of the personal tax return. No legal separation between the business and the owner. All net income subject to self-employment tax.

**Single-Member LLC (Limited Liability Company):** One owner. Registered with the state. Provides liability protection (business debts and lawsuits generally can't reach personal assets if the LLC is properly maintained). Taxed the same as a sole proprietorship by default (Schedule C) unless an S-Corp election is made.

**Multi-Member LLC:** Two or more owners. Taxed as a partnership by default (each partner reports their share on Schedule K-1). Can also elect S-Corp taxation.

**S-Corporation:** A tax election (not a separate entity type) available to LLCs or corporations. Does not change the legal structure — changes how income is taxed. The critical tax advantage: not all income is subject to self-employment tax.

**C-Corporation:** A separate tax-paying entity. Income is taxed at the corporate level, and then dividends are taxed again at the personal level (double taxation). Not typically optimal for small businesses unless specific circumstances apply (certain tech startup structures, businesses retaining significant cash).

## Chapter 2: The LLC — Why Almost Everyone Should Have One

The limited liability company provides one primary benefit: separation between business and personal liability.

Without an LLC, a sole proprietor's business debt, lawsuit judgments, and other business liabilities can be satisfied from personal assets (savings, home equity, investment accounts). With an LLC properly maintained, business obligations generally cannot reach personal assets.

**When LLC liability protection matters:**
- If you own rental property (tenant injury, property damage lawsuits)
- If your business could face professional liability claims
- If you have client contracts with significant financial implications
- If you handle physical goods that could cause injury
- Essentially: if there's any realistic scenario where a business-related lawsuit could arise

**How to maintain the LLC protection:**
- Keep separate business bank account and credit card
- Never commingle personal and business funds
- Sign contracts as "Jane Smith, Member, Jane Smith LLC" not as "Jane Smith" personally
- Maintain state-required annual filings
- Keep simple business records

The LLC does not provide protection if the "corporate veil" is pierced — i.e., if a court determines the LLC and the owner are not genuinely separate. This typically requires either fraud or failure to maintain proper separation.

**Cost:** State filing fees for LLC formation: $50-$500 depending on state. Annual LLC maintenance fees: $0-$800 depending on state (California is expensive at $800/year minimum franchise tax; most states are $0-$150).

## Chapter 3: The S-Corp Election — The Tax Math

The S-Corporation tax election is where the significant tax savings lie for higher-income self-employed individuals.

**How S-Corp taxation works:**

In an S-Corp, the owner is also an employee. They pay themselves a "reasonable salary" for the services they provide to the business. This salary is subject to payroll taxes (FICA — Social Security + Medicare) from both the employee (the owner) and the employer (the business). But the remainder of the business profit — after paying the salary — flows through to the owner's personal return as a "distribution" that is NOT subject to self-employment tax.

**The math:**
- Net business income: $120,000
- Reasonable salary: $60,000 (subject to FICA)
- Remaining distribution: $60,000 (NOT subject to FICA)
- SE tax saved on distribution: $60,000 × 15.3% = $9,180 (minus some complications for the Social Security wage base)
- Approximate SE tax savings: $4,500-$7,000 depending on specific income levels

This is not aggressive tax avoidance — it is the specific benefit Congress created for S-Corps. But the IRS requires that the salary paid to owner-employees be "reasonable" for the services performed. Paying a salary of $1 to capture $119,999 of distributions is not reasonable and will be challenged.

---

## Chapter 4: What Is a "Reasonable Salary"?

The IRS requires that S-Corp owner-employees receive a salary that represents reasonable compensation for the services they provide. There is no bright-line rule, but general guidance:

**Factors the IRS considers:**
- What would you pay an employee to do the same work?
- What do others in your industry and market earn doing similar work?
- Your qualifications and experience
- Time devoted to the business
- The nature and scope of the work performed
- Your business's revenue and profitability

**Safe harbor approximations commonly used:**
- 40-60% of net profit as salary (common CPA guidance for service businesses)
- Equivalent to market salary for your role if you were employed elsewhere
- At least $40,000-$50,000/year for a profitable business (very rough floor)

**Example:**
- Ruben's business nets $180,000/year
- Market salary for a senior graphic designer/creative director: $90,000-$110,000
- His CPA recommends $85,000 salary
- Distribution: $95,000
- SE/payroll tax savings vs. sole proprietor: approximately $7,000-$9,000/year

## Chapter 5: When Does S-Corp Make Sense?

The S-Corp election adds administrative complexity and cost. The breakeven point where the tax savings exceed the additional cost is approximately $60,000-$80,000 in net self-employment income.

**S-Corp costs:**
- Payroll service (to run payroll for yourself): $50-$150/month ($600-$1,800/year)
- Additional CPA fees for S-Corp return (Form 1120-S): $500-$1,500/year
- State-specific fees (some states have S-Corp fees or minimum taxes)
- Total additional cost: approximately $1,500-$3,500/year

**Net benefit analysis:**

At $60,000 net income:
- Tax savings: approximately $1,500-$2,500
- Additional cost: $1,500-$2,500
- Net benefit: approximately break-even to marginal

At $100,000 net income:
- Tax savings: approximately $4,000-$6,000
- Additional cost: $1,500-$3,000
- Net benefit: approximately $1,500-$4,500

At $200,000 net income:
- Tax savings: approximately $10,000-$14,000
- Additional cost: $2,000-$4,000
- Net benefit: approximately $8,000-$12,000

For most self-employed individuals above $80,000 in net income, the S-Corp election produces meaningful net savings.

## Chapter 6: How to Establish an S-Corp

**Step 1: Form the LLC** (if you don't already have one) or form a corporation.

**Step 2: File Form 2553 (S-Corp Election)** with the IRS within 75 days of the start of the tax year you want the election to take effect, or any time before the year begins for the following year. Late elections are possible in some circumstances.

**Step 3: Set up payroll.** You must run actual payroll to pay yourself a W-2 salary. Options:
- Gusto, ADP, QuickBooks Payroll: automated payroll services; $50-$100/month
- Must calculate, withhold, and deposit federal and state payroll taxes
- File quarterly payroll returns (Form 941) and annual returns (Form W-2, Form 940)

**Step 4: Open a dedicated business bank account** (if not already done) and maintain the corporate formalities.

**Step 5: Coordinate with your CPA.** The S-Corp requires filing Form 1120-S (the S-Corp income tax return) in addition to your personal return. This is best handled by a CPA.

## Chapter 7: LLC Without S-Corp Election — Still Valuable

Many self-employed people benefit from an LLC without making the S-Corp election:
- Income below $60,000 (S-Corp savings don't exceed costs)
- Income from multiple sources where only one portion is SE income
- Business activities with liability risk where the LLC protection is the primary goal
- Businesses with the intention to add partners or sell equity in the future

An LLC without S-Corp election pays SE tax the same as a sole proprietor but provides liability protection that the sole proprietor doesn't have. This is often the right starting structure for new businesses.

**The progression for many self-employed individuals:**
1. Start as sole proprietor (no formal structure, minimum complexity)
2. Form LLC when income and liability risk justify the cost
3. Elect S-Corp when income exceeds $80,000+ net and the savings justify the additional complexity

## Chapter 8: Retirement Accounts in an S-Corp

The S-Corp structure affects retirement account options:

**Solo 401(k) in an S-Corp:**
- Employee contribution: up to $23,000 as W-2 employee of the S-Corp
- Employer contribution: up to 25% of W-2 salary
- Combined: up to $69,000 (2024)

The Solo 401(k) in an S-Corp calculates the employer contribution differently than in a sole proprietor Solo 401(k) — it's 25% of the W-2 salary, not 25% of net self-employment income. This can result in slightly different maximum contributions.

**Example:**
- W-2 salary: $85,000
- Employee 401(k) contribution: $23,000
- Employer contribution (25% of $85,000): $21,250
- Total Solo 401(k): $44,250

**SEP-IRA in an S-Corp:** Also based on W-2 compensation rather than self-employment net income. The Solo 401(k) is generally more flexible for S-Corp owner-employees.

## Chapter 9: Multi-Member LLCs and Partnerships

When a business has two or more owners, the structure and tax implications become more complex.

**Default taxation for multi-member LLC:** Partnership (pass-through taxation). Each member reports their share of income/loss on Schedule K-1.

**S-Corp election for multi-member LLC:** Available if all members are US persons (no non-resident aliens) and there are no more than 100 members. Allows the same payroll/distribution split benefit for multiple owners.

**C-Corp for investment-attracting businesses:** Venture capital and many institutional investors prefer (or require) C-Corp structure for ease of investment and stock option administration. If you plan to raise external investment, consult an attorney about C-Corp vs. LLC early.

**Key documents for multi-member LLCs:**
- **Operating agreement:** The governance document that specifies how income is allocated, how decisions are made, what happens when a member leaves, and other critical operational rules. Essential for multi-member LLCs.
- **Buy-sell agreement:** Agreement specifying what happens to an owner's interest upon death, disability, or departure. Prevents disputes; enables orderly transitions.

## Chapter 10: Working With Professionals

Entity structure decisions benefit from professional guidance. The stakes are meaningful, the rules are complex, and the optimal structure varies by individual situation.

**Who to consult:**

**CPA or tax professional:** Should be the first call for tax optimization analysis (SE tax savings calculation, S-Corp breakeven analysis, retirement account optimization).

**Business attorney:** Should be involved in entity formation (especially for multi-member structures), operating agreement drafting, and any situation with significant assets or partners.

**Timing:** Have the entity structure conversation with your CPA before the year where you expect to exceed $60,000 in net self-employment income. The S-Corp election has deadline constraints; planning ahead avoids missing them.

**Ongoing relationship:** This is not a one-time decision. Annual review with your CPA should include: Is the current structure still optimal? Have income levels or business complexity changed? Are there new structure-related planning opportunities?

**Immediate exercise:** Look up your state's LLC formation requirements and annual fees. If you're self-employed and haven't formed an LLC, calculate the cost in your state and compare it to the liability exposure of operating as a sole proprietor. For most self-employed professionals, the cost-benefit clearly favors forming the LLC.

## Epilogue

Entity structure is not exciting. It is paperwork, filings, and meetings with accountants. But it is one of the highest-leverage financial decisions available to self-employed individuals: potential savings of $4,000-$12,000 per year with the right structure.

Most people stay as sole proprietors because changing requires effort. The effort, measured against the tax savings over a 10-year freelance career, is justified by a significant margin.

Do the math with your CPA. Make the decision deliberately, not by default.
