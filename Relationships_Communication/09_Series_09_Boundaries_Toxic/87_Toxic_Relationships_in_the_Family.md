# Toxic Relationships in the Family
## How to survive and thrive when the source of harm is family

**Series 9: Boundaries and Toxic Relationships — Book 7 of 10**

---

## Prologue

Patrick had, in a specific moment in his forty-fourth year, understood that he had been trying to solve a problem for thirty-six years that could not be solved.

The problem was his mother. Not his mother as a person — she was a complex person with her own history and her own genuine love for him, a love that was real even as it was consistently organized around her own needs. The problem was the specific project he had been engaged in since early childhood: the project of finding the combination of responses, accommodations, and self-modifications that would produce a different kind of relationship with her. A relationship in which she could genuinely see him. In which his experience mattered in the specific way that it mattered to him. In which the fundamental dynamic of their relationship — his attunement to her needs, her limited attunement to his — could be different.

He had, in thirty-six years, not found the combination. He had found, in his forty-fourth year, the evidence that the combination did not exist. Not because his mother did not love him, but because the specific change his project required was a change in her — and he could not change her.

The understanding that he could not change her had been the most freeing and most grief-producing understanding of his adult life.

## Chapter 1: The Specific Difficulty of Family Harm

The harm that occurs within family systems — between parents and children, between siblings, within extended family networks — is among the most psychologically complex and most difficult to navigate forms of relational harm that people encounter.

The specific features that make family harm distinct: the involuntary nature of family membership (one does not choose one's family of origin); the developmental context in which family harm typically begins (in childhood, before the person has the psychological resources to process, contextualize, or protect themselves from the harm); the embeddedness of family in identity (family relationships are foundational to most people's sense of who they are); and the social and cultural norms that treat family as requiring a form of loyalty and continuity that is not expected of other relationships.

The research by Dr. Karl Pillemer at Cornell University on family estrangement — one of the most extensive research programs on adult family relationship dissolution — documents that family estrangement is more common than popularly assumed, with approximately 27% of Americans reporting being estranged from a family member, and that the reasons most commonly cited are related to toxic behavior, abuse, and the failure of the family member to respect basic dignity and limits.

## Chapter 2: The Loyalty Bind

One of the most distinctive and most psychologically costly features of harmful family relationships is what the clinical literature calls the "loyalty bind" — the specific conflict between genuine love and attachment to the family member on one side, and genuine harm being experienced at their hands on the other.

The loyalty bind is not merely a social or moral discomfort. It is a genuine psychological conflict between two real dimensions of one's experience: the genuine love and attachment that most people feel for their family members even when those family members are causing harm, and the genuine self-protective response to the harm itself.

The research on parent-child relationships and their complexity (including work by Karyl McBride on daughters of narcissistic mothers, and by adult children of alcoholics researchers at organizations like the National Association for Children of Alcoholics) documents that the people most reliably trapped in the loyalty bind are those who were closest to the harmful family member — those who received both the most care and the most harm from the same person.

## Chapter 3: Patterns of Family Toxicity

The research on harmful family dynamics identifies several recurring patterns that are associated with systematic psychological harm to family members.

**The enmeshed family system**: the family system in which individual members' autonomy, identity, and separate experience are not adequately supported — in which the family functions as a unit that requires each member's subordination of their own experience to the family's collective experience. The harm in enmeshed systems is often subtle and often not experienced as harm by the people within it, because the subordination of individual experience has been normalized.

**The parentified child**: the specific role assigned to a child in families where the parent's emotional or practical needs consistently come before the child's, and in which the child's development is organized around meeting parental needs rather than the child's own developmental requirements. Patrick's childhood had had significant features of this pattern.

**The scapegoated child**: the child who is assigned the family's collective negative projections — who becomes the repository for the family system's unacknowledged difficulties — and who experiences consistent criticism, blame, and negative evaluation that has more to do with the family system's needs than with the child's actual behavior.

## Chapter 4: Options for Managing Family Relationships

The research on how adults navigate harmful family relationships identifies a range of options that are not binary — the choice between complete estrangement and accepting unlimited harm is not the only available frame.

**Full contact with limits**: the maintenance of the family relationship while establishing and maintaining specific limits on what behavior is acceptable. This option requires the genuine limit-setting capacity discussed in book 82 of this series, and is most viable when the harmful family member is capable of some degree of limit respect and when the harm is not severe enough to make continued contact genuinely dangerous.

**Reduced contact**: the deliberate reduction of contact frequency and intensity without complete estrangement. This option is associated with better outcomes for many people than either full contact without limits or complete estrangement, and allows for the maintenance of family relationship at a level of involvement that is sustainable.

**Estrangement**: the deliberate cessation of contact with the harmful family member. The research on estrangement documents that it is sometimes the most psychologically healthy option available, and that the cultural framing of estrangement as always a failure or always an overreaction is not supported by the evidence.

## Chapter 5: The Estrangement Option

The cultural treatment of family estrangement is substantially more negative than the research evidence supports. The narrative that families should always be maintained, that children owe parents connection regardless of parents' behavior, and that estrangement represents a failure of the person who estranges, rather than a reasonable response to unreasonable harm, is deeply embedded in many cultural contexts and is directly contradicted by the research.

The research on estrangement outcomes — specifically, research by Lucy Blake and colleagues in the United Kingdom and by Karl Pillemer at Cornell — documents that people who have estranged from harmful family members typically report improved psychological wellbeing following the estrangement, that the decision is usually made after extended periods of attempting other approaches, and that regret about the estrangement is less common than cultural narratives suggest.

The specific finding on parental harm: the research consistently documents that the vast majority of parent-adult child estrangements are initiated by the adult child, not the parent, and that the adult child's reasons are overwhelmingly related to the parent's behavior rather than to trivial or selfish motivations. The cultural narrative that estranging adult children are typically unreasonable or ungrateful is not supported by the evidence.

## Chapter 6: Family Relationships and Identity

One of the most challenging dimensions of navigating harmful family relationships is the specific relationship between family membership and personal identity.

The research on identity and family of origin documents that family relationships are among the most identity-constitutive experiences people have — that who one is, in terms of fundamental self-concept, is substantially shaped by one's experience of one's family of origin. This means that the examination and modification of harmful family relationships is not simply a relational adjustment; it is, in many cases, an identity project.

The person who grew up in a family that organized their functioning around harm — whether through direct abuse, emotional neglect, or the enmeshed or parentified dynamics described above — grew up developing a self in response to and within those dynamics. The self that developed was adaptive to those specific conditions. Some of what was adaptive to those conditions is less adaptive to the conditions of adult life, and the work of recognizing and modifying those adaptive patterns is part of what recovery from family harm requires.

**Practical Exercise — The Inheritance Inventory:** Identify three beliefs about yourself that you received from your family of origin — beliefs about your worth, your capability, your role in relationships, or your right to have needs. For each, ask: Is this belief accurate? Is it serving me well in my current life? Where did I receive it, and was that source reliable? The beliefs do not need to be discarded if they are accurate and serving you well; they need to be examined and chosen rather than simply inherited.

## Chapter 7: Generational Patterns

The research on the intergenerational transmission of relational patterns — the way harmful dynamics and attachment patterns are transmitted from one generation to the next — is one of the most important areas of family systems research.

The foundational finding: the patterns of harm, neglect, and dysfunctional relating that produce psychological harm in children are not random. They reflect the transmission of similar patterns from the previous generation, which transmitted them from the generation before. Family harm has genealogies.

Patrick had understood this through the work he had done on his mother's history. His mother's self-centeredness and emotional unavailability had not appeared from nowhere; they were the product of her own developmental history in a family that had organized her experience in specific ways. This did not excuse her behavior; it located it within a larger pattern that Patrick was now, with his own therapeutic work, attempting to interrupt.

The research on "earned security" — the attachment security developed by adults who did not have secure childhood attachment but who have processed their histories sufficiently to achieve coherent self-understanding — is directly relevant here: the interruption of intergenerational transmission is possible, and the work of interrupting it is among the most significant things a person can do for both themselves and for the people who will come after them.

## Chapter 8: When Family Is Currently Harmful

For people whose harmful family relationships are ongoing — who are currently in regular contact with family members who are causing harm — the practical question of how to manage the contact requires specific attention.

The research on harm reduction strategies in ongoing family relationships identifies several consistent principles. The first: clarity about the specific behaviors that are harmful (rather than a general sense that the relationship is "bad") allows for more targeted limit-setting. The second: the maintenance of contact at a level that the person can sustain without significant harm, rather than the maximum contact that the family context demands. The third: the cultivation of alternative support systems — relationships with people who are not part of the harmful family system — that provide the specific needs for belonging, validation, and genuine care that the family system is not meeting.

## Chapter 9: Patrick's Decision

Patrick had decided, after two years of therapy and several specific attempts at limit-setting with his mother, on a form of the "reduced contact" approach that he had been slowly implementing.

He called monthly rather than weekly. He did not provide detailed information about his work, his romantic life, or his inner experience — not from withholding, but from the specific recognition that these were things his mother would use as material for the specific form of engagement they had always had, and that he was not interested in that engagement anymore.

When she said things that were untrue about him or unkind, he said, simply: "That's not true" or "That's not something I'm willing to agree to." He did not argue. He did not explain. He said what was accurate and moved on.

The approach was not comfortable. It was, compared to the thirty-six years that had preceded it, substantially better.

## Chapter 10: What Surviving Family Harm Looks Like

The research on outcomes for people who have navigated harmful family relationships — whether through maintained contact with limits, reduced contact, estrangement, or some combination — is ultimately more optimistic than the long shadow of family harm would suggest.

The people who show the best outcomes are, consistently, those who have engaged genuinely with the history — who have neither denied the harm nor been immobilized by it — and who have developed a coherent understanding of their experience that allows them to see themselves as more than the product of their family's limitations.

This understanding requires work, often requires support, and typically requires time. But the evidence for its possibility — for the genuine capacity of people to develop, from the specific soil of difficult family histories, lives characterized by genuine wellbeing, genuine connection, and genuine self-knowledge — is substantial.

The family you came from does not determine the person you become. It is the starting point, not the destination.

The family you were born into is not the family you have to stay in.

Choose what you keep. Choose what you change. Choose how to live.
