### Prologue
Keiko had, for the third time in two years, failed to get what she needed in her performance review.

The failure was not dramatic. The review had been positive — her work was consistently recognized as among the strongest in her function, her projects delivered results, and her manager expressed genuine appreciation of her contributions. What the review had not done was produce the specific outcomes she needed: a clear development plan for the leadership track she was targeting, a specific commitment to the high-visibility project that would make that track possible, and an honest conversation about what would be required to advance within the next twelve months.

She had not raised any of these things during the review. She had accepted the positive general assessment, expressed gratitude for the appreciation, and returned to her desk with the uncomfortable awareness that another year had passed without the specific conversation she needed to have had.

The dynamic was not new. Keiko was good at her work and poor at advocating for what that work required. Her manager was good at managing and poor at noticing what was not raised. The gap between what she needed and what she had was almost entirely explained by the specific conversation that had not happened.

She had begun to understand that managing her own career in this organization required, as a specific skill, something she had not previously thought of as a skill: managing up.

## Chapter 1: What "Managing Up" Actually Means

The concept of "managing up" — the deliberate management of the relationship with one's own manager — carries unfortunate connotations of manipulation or self-promotion that tend to make people uncomfortable with it before they have understood what it actually involves.

The management researchers John Gabarro and John Kotter, in the foundational Harvard Business Review article "Managing Your Boss" (1980), define managing up as the development of a mutually supportive, productive working relationship with one's manager that serves both parties' professional goals and the shared organizational mission. The definition is notable for what it includes and excludes: it includes mutual support and the serving of shared goals; it excludes any form of manipulation, flattery, or subordination of one's own professional integrity to the manager's preferences.

Managing up, properly understood, is the specific skill of making the manager-subordinate relationship work — of creating the conditions in which both people can do their best work, can communicate effectively, and can navigate the inevitable difficulties of the relationship in a way that is productive for both.

## Chapter 2: Understanding Your Manager

The foundational practice of effective upward management is the deliberate effort to understand the manager as a person — their professional priorities, their preferred communication styles, their particular pressures and concerns, their management philosophy, and the specific dimensions of their role that most affect their ability to support their direct reports.

The research on manager-subordinate relationship quality consistently finds that the direct reports who most effectively manage their manager relationships are those who have invested the most deliberate attention in understanding their manager's perspective — who have developed an accurate model of what their manager is trying to accomplish, what makes their manager's job harder, and what the manager needs from the people who report to them.

Victor, who had been on the managed side of the relationship with four different organizational leaders over his twenty-two-year career, had developed a specific practice in each new reporting relationship: he scheduled a conversation in the first month specifically for the purpose of understanding his new manager's priorities, their preferred way of receiving information, and their expectations for the working relationship. The conversation was not primarily about himself; it was primarily about them. He had found that this investment almost always produced a better working relationship than the alternative of figuring out the manager's preferences through trial and error over six months.

## Chapter 3: Communicating in Your Manager's Style

One of the most consistently applicable findings in the research on manager-subordinate communication is the value of adapting one's communication to the manager's preferences rather than communicating in the style most natural to oneself.

The research on communication style preferences in professional relationships (including work by Roger Gill and colleagues) documents that managers vary significantly in their preferred communication modes (written versus verbal, formal versus informal, detailed versus summary-level), their preferred communication frequency, and their preferred level of involvement in their direct reports' work. These preferences are genuine and consequential: the direct report who communicates in ways that match their manager's preferences is consistently perceived as more competent and more reliable than one who communicates in ways that create friction.

Keiko had discovered, through some experience, that her manager preferred brief, verbally delivered updates in advance of formal meetings rather than the detailed written reports she naturally gravitated toward. Shifting her communication to match this preference had produced, with no change in the substance of her work, a measurable improvement in her manager's perception of her professional presence and judgment.

## Chapter 4: Managing Expectations

One of the most practical and most commonly neglected dimensions of the upward management skill set is the proactive management of the manager's expectations — the specific practice of ensuring that the manager has an accurate and up-to-date understanding of what is being worked on, when it will be delivered, and what obstacles exist.

The research on employee credibility and trust in professional relationships consistently finds that the single most reliable predictor of a manager's perception of a direct report's competence is not the quality of the work — it is the accuracy of the direct report's estimates and commitments. The employee who consistently delivers what they said they would deliver, when they said they would deliver it, is perceived as significantly more competent than the employee who delivers work of higher quality but on an unpredictable or unreliable schedule.

The practical implication: the direct report who proactively communicates about delays, changes in scope, or emerging obstacles — before these become surprises for the manager — is consistently better positioned in the relationship than the one who absorbs these difficulties privately and manages them reactively.

## Chapter 5: Asking for What You Need

The specific practice of clearly and directly asking for what one needs from one's manager — the feedback, the development opportunity, the resource, the decision — is consistently identified in the research on career advancement as one of the most important and most neglected career skills.

The research on help-seeking in professional relationships (introduced in Series 1 of this collection in the context of the general difficulty of asking for help) finds that people consistently underestimate their manager's willingness and ability to provide what they need — that the request goes unasked far more often than it should, and that when it is asked, it is more often granted than the asker had expected.

This underestimation is particularly consequential for career development. The research on career advancement and managerial support consistently finds that the direct reports who receive the most investment in their development are those who explicitly request and engage with development opportunities — not those who wait for the manager to identify and provide them. The manager who would support the right development opportunity if asked is not, in most cases, proactively identifying what each of their reports needs in the absence of that request.

## Chapter 6: Giving Honest Upward Feedback

One of the most challenging dimensions of the upward management relationship is the specific challenge of providing honest, direct feedback to one's manager — communicating concerns, disagreements, or the specific information that the manager needs but that the organizational power dynamic makes difficult to deliver.

The research on upward communication and its organizational value consistently finds that managers who receive accurate, honest information from their direct reports make better decisions, identify problems earlier, and manage more effectively than those who receive filtered, upwardly managed information. The organizational cost of the direct report who tells the manager what the manager wants to hear is consistently significant; the organizational benefit of the direct report who provides honest, direct upward feedback is consistently real.

The practical challenge is the power asymmetry of the relationship: the honest communication of a concern or disagreement to a manager carries genuine risks (the manager may respond poorly, and the manager has authority over the direct report's professional outcomes) that make the communication more difficult than equivalent communication between peers.

**Practical Exercise — The Manager Meeting:** Request a thirty-minute meeting with your manager with the explicit purpose of discussing the working relationship: what they need from you that they're not currently getting, what you need from them, and what would make the professional relationship most productive for both of you. Frame it as a relationship conversation rather than a performance conversation. This conversation — which most people never have — typically produces more value than three performance reviews.

## Chapter 7: When the Manager Is Difficult

The research on difficult manager types and the strategies most associated with managing them productively identifies several characteristic patterns and their appropriate responses.

The micromanager — the manager whose need for control produces excessive oversight of the direct report's work — is most effectively addressed through the proactive provision of information: the direct report who provides frequent, accurate updates on work progress reduces the manager's anxiety (which is typically the driver of micromanagement) and thereby reduces the manager's need to seek control through direct intervention.

The distant manager — the manager who is unavailable, unresponsive, or simply not engaged with their direct reports' development and work — requires the direct report to be more proactive in securing the access and information they need: scheduling regular meetings that the manager might not initiate, being explicit about decisions that require managerial input, and finding other mentors and advocates in the organization who can provide what the manager is not providing.

The bad manager — the one whose behavior is genuinely harmful, whose conduct violates professional norms, or whose treatment of direct reports creates genuine distress — requires a different and more serious response, addressed in the next chapter.

## Chapter 8: When the Relationship Cannot Be Managed

The research on organizational exit and the factors that predict it consistently identifies the direct manager relationship as the primary reason employees leave organizations — not the organization, not the work, and not the compensation, but the specific person one reports to.

The research supports the maxim "people leave managers, not companies": a significant majority of voluntary departures are primarily motivated by the direct report's desire to exit the manager relationship rather than the organization, even when the stated reason for leaving is other factors.

The practical implication is a genuine choice that the direct report who has a genuinely bad manager must eventually face: the choice between the investment required to manage the relationship toward acceptable functioning, the formal processes available for addressing manager misconduct, and the exit that puts the manager relationship behind them. None of these options is without cost; all of them are legitimate.

## Chapter 9: Building the Relationship Before You Need It

One of the most consistently useful findings in the research on professional relationships across the career span is the value of relationship investment before the crisis that will require the relationship to perform.

The manager relationship, like all professional relationships, functions better under stress when it has been invested in during the preceding period of relative calm. The direct report who has built genuine trust, genuine communication quality, and genuine mutual understanding with their manager over the course of the ordinary working relationship has significantly more capital to draw on when something goes wrong — a project fails, a decision is challenged, a professional mistake is made — than the direct report who has managed the relationship only functionally.

Keiko had scheduled the career development conversation. Her manager had been, to her surprise, significantly more engaged with the specific questions she raised than he had been in the previous year's review — partly because she had framed the conversation explicitly as a working partnership investment rather than a performance assessment. The conversation had produced a specific plan and a specific commitment to the high-visibility project she had been targeting.

It had taken thirty minutes of direct, clear communication that she had been avoiding for two years.

## Chapter 10: The Relationship as Partnership

The most useful frame for the manager-direct report relationship — the one most consistently associated with productive, satisfying working relationships on both sides — is the partnership frame: the understanding that both people in the relationship are responsible for its quality and its productivity, and that both benefit when it functions well.

Victor had always thought about his relationship with his own managers in these terms. He had not experienced himself as a subordinate — as someone simply receiving direction from above — but as a professional in a relationship with another professional whose formal authority he respected without allowing that authority to be the primary feature of the relationship. He had been, in every upward relationship he had had, genuinely interested in his manager's success, genuinely helpful to their work, and genuinely honest about his own perspective.

This orientation had not always produced easy relationships. It had consistently produced productive ones.

## Epilogue

The most useful thing you can do for your manager is to understand them well.

The most useful thing you can do for yourself is to let them know what you need.

Both are your responsibility.
