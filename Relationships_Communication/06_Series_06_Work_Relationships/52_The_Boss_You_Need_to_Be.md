## Prologue

Sandra's predecessor as team manager had been widely regarded as effective. Results had been consistently delivered. The team had met its targets. By the metrics that management typically uses to evaluate management, the previous role-holder had performed.

He had also, in the three years of his tenure, lost seven of the team's twelve members to transfers, resignations, and one case of a medical leave that no one in the team attributed entirely to the workload. The people who remained were performing their assigned functions with professional adequacy and approximately no excess energy or discretionary effort. They were doing what was required. They were doing nothing more.

Sandra had inherited this team at the moment when the organization had already begun to recognize that something had been wrong, without quite being able to name what. She had been given the mandate to "build team culture," which was the organization's way of communicating that the previous approach had not produced the environment it needed without clearly naming what that approach had been.

What it had been, Sandra came to understand across her first two months, was this: a management style organized around control, accountability for errors, and the implicit threat of negative consequences for failure. The team had worked. The people had not flourished. The difference between those two outcomes was the difference between the management Victor had practiced for twenty-two years and the management Sandra's predecessor had practiced for three.

## Chapter 1: The Research on Leadership Effectiveness

The study of leadership effectiveness is one of the most extensively researched areas in organizational psychology, and its primary findings over the past half-century have been remarkably consistent.

The early research on leadership, conducted in the 1940s and 1950s by researchers at Ohio State University and the University of Michigan, identified two primary dimensions of leadership behavior: task-orientation (the degree to which the leader focuses on task accomplishment, structure, and the achievement of results) and people-orientation (the degree to which the leader focuses on relationships, the wellbeing of subordinates, and the quality of the social environment).

Subsequent decades of research have confirmed that the most effective leadership combines both dimensions — that the false choice between "task leader" and "relationship leader" is, in the evidence, not a genuine choice at all. The most effective leaders in the research are those who are both task-oriented and people-oriented, and the effective combination of these orientations is the specific definition of what the research has come to call "transformational leadership."

## Chapter 2: Transactional vs. Transformational Leadership

The distinction between transactional and transformational leadership, developed by James MacGregor Burns and subsequently operationalized by Bernard Bass and colleagues at the State University of New York, is among the most influential frameworks in the leadership research literature.

Transactional leadership is organized around the exchange of rewards and punishments for performance: the employee does what is required, the leader provides the promised reward (or avoids the threatened punishment). The relationship is fundamentally contractual — the employee's effort is purchased by the organization's compensation and job security, and the leader's role is primarily to enforce the contract.

Transformational leadership is organized around inspiration, genuine care, and the development of the employees' own motivation and capabilities: the leader articulates a compelling vision, models the values they expect of others, provides genuine intellectual stimulation for the challenges of the work, and offers individualized consideration — genuine interest in and support for each person's development and wellbeing.

The research on the outcomes associated with each approach is unambiguous: transformational leadership is consistently associated with higher employee engagement, higher team performance, higher employee retention, and better organizational outcomes across a wide range of industries and contexts. The meta-analyses of leadership research find that transformational leadership is the most consistently effective leadership style identified in the empirical literature.

## Chapter 3: The Power of Genuine Regard

One of the most practically significant findings in the leadership research is the specific importance of what researchers call "individualized consideration" — the specific dimension of transformational leadership that involves genuine interest in and concern for each individual employee.

The Gallup research on employee engagement finds that employees who agree with the statement "My supervisor, or someone at work, seems to care about me as a person" are significantly more engaged, more productive, and less likely to leave than those who do not. The care is not only the perception of care; the research on leadership authenticity (including work by Bruce Avolio at the University of Washington) finds that the employees most consistently engaged are those who perceive their leaders as genuinely authentic — whose expressed care matches their demonstrated behavior over time.

Victor had made it a personal practice, twenty-two years ago, to know specific things about each person he managed: not their professional metrics but their professional ambitions, their current challenges, their specific circumstances outside work that affected their professional capacity. Not in a way that violated appropriate professional privacy, but in the specific way that genuine interest in a person expresses itself: remembering what they said last time, asking how the situation they mentioned had developed, noticing when something seemed different.

## Chapter 4: Feedback as Care

The specific communication practice most associated with effective leadership — and most commonly avoided or mishandled in practice — is feedback: the direct, honest communication about an employee's performance, behavior, or development.

The research on feedback effectiveness in organizational contexts (including the extensive work of Peter Heslin at Southern Methodist University and colleagues) finds that the feedback practices most associated with employee development and performance improvement are: specific (focused on particular behaviors or outcomes rather than general character evaluations), timely (provided close in time to the behavior that prompted it), balanced (addressing both what is working and what needs development), and genuinely oriented toward the recipient's development rather than the leader's relief or the organization's accountability.

The absence of feedback — the manager who does not provide honest, direct communication about performance — is consistently identified in the research as more damaging to employee development than negative feedback skillfully delivered. The employee who does not know how their work is perceived cannot improve it; the manager who withholds honest feedback is withholding a resource that the employee needs.

## Chapter 5: Psychological Safety and Its Creation

Amy Edmondson's psychological safety research (introduced in Book 51) identifies specific leadership behaviors most associated with the creation of psychological safety in teams.

The behaviors most consistently associated with high psychological safety include: the leader's own modeling of intellectual humility (acknowledging what they don't know, asking genuine questions, admitting mistakes without defensiveness), proactive inclusion of less powerful voices in discussions (specifically asking for input from those less likely to volunteer it), and genuine responsiveness to the ideas and concerns that team members raise (demonstrating that speaking up has consequences other than being ignored or penalized).

The behaviors most consistently associated with low psychological safety are the logical opposites: the leader who responds to raised concerns with dismissal or blame, who publicly criticizes errors rather than using them as learning opportunities, and who — whether intentionally or not — communicates through their behavior that the risks of speaking up exceed the rewards.

## Chapter 6: The Authority Paradox

One of the specific challenges of leadership that the research literature has grappled with across multiple decades is what might be called the authority paradox: the specific way in which formal authority — the power of the leader's position — tends to undermine the specific forms of relationship quality that make leadership effective.

The psychologist Dacher Keltner's research on power and its psychological effects documents a consistent finding: the acquisition of formal power tends to produce specific psychological changes in the person who holds it — including increased self-focus, decreased empathy, and increased insensitivity to the social environment — that directly undermine the relationship qualities most associated with effective leadership. Power, in Keltner's formulation, tends to produce the specific psychological deficits that effective leadership requires its holder not to have.

The implication is not that all leaders inevitably succumb to these effects, but that the cultivation of effective leadership requires specific and sustained counteraction against the psychological tendencies that power naturally produces. The most effective leaders are those who have developed specific practices — deliberate empathy cultivation, genuine curiosity about others' experience, the specific humility of acknowledging what they do not know — that counteract the power-produced tendencies toward self-focus and insensitivity.

**Practical Exercise — The Individual Meeting:** If you lead people, schedule a thirty-minute one-on-one with each direct report this month with this specific question: "What is one thing I do that makes your work harder, and one thing I could do differently that would make it easier?" Listen to the answers without defensiveness. Take notes. Follow up on at least one specific thing from each meeting within two weeks. The exercise is not about agreement with the feedback; it is about demonstrating that speaking honestly to you has positive rather than negative consequences.

## Chapter 7: Delegation and Development

One of the most consistent findings in the leadership research is the correlation between effective delegation — the genuine transfer of both responsibility and authority to employees — and leadership effectiveness, employee development, and team performance.

The effective delegation that the research documents as most associated with positive outcomes is not the dumping of work the leader does not want to do; it is the deliberate assignment of challenging work that develops the employee's capabilities while providing the support and oversight that makes the challenge productive rather than overwhelming.

Sandra had discovered, in her second month, that her primary tendency was to do things herself when she was uncertain whether her reports could do them as well as she could. This tendency had been an asset as an individual contributor. As a manager, it was preventing her reports from developing the capabilities that the role now required them to develop, and it was preventing her from doing the work that only she could do.

## Chapter 8: Managing Performance Failures

The specific challenge of addressing performance failures — the situation in which a team member is not meeting the expectations of their role — is one of the most practically demanding and most commonly mishandled dimensions of leadership.

The two most common failure modes in performance management are: the avoidance failure (the manager who does not address underperformance until it becomes a crisis, accumulating frustration and losing the opportunity for early course correction) and the punishment failure (the manager who addresses underperformance primarily through negative consequences rather than through the genuine understanding and development that most underperformance actually requires).

The research on performance improvement (including the extensive clinical and organizational literature on feedback, coaching, and development) consistently finds that most underperformance is not the product of lack of motivation or effort, but of some combination of unclear expectations, inadequate skills or resources, or personal circumstances that are affecting the employee's capacity. The leader whose first response to underperformance is genuine curiosity — "What's getting in your way?" — rather than accountability language — "You need to improve or else" — is better positioned to actually address the underperformance rather than simply punishing it.

## Chapter 9: Leading Through Difficulty

The research on leadership under stress and adversity identifies specific qualities and practices most associated with the maintenance of effective leadership when external circumstances are difficult.

The most consistently effective leaders under difficulty are those who maintain three specific orientations simultaneously: genuine honesty about the situation (not false reassurance that everything is fine when it is not), genuine confidence in the team's capacity to navigate the situation (not performed positivity but genuine belief in what the people around them can do), and genuine presence with the difficulty (not emotional detachment but regulated engagement with what is actually happening).

Victor had led his team through two significant organizational restructurings and one genuine business crisis in his twenty-two years. The teams he had led through each of these had not experienced them as pleasant. They had experienced them — consistently, according to the feedback he had received — as fair, honest, and supported. He had never offered reassurance he didn't believe. He had never been absent when things were hard. He had never asked people for what they didn't have.

## Chapter 10: The Leader They Need You to Be

Sandra had been a manager for eight months when she received, unexpectedly, an email from one of her direct reports who had just accepted a position at another organization. The email had been professional and warm, and at the end of it was a paragraph that Sandra read three times:

"I wanted to say that this past year under your management has been genuinely different from my prior experience. I feel like I actually developed here. I feel like you saw me, not just my output. That's not a small thing and I wanted to name it before I left."

She had not told this person anything she hadn't believed. She had not done anything she hadn't genuinely cared about doing. She had simply tried, across eight months, to be the kind of manager she herself would have wanted.

It had not been the most ambitious management program in the organization's history.

It had mattered to someone.

## Epilogue

People do not work their hardest for people they fear.

They work their hardest for people they trust.

The difference is entirely within the leader's control.
