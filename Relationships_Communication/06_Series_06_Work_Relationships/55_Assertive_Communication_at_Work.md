## Learn to say what you think without hurting or staying silent

### Prologue
Keiko had a hypothesis about herself: she was the kind of person who thought of exactly what she should have said about twenty minutes after the conversation was over.

The phenomenon had a name — l'esprit de l'escalier, "the wit of the staircase" — the perfect response that arrives when you're already out the door. Keiko's version was less about wit than about professional substance: the point she should have made in the meeting, the pushback she should have offered on the timeline that she could see was unrealistic, the credit she should have claimed for the work that had just been attributed to someone else in the all-hands presentation.

She was not, by any other measure, an unconfident person. She was good at her work and she knew it. She had strong professional opinions and she held them. She had a clear sense of her own value and what she contributed. The gap was between all of this internal clarity and what she actually said in the moment — the consistent undersell, the consistent silence, the consistent deference to what the room seemed to expect.

She had described this pattern to her therapist, who had given it a name: passive communication. Keiko had found the name both accurate and unsatisfying, because what she did was not feel passive. It was feel very clear about what she thought, while somehow failing to say it.

## Chapter 1: The Communication Styles

The clinical and organizational psychology literature on communication styles in professional settings identifies four characteristic patterns: aggressive, passive, passive-aggressive, and assertive.

Aggressive communication prioritizes the speaker's own needs and perspectives at the expense of the other person's — it is direct and clear about what the speaker wants or thinks, but in a way that dismisses, overrides, or explicitly devalues the other person's perspective. The aggressive communicator gets their point across; they do so in ways that damage relationships and produce resistance rather than genuine cooperation.

Passive communication prioritizes the maintenance of relational harmony and the avoidance of direct conflict at the expense of the speaker's own needs and perspectives — it does not clearly express what the speaker actually thinks or wants. The passive communicator avoids immediate conflict; they do so at the cost of their own advocacy and at the longer-term cost of accumulated unspoken frustration.

Passive-aggressive communication attempts to express negative feelings or needs through indirect means — through sarcasm, through strategic neglect, through the behavior that communicates the feeling without acknowledging it — rather than through direct statement. It combines the damage of the unexpressed need with the relational damage of the indirect expression.

Assertive communication expresses the speaker's needs, perspectives, and feelings directly and clearly while genuinely respecting the other person's equivalent needs, perspectives, and feelings. It is direct without being aggressive, clear without being dismissive, and relational without being passive.

## Chapter 2: Why Assertiveness Is Difficult

The research on assertiveness and its development identifies several distinct sources of the difficulty that many people — and a disproportionate number of women and people in lower-status organizational positions — experience in assertive professional communication.

The social cost asymmetry: the research on social responses to assertiveness in professional settings documents a consistent and troubling finding: assertive behavior is responded to differently depending on the social identity of the person displaying it. The research by Laurie Rudman and colleagues on "backlash effects" finds that women who display assertive behaviors in professional settings are significantly more likely than men to experience negative social responses — being perceived as aggressive, unfriendly, or violating social expectations — even when the assertive behavior is identical in content and tone. This asymmetry means that the costs of assertive communication are not equally distributed, and that the appropriate response to the differential costs is both individual skill development and organizational culture change.

The approval need: the research on attachment and people-pleasing behavior (including the work on anxious attachment patterns introduced in Series 1 of this collection) documents the specific way that the need for others' approval — particularly in contexts of unequal power — can inhibit direct, clear communication. The person whose sense of relational security depends on being approved of by the people around them is structurally inhibited from the kind of direct disagreement or self-advocacy that assertive communication requires.

The conflict avoidance default: the anticipation of negative responses to direct communication — the expectation that assertiveness will be received as aggression, will damage the relationship, or will produce negative professional consequences — is a primary barrier to assertive communication even when these anticipated consequences are unlikely to materialize.

## Chapter 3: The Assertiveness Framework

The clinical research on assertiveness training — which has a substantial evidence base dating from the 1970s work of Joseph Wolpe and Arnold Lazarus and extending through contemporary cognitive-behavioral approaches — identifies several specific practices that consistently support the development of assertive communication skills.

The DESC script (developed by Sharon and Gordon Bower in *Asserting Yourself*) provides a specific structure for assertive communication in challenging situations: Describe the specific behavior or situation (without evaluation or attribution); Express your feeling or response to it (using "I" statements focused on your own experience); Specify what you want (a specific, reasonable, concrete request); and indicate the Consequences — the positive consequences of the requested change, or the natural consequences if the situation continues unchanged.

The specific utility of the script is not that it should be used verbatim in professional communication — that would produce an awkward, formulaic quality — but that it forces the organization of one's communication around the features most associated with assertive effectiveness: specific description, personal feeling expression, clear request, and honest consequence statement.

## Chapter 4: Saying No

One of the most practically significant and most commonly difficult assertive communication challenges in professional settings is the clear, direct, professional decline of a request — saying no to something being asked of you.

The research on professional help-seeking and decline finds that people significantly overestimate the relational cost of declining requests — that the anticipated damage to the relationship from saying no is consistently larger in the anticipation than in the reality. The manager who is told "I can't take on another project this quarter without one of my current projects moving to another team" is significantly more likely to respond by reorganizing the workload than by damaging the relationship.

The specific language of the professional decline that the research and clinical literature supports: direct (not hedged with excessive apology or explanation that dilutes the message), honest (explaining the actual reason for the decline rather than offering excuses that may not be believed and do not address the real issue), and where possible constructive (offering an alternative or identifying someone else who can meet the need).

## Chapter 5: Taking Up Space in Meetings

The specific context of the professional meeting — the recurring situation in which multiple people with different organizational statuses and communication styles interact in a group — is one of the most challenging contexts for assertive communication and one in which the specific patterns of under-participation are most clearly visible.

The research on participation patterns in meetings (including work by organizational psychologists studying gender and status in professional settings) documents consistent patterns: people with lower organizational status participate less, people whose ideas are interrupted or not acknowledged reduce subsequent participation, and the psychological safety of the broader group significantly moderates these effects.

The practices most associated with effective participation for people who tend toward passive behavior in group settings include: the preparation of one specific contribution in advance of each meeting (a question, a perspective, a piece of information) that reduces the cognitive load of in-the-moment contribution generation; the use of bridging language that links one's contribution to what has just been said ("Building on what was just said, I think...") rather than competing for interruption space; and the specific practice of asserting interrupted contributions ("I'd like to finish the thought I was making...") rather than accepting the interruption as a signal that the contribution was not valued.

**Practical Exercise — The One Contribution Rule:** For the next two weeks, commit to making one specific, genuine contribution in every meeting you attend — a question, a perspective, or a piece of relevant information that you would otherwise have kept to yourself. The contribution does not need to be groundbreaking; it needs to be genuine and relevant. Notice what happens when you make it. Notice what happens when you don't.

## Chapter 6: Claiming Credit

One of the specific assertiveness challenges that research consistently identifies as disproportionately affecting people in lower-status positions is the claiming of appropriate credit for professional contributions.

The research on attribution of professional credit in organizations (including work by Hannah Riley Bowles and Linda Babcock) documents the specific ways in which ambiguous credit attribution tends to flow toward people with higher organizational status or belonging to majority identity groups — the team project whose leadership is attributed to the visible authority figure rather than to the person who did the work; the idea that gains organizational traction only when endorsed by someone with more status than the person who originated it.

The assertive response to this pattern requires the specific skill of explicit credit claiming — the direct, professional statement of one's own contribution without excessive hedging or self-deprecation. "I developed this approach in the research I did last quarter" is assertive. "Well, I mean, I did some preliminary work on this idea, I'm not sure how much of the final version came from that" is passive, even if technically accurate.

## Chapter 7: Giving Assertive Feedback

The specific challenge of providing assertive feedback — direct, honest, specific communication about someone's professional performance or behavior — is among the most practically significant and most commonly avoided assertive communication challenges in professional settings.

The research on feedback avoidance in professional relationships documents the specific costs of the failure to provide honest, direct feedback: the employee who continues to make an avoidable mistake because no one has told them clearly; the colleague whose behavior affects the team's functioning while everyone talks about it to each other but not to them; the professional relationship that accumulates unexpressed frustration until it becomes unsalvageable.

The assertive feedback that research supports as most effective is: specific (focused on observable behavior rather than inferred character), timely (close in time to the behavior that prompted it), and oriented toward the other person's development rather than the feedback-giver's relief. "In the meeting this morning, you interrupted me three times while I was presenting. I'd appreciate it if you'd let me finish before responding." This is direct, specific, and timely — the assertive communication that actually produces change.

## Chapter 8: The Cultural Dimension

The practice of assertive communication is culturally variable in ways that the standard Western clinical literature does not always adequately acknowledge.

The communication norms that define "assertive" in high-directness cultures (including mainstream American and Northern European professional cultures) overlap imperfectly with the communication norms of lower-directness cultures (including many East Asian, Latin American, and Middle Eastern professional cultures) in which the same directness is experienced as aggressive, rude, or socially harmful.

The cross-cultural research on professional communication (including work by Erin Meyer, whose *The Culture Map* provides one of the most accessible treatments) documents the specific ways in which these cultural differences produce communication difficulties in diverse professional teams — particularly when the assessment of whether someone is "assertive" or "passive" or "aggressive" is being made by people operating within one cultural framework about people operating within another.

The practical implication: assertiveness is not a universal standard that can be applied without cultural calibration. The goal is not assertiveness as defined by any particular cultural norm, but the effective expression of one's needs and perspectives in ways that are clear and genuine within the specific cultural and professional context.

## Chapter 9: When It Goes Well

Keiko had asked for the meeting with the senior colleague to address the attribution of her work. She had prepared, specifically and carefully. She had used the DESC structure, not as a script but as an organizational guide.

The conversation had not gone perfectly. The senior colleague had been, initially, defensive — had offered an explanation of the attribution that did not fully account for the pattern Keiko had observed. She had named the pattern specifically, with specific examples, without allowing the defensiveness to deflect her from the conversation.

The senior colleague had, at the end of the meeting, acknowledged that the attribution had been careless and had committed to being more specific about contribution credit in future all-hands presentations. The acknowledgment had been genuine — not enthusiastic, but genuine.

Keiko had left the meeting with a specific quality of experience she had not had before in this professional relationship: the experience of having advocated for herself clearly and directly and having the advocacy received as legitimate rather than as a violation of professional norms.

The l'esprit de l'escalier had not appeared. She had said what she needed to say in the room.

## Chapter 10: The Practice of Your Voice

Assertiveness is not a personality trait that some people have and others don't. The research on assertiveness training consistently finds that it is a learnable skill — that people who score low on assertiveness measures before training score significantly higher after appropriate skill development, and that these gains are maintained over time.

The development of assertive communication in professional settings, like the development of any skill, requires practice — the repeated attempt, in real situations, to communicate more directly and clearly than comes naturally, to tolerate the specific discomfort of self-advocacy, and to accumulate the evidence that direct communication produces better outcomes than the alternatives.

The accumulation of this evidence is what makes assertiveness sustainable: not the belief that directness is always safe or always received well, but the specific, experience-based knowledge that it is more often effective than its absence, and that the professional relationships capable of holding it are the ones most worth investing in.

## Epilogue

Your professional voice is one of your most important professional assets.

Using it clearly is a skill. Like all skills, it gets better with practice.
