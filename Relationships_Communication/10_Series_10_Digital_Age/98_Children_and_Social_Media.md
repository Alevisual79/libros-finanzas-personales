# Children and Social Media
## How to navigate the digital world with teenagers

**Series 10: Relationships in the Digital Age — Book 8 of 10**

---

## Prologue

Ted's daughter, Zoe, was fifteen when she showed him the account.

It was not her main Instagram account — the one he knew about, the one with the photographs of her friends and her dog and the hiking trips they had taken as a family. It was another account. A "finsta" — a fake Instagram, in the terminology her generation used — with a different name, a small and carefully selected list of followers, and a completely different quality of content: more honest, more raw, more openly anxious than the public account.

She had not shown it to him because she wanted him to monitor it. She had shown it to him because she wanted him to understand something about the experience of being her — about the specific duality of her digital social life, the carefully managed public face and the less managed private one, the specific effort that the management required.

He had looked at it for approximately ten minutes. He had asked her two or three questions. He had then done something that she told him, six months later, had been the most important thing: he had not made any of it about the rules. He had not asked what the rules should be, or whether she was being safe, or what his access to the account would be going forward. He had said: "Thank you for showing me. What's it like, carrying all of this?"

She had talked for an hour.

## Chapter 1: The Digital World Adolescents Actually Inhabit

The research on adolescent social media use — its prevalence, its patterns, and its psychological effects — is among the most actively developing areas of contemporary developmental psychology, driven by the urgency of understanding what the first generation to grow up with smartphones is experiencing and what adults can do to support them.

The foundational facts: by their mid-teens, the majority of adolescents in high-income countries use multiple social media platforms regularly, with usage averaging two to four hours per day for the typical teenager and substantially more for heavy users. This usage is not evenly distributed across platforms: the current landscape involves multiple simultaneous platforms serving different social functions, and the specific platforms most used shift rapidly as teen adoption patterns change.

The social centrality of digital media for contemporary adolescents is not a failure of character or parenting. It is the specific social reality of their developmental moment — a reality in which the peer relationships that are developmentally central to adolescence now partly play out in digital spaces, and in which social exclusion from digital participation carries real social costs that parents who did not grow up in this context may underestimate.

## Chapter 2: What the Research Shows About Social Media and Teens

The research on social media's effects on adolescent psychological wellbeing has become one of the most hotly debated areas in contemporary psychology, with well-credentialed researchers holding significantly different positions on the strength and direction of the evidence.

The most widely publicized position: the research by Jean Twenge and Jonathan Haidt, presented most accessibly in Haidt's book *The Anxious Generation* (2024), argues that smartphone and social media adoption is the primary driver of a significant and real increase in adolescent mental health problems — particularly depression and anxiety in girls — that began around 2012. Haidt's four "norms" framework advocates for significant restrictions on smartphone and social media access for adolescents.

The contrasting position: researchers including Amy Orben, Andrew Przybylski, and Candice Odgers argue that the evidence linking social media specifically to the adolescent mental health decline is considerably weaker than the Haidt/Twenge position claims — that the associations are small, that the evidence for causation is limited, and that attributing the mental health trends to a single technological cause oversimplifies a complex picture.

The practical implication of this debate: the evidence is more nuanced than either extreme position suggests, and the most defensible conclusion is that social media's effects are substantially heterogeneous — that some adolescents are significantly harmed by specific patterns of use, that others are largely unaffected, and that the specific patterns of use matter more than the fact of use.

## Chapter 3: The Specific Harms to Watch For

Regardless of the broader debate about social media's overall effects on adolescent mental health, the research identifies several specific patterns of use that are consistently associated with worse outcomes, and that are worth monitoring and addressing.

Passive consumption: the research consistently documents that passive scrolling — consuming others' content without producing one's own or engaging actively with others — is more strongly associated with negative wellbeing outcomes than active engagement. The adolescent who spends hours scrolling through others' highlight-reel content is in a fundamentally different psychological situation than the adolescent who uses the same platforms to actively engage with friends.

Nighttime use: the research on sleep and adolescent wellbeing is among the most consistent in developmental psychology, and the specific contribution of nighttime phone use to adolescent sleep disruption is well-documented. Adolescents who use phones in the hour before sleep, or who sleep with phones in their bedrooms with notifications enabled, show consistently worse sleep outcomes than those who do not.

Social comparison and appearance content: the research on Instagram and body image reviewed in book 92 of this collection is particularly relevant for adolescent girls, who show the strongest associations between exposure to appearance-focused content and body dissatisfaction.

## Chapter 4: What Parents Can Actually Do

The research on parenting approaches and their effectiveness in reducing social media harms for adolescents identifies several approaches that are consistently supported by evidence, and several that are not.

What works: the maintenance of genuine open communication about social media experiences — conversations in which adolescents feel safe to share what they are encountering online without fear of having technology removed as a consequence; collaborative rule-setting in which adolescents are involved in developing the family's technology norms rather than having them imposed; and the deliberate cultivation of in-person social connection and offline engagement as alternatives to screen time.

What does not work as intended: surveillance and monitoring of adolescents' online activity without their knowledge, which research consistently finds produces worse outcomes than transparent family agreements — not because monitoring is inherently harmful, but because covert monitoring damages the trust relationship that is the most protective factor in adolescent wellbeing; and punitive removal of devices in response to discovered problems, which tends to produce concealment rather than behavioral change.

## Chapter 5: The Conversation Rather Than the Rule

The research on how parents can effectively support adolescent wellbeing in the digital environment identifies the quality of the parent-child relationship — specifically, the degree to which the adolescent experiences the parent as genuinely interested in and supportive of their experience rather than primarily focused on control — as the most consistently important protective factor.

The specific quality of parental engagement most associated with better adolescent outcomes: genuine curiosity about the adolescent's digital social world, without judgment or surveillance; consistent presence and availability that allows the adolescent to bring digital-related concerns to the parent without fear of the parent's response; and the specific skill of asking questions that invite disclosure rather than making statements that produce defensiveness.

The research on digital resilience in adolescents — the capacity to navigate the specific challenges of digital social life without significant psychological harm — finds that it is substantially cultivated through relationships with adults who are genuinely engaged with and curious about the adolescent's experience, rather than through external restrictions that do not develop the adolescent's own navigational capacity.

**Practical Exercise — The Curiosity Approach:** If you have an adolescent in your life, spend one conversation this week asking genuine questions about their digital social life without any agenda of rule-setting or problem-solving. What platforms do they use and why? What do they enjoy about them? What frustrates them? What have they seen that was confusing or upsetting? Your goal is not to gather information for intervention but to understand their experience. What you learn will inform any future conversations about norms more effectively than any rule you could impose without that understanding.

## Chapter 6: Priya's Research and Ted's Experience

Priya's research on adolescent social media use had led her, several years before, to develop a set of guidelines that she shared with parents at public presentations — guidelines that she had always believed were well-supported by the evidence and practically sound.

When she had shared these guidelines with Ted, in a professional context in which he had sought her input on his daughter's situation, he had been genuinely grateful. He had also, as a follow-up several months later, told her something that she had found valuable enough to incorporate into subsequent presentations: "The research tells you what to do. It doesn't tell you how to do it in a real relationship with a real fifteen-year-old who doesn't want to do what you're suggesting."

He had been right. The gap between the evidence-supported approach and the implementation of that approach in an actual parent-child relationship was a gap that the research literature addressed inadequately. It was a gap that required the specific relational skill of maintaining genuine connection with an adolescent who was navigating a developmental stage specifically organized around individuation and resistance to parental authority.

Priya had updated her presentations. The new version spent substantially more time on the relationship than on the rules.

## Chapter 7: The Developmental Context

One of the most important framings for understanding adolescent social media use is the developmental context in which it occurs — the specific psychological work of adolescence, and how digital environments both serve and complicate that work.

The foundational developmental task of adolescence: the construction of identity — the specific psychological work of developing a coherent sense of who one is, what one values, and how one relates to the social world — that is the defining developmental project of the teenage years. The research by Erik Erikson and subsequent developmental psychologists consistently identifies identity formation as the central task of adolescence.

The digital dimension of identity formation: social media provides adolescents with a specific set of tools for identity exploration and social negotiation — for trying out different versions of themselves, for receiving social feedback on these versions, and for navigating the peer relationships that are the primary context for adolescent identity development. These are genuine developmental functions, not merely entertainment.

The specific risk: the social media environment's structural features — the visibility of social metrics, the permanence of digital content, the specific quality of social comparison it enables — can make the already difficult process of adolescent identity formation more fraught than it would otherwise be.

## Chapter 8: Zoe's Digital Life

Ted had, after the conversation that Zoe had initiated by showing him her finsta, developed a different relationship with his daughter's digital life than the one he had previously had.

The previous relationship: monitoring from a distance, a set of parental rules about phone use that had been negotiated when she was twelve and had not substantially evolved since, and a consistent awareness that he did not actually understand what her digital social life looked like.

The new relationship: periodic genuine conversations about what was happening online, conversations that she now initiated as often as he did, and a specific quality of mutual trust that had developed from his demonstrated willingness to be curious rather than controlling.

He had not removed the rules. He had stopped relating to her digital life primarily through the lens of rules. The shift had produced a quality of information about her actual experience that the rules alone had never generated, and had produced something more valuable: a daughter who talked to him about things that were difficult because she trusted that he would listen rather than react.

## Chapter 9: The Longer View

The research on adolescents who develop healthy relationships with digital technology — who emerge from the adolescent years with the capacity to use digital tools in ways that serve rather than harm their wellbeing — identifies several consistent features of their developmental context.

The presence of genuine in-person connection: adolescents who have strong, genuine in-person friendships and family relationships show consistently better outcomes in digital environments than those who are primarily socially connected online. The in-person connection provides the specific developmental foundation that makes online connection supplementary rather than compensatory.

The cultivation of offline competence: adolescents who have developed genuine competence and engagement in offline domains — sports, arts, academics, community involvement, any arena in which genuine skill and genuine social connection are developed outside digital spaces — show better resistance to the specific harms of digital social environments.

The quality of adult relationships: the research returns, consistently, to the quality of the adolescent's relationships with the adults in their life as the most important protective factor. The adolescent who experiences genuine connection with at least one adult who takes their experience seriously and engages with them with genuine curiosity and care is substantially more resilient in digital environments than the adolescent who does not.

## Chapter 10: What This Generation Is Teaching Us

The first generation to grow up with smartphones is now, in many cases, old enough to reflect on what that experience has been — and what they are saying is considerably more nuanced than either the technology-enthusiastic or the technology-alarmed adult positions would predict.

Many younger adults report genuine ambivalence about social media: recognition of both the genuine connection and belonging it provides and the specific ways in which it has complicated their sense of self, their experience of friendship, and their relationship with their own attention. This ambivalence is not confusion; it is accurate perception of a genuinely complex situation.

What they consistently report wanting from the adults in their lives: not to have been protected from digital technology by restriction, but to have been genuinely accompanied through it — to have had adults who were curious rather than alarmed, who engaged rather than monitored, and who trusted them with increasing autonomy in digital spaces rather than treating digital life as a domain of pure risk.

The lesson for parents and adults who care about young people: the goal is not to prevent their engagement with the digital world. It is to help them develop the wisdom to engage with it well.

Be curious. Be present. Be the person they can talk to.
