## What you share about your life and bonds online

### Prologue
Ted had made a decision, early in his relationship with social media, that he had not revisited until his daughter asked him a question that stopped him.

His daughter was fifteen, had been on Instagram for two years, and had recently discovered a photograph of herself that her father had posted when she was twelve — a photograph she had found through a friend's comment, that she had never seen before, that she had not known existed in that form in that place, and that she did not like.

She had asked: "Did you think about whether I'd want people to see this?"

He had not, he realized. He had not thought about it at all. He had thought about sharing a moment from his family's life that felt warm and genuine, and had not specifically thought about his daughter as a person with her own preferences about her digital representation — a person whose consent to her own public image he had not asked for and had not, until this conversation, understood he needed to consider asking for.

The conversation had been, Ted had thought afterward, about much more than the photograph.

## Chapter 1: Privacy in the Digital Age

The concept of privacy — the right to control information about oneself and to determine who has access to one's personal information — has been profoundly changed by the digital environment in ways that are still being worked out at both the individual and societal levels.

The foundational privacy concept: the idea, developed in the philosophical tradition and enshrined in various legal frameworks, that individuals have a legitimate interest in controlling information about themselves — including information that exists in public spaces but that they have not chosen to make widely available.

The digital disruption of this concept: the permanent, searchable, shareable character of digital information has eliminated the practical privacy that physical impermanence and limited reach previously provided. The comment made at a party in 1985 was heard by the people who were there and forgotten; the comment made on social media in 2025 is permanent, searchable, and potentially findable by anyone forever.

## Chapter 2: The Research on Privacy and Wellbeing

The research on the relationship between privacy and psychological wellbeing documents a consistent finding: the experience of having genuine control over information about oneself — what researchers call "privacy control" — is positively associated with wellbeing, while the loss of this control is associated with anxiety, distress, and the specific vulnerability of feeling exposed without consent.

The research by Irving Altman on privacy as a process of regulating social interaction identifies privacy not as isolation but as control — the capacity to regulate the flow of information about oneself in ways that match the specific relational context. Altman's concept of "boundary regulation" — the management of what is shared and with whom, across different relational contexts — is directly relevant to the digital age's collapse of contextual boundaries.

The collapse of context: in traditional social environments, different information was appropriate to different contexts — what one shared with close friends was different from what one shared with colleagues, which was different from what one shared with strangers. Digital social media has collapsed these contexts into a single space in which all audiences exist simultaneously, creating the specific privacy challenge of managing disclosure across radically different relational contexts in a single medium.

## Chapter 3: Relationship Privacy and Its Negotiations

One of the most practically important privacy questions in the digital age concerns the privacy of relationships themselves — what aspects of one's romantic partnerships, friendships, and family relationships to share publicly, and whose consent is involved in these sharing decisions.

The research on relationship disclosure and its effects on the relationships disclosed is a relatively new area, but its findings are consistent with what intuition would suggest: sharing details of a relationship publicly without the other party's explicit consent is a form of privacy violation that is associated with relationship damage, particularly when the sharing involves private conflict, intimate information, or representations of the other person that they have not approved.

The "relationship oversharing" phenomenon documented in research on social media and romantic relationships (including work by Catalina Toma and colleagues) identifies a specific pattern: the couple who shares extensively about their relationship on social media is not consistently the couple whose relationship is genuinely good. The research finds no positive correlation between public relationship celebration and actual relationship satisfaction, and some evidence of a negative association — with the most publicly celebration-intensive couples showing, in some studies, lower private relationship satisfaction than those who share less.

## Chapter 4: Children's Digital Privacy

The specific privacy question that Ted's conversation with his daughter had illuminated — the question of parents' sharing of images and information about their children — is one of the most significant and most under-examined privacy questions in the digital age.

The specific phenomenon, given the term "sharenting" in the research literature, involves parents sharing photographs, videos, and personal information about their children on social media before the children are old enough to consent or even to have preferences about their digital representation.

The research on the long-term implications of sharenting documents several consistent concerns: the permanent digital footprint that sharenting creates for children who have not consented to it; the specific vulnerability that childhood photographs shared without consent create for children as they grow (including the well-documented use of these photographs in ways the parents did not intend); and the specific relational harm that children may experience when they discover — as Ted's daughter had — that images of themselves exist in public spaces without their knowledge or consent.

## Chapter 5: Digital Privacy in Romantic Relationships

The research on privacy within romantic relationships in the digital age identifies a set of genuinely complex questions about the appropriate balance between privacy within the relationship (what partners share with each other about their digital lives) and privacy from the outside world (what the couple shares publicly about the relationship).

The most consistent finding in the research on technology and romantic relationships: the monitoring and surveillance of a partner's digital activity — reading private messages, tracking location, monitoring social media activity — is consistently associated with lower relationship quality and higher relationship conflict, even when the monitoring is framed as reasonable concern rather than controlling behavior.

The research distinguishes between the transparent sharing of digital lives (both partners having access to each other's digital lives with mutual knowledge and consent) and covert monitoring (one partner accessing the other's digital activity without knowledge or consent). The former is associated with better outcomes than the latter, though the research also finds that even transparent mutual monitoring is associated with relationship anxiety when it is motivated by insecurity rather than by genuine preference for shared transparency.

**Practical Exercise — The Sharing Audit:** Before posting something about another person — a partner, a child, a friend — ask three questions: Have I considered this person's preferences about their digital representation? If I asked them, would they say yes? Is there a version of sharing this moment that respects their privacy while still expressing what I want to express? These questions take thirty seconds and can prevent the specific relational harm that unsolicited sharing produces.

## Chapter 6: Privacy and Trust

One of the most important connections in the research on privacy in the digital age is the connection between privacy and trust — the specific relationship between a person's experience of having their privacy respected and their trust in the people who respect (or violate) it.

The research on trust and privacy violations documents a consistent pattern: privacy violations — even relatively minor ones, like sharing someone's information without their consent — produce significant trust damage that is disproportionate to the apparent severity of the violation. This disproportionate effect reflects what the violation communicates about the person who committed it: the privacy violator has demonstrated that they do not adequately consider the other person's interests, that they prioritize their own immediate desire to share over the other person's preferences, and that they cannot be trusted with private information.

## Chapter 7: Mia's Professional Dilemma

Mia had, in the course of her professional work, made decisions about other people's privacy that she had not always been comfortable with.

The specific discomfort: she was paid to produce content that represented clients — their products, their events, their employees — in ways that served the clients' interests. The clients' interests and the subjects' interests were not always aligned. The employee who appeared in a client's social media campaign had typically consented in some formal sense; whether they had been given a genuine choice about participating, or whether the implications of the consent they had given had been fully clear to them, was more variable.

She had developed, over ten years, a set of internal guidelines that governed what she would and would not do in her professional work — practices that reflected a genuine concern for the privacy and dignity of the people who appeared in the content she managed. She was also aware that these practices put her somewhat outside the norms of her industry.

She had decided that she preferred being outside those norms to being inside them without this conscience.

## Chapter 8: The Right to Be Forgotten

The European Union's "right to be forgotten" — established in the 2014 Google Spain decision and formalized in the General Data Protection Regulation — is the most significant legal recognition of the specific digital-age privacy interest in being able to control what information about oneself persists in searchable digital spaces.

The right to be forgotten reflects a genuine human interest that digital technology has newly threatened: the interest in being able to change, develop, and make mistakes without those mistakes being permanently and easily accessible to everyone one might ever encounter. The research on reputation and its effects on life outcomes documents the significant practical consequences of digital permanence for people whose youthful mistakes, past conflicts, or simply unflattering moments are perpetually findable.

The research on the psychological effects of digital permanence documents the specific burden that the awareness of permanent digital records produces — the specific self-consciousness that comes from knowing that what one says or posts today may be findable indefinitely.

## Chapter 9: Priya's Position

Priya had thought extensively about the ethics of her research on online social behavior — research that involved the analysis of publicly available social media data about real people who had not specifically consented to being research subjects.

The ethical framework she worked within — developed through years of engagement with institutional review boards, ethics committees, and the ongoing debate within her field about appropriate research practices — held that publicly available information was available for research purposes but that this availability did not eliminate the obligation to treat the people behind the data with genuine ethical care.

What this meant in practice: anonymization of all individual cases before publication, genuine attention to whether research findings could be used to harm specific populations, and a consistent refusal to participate in research projects that used publicly available data in ways that the data's owners would reasonably find objectionable even if legally they had no recourse.

She had been told by some colleagues that this was excessive caution for academic research. She had said that she found the distinction between "legal" and "ethical" worth maintaining.

## Chapter 10: Privacy as Care

The research on privacy in the digital age, taken as a whole, suggests a reframe that is both practically useful and ethically clarifying: privacy — the respect for other people's right to control information about themselves — is a form of care. It is the specific recognition that the people in one's life are not content for one's digital expression; they are people whose experience of their own representation matters.

This reframe is not primarily about legal rights, though legal rights are relevant. It is about the specific quality of respect for others' full personhood that genuine care requires.

Ted had understood this, from his daughter's question, in a way that no amount of reading about privacy rights would have produced as efficiently. His daughter had taught him something important with four words.

He had thanked her for it, had taken the photograph down, and had asked her to tell him if she had concerns about anything else he had posted about her.

She had said: "There are a few things." They had gone through them together.

Sharing someone else's story online is not your right.

It is their gift to you.

Ask before you take it.
