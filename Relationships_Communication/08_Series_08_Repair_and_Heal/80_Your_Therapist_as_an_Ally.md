## How to get the most out of psychotherapy

### Prologue
Marcus had been skeptical about therapy.

Not in the aggressive, dismissive way that some men in his generation were skeptical — the "I don't need a stranger to tell me about myself" skepticism that was more about fear of vulnerability than genuine philosophical objection. His skepticism had been more specific: a sense that talking about things, however skillfully facilitated, would not actually change them. That understanding what was wrong was not the same as the wrong thing being fixed.

He had gone, initially, because Rachel's therapist had recommended couples work and couples work required individual work first. He had sat in his first session with the specific reserve of someone who has decided to be present without being fully available, and had answered his therapist's questions with the particular economy of a person managing his self-disclosure.

Four months later, he had understood, through the specific experience of change he had not been able to produce through understanding alone, what therapy was doing that understanding wasn't.

## Chapter 1: What Therapy Actually Is

The popular understanding of psychotherapy — drawn largely from cultural representations in film, television, and popular psychology — is substantially different from what the research on effective psychotherapy documents.

The cultural representation: therapy as a process in which a knowledgeable expert helps the patient understand the hidden causes of their problems, and in which this understanding produces resolution. The research picture: considerably more complex and considerably more varied.

The research on psychotherapy and its mechanisms of change documents that there are currently several hundred distinct psychotherapeutic approaches with varying evidence bases, and that the specific techniques of different approaches contribute less to outcomes than the relationship between therapist and client — a finding that has been replicated so consistently across so many studies and so many different therapeutic approaches that it is among the most robust findings in the entire field.

Jerome Frank's classic analysis of the common factors in psychotherapy — the factors that produce improvement across therapeutic approaches — identifies the therapeutic relationship, the therapeutic frame (the expectation that the therapy can help), and the therapeutic rationale (an explanation of the problem and how to address it) as the core mechanisms. The specific techniques are secondary to these common factors.

## Chapter 2: The Evidence Base for Psychotherapy

The research on the effectiveness of psychotherapy is substantially more optimistic than the skepticism that has historically surrounded it suggests.

The most comprehensive meta-analyses of psychotherapy effectiveness — including the landmark work by Mary Lee Smith and Gene Glass in the 1970s and the subsequent decades of meta-analytic research — consistently find that psychotherapy produces significantly better outcomes than no treatment, and that the size of the effect is comparable to many medical interventions. The average person who receives therapy reports better psychological functioning than approximately 80% of people with comparable problems who do not receive therapy.

The specific finding on duration: the research documents a specific relationship between therapy dose and outcome, with most therapeutic improvement occurring in the first fifteen to twenty sessions for many conditions, and with continued but smaller increments of improvement in longer therapy. The implication: short-term therapy produces meaningful improvement, and long-term therapy produces additional improvement for many people, but the largest gains typically occur relatively early.

## Chapter 3: Finding the Right Fit

One of the most practically significant findings in the psychotherapy research is the centrality of the therapeutic alliance — the quality of the relationship between therapist and client — as a predictor of therapy outcomes.

The research on therapeutic alliance (particularly the work of John Norcross and colleagues at Scranton University) documents that the therapeutic alliance is the most consistent predictor of psychotherapy outcomes across all studied therapeutic approaches, and that the alliance explains a larger proportion of outcome variance than the specific techniques or modality used.

The practical implication: finding a therapist with whom the client experiences a genuine working relationship — a sense of being genuinely understood, of the therapist's genuine competence and genuine care, and of agreement about the goals and methods of the therapy — is more important than finding a therapist who practices a specific theoretical approach.

The research also finds that most therapeutic alliance problems can be addressed within the therapy if the client is willing to raise them: discussing concerns about the therapy's direction, the therapist's responses, or the nature of the relationship is not a breach of the therapeutic frame but the specific use of the therapeutic relationship that the research most consistently identifies with good outcomes.

## Chapter 4: How to Use Therapy Well

The research on what clients do that is associated with better therapy outcomes provides practically useful guidance for people entering or continuing therapy.

The most consistent finding: clients who engage most actively in their therapy — who work between sessions as well as within them, who bring their genuine experience rather than a managed presentation of it, and who are willing to engage with therapeutic work that is uncomfortable — show better outcomes than those who engage more passively.

The specific practices associated with better outcomes: completing homework or between-session exercises when they are part of the therapeutic approach; being genuine and fully honest with the therapist even when what is genuine or honest is uncomfortable or unflattering; raising concerns about the therapy or the therapist directly within the therapeutic relationship rather than simply not returning; and the specific practice of bringing the full complexity of one's experience — including the material that seems irrelevant or embarrassing or too small — rather than curating one's disclosure.

## Chapter 5: Different Therapies for Different Needs

The research on matching therapeutic approach to presenting problem — on which therapeutic approaches are most effective for which conditions — is more nuanced and less definitive than the marketing of specific therapeutic approaches suggests.

The most evidence-based therapeutic approaches for specific conditions: cognitive behavioral therapy (CBT) has the most extensive evidence base for depression and anxiety disorders; eye movement desensitization and reprocessing (EMDR) and trauma-focused CBT have strong evidence for post-traumatic stress; dialectical behavior therapy (DBT) has strong evidence for borderline personality and emotion dysregulation; and interpersonal psychotherapy (IPT) has strong evidence for depression, particularly when relational difficulties are a significant component.

The important caveat: the research on "common factors" finds that the therapist's competence, genuine care, and the quality of the therapeutic alliance predict outcomes across all these approaches more consistently than the specific techniques of the approach. A skilled, genuinely engaged therapist practicing an approach with less specific evidence for a given condition may produce better outcomes than a disengaged therapist practicing the approach with the best evidence base.

## Chapter 6: What to Expect — and Not to Expect

The research on realistic expectations for therapy outcomes is important for the many people who enter therapy with either excessively pessimistic or excessively optimistic expectations.

Realistic expectations the research supports: meaningful improvement in the presenting problem for most people who engage genuinely with therapy; the development of specific skills and understanding that extend beyond the presenting problem; and the specific experience of being genuinely known by a professional who has no other agenda than the client's wellbeing.

Expectations the research does not support: the resolution of all psychological difficulties through therapy; immediate, dramatic change; the permanent elimination of the tendency to experience the difficulties that brought the client to therapy; or the bypassing of the hard work that genuine psychological change requires.

Elena had found, in her therapy, something she had not anticipated: that the work she had done on the toxic friendship had unexpectedly illuminated dimensions of her earlier relationships — with her family, with previous friends, with her own self-concept — that she had not known she was carrying. The therapy had been more expansive than she had expected. It had also been more demanding.

**Practical Exercise — The Therapy Preparation:** Before each therapy session, spend five minutes writing down: What has come up for me this week that I want to bring to therapy? Is there anything I have been avoiding bringing? Is there anything I want to tell my therapist about how the therapy is going — what's working and what isn't? The preparation is not about planning a presentation; it is about coming to the session with genuine clarity about what you need.

## Chapter 7: The Specific Value for Relational Healing

The research on the specific value of therapy for relational healing — for the processing of relational wounds, the development of healthier relational patterns, and the preparation for more genuinely connected relationships — is particularly relevant to the themes of this series.

The specific mechanisms through which therapy supports relational healing: the therapeutic relationship itself provides a genuinely safe relational experience that can begin to update insecure attachment patterns — what John Bowlby called the "corrective emotional experience" that secure therapy relationships provide. The deliberate examination of relational patterns in therapy — how the client relates to the therapist, what relational behaviors they bring to the therapeutic relationship, and what these behaviors reflect about their relational patterns more generally — provides a specific and uniquely observable window into patterns that are typically invisible to the person carrying them.

Dara had, in her therapy, specifically done this work: had examined how her tendency to focus on her own experience and have difficulty genuinely attending to others' operated in the therapy room itself. Her therapist had gently noted specific moments when this tendency had appeared in their conversation. The noticing had been uncomfortable and had been directly useful — had given her a specific, observable reference point for a pattern that had previously been abstract.

## Chapter 8: Knowing When to Stop

The research on the appropriate duration of therapy and the indicators of completion is more nuanced than either "therapy is a lifetime commitment" or "a few sessions is enough" positions suggest.

The research-supported indicators of therapeutic completion: the achievement of the presenting goals that brought the client to therapy; the development of a sufficient internal framework for continuing the growth the therapy has initiated without ongoing therapeutic support; and the specific sense, shared by client and therapist, that the work they have been doing is sufficiently integrated to sustain without the structure of ongoing sessions.

The research also documents the specific value of planned termination — the deliberate conclusion of therapy that is processed and prepared for within the therapeutic relationship, rather than the abrupt cessation that many clients engage in — as part of the therapeutic work itself. The ending of therapy is often a significant relational experience that can itself be therapeutically valuable when approached with the same deliberateness as the rest of the work.

## Chapter 9: Therapy's Limitations

The research on psychotherapy is clear about its value; the same research is also honest about its limitations, and a genuine account of what therapy can and cannot do is part of the accurate picture.

Therapy cannot resolve problems that are not psychological in origin — the material deprivation, the ongoing abusive situation, the chronic illness, the external circumstances that produce genuine suffering. The psychological processing of difficult external circumstances can improve the person's experience of them and their capacity to navigate them; it cannot change the circumstances themselves.

Therapy also cannot substitute for the relational experience that genuine connection provides: the research on healing from relational wounds consistently finds that the experiences that most powerfully update insecure relational patterns are genuine relational experiences — including but not limited to the therapeutic relationship — rather than therapeutic insight alone. Insight is valuable; it is not sufficient.

---

## Chapter 10: The Alliance Between You and Your Growth

The research on psychotherapy, taken as a whole, points toward a conclusion that is simultaneously encouraging and demanding: psychological change is possible, is well-evidenced, and requires genuine engagement.

The specific quality of genuine engagement: not the passive receipt of expertise from a knowledgeable professional, but the active partnership between the client's genuine willingness and the therapist's genuine competence in a relationship that is directed toward the client's genuine wellbeing.

Marcus had, by the time his therapy reached its natural first phase of completion, understood what he had not understood at the start: therapy was not something done to him. It was something he had done, with support. The changes that had occurred — the increased clarity about his own relational patterns, the more genuine access to his own emotional experience, the capacity to stay present with difficulty rather than managing it from a distance — were changes he had made.

His therapist had not made them for him. His therapist had made them possible.

That distinction was, in his understanding, the most important thing to know about what therapy was.

## Epilogue

A therapist is not someone who fixes you.

A therapist is someone who stands with you while you fix yourself.

Know the difference.
