## The critical work of building a success definition that actually serves your life

### Prologue
The success definition that most professionals are working toward is not one they chose. It arrived through a combination of family expectations, educational institutions, peer comparison, professional culture, and the social media environment — a composite image of the professional life that would constitute success, assembled from sources that have no specific knowledge of the individual's values, temperament, relationships, or the specific kind of life that would make them genuinely flourish.

The explicit examination of this inherited success definition — the specific investigation of whether it is the definition the professional would choose with full information and genuine reflection — is among the most important and most consistently avoided professional development exercises available. It is avoided partly because the examination is uncomfortable (it may reveal that one has been pursuing the wrong thing for a long time), partly because the inherited definition is so embedded in the professional culture that questioning it feels like questioning the professional identity itself, and partly because the alternative — defining success for oneself — requires the kind of genuine self-knowledge that the busyness of a demanding professional life systematically prevents.

Marcus's executive crossroads had made the examination unavoidable. Elena's post-exit question had arrived at the same place by a different route. Both were doing, for the first time in their professional lives, the specific work of defining success on terms they had genuinely chosen.

## Chapter 1: The Inherited Success Definition

The success definition that the professional inherits from the ambient culture is not random; it has specific, identifiable origins and specific, predictable limitations.

The cultural success template: the dominant success template in most professional cultures in the developed world is a composite of: income (measured in specific quantitative terms that the professional's peer reference group uses as the implicit benchmark), title and status (the organizational hierarchy position that signals achievement to the social environment), and external recognition (the professional reputation, awards, and visibility that distinguish the high-achieving from the merely competent). This template is visible, measurable, and directly comparable — qualities that make it a highly functional motivational driver for the career's earlier stages and an increasingly problematic compass for the career's later ones.

The limitations of the template: the research on the inherited success template's limitations is summarized in the hedonic adaptation and arrival fallacy findings that Book 90 describes in detail: the template is designed to be achieved and then raise the bar rather than to provide the sustained satisfaction that achievement is meant to produce. The professional who reaches each milestone of the inherited template consistently finds that the milestone's attainment produces less satisfaction than its pursuit, and that the template then resets to a higher threshold. This dynamic is not a personal failure; it is the specific motivational design of a template that has been optimized for the cultural function of driving professional achievement rather than for the individual function of producing personal flourishing.

## Chapter 2: The Research on Success and Wellbeing

The research on subjective wellbeing and the various dimensions of professional success provides the empirical basis for the evaluation of the inherited success template against the actual human outcomes it produces.

The income research: the relationship between income and subjective wellbeing is one of the most extensively studied in the happiness economics literature. The Kahneman-Deaton finding (2010, updated by Killingsworth in 2021) that the relationship between income and wellbeing is more complex than a simple diminishing returns curve — with emotional wellbeing continuing to improve with income for most people but with important individual variation — suggests that the role of income in a personally designed success definition depends more on the individual's specific circumstances and values than on any universal threshold.

The purpose over status: the research comparing status-based and purpose-based professional motivations consistently finds that purpose-based motivation is more strongly associated with long-term wellbeing, sustained performance, and genuine professional satisfaction. The findings by Michael Norton and colleagues on the hedonic benefits of "prosocial spending" (money spent on others produces more happiness than the equivalent spent on oneself) are consistent with the broader pattern: the professional success that serves something beyond the professional's personal accumulation is more reliably satisfying than the success that primarily serves personal advancement.

## Chapter 3: The Success Definition Framework

The research on personal success definitions and professional thriving provides a framework for the deliberate construction of a success definition that serves the individual's actual values and the actual conditions of their flourishing.

The multi-domain success definition: the most comprehensive success definition research suggests that a genuinely useful personal success definition includes explicit criteria across multiple life domains — not only the professional achievements but also the relationship quality, the health, the personal development, the contribution, and the present-moment experience that constitute the totality of the person's life. The single-domain success definition (exclusively professional) is specifically the definition most likely to produce the satisfaction deficit that Book 90 documents.

The process and outcome balance: the research on goal pursuit and wellbeing finds that the success definitions that include both process criteria (the quality of the daily experience of doing the work) and outcome criteria (the achievements that the work produces) are more sustainably satisfying than pure outcome definitions. The professional who defines success only in terms of the outcomes to be achieved is the professional who has defined their satisfaction as permanently future-oriented; the one who includes process quality in the success definition is the one who can access satisfaction in the present.

The intrinsic-extrinsic mix: the success definitions with the most robust research support include a mix of intrinsic criteria (the criteria that reflect the professional's own values — the quality of the work, the quality of the relationships, the contribution to the values-relevant purposes) and extrinsic criteria (the criteria that reflect external standards — the income, the recognition, the career advancement). The exclusively extrinsic success definition is the most vulnerable to the arrival fallacy; the exclusively intrinsic definition may be insufficiently connected to the material conditions that professional life requires.

## Chapter 4: The Values-Grounded Success Design

The specific process of designing a personal success definition begins with the values clarity that Book 91 supports and extends to the specific, operationalizable criteria that the values imply.

The values-to-criteria translation: the practical work of translating values into success criteria requires the specific step of identifying what a life that fully expressed the relevant value would look like in operational terms. The professional who values "meaningful contribution" needs to identify what meaningful contribution looks like in the specific context of their professional capabilities and opportunities: which specific contributions, to whom, through what mechanisms. The translation from abstract value to specific success criterion is the step that the values clarification work most often omits and that the personal success design most requires.

Elena's design: Elena's values archaeology (Book 91) had identified honesty and respect in the technology-human relationship as her deepest professional values. The translation into success criteria produced specific criteria that the general "success" of the exited company had not included: the number of people whose technology experience had been genuinely improved by her work (not the number of users acquired); the degree to which the products she built treated users as intelligent adults capable of informed choice; and the quality of the team culture she built, measured by the degree to which the people who worked for her had grown and flourished in the experience. These criteria provided a success definition that the exit's financial success had only partially addressed — and that clarified what the "now what?" required in specific, actionable terms.

## Chapter 5: The Social Comparison Problem

The most significant practical obstacle to the maintenance of a personally designed success definition is the social comparison pressure that the professional environment perpetually generates.

The reference group effect: the research on social comparison and wellbeing (referenced in Books 87 and 90) finds that the impact of income, achievement, and status on wellbeing is substantially mediated by the comparison reference group — the specific set of peers against whom the professional implicitly measures their position. The professional who has designed a personal success definition that includes non-financial criteria but who is surrounded by a peer group that exclusively uses financial criteria as the success measure will experience continuous social comparison pressure toward the financial standard, even when their personal definition explicitly deemphasizes it.

The comparison management: the management of the social comparison pressure requires the specific practices that the Book 90 discussion describes: the deliberate diversification of the comparison reference group to include peers who define success on terms consistent with the professional's personal definition; the cultivation of the internal success assessment that reduces the dependency on external comparison; and the deliberate engagement with the research on what actually produces wellbeing — which consistently shows that the dimensions the professional's personal success definition emphasizes (meaning, relationships, health, growth) are the dimensions with the strongest wellbeing research support.

**Practical Exercise — The Personal Success Definition:** Write down, in detail, your current success definition — the specific criteria you would use to evaluate whether your professional life has been a success. Then ask, for each criterion:

1. Did I choose this criterion, or did I inherit it?
2. Does this criterion reflect what I actually value, or what I've been told to value?
3. If this criterion is met, will I genuinely be satisfied, or will I immediately reset to a higher bar? Based on this analysis, write a revised success definition that is explicitly your own, includes criteria across the life domains you value, and includes both process and outcome criteria.

## Chapter 6: Success at Different Career Stages

The personal success definition that serves the professional at thirty-five may not be the definition that serves them at fifty-five. The research on adult development and career stage suggests that the success definition requires deliberate revision as the career and the person's life stage evolves.

The early career success definition: the success definition most appropriate for the early career typically includes a heavy emphasis on competence development, role exploration, and the building of the professional foundation — the specific skills, networks, and professional identities that the later career's more sophisticated success definitions will require. The early career success definition that exclusively emphasizes the financial and status dimensions may optimize for the dimensions least important at this stage while neglecting the developmental investments that the later career's success requires.

The mid-career success definition revision: the mid-career is the period in which the success definition most commonly requires the most significant revision — because the achievements that the early career definition targeted have been reached, and the questions of what they were for and what the second half of the career should be directed toward have become unavoidable. The deliberate, explicit revision of the success definition at the mid-career inflection point is one of the most important professional development activities available at this stage.

## Chapter 7: Success and Comparison to Past Self

The most psychologically healthy comparison reference for the personal success definition is not the peer group (which drives social comparison) or the cultural template (which drives the inherited definition) but the professional's own past self — the degree to which the current professional life represents growth, learning, and increasing alignment with the values that the professional has identified as fundamental.

The temporal comparison research: the research on temporal self-comparison (comparing current self to past self rather than to others) finds consistent wellbeing advantages over social comparison: the temporal comparison reference provides a growth-oriented rather than competition-oriented frame, allows the assessment of progress toward individually relevant goals rather than socially defined ones, and is substantially more resistant to the zero-sum dynamics that social comparison in competitive professional environments produces.

## Chapter 8: The Success That Serves Others

The research on prosocial success definitions — the definitions that include the contribution to others as a primary success criterion — finds consistent advantages for both individual wellbeing and professional performance.

The contribution criterion: the professional whose success definition includes a specific contribution criterion — the explicit identification of the specific people or purposes whose wellbeing the professional's work is meant to serve — has a success standard that is both more durable (because contribution, unlike the achievement of specific milestones, is ongoing) and more motivationally resilient (because the awareness of beneficiaries is a powerful performance motivator, as Adam Grant's research consistently documents).

Marcus's contribution criterion: Marcus's revised success definition included, as its primary criterion, the development of the people who worked with him — the specific, assessable question of whether the people who had spent significant career time under his leadership had grown, developed, and been equipped for the professional challenges ahead of them. This criterion was both more intrinsically motivating and more genuinely satisfying than the organizational metrics that the inherited success template had provided.

## Chapter 9: Living With the Revised Definition

The professional who has revised their success definition faces the specific challenge of maintaining the new definition in the face of the organizational and cultural pressure toward the inherited one.

The internal maintenance: the internal maintenance of the revised success definition requires the specific practices that the mindfulness and self-awareness literature describes: the regular review of actual choices against the stated criteria, the deliberate disengagement from the social comparison dynamics that pull toward the inherited definition, and the cultivation of the peer community that supports and reinforces the alternative definition.

The organizational tension: the professional whose personal success definition diverges significantly from the organizational success criteria faces a specific organizational navigation challenge — the management of the gap between the organizational measures of success (the metrics, the advancement criteria, the performance reviews) and the personal success criteria that the revised definition emphasizes. This tension is real and requires deliberate management; the complete alignment between personal and organizational success definitions is a fortunate condition that most professionals do not continuously enjoy.

---

## Chapter 10: The Definition Worth Pursuing

The success definition worth pursuing is the one the professional would choose if they had full information about what produces genuine, sustained flourishing, full knowledge of their own values and temperament, and genuine freedom from the social comparison and cultural pressure that the inherited definition creates.

This definition is not the one that looks best in the annual performance review or the LinkedIn update. It is the one that, when the professional assesses their life at its end, they would recognize as genuinely having been their own.

Build this definition. Pursue it consistently. Revise it as you learn more about yourself and what you actually need.

The success worth having is the one you defined.

Define it.
