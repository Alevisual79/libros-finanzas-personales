## Hydration, Water Quality, and What Science Knows

### Prologue
Water is the medium in which virtually all biological chemistry occurs. Adequate hydration is prerequisite to normal cognition, physical performance, thermoregulation, and organ function. And yet hydration is simultaneously one of the most evidence-based topics in health and one of the most confused by marketing, mythology, and genuine complexity around water quality.

This book covers both the science of hydration — how much, when, and what quality of water — and the more complex question of what contaminants are in tap water, what water filtration achieves, and what the evidence says about water choices.

---

## Chapter 1: The Research

**Hydration and cognitive function:**

Even mild dehydration (1-2% body water loss) impairs:
- Attention and concentration
- Short-term memory
- Mood (increased fatigue, tension, anxiety)
- Physical performance (particularly endurance)

Adequate hydration is the simplest, lowest-cost cognitive performance intervention available.

**Hydration and kidney health:**

Adequate fluid intake:
- Reduces kidney stone formation risk (most kidney stones form in concentrated urine)
- Reduces UTI risk (dilution and flushing)
- May reduce bladder cancer risk (dilution of carcinogen contact time with bladder epithelium)

## Chapter 2: The Mechanism

**Water in the body:**

Total body water: approximately 60% of body weight in men; 50-55% in women (less adipose)
- Intracellular fluid: ~66% of total body water
- Extracellular fluid: ~33% (interstitial fluid + plasma)

Functions of water:
- Transport medium (blood, lymph)
- Chemical reaction medium
- Thermoregulation (sweat evaporation)
- Shock absorption (cerebrospinal fluid, joint fluid)
- Waste excretion (urine, sweat)

**Water regulation:**

The body tightly regulates fluid balance through:
- *Thirst:* Stimulated by hypothalamic osmoreceptors when plasma osmolality rises ~1-2%; reliable in healthy adults (less reliable in elderly, during exercise, in hot environments)
- *Antidiuretic hormone (ADH/vasopressin):* Regulates renal water reabsorption in response to osmolality and volume
- *Aldosterone:* Regulates sodium and water balance

Healthy individuals do not need to meticulously count water intake — the thirst mechanism provides adequate guidance in most circumstances.

## Chapter 3: Key Concepts

**How much water to drink:**

The commonly cited "8×8" rule (eight 8-oz glasses per day) has no specific evidence base.

Current scientific recommendations:
- *Total water intake (including from food, ~20% of total):* 3.7 liters (125 oz) for adult men; 2.7 liters (91 oz) for adult women
- *From beverages:* approximately 3 liters/day for men; 2.2 liters/day for women — but highly variable based on activity, climate, diet

*Practical guidance:*
- Drink to thirst (in healthy adults, this is adequate)
- Urine should be pale yellow (darker = inadequate; completely clear = overhydration)
- Increase intake significantly with exercise, heat, and illness
- Athletes in prolonged endurance events: replace sodium as well as water (prevents hyponatremia — dangerous overhydration of cells)

**Do other beverages count?**

Yes — all beverages (including coffee, tea, juice, milk) contribute to hydration. Caffeinated beverages have a mild diuretic effect but the net hydration contribution remains positive.

## Chapter 4: Signs and Symptoms

**Dehydration:**

Mild (1-2% body weight loss): thirst, dry mouth, slightly darker urine, mild fatigue
Moderate (3-5%): headache, reduced concentration, irritability, reduced physical performance, dry skin
Severe (>5%): rapid heart rate, confusion, dizziness, sunken eyes — medical emergency

*Who is at elevated dehydration risk:*
- Older adults (reduced thirst sensitivity)
- Athletes (sweat losses)
- People in hot environments (occupational or climate)
- People with fever, vomiting, or diarrhea
- Young children (high surface area to volume ratio)

**Overhydration (hyponatremia):**

Excessive water intake without sodium replacement can cause hyponatremia (low serum sodium):
- Dangerous, occasionally fatal — primarily seen in marathon runners drinking excessive plain water
- Symptoms: nausea, headache, confusion, seizure
- Treatment: sodium replacement

## Chapter 5: Practical Interventions

**Water quality in the United States:**

The EPA regulates tap water quality under the Safe Drinking Water Act (SDWA):
- Sets Maximum Contaminant Levels (MCLs) for 90+ contaminants
- Public water systems must test regularly and provide annual Consumer Confidence Reports (CCRs)

*Known concerns in US tap water:*

*Lead:* Lead pipes (service lines) and lead solder in older homes can leach lead into tap water. Lead has no safe level — particularly dangerous for children and pregnant women. Lead is most concentrated in the first draw of water from the tap after sitting overnight. Solution: run the tap 30-60 seconds before drinking from pipes that may have lead; use NSF/ANSI 53 certified filter; request lead testing.

*PFAS ("forever chemicals"):* Per- and polyfluoroalkyl substances (PFAS) — used in non-stick cookware, food packaging, firefighting foam — are found in water supplies of significant portions of the US population. EPA set enforceable limits for 6 PFAS in 2024 at very low levels (4 parts per trillion for PFOA/PFOS). Associated with cancer, thyroid disruption, immune suppression. Granular activated carbon (GAC) and reverse osmosis are effective at removing PFAS.

*Chlorine and disinfection byproducts (DBPs):* Chlorine used in water treatment reacts with organic matter to produce trihalomethanes (THMs) and haloacetic acids — classified as possible or probable carcinogens. Levels in treated water are regulated; filtration or letting water sit uncovered (volatilizes chlorine) reduces levels.

*Nitrates:* From agricultural runoff; highest concern near farming areas; associated with infant methemoglobinemia ("blue baby syndrome") and possible cancer risk.

## Chapter 6: Environmental Factors

**Checking your local water quality:**

*Consumer Confidence Report (CCR):* Your water utility must provide an annual report of water testing results. Find it on your utility's website or at epa.gov/ccr.

*EWG Tap Water Database:* Environmental Working Group's database provides testing data and analysis for utilities nationwide, including contaminants detected (including those below legal limits but above health guidelines).

*Private well water:* Not regulated by EPA; owners responsible for own testing. Recommend testing annually for bacteria, nitrates, and as appropriate for local geology (arsenic, radon) and land use (pesticides, PFAS).

**Bottled water:**

Bottled water is regulated by the FDA (as a food product) rather than EPA:
- Generally comparable safety to municipal water; not categorically safer
- Some brands are simply municipal tap water (filtered or not)
- Environmental cost: significant plastic waste and carbon footprint
- Not appropriate as a default alternative to tap water in most US municipalities

## Chapter 7: Behavioral Approaches

**Water filtration:**

For concerned consumers, filtration offers additional protection. Types and effectiveness:

*Activated carbon (pitcher or faucet filters):*
- Removes: chlorine, chlorine taste/odor, some VOCs, some DBPs, some PFAS (partial)
- Does NOT remove: fluoride, nitrates, heavy metals, most bacteria
- NSF/ANSI 42 certification: taste/odor; NSF/ANSI 53: health effects contaminants (lead, cysts)

*Reverse osmosis (under-sink):*
- Removes: PFAS, lead, nitrates, heavy metals, fluoride, most dissolved solids
- Most effective at contaminant removal
- Wastes 3-5 gallons per gallon produced; removes beneficial minerals
- NSF/ANSI 58 certification

*Pitcher filters:* Convenient and affordable; limited capacity
*Whole-house carbon filters:* Comprehensive protection for all household water; expensive installation

**Elena's water approach:**

Elena, aware of her city's older infrastructure, had her apartment's tap water tested through a certified lab. Results: detectable lead (2 ppb — below EPA action level of 15 ppb, but above EWG health guideline). She installed an NSF/ANSI 53-certified faucet filter (specifically certified for lead reduction). She also subscribed to her utility's CCR mailing list.

## Chapter 8: Cognitive Approaches

**Water quality anxiety:**

Media coverage of water quality crises has created significant anxiety about drinking water that is sometimes proportionate to actual risk and sometimes not. Framework for calibration:
- Read your CCR — what are the actual measured levels vs. standards?
- Use EWG database — how do detected levels compare to health-based guidelines vs. legal limits?
- Consult NSF for certified filter recommendations for specific contaminants of concern
- Act proportionately — filtration for concerns; testing if uncertain; not expensive "special" water for unsubstantiated claims

**Alkaline water and other claims:**

The market for "premium" water (alkaline water, hydrogen water, spring water) makes numerous health claims with limited evidence:
- *Alkaline water:* Blood pH is tightly regulated regardless of water pH; no evidence of health benefit in healthy adults
- *Hydrogen water:* Limited small studies; insufficient evidence for claimed benefits
- The only proven water health attribute: adequate consumption of clean water

## Chapter 9: When to Seek Help

**When to get professional assessment:**

- *Lead:* If you have older plumbing, a child under 6, or are pregnant: get tap water tested; contact local health department for testing resources (often free or low-cost)
- *Private well:* Annual testing recommended; EPA has guidance on what to test for based on local geology and land use
- *Symptoms of concern:* GI illness clustering in household users of tap water → test for bacteria; report to health department

**Water quality and hydration protocol:**

☐ Increase intake with heat and exercise
☐ All beverages count (including coffee and tea)

*Know your water:*
☐ Read your annual Consumer Confidence Report (from utility website)
☐ Check EWG Tap Water Database for your zip code
☐ If on a private well: test annually

*Act based on findings:*
☐ Lead concern: flush tap before drinking; install NSF/ANSI 53 certified filter
☐ PFAS concern: install NSF/ANSI 58 reverse osmosis or certified pitcher/faucet filter
☐ Chlorine taste: activated carbon pitcher filter; or let water sit uncovered 30 minutes

## Epilogue

Water is not glamorous — but it is fundamental. Adequate hydration is the simplest, most accessible health practice available; clean drinking water is a right that most Americans have access to but many take for granted. Taking 20 minutes to read your water utility's annual report and understand what you're drinking is a meaningful act of health stewardship. And drinking enough of whatever clean water is available to you remains the most evidence-based thing you can do for daily physical and cognitive function.
