### Prologue
One of the most persistent myths in our culture is that sexuality ends with middle age. The research emphatically does not support this view. Most adults over 60, 70, and 80 remain sexually active and describe sexual intimacy as important to their quality of life and relationships. What changes with age — and what medicine can address — is the nature of age-related physiological changes that affect sexual function.

Sexuality in aging also encompasses more than physical function. Intimacy, connection, touch, and relational closeness matter across the entire lifespan — and may deepen rather than diminish with age, as the urgency and performance orientation of younger sexual life gives way to a more intentional, connected approach.

## Chapter 1: The Research

**Sexuality and aging — the data:**

The Global Study of Sexual Attitudes and Behaviors (GSSAB) and the National Social Life, Health, and Aging Project (NSHAP) provide substantial data on sexual activity in older adults:
- Approximately 50-80% of adults 60-70 are sexually active
- Approximately 40-65% of adults 70-80 remain sexually active
- Up to 25-35% of adults 80+ report sexual activity
- Older adults consistently report that sexual activity remains important to quality of life
- The primary predictors of sexual activity in older adults: overall health, partner availability, and partner health

The data challenge the cultural narrative that sexuality is the province of the young.

**Why sexual activity declines with age:**

The most common reasons for reduced sexual activity in older adults:
1. Partner unavailability (widowhood, partner illness)
2. Partner health problems
3. Physiological changes in sexual function (addressed in this book)
4. Own health problems
5. Reduced desire (hormonal and other factors)

Notably, social factors (partner availability, relationship quality) are more predictive of sexual activity than physiological factors in most studies.

## Chapter 2: The Mechanism

**Age-related physiological changes in women:**

*Post-menopause:*
- Estrogen decline → vaginal atrophy: reduced lubrication, reduced elasticity, thinning of vaginal epithelium → dyspareunia (painful intercourse), increased susceptibility to UTIs
- Genitourinary Syndrome of Menopause (GSM): encompasses vaginal dryness, pain, and urinary symptoms — often the primary barrier to sexual comfort in older women
- Longer time to arousal (normal)
- Orgasm may require more direct stimulation (normal)
- Orgasmic intensity may change (not uniformly reduced — can be maintained or enhanced)

**Age-related physiological changes in men:**

- Longer time to achieve erection (normal aging)
- Less firm erections (vasculature)
- Longer refractory period
- Reduced ejaculatory force and volume
- Testosterone decline (modest contribution to libido; significant only if clinical hypogonadism develops)
- Higher prevalence of ED (described in Book 78)

**Neither partner's changes preclude satisfying sexual activity:**

This is the key clinical point — these are physiological changes that alter the nature and timing of sexual response, not changes that eliminate sexual capacity. Adapting to these changes, with medical treatment when appropriate and intentional communication, allows satisfying sexual activity to continue indefinitely.

## Chapter 3: Key Concepts

**Genitourinary Syndrome of Menopause (GSM):**

GSM (previously called "vaginal atrophy" or "atrophic vaginitis") is the most common and most undertreated sexual health condition in post-menopausal women:
- Affects approximately 50-60% of post-menopausal women
- Unlike vasomotor symptoms (hot flashes), GSM does NOT improve over time — it worsens without treatment
- Treatment is highly effective
- Most affected women don't mention it to their physicians; most physicians don't screen for it

*Treatment options:*
- *Local vaginal estrogen:* Most effective treatment; cream, tablet, ring, or suppository; minimal systemic absorption; safe for most women (including most breast cancer survivors — discuss with oncologist)
- *Ospemifene (Osphena):* Oral selective estrogen receptor modulator; FDA-approved for dyspareunia due to GSM
- *Prasterone (Intrarosa):* Vaginal DHEA; converts to estrogen and testosterone locally; FDA-approved for dyspareunia
- *Moisturizers and lubricants:* Non-hormonal management; used regularly (moisturizers) and with sexual activity (lubricants)

**The lubricant gap:**

Many older adults don't use lubricants during sexual activity — yet lubricants dramatically improve comfort and sexual satisfaction for adults with any degree of vaginal dryness:
- *Water-based lubricants:* Compatible with condoms and sex toys; need frequent reapplication
- *Silicone-based lubricants:* Longer-lasting; not compatible with silicone sex toys
- *Oil-based lubricants:* Long-lasting; not compatible with latex condoms

Regular vaginal moisturizers (Replens, K-Y Liquibeads) provide baseline hydration, distinct from lubricants for activity.

## Chapter 4: Signs and Symptoms

**Warning signs that sexual changes need evaluation:**

*In women:*
- Post-menopausal or post-coital bleeding (always warrants evaluation — endometrial cancer screening)
- Dyspareunia that is new, severe, or changing (pelvic pathology evaluation)
- Sudden loss of desire (hormonal, depression, medication evaluation)
- Vaginal dryness interfering significantly with sexual activity or comfort (GSM evaluation and treatment)

*In men:*
- Erectile dysfunction (cardiovascular evaluation — see Book 78)
- Penile pain (Peyronie's disease evaluation — scar tissue causing painful erections and curvature)
- Hematospermia (blood in semen — usually benign; warrants evaluation to rule out prostate or urethral pathology)
- Sudden loss of desire in the context of other low testosterone symptoms

## Chapter 5: Practical Interventions

**Adapting sexual activity to changing physiology:**

Adapting to age-related sexual changes often requires conscious adjustment:
- More time for arousal (not a dysfunction — plan for it)
- More direct stimulation
- Different positions that are more comfortable with joint pain or mobility limitations (pillows, wedges)
- Lubricants routinely (especially for women with any vaginal dryness)
- New activities or focus (pleasure and intimacy beyond performance and penetration)

**STIs in older adults:**

STI rates in adults over 55 have risen significantly — partly from dating app-facilitated new partnerships after divorce or widowhood, partly from the absence of STI education that targeted this age group. STI prevention:
- Condoms prevent STI transmission at any age (they are not only for pregnancy prevention)
- STI testing for any new sexual partner
- HIV risk is real in adults over 50 who have new partners

**Isabel's experience:**

Isabel, at 52, began experiencing vaginal dryness and mild dyspareunia approximately 1 year after menopause. She attributed it to "getting older" and stopped initiating sexual activity with her new partner (whom she had met in a support group 6 months prior).

At her menopause follow-up, her physician screened for GSM — Isabel had not mentioned the issue. Treatment: low-dose vaginal estradiol tablet + lubricant. At 3 months: dyspareunia resolved; she reengaged in her relationship. Her physician noted that "the women who are most bothered by GSM are often the ones who say nothing about it."

## Chapter 6: Environmental Factors

**Partner health and availability:**

The most significant environmental factor for sexuality in older adults is partner health and availability:
- Widowhood (more common in women, given differential life expectancy) is the primary reason for sexual inactivity in older women
- Partner illness (dementia, mobility limitation, serious illness) significantly affects sexual activity and requires sensitive management
- The decision to seek new sexual partnerships after widowhood or divorce carries different considerations at 70 vs. 30 — but is equally legitimate

**Care settings and sexuality:**

Sexuality in residential care settings (nursing homes, assisted living) is often inadequately acknowledged and sometimes actively suppressed — despite the legal rights of residents to sexual expression. This is beginning to be addressed in long-term care policy.

## Chapter 7: Behavioral Approaches

**Expanding the definition of sex:**

A significant behavioral shift that supports sexual wellbeing in older adults: expanding the definition of sexuality beyond intercourse-focused activity:
- Physical intimacy (touch, cuddling, massage, kissing) is valuable independent of intercourse
- Oral sex, manual stimulation, and other activities remain pleasurable and available when intercourse is difficult
- Masturbation continues to maintain genital health and sexual wellbeing throughout life
- Vibrators and other aids can facilitate sexual pleasure when physiological changes reduce responsiveness to manual stimulation alone

This reframing is not a consolation prize — it is a more expansive understanding of sexuality that many older adults report as genuinely better than the more narrowly performance-focused sexuality of youth.

**Communication with a partner:**

The physiological changes of aging sexuality require more explicit communication than younger sexual relationships may have:
- "That position hurts my hip — let's try another"
- "I need more time"
- "I'd like to use some lubricant"
- "I'd like to try ___"

These conversations require comfort with direct sexual communication that many couples have not developed — and that is available to develop at any age.

## Chapter 8: Cognitive Approaches

**Sexual self-concept in aging:**

Sexual self-concept — how we see ourselves as sexual beings — is significantly influenced by cultural messaging that desexualizes older adults. The cognitive work for many older adults is reclaiming sexual identity in the face of cultural invisibilization:
- Rejecting the premise that sexual desire is inappropriate at 65, 70, or 80
- Recognizing that the body's capacity for pleasure does not expire
- Allowing sexual expression to evolve rather than disappear

**The new relationship context:**

Many adults 50+ re-enter sexual relationships after divorce or widowhood — a context with its own psychological dimensions:
- New partner anxiety after years in a single relationship
- Body image concerns about an aged body
- Medical conditions affecting sexual function
- STI knowledge gap (many adults in this cohort did not receive comprehensive STI education)

Sex therapy and individual therapy are appropriate supports for adults navigating new sexual relationships at any age.

## Chapter 9: When to Seek Help

**Sexual medicine:**

Sexual medicine is a subspecialty addressing sexual dysfunction in older adults:
- Female sexual medicine: GSM treatment, sexual pain, orgasm difficulties, desire disorders
- Male sexual medicine: ED management across treatments, Peyronie's disease, hypogonadism
- Couples work: relationship-based sexual concerns

**Dementia and sexual consent:**

An ethically complex area: when one partner in a long-term relationship develops dementia, the sexual consent capacity of that partner requires assessment. This is handled differently by different care settings and jurisdictions. No universal standard exists; the guiding principle is the wellbeing and rights of the person with dementia, including their right to sexual expression while their capacity to consent exists.

## Chapter 10: Your Protocol

**Maintaining sexuality through the decades:**

*For women post-menopause:*
☐ Discuss GSM symptoms with your physician (vaginal dryness, discomfort, dyspareunia)
☐ Consider vaginal estrogen (local treatment, low risk)
☐ Use lubricant routinely during sexual activity
☐ STI prevention with new partners (condoms; STI testing)

*For men:*
☐ Evaluate ED — it's treatable and tells you something about cardiovascular health
☐ Testosterone evaluation if significant desire/energy changes
☐ More time, more stimulation, realistic expectations about refractory period

*For all older adults:*
☐ Expand your definition of sexual activity to include non-penetrative intimacy
☐ STI awareness and prevention with new partners
☐ Maintain communication with your partner about changing needs
☐ Discuss sexual health concerns with your physician — they likely won't ask

## Epilogue

Sexuality does not end at a particular birthday, menopause, or prostatectomy. It evolves. The adults who maintain the richest sexual and intimate lives into old age are those who adapt to changes rather than surrendering to them — who treat sexual health with the same evidence-based attention as cardiovascular health or bone density — and who communicate openly with partners and healthcare providers rather than suffering silently.

The human capacity for connection, pleasure, and intimacy is more durable than our culture's narrow version of sexuality suggests. This series has been an invitation to claim it, at whatever age and in whatever form it takes.
