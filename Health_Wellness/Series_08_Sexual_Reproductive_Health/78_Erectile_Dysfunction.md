# Erectile Dysfunction
## Understanding, Treating, and What It Tells You About Your Health

**Series 8: Sexual and Reproductive Health — Book 8 of 10**

---

## Prologue

Erectile dysfunction (ED) affects an estimated 30 million American men. It is among the most common sexual health conditions across all ages, with a prevalence that rises dramatically from approximately 5% in men under 40 to 15-25% in men in their 60s and 70% in their 70s. Despite this prevalence, it is underdiagnosed and undertreated — primarily because men don't discuss it with their physicians, and physicians don't routinely ask.

The silence does harm in two directions: men suffer from a treatable condition unnecessarily, and a significant diagnostic opportunity is missed. Erectile dysfunction is often the first clinical sign of cardiovascular disease — preceding a cardiac event by 2-5 years. Treating ED without investigating the underlying cause is like treating chest pain without evaluating the heart.

## Chapter 1: The Research

**ED and cardiovascular disease:**

The connection between ED and cardiovascular disease is causal, not coincidental:
- Both conditions share the same pathophysiology: endothelial dysfunction → impaired vasodilation
- The penile arteries are smaller in diameter than coronary arteries — endothelial damage manifests earlier in smaller vessels
- ED typically precedes cardiovascular events by 2-5 years; in younger men (under 50), ED is a stronger cardiovascular predictor than traditional risk factors
- Men with ED have approximately 2× the cardiovascular event rate of men without ED, independent of traditional risk factors

**Treatment evidence:**

PDE5 inhibitors (sildenafil, tadalafil, vardenafil, avanafil) are among the most extensively studied medications in medical history — billions of patient-doses of data. Efficacy rates in the range of 60-80% depending on ED severity and etiology. Safe for most men; important drug interactions and contraindications (see Chapter 5).

## Chapter 2: The Mechanism

**Physiology of erection:**

Erection is a neurovascular event:
1. Sexual stimulation (psychological or physical) → parasympathetic activation
2. Nitric oxide (NO) release from endothelium and nerve terminals → cGMP production → smooth muscle relaxation in corpus cavernosum
3. Arterial dilation → blood fills the corpora cavernosa → compression of venous outflow → maintained erection
4. PDE5 enzyme breaks down cGMP → detumescence

**Why ED occurs:**

*Vascular ED (most common):*
Arteriosclerotic disease and endothelial dysfunction reduce arterial dilation capacity. Risk factors: hypertension, diabetes, hyperlipidemia, smoking, obesity, metabolic syndrome.

*Neurogenic ED:*
Nerve damage disrupts the signaling pathway. Causes: diabetes (peripheral neuropathy), multiple sclerosis, spinal cord injury, pelvic surgery (radical prostatectomy, colectomy)

*Hormonal ED:*
Low testosterone reduces libido and can impair erectile function; hypothyroidism; rarely hyperprolactinemia.

*Psychogenic ED:*
Performance anxiety, depression, relationship conflict, stress — activate the sympathetic nervous system, which directly inhibits erections. Most common in younger men.

*Medication-induced ED:*
- Antihypertensives (beta-blockers, thiazide diuretics — less with newer agents)
- Antidepressants (SSRIs, particularly sertraline and fluoxetine)
- Anti-androgens (treatment for BPH, prostate cancer)
- Opioids
- Recreational drugs (alcohol, tobacco, cocaine acutely, marijuana)

## Chapter 3: Key Concepts

**The SHIM (Sexual Health Inventory for Men) / IIEF-5:**

A 5-question validated questionnaire that assesses ED severity:
- Score 22-25: No ED
- Score 17-21: Mild
- Score 12-16: Mild-moderate
- Score 8-11: Moderate
- Score 5-7: Severe

Useful for establishing severity, monitoring treatment response, and facilitating clinical conversation.

**Age and ED:**

Age-related ED reflects:
- Cumulative endothelial damage from cardiovascular risk factors
- Declining testosterone (modest contribution)
- Increased medication burden
- Reduced psychogenic/central arousal pathway efficiency

Distinguishing age-related ED from ED due to a specific, treatable cause guides management.

## Chapter 4: Signs and Symptoms

**What counts as ED:**

ED is defined as the persistent inability to achieve or maintain an erection sufficient for satisfactory sexual performance. Key components:
- *Persistent:* Occasional episodes (particularly in the context of stress, alcohol, or fatigue) are normal for most men
- *Sufficient for sexual activity:* The functional threshold, not an absolute physiological standard

**Differentiating psychogenic from organic ED:**

*Features suggesting psychogenic:*
- Sudden onset, often situation-specific (with partner but not alone, or with new partner)
- Normal morning erections
- Younger age
- Obvious psychological precipitant (relationship conflict, performance anxiety, depression, stress)
- Erection achieved with masturbation but not partnered sex

*Features suggesting organic (vascular/neurogenic):*
- Gradual onset, progressive worsening
- Loss of morning erections
- Cardiovascular risk factors present
- No obvious psychological cause
- Impaired erection in all contexts

In reality, most ED has both organic and psychogenic components — organic impairment creates performance anxiety which then amplifies the functional impairment.

## Chapter 5: Practical Interventions

**PDE5 inhibitors:**

First-line pharmacological treatment:

*Sildenafil (Viagra):* 50-100mg; taken 30-60 min before activity; effect lasts 4-6 hours; most well-studied

*Tadalafil (Cialis):* 10-20mg as-needed or 5mg daily; effect lasts up to 36 hours; daily dosing allows spontaneity and may also treat BPH symptoms

*Vardenafil, avanafil:* Similar to sildenafil with slightly different profiles

*Key interaction — absolute contraindication:* Nitrates (nitroglycerin, isosorbide mononitrate/dinitrate — used for chest pain). PDE5 inhibitors + nitrates can cause severe, potentially fatal hypotension.

*Other cautions:* Alpha-blockers (BP medication) — can cause orthostatic hypotension; significant caution. Recent cardiovascular event — discuss with cardiologist before use.

**Lifestyle modifications:**

Multiple lifestyle interventions improve erectile function:
- *Exercise:* Regular aerobic exercise improves erectile function, independent of weight loss; exercise + PDE5 inhibitor combinations produce better outcomes than medication alone
- *Weight loss:* Obesity significantly impairs erectile function; weight loss in obese men with ED substantially improves function
- *Smoking cessation:* Direct endothelial damage; cessation improves erectile function
- *Alcohol reduction:* Heavy drinking acutely and chronically impairs erectile function
- *Mediterranean diet:* Associated with lower ED prevalence in observational studies

A Massachusetts Male Aging Study analysis found that men with lower risk lifestyles had ~50% lower ED prevalence at follow-up.

## Chapter 6: Environmental Factors

**Relationship environment:**

ED often exists within a relationship context that shapes its impact and treatment:
- Partner response significantly affects the psychological burden of ED — a partner who responds with patience vs. one who responds with frustration or blame dramatically changes the experience
- Couple-focused treatment (including partners in evaluation and treatment planning) produces better outcomes
- Sexual communication skills — which are rarely taught — are particularly important in navigating ED within relationships

**Pornography and ED:**

"Porn-induced ED" — erectile dysfunction with partners but normal erectile function with pornography — is described by clinicians and researchers. Proposed mechanism: pornography overstimulates the dopamine reward system, creating tolerance and reducing responsiveness to "real" sexual stimulation. Evidence is debated and mixed; behavioral approaches (reducing pornography use) are recommended for men who notice this pattern.

## Chapter 7: Behavioral Approaches

**Sensate focus for psychogenic ED:**

Sensate focus (Masters and Johnson's approach) is first-line behavioral treatment for psychogenic ED:
- Removes performance pressure by taking intercourse "off the table" initially
- Progressively reintroduces non-genital, then genital, then full sexual activity
- Allows positive sexual experiences without the performance anxiety that maintains ED
- Typically 6-12 sessions with a sex therapist; highly effective for psychogenic ED

**The performance anxiety cycle:**

Performance anxiety is the most common psychological factor in ED, particularly in younger men. The cycle:
1. Single failure episode (often from stress, alcohol, or fatigue)
2. Performance anxiety in subsequent encounters
3. Sympathetic activation inhibits erection
4. Failure confirms fear
5. Avoidance or increased anxiety in future encounters

Breaking the cycle requires reducing the stakes of individual encounters and building confidence through positive experiences — often with medical assistance (PDE5 inhibitors) in the short term, with behavioral work addressing the underlying anxiety.

## Chapter 8: Cognitive Approaches

**Masculinity and ED:**

ED is frequently experienced as a threat to masculine identity. This psychological burden — the equation of erectile function with masculinity, sexual worth, or virility — significantly amplifies the distress beyond what the functional impairment would otherwise cause.

Cognitive reframes:
- Erectile function is one component of sexual experience, not its entirety
- ED is a medical condition, not a character defect
- Partner satisfaction with sexual interaction is not primarily dependent on erection quality in most relationships
- Seeking treatment is the competent, self-caring response — not a further evidence of inadequacy

**When medication isn't enough:**

PDE5 inhibitors treat the symptom (erection difficulty) but not the cause. For men with significant vascular disease: treat the vascular disease. For men with psychogenic ED: medication may break the cycle, but psychological work addresses the cause. For men with testosterone deficiency: testosterone may need to be optimized. Good evaluation identifies the cause; good treatment addresses it.

## Chapter 9: When to Seek Help

**Evaluation workup:**

When ED is reported to a physician, the evaluation should include:
- History: onset, severity, morning erections, situational vs. global, medications, risk factors, relationship context
- IIEF-5 score
- Physical exam: BP, BMI, penile and testicular examination, digital rectal exam if indicated
- Blood work: fasting glucose or HbA1c, lipid panel, testosterone (morning), PSA if indicated, thyroid if indicated
- Cardiovascular risk stratification (the Princeton III Consensus guidelines classify men into low, intermediate, and high cardiovascular risk for sexual activity)

**Second-line and third-line treatments:**

For ED not responding to PDE5 inhibitors:
- *Intraurethral alprostadil (MUSE):* Pellet inserted into urethra; less popular due to side effects (penile pain, hypotension)
- *Intracavernosal injection (ICI):* Direct injection of vasoactive agents (alprostadil, papaverine, phentolamine); highly effective; requires self-injection technique training; used for neurogenic ED and PDE5 inhibitor non-responders
- *Vacuum Erectile Device (VED):* Non-pharmacological; creates vacuum to draw blood into penis; effective, particularly post-prostatectomy
- *Penile prosthesis (penile implant):* Surgical; inflatable or malleable rods; very high satisfaction rates in appropriately selected patients; last resort for men who have failed all other treatments; irreversible (destroys remaining erectile tissue)

## Chapter 10: Your Protocol

**ED evaluation and management:**

*Step 1: Have the conversation*
With your physician or urologist: "I've been having difficulty with erections. Can we evaluate this?"

*Step 2: Lifestyle first*
Optimize cardiovascular risk factors, exercise, weight, smoking cessation, alcohol — these improve ED and should precede or accompany pharmacological treatment

*Step 3: Evaluate for underlying cause*
Blood work, BP assessment, medication review

*Step 4: Consider PDE5 inhibitor trial*
With appropriate contraindication check; discuss with prescriber

*Step 5: Behavioral component*
If psychogenic factors present (performance anxiety, depression, relationship conflict): sex therapy or couples therapy alongside medical treatment

*Step 6: If PDE5 inhibitor fails*
Urological referral for second-line evaluation

---

## Epilogue

Erectile dysfunction is both a common sexual health condition and a window into cardiovascular health. Men who address it — with their physician, with lifestyle changes, and with available treatments — discover that it is far more manageable than the silence surrounding it suggests. And they sometimes discover a cardiovascular risk profile that, addressed early, prevents a heart attack they didn't know was coming. The conversation worth having turns out to be worth more than one thing.
