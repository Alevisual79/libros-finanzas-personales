### Prologue
Contraception is among the most significant health decisions most sexually active people make — and yet the information most people receive about contraceptive options is incomplete, outdated, or distorted by both cultural bias and clinical inertia. The most effective methods are often underutilized; the least effective methods are often overused because they require no prescription.

This book provides an accurate, evidence-based overview of contraceptive options — not to recommend one method over another, but to provide the information needed for an informed choice that matches individual values, health circumstances, and reproductive goals.

## Chapter 1: The Research

**Contraceptive effectiveness:**

Contraceptive effectiveness is reported two ways:
- *Perfect use:* Used exactly as intended, every time
- *Typical use:* Real-world use, including human error and inconsistency

The gap between perfect and typical use is largest for methods dependent on consistent daily or per-encounter action (pills, condoms) and zero for methods that remove user error (IUDs, implants).

*Effectiveness comparison (typical use, % pregnancy-free per year):*
- No contraception: ~85% conceive in 1 year
- Male condom (typical): 87% effective (13% annual failure rate)
- Combined oral contraceptive pill (typical): 91-93% effective
- Progestin-only pill: 91-93% effective
- Hormonal patch, ring (typical): 91-93% effective
- Injectable (Depo-Provera): 94% typical
- Copper IUD: >99.2%
- Hormonal IUD (Mirena/Kyleena): >99.8%
- Subdermal implant (Nexplanon): >99.9%
- Female sterilization (tubal ligation): >99.5%
- Male sterilization (vasectomy): >99.9%

## Chapter 2: The Mechanism

**Combined hormonal contraceptives (estrogen + progestin):**

Mechanism of action:
1. *Suppression of ovulation* (primary): estrogen and progestin suppress FSH and LH, preventing follicular development and ovulation
2. *Cervical mucus thickening:* Progestin thickens mucus, impairing sperm penetration
3. *Endometrial thinning:* Reduces endometrial receptivity

**Progestin-only methods:**

Includes the "mini-pill," hormonal IUDs, Depo-Provera injection, and Nexplanon implant. Primary mechanisms by method:
- Progestin-only pill: primarily cervical mucus thickening; ovulation suppression occurs in ~40% of cycles
- Hormonal IUD: primarily local endometrial and cervical mucus effects; Mirena may also suppress ovulation
- Nexplanon: primarily ovulation suppression (most reliable)
- Depo-Provera: ovulation suppression

**Copper IUD:**

Non-hormonal; works by copper ion toxicity to sperm (immobilizes and kills sperm); also affects endometrial environment. No hormonal systemic effects. Also the most effective emergency contraception when inserted within 5 days of unprotected sex.

**Barrier methods:**

Physically prevent sperm from reaching egg. Condoms (male and female) are the only contraceptive methods that also prevent STI transmission.

## Chapter 3: Key Concepts

**Long-Acting Reversible Contraceptives (LARCs):**

IUDs and implants are designated as "highly effective LARC" because:
- No user-action error possible (set and forget)
- Immediately reversible upon removal
- Cost-effective (high upfront cost, but low annual cost over the use period)
- Among the most effective reversible methods available

LARCs are recommended as first-line contraception by ACOG for most individuals, including adolescents and those who have not previously been pregnant.

**Emergency contraception:**

*Levonorgestrel pills (Plan B, etc.):*
- Most effective within 72 hours of unprotected sex (reduces pregnancy risk ~89%)
- Available OTC
- Less effective above 165 lbs body weight

*Ulipristal acetate (ella):*
- More effective than levonorgestrel, particularly at 72-120 hours
- Requires prescription
- Works up to 120 hours

*Copper IUD:*
- Most effective EC (>99.9%)
- Works up to 5 days after unprotected sex
- Continues as ongoing contraception

## Chapter 4: Signs and Symptoms

**Side effects and choosing between methods:**

*Combined hormonal contraceptives (pill, patch, ring):*
- Most common: nausea (usually transient), breast tenderness, mood changes, reduced libido
- VTE (blood clot) risk: approximately 3-4× increased vs. non-users (though absolute risk remains low; 3-9 per 10,000 women-years vs. pregnancy risk of 29 per 10,000)
- Contraindications: prior VTE, history of migraines with aura, smoking + age > 35, hypertension (certain thresholds), certain cardiovascular conditions

*Progestin-only:*
- Irregular bleeding (particularly with progestin-only pill and Depo-Provera)
- Depo-Provera: delayed return to fertility (up to 12-18 months after last injection)
- Nexplanon: irregular bleeding is the most common side effect (amenorrhea in ~20%)

*Hormonal IUDs:*
- Insertion discomfort (varies from minimal to significant)
- Irregular spotting in first 3-6 months; 20% of Mirena users develop amenorrhea
- Rare: expulsion (2-5%), perforation (<1%), PID risk in first 20 days (if insertion is in the context of STI)

*Copper IUD:*
- Heavier, more crampy periods (particularly in first 3-6 months)
- No hormonal side effects

## Chapter 5: Practical Interventions

**Choosing a contraceptive method:**

The best contraceptive method is the one that will actually be used consistently. Key decision factors:
- *Effectiveness priority:* If pregnancy prevention is critical (health risk from pregnancy, strong preference) → LARC is most reliable
- *Hormonal concerns:* History of blood clots, migraine with aura, or preference for non-hormonal → copper IUD or barrier methods
- *Menstrual changes:* Desire for lighter/absent periods → hormonal IUD; copper IUD may worsen periods
- *STI protection:* Condoms additionally required (no hormonal method prevents STIs)
- *Return to fertility:* IUD and implant — immediate; Depo-Provera — potentially 12-18 months delayed
- *Convenience:* Implant or IUD for lowest maintenance; pill requires daily compliance

**Elena's contraception:**

Elena, at 33, had been using the combined pill since her mid-20s. She developed migraines with aura at 32 — a contraindication to estrogen-containing contraceptives (increased stroke risk with estrogen in migraine-with-aura patients). She switched to the levonorgestrel IUD (Mirena) — highly effective, hormonal (progestin-only, low systemic absorption), and resulted in lighter periods. She described the switch as "one of the best health decisions I made."

## Chapter 6: Environmental Factors

**Contraceptive access:**

Access to contraception varies significantly by:
- Insurance coverage (ACA requires coverage of FDA-approved contraceptives without cost-sharing; this has changed in some states)
- Geographic access to prescribers
- Income and ability to pay out-of-pocket
- Cultural and religious context

Title X family planning clinics provide contraceptive care regardless of ability to pay — available at planned parenthood clinics and federally qualified health centers.

**Medication interactions with hormonal contraceptives:**

Several medications reduce hormonal contraceptive effectiveness:
- Rifampin (antibiotic): strongly induces CYP3A4; significantly reduces hormonal contraceptive levels
- Certain antiepileptic medications (carbamazepine, phenytoin, phenobarbital): enzyme inducers
- St. John's Wort: CYP3A4 induction
- HIV medications (certain antiretrovirals)

Women on these medications should use additional or alternative contraception.

## Chapter 7: Behavioral Approaches

**Consistency and typical-use failure:**

The gap between perfect-use and typical-use failure rates is almost entirely human behavior:
- Pill: forgetting daily doses is the primary failure cause; phone reminders, taking at a consistent daily time, and having a clear plan for missed doses significantly improves effectiveness
- Condoms: inconsistent use, delayed application, and incorrect use are primary failure sources

**Dual protection:**

For sexually active people at risk of both pregnancy and STIs:
- Hormonal contraception + condoms = dual protection
- Particularly recommended for new relationships before STI status is established

## Chapter 8: Cognitive Approaches

**The contraceptive decision and reproductive autonomy:**

Contraceptive choice is a core reproductive autonomy decision — a values-laden decision that medicine should support without imposing provider preferences. Concerns to be aware of:
- *Provider bias:* Some providers promote specific methods based on their preferences rather than patient values; patients should feel empowered to ask about alternatives
- *The "perfect" method trap:* No method is perfect; trade-offs exist for every option; choosing the method that fits the individual's specific life and values is more important than choosing the theoretically "best" method

**Talking to partners:**

Contraceptive decision-making in relationships involves explicit communication:
- Responsibility is not inherently the person who can become pregnant
- Clear agreement about contraceptive method and what happens in the event of contraceptive failure (pregnancy decision) is part of informed sexual relationship
- Reproductive coercion (deliberately sabotaging contraception) is a form of intimate partner violence

## Chapter 9: When to Seek Help

**Contraceptive counseling:**

All licensed contraceptive methods require (or benefit from) prescriber involvement:
- Primary care physician
- Gynecologist
- Planned Parenthood or family planning clinic
- Nurse practitioner or PA with prescribing authority

The contraceptive consultation should include:
- Review of all options relevant to the patient
- Medical history assessment for contraindications
- Discussion of the patient's specific values and priorities
- Follow-up plan if initial choice has problematic side effects

**When to change methods:**

- Significant mood changes after initiating hormonal contraception: discuss with prescriber; changing formulation, progestin type, or method may help
- Irregular bleeding persisting beyond 3-6 months: evaluation and possible method change
- Any contraceptive failure requiring emergency contraception: review method adherence and consider switch to higher-efficacy option

## Chapter 10: Your Protocol

**Contraceptive decision checklist:**

*My pregnancy prevention priority:* (How important is it that I don't become pregnant right now? This guides how critical high effectiveness is.)

*My STI prevention need:* (Do I need condoms for STI protection in addition to pregnancy prevention?)

*My hormonal preference:* (Do I prefer non-hormonal? Am I comfortable with progestin? Am I comfortable with estrogen?)

*My maintenance preference:* (Daily pill vs. monthly ring vs. every-3-years implant vs. every-5-10 years IUD)

*My menstrual preferences:* (Do I want lighter/absent periods? Or do I not want changes to my cycle?)

Once these five questions are answered, the options that fit are typically narrow — which simplifies the decision.

## Epilogue

Effective contraception is one of the most significant health and life-management tools available. The method that works best is the method that is used reliably, that is medically appropriate, and that fits the individual's values and life circumstances. That determination requires accurate information — which is what this book aimed to provide.
