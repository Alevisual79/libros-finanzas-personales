### Prologue
Sexually transmitted infections (STIs) are among the most common infectious diseases in the world — and among the most stigmatized, which creates significant harm. Stigma delays testing and treatment, reduces partner notification, and prevents people from having accurate conversations with healthcare providers. Meanwhile, STI rates in the United States have risen substantially since 2014 across all major STIs.

This book is about facts, not stigma. STIs are medical conditions with known causes, effective treatments (or management for those without cure), and evidence-based prevention strategies. They can happen to anyone who is sexually active. Having one does not reflect character, worth, or moral standing.

## Chapter 1: The Research

**STI epidemiology:**

CDC 2022 STI surveillance data:
- *Chlamydia:* 1.6 million reported cases (most common reportable STI; vast majority of cases go unreported)
- *Gonorrhea:* 648,000 cases; antibiotic resistance is a growing concern
- *Syphilis:* 207,000 cases; 3,700 congenital syphilis cases (preventable; represents a public health failure)
- *Genital herpes (HSV-2):* Approximately 11-12% of Americans 14-49 have HSV-2; HSV-1 also increasingly causing genital herpes
- *HPV:* Most common STI; ~80% of sexually active people will contract HPV at some point; most infections clear spontaneously

STI rates have increased approximately 50% since 2014 — attributed to reduced condom use, dating app-facilitated partner change, and reduced STI testing during COVID.

## Chapter 2: The Mechanism

**Major STI pathogens:**

*Bacterial STIs (curable with antibiotics):*
- *Chlamydia (Chlamydia trachomatis):* Intracellular bacterium; treated with doxycycline or azithromycin
- *Gonorrhea (Neisseria gonorrhoeae):* Increasing antibiotic resistance; current treatment requires ceftriaxone injection
- *Syphilis (Treponema pallidum):* Complex multi-stage disease; treated with penicillin
- *Bacterial vaginosis (BV) / Trichomoniasis:* BV is polymicrobial dysbiosis; Trichomonas is a protozoan; both treatable

*Viral STIs (manageable, not curable):*
- *HIV:* Retrovirus; antiretroviral therapy makes HIV a manageable chronic condition with near-normal life expectancy; transmission prevention with undetectable viral load (U=U)
- *Herpes simplex virus (HSV-1 and HSV-2):* Latent in sensory ganglia; antivirals (acyclovir, valacyclovir) reduce outbreak frequency and transmission risk
- *HPV:* >100 strains; high-risk strains (16, 18) cause cervical and other cancers; low-risk strains (6, 11) cause genital warts; most cleared by immune system; vaccine prevents the most pathogenic strains
- *Hepatitis B:* Vaccinepreventable; antiviral treatment available for chronic hepatitis B

## Chapter 3: Key Concepts

**STI testing — what, when, and who:**

Testing recommendations by population:

*All sexually active adults:*
- HIV testing at least once (USPSTF recommends screening for adults 15-65)
- Hepatitis B and C testing (once; more if ongoing risk)

*Sexually active women under 25:*
- Annual chlamydia and gonorrhea screening (CDC recommendation)

*MSM (men who have sex with men):*
- Every 3-6 months: HIV, gonorrhea, chlamydia (genital, rectal, pharyngeal), syphilis

*Pregnant women:*
- HIV, syphilis, hepatitis B, chlamydia, gonorrhea, hepatitis C at first prenatal visit

*Anyone with new or multiple partners:*
- Discuss testing frequency with provider based on risk

**The U=U principle:**

"Undetectable = Untransmittable": a person living with HIV who is on antiretroviral therapy (ART) and has achieved an undetectable viral load cannot sexually transmit HIV. This is one of the most important HIV treatment and prevention advances in recent decades.

**PrEP (Pre-Exposure Prophylaxis) for HIV:**

- Tenofovir/emtricitabine (Truvada, Descovy) or cabotegravir injection every 2 months
- Reduces HIV transmission risk by >99% with correct use
- Recommended for HIV-negative individuals at substantial risk (multiple partners, partner with HIV, STI history)
- Covered by most insurance; manufacturer assistance programs available

## Chapter 4: Signs and Symptoms

**Common presentations:**

*Many STIs are asymptomatic — which is why testing is more important than symptom monitoring.*

*Chlamydia:* 70-95% asymptomatic; when symptomatic: discharge, dysuria, pelvic pain in women (sign of PID)

*Gonorrhea:* More symptomatic in men (urethral discharge, dysuria); women often asymptomatic

*Syphilis stages:*
- Primary: painless chancre at infection site (heals spontaneously)
- Secondary: rash (classically on palms and soles), flu-like symptoms, mucous membrane lesions
- Latent: asymptomatic
- Tertiary: cardiovascular and neurological involvement (rare with treatment)

*Herpes:* Primary outbreak: painful blisters/ulcers ± flu-like symptoms; recurrences are typically less severe; many people have asymptomatic or minimally symptomatic infection

*HPV:* Mostly asymptomatic; genital warts (low-risk strains); detected on Pap smear/HPV test (high-risk strains)

**Complications of untreated STIs:**

- Chlamydia and gonorrhea → Pelvic Inflammatory Disease (PID) → chronic pelvic pain, ectopic pregnancy, infertility
- Congenital syphilis: severe fetal outcomes (still-birth, neurological damage, bone/organ abnormalities)
- HPV → cervical, anal, oropharyngeal cancers
- Untreated HIV → AIDS

## Chapter 5: Practical Interventions

**Prevention:**

*Condoms:*
- Highly effective against HIV, gonorrhea, chlamydia, trichomoniasis
- Less effective against herpes and HPV (which spread through skin-to-skin contact, not just fluid exchange); more effective than no protection

*Vaccination:*
- HPV vaccine (Gardasil 9): prevents 9 strains including the highest-cancer-risk strains; recommended through age 26; shared decision-making 27-45; most effective before sexual activity begins but beneficial in those not yet exposed to all covered strains
- Hepatitis B vaccine: recommended for all adults not previously vaccinated
- Hepatitis A vaccine: recommended for high-risk individuals

*PrEP:* For HIV-negative individuals at high HIV risk (described above)

*Regular testing:* The most impactful strategy for sexually active people — finding and treating infections prevents transmission

## Chapter 6: Environmental Factors

**Partner communication:**

Partner notification ("contact tracing") — informing recent partners when you have been diagnosed with an STI — is both ethically important and public health-critical. Resources:
- CDPH partner services for some STIs (syphilis, gonorrhea, HIV)
- DontSpreadIt.com and similar platforms allow anonymous notification
- Healthcare providers can assist with partner notification in some cases

**Social determinants of STI:**

STI rates are substantially higher in populations experiencing:
- Poverty
- Housing instability
- Reduced healthcare access
- Racial discrimination (Black Americans have significantly higher STI rates, reflecting healthcare access disparities, not behavioral differences)

These are health equity issues, not individual failures.

## Chapter 7: Behavioral Approaches

**Reducing barriers to testing:**

- *At-home STI testing kits:* Available without a healthcare visit; improve testing access; vary in comprehensiveness
- *Telehealth STI prescribing:* Prescription treatment for chlamydia and gonorrhea without in-person visit in some states
- *Campus and community health centers:* Lower-barrier access than primary care

**Disclosure and relationships:**

For people with herpes, HIV, or HPV — disclosure to partners is both an ethical obligation and a legal one in many jurisdictions (for HIV specifically). Disclosure conversations are:
- Often feared more than they need to be — most people handle them better than anticipated
- Supported by accurate information (herpes transmission risk with treatment is < 2%/year; HIV with U=U is 0)

Sex therapists and support organizations can help with disclosure anxiety and communication skills.

## Chapter 8: Cognitive Approaches

**STI stigma:**

The stigma surrounding STIs is disproportionate to their medical severity — herpes carries more social stigma than many serious medical conditions but carries far lower mortality risk. This stigma is a major barrier to testing, treatment, and disclosure.

Cognitive reframe:
- An STI is a medical condition, not a moral failure
- The most responsible people are those who test regularly, treat when positive, disclose to partners, and prevent transmission
- The most risky people (in terms of transmission) are those who avoid testing and thus don't know their status

**Ruben's STI experience:**

At 30, Ruben was treated for chlamydia — discovered incidentally on routine testing. He had been asymptomatic. He notified his two recent partners (awkward but straightforward conversations). Treatment resolved the infection within a week. He felt disproportionate shame about the diagnosis.

His therapist helped him reframe: he discovered and treated an asymptomatic infection through responsible testing and notified people who needed to know. That is exactly what responsible sexual health looks like.

## Chapter 9: When to Seek Help

**STI evaluation resources:**

*Primary care physician:* Full STI evaluation; prescription treatment
*Planned Parenthood:* Comprehensive STI testing and treatment regardless of ability to pay
*Local health department:* STI screening and treatment (often low or no cost)
*At-home testing:* Companies like Everlywell, LetsGetChecked — useful but not comprehensive; positive results need clinical confirmation

**HIV care:**

HIV-positive diagnosis has transformed in the treatment era:
- Antiretroviral therapy (ART): once-daily combination pills achieve undetectable viral load within weeks to months
- People diagnosed with HIV today who start treatment promptly have near-normal life expectancy
- Specialist care: infectious disease specialist for initial management; can transition to primary care for stable patients

## Chapter 10: Your Protocol

**STI prevention and testing protocol:**

*Prevent:*
☐ Condoms with new or non-monogamous partners
☐ HPV vaccination (if not completed)
☐ Hepatitis B vaccination (if not completed)
☐ PrEP discussion if at high HIV risk

*Know your status:*
☐ HIV test (at least once; annually if at risk)
☐ Chlamydia + gonorrhea (annually if sexually active woman < 25; as appropriate based on risk)
☐ Syphilis (at least once; more frequently if multiple partners)
☐ Hepatitis C (once)
☐ HPV/cervical screening (per standard schedule)

*If diagnosed:*
☐ Complete treatment as prescribed
☐ Notify recent partners
☐ Retest after treatment to confirm cure (particularly gonorrhea/chlamydia)
☐ Review prevention strategies with provider

## Epilogue

STI prevention, testing, and treatment are among the most straightforward and effective public health interventions available. Regular testing, appropriate vaccination, consistent condom use, and prompt treatment protect both individuals and their partners. The only real barrier is stigma — and the most effective way to reduce stigma is to discuss these topics with the same matter-of-fact attitude reserved for any other medical condition.
