## Preventing Osteoporosis and the Fractures That Change Lives

### Prologue
A hip fracture in a person over 75 has a 30% mortality rate within one year. A vertebral fracture significantly impairs quality of life, lung function, and mobility. Osteoporosis — the progressive loss of bone density that precedes fractures — is largely silent until it isn't: until the fall that breaks the hip, or the sneeze that fractures a vertebra.

This is a preventable trajectory. Bone density is built primarily in the first three decades of life, maintained in middle age, and lost in the decades beyond — but the rate of loss is substantially modifiable. Interventions in bone health are most effective when begun early, but they are effective at any age.

## Chapter 1: The Research

**Osteoporosis epidemiology:**

- Approximately 10 million Americans have osteoporosis; 44 million more have low bone density (osteopenia)
- Half of all women and 25% of men over age 50 will have an osteoporosis-related fracture in their lifetime
- Hip fractures: one-third of patients cannot return to independent living; 20-25% die within 6 months
- Vertebral fractures: often minimally symptomatic initially; can cause height loss, kyphosis, and significant pain

**The evidence on prevention:**

- *Exercise:* Weight-bearing and resistance exercise are the most effective non-pharmacological interventions for bone maintenance and building. The NOF (National Osteoporosis Foundation) and major endocrinology guidelines strongly recommend weight-bearing exercise.
- *Calcium and Vitamin D:* Evidence supports adequate intake; the benefit of supplementation beyond dietary adequacy is more contested.
- *Pharmacological treatment:* Bisphosphonates (alendronate, risedronate, zoledronic acid) reduce vertebral fracture risk by 40-70% and hip fracture risk by 40-50% in high-risk individuals.

## Chapter 2: The Mechanism

**Bone remodeling:**

Bone is a living tissue continuously remodeled by two cell types:
- *Osteoclasts:* Resorb (break down) old bone
- *Osteoblasts:* Form new bone

In youth, formation exceeds resorption — net bone building. In young adulthood, remodeling is balanced — stable bone density. After peak bone mass (typically late 20s), resorption gradually exceeds formation — net bone loss.

**Menopause and bone loss:**

Estrogen normally suppresses osteoclast activity — it brakes bone resorption. At menopause, estrogen levels fall rapidly. Without this brake, osteoclast activity increases dramatically:
- Women lose 1-3% of bone density per year in the first 5-10 years after menopause
- This accelerated phase accounts for much of lifetime fracture risk
- By the end of this phase, women may have lost 20% of trabecular bone (which constitutes the interior of vertebrae and the femoral neck — the most fracture-prone sites)

## Chapter 3: Key Concepts

**DEXA scan and T-scores:**

DEXA (Dual-Energy X-ray Absorptiometry) measures bone mineral density (BMD) and produces a T-score:
- T-score ≥ -1.0: Normal bone density
- T-score -1.0 to -2.5: Osteopenia (low bone density)
- T-score ≤ -2.5: Osteoporosis

T-score indicates how far the individual's BMD is from the average peak bone density of a young adult (in SD units).

**FRAX (Fracture Risk Assessment Tool):**

The WHO FRAX tool calculates 10-year fracture risk based on:
- Age, sex, weight, height
- Prior fracture history
- Parental hip fracture history
- Smoking, alcohol use
- Corticosteroid use
- Rheumatoid arthritis
- Secondary osteoporosis causes
- BMD (optional input)

FRAX guides treatment decisions — pharmacological treatment is typically recommended when 10-year hip fracture risk ≥ 3% or major osteoporotic fracture risk ≥ 20%.

**Calcium and Vitamin D intake:**

*Calcium recommendations (from food + supplements combined):*
- Adults 19-50: 1,000 mg/day
- Women 51+: 1,200 mg/day
- Men 51-70: 1,000 mg/day; Men 71+: 1,200 mg/day

*Vitamin D:*
- Most guidelines recommend 600-800 IU/day for most adults
- Many experts and the Endocrine Society recommend 1,500-2,000 IU/day for adults at risk of deficiency
- Vitamin D 25(OH)D serum level target: 30-50 ng/mL for bone health

## Chapter 4: Signs and Symptoms

**Osteoporosis is silent until fracture:**

Osteoporosis produces no pain, no visible symptoms, and no detectable loss of function until a fracture occurs. This makes screening important.

**Signs of vertebral fracture:**

- Sudden-onset back pain (may occur with minor activity or no activity)
- Height loss (each vertebral fracture causes approximately 1-2 cm of height reduction; multiple fractures produce the "dowager's hump" — thoracic kyphosis)
- Visible postural changes

**Fall risk factors:**

Falls cause most osteoporosis fractures. Reducing fall risk is as important as treating osteoporosis:
- Muscle weakness (particularly lower extremity)
- Balance impairment
- Visual impairment
- Medications causing sedation or orthostatic hypotension
- Household hazards (rugs, inadequate lighting, no grab bars)
- Footwear (slippery soles, inadequate support)

## Chapter 5: Practical Interventions

**Exercise for bone:**

*Weight-bearing exercise:* Any exercise where you support your body weight against gravity
- Walking: modest benefit (loading insufficient for significant BMD stimulus in most adults)
- Running/jogging: more significant bone stimulus
- Tennis/impact sports: impact loading most osteogenic (bone-stimulating)
- Dancing, stair climbing

*Resistance training:* The most evidence-based bone-building exercise:
- Mechanical loading stimulates osteoblast activity
- Each muscle contraction also pulls on the bone at its insertion — a direct bone stimulus
- 2-3× weekly resistance training produces measurable BMD gains in older adults

*Balance training:* Reduces fall risk
- Tai chi: strong evidence for fall reduction in older adults
- Single-leg balance exercises
- Balance board training

**Dietary calcium:**

Food sources of calcium:
- Dairy: 300mg per cup of milk or yogurt; 200-300mg per oz of cheese
- Fortified plant milks: typically 300mg per cup
- Canned sardines/salmon with bones: 300-350mg per serving
- Tofu (calcium-set): 200-400mg per half-cup
- Leafy greens (kale, bok choy, broccoli): 100-200mg per cup

## Chapter 6: Environmental Factors

**Fall prevention in the home:**

The home environment is where most falls occur:
- *Remove tripping hazards:* Rugs (particularly loose ones), cords, clutter
- *Adequate lighting:* Nightlights for nocturnal bathroom trips
- *Grab bars:* In bathrooms (particularly shower/tub and next to toilet)
- *Non-slip surfaces:* Shower mats, bath mats
- *Stair handrails:* Both sides if possible
- *First-floor bedroom and bathroom:* When stairs become a hazard

**Medications and fall risk:**

Review of medications that increase fall risk is a critical component of fracture prevention:
- Sedatives and sleep medications (benzodiazepines, z-drugs, antihistamines)
- Antidepressants (particularly SSRIs, TCAs)
- Antihypertensives causing orthostatic hypotension
- Opioids
- Anticholinergics

Deprescribing fall-risk medications in older adults reduces fracture risk.

## Chapter 7: Behavioral Approaches

**Isabel's bone health management:**

Isabel's RA management includes corticosteroids — among the most potent causes of secondary osteoporosis. Her bone health protocol:

*Medical:*
- DEXA scan at RA diagnosis (T-score -1.8, osteopenic)
- Alendronate 70mg weekly (bisphosphonate, recommended for patients on corticosteroids)
- Calcium 1,200mg/day from food + supplement (500mg supplement to reach target)
- Vitamin D 2,000 IU/day
- 25(OH)D level: maintained at 42 ng/mL

*Exercise:*
- Weight-bearing resistance training 3×/week (swimming adapted to include resistance exercises)
- Balance: yoga 1×/week (significant fall-prevention benefit demonstrated for yoga)

*Monitoring:*
- DEXA every 2 years (corticosteroid use warrants more frequent monitoring)

At 2-year scan: T-score stable at -1.7 (essentially unchanged — preventing further loss is the goal while on corticosteroids).

## Chapter 8: Cognitive Approaches

**The "invisible threat" problem:**

Osteoporosis is psychologically difficult to address because it produces no symptoms. People prioritize visible, symptomatic problems. An asymptomatic T-score of -2.3 feels less urgent than a visible rash or a painful shoulder — even though the long-term consequences of the former may be more severe.

Reframe: the fracture that the osteoporosis causes will not be invisible or painless. The asymptomatic stage is the window of opportunity. Act before the fall.

**Post-fracture psychology:**

A significant fall with fracture produces fear of falling — which can be as disabling as the fracture itself. Fear of falling leads to activity restriction, which produces deconditioning, which increases actual fall risk — a fear-driven spiral.

Evidence-based fall fear management:
- Graduated exposure to activities that provoked fear
- Balance training to improve actual capacity
- Cognitive restructuring of fall risk perception
- Tai chi (both physical and psychological benefit)

## Chapter 9: When to Seek Help

**DEXA screening recommendations:**

*Women:*
- All women 65+ (USPSTF Grade B)
- Women under 65 with a risk factor: early menopause, corticosteroid use, low body weight, parental hip fracture, smoking, excessive alcohol

*Men:*
- Men 70+ (no universal recommendation, but widely practiced)
- Men with risk factors (hypogonadism, corticosteroid use, alcohol use disorder)

**Pharmacological treatment:**

First-line pharmacological options:
- *Bisphosphonates (alendronate, risedronate):* Oral weekly; prevent bone resorption by inhibiting osteoclast activity; reduce vertebral fracture risk 40-70%, hip fracture risk 40-50%
- *Zoledronic acid:* IV annually; for patients unable to tolerate oral bisphosphonates; equally effective
- *Denosumab:* Subcutaneous injection every 6 months; potent; cannot be discontinued abruptly
- *Romosozumab, teriparatide:* For severe osteoporosis; bone-building (anabolic) effect; used for 1-2 years followed by antiresorptive therapy

## Chapter 10: Your Protocol

**Bone health across the decades:**

*In your 30s:*
- Maximize peak bone mass window (closes in late 20s-early 30s)
- Resistance training
- Calcium 1,000mg/day from food
- Vitamin D adequate

*In your 40s:*
- Maintain through perimenopause transition
- Continue resistance training
- Discuss bone health with physician if entering menopause

*At menopause:*
- DEXA scan
- Assess need for hormone therapy (significant bone-protective effect of estrogen)
- Bisphosphonate consideration if osteopenic with risk factors

*50s and beyond:*
- DEXA every 2 years if normal; more frequently if on corticosteroids or treating osteoporosis
- Resistance and balance training
- Home fall prevention assessment
- Medication review for fall-risk drugs

## Epilogue

Bone is living tissue, continuously remodeling, responsive to exercise, nutrition, and medical management. The trajectory toward fracture is not predetermined — it is modifiable through the choices made in midlife and beyond. The hip fracture that did not happen because a 65-year-old had been doing resistance training for 10 years and fell on a strength-trained leg is invisible. The hip fracture that does happen is not. Bone health is one of those areas where the return on prevention is extraordinary — and largely invisible until you need it.
