## Prologue

Vaccines are the subject of more misinformation than any other area of medicine. This is unfortunate because the evidence base for adult vaccination is clear, the safety profile is excellent, and the preventable harm from vaccine-preventable diseases remains substantial even in vaccinated populations.

This book addresses adult vaccines specifically — most people understand that children are vaccinated, but adult vaccine schedules are less well understood and significantly undertaken.

---

## Chapter 1: The Research

**Vaccine effectiveness in adults:**

Adult vaccines are among the most cost-effective preventive health interventions:
- Influenza vaccination reduces hospitalization by 30-50% in adults over 65
- Shingles vaccine (Shingrix) is 97% effective at preventing shingles in adults 50+
- Pneumococcal vaccination reduces pneumonia-related hospitalization and death in high-risk populations
- COVID-19 vaccines reduced severe illness, hospitalization, and death dramatically during the pandemic
- HPV vaccination prevents approximately 90% of HPV-related cancers when given before exposure

**Vaccine safety:**

The modern vaccine safety monitoring system is the most rigorous in history:
- VAERS (Vaccine Adverse Event Reporting System): passive surveillance system
- VSD (Vaccine Safety Datalink): active surveillance in 9 managed care organizations
- CISA (Clinical Immunization Safety Assessment): evaluates complex vaccine safety questions
- WHO Global Advisory Committee on Vaccine Safety

Vaccine serious adverse events are rare and consistently substantially outweighed by disease prevention benefits in recommended populations.

## Chapter 2: The Mechanism

**How vaccines work:**

Vaccines train the immune system to recognize and respond to specific pathogens without causing the disease itself:

*Live attenuated vaccines:* Contain weakened (attenuated) pathogen that replicates but doesn't cause disease. Produce strong, long-lasting immune response. Examples: MMR, varicella, yellow fever. Not used in immunocompromised individuals.

*Inactivated vaccines:* Killed pathogen; cannot replicate; require boosters for sustained immunity. Examples: inactivated influenza, hepatitis A.

*Subunit vaccines:* Contain specific protein components of the pathogen. Examples: Shingrix (shingles), Tdap (pertussis component), HPV vaccine. Very safe because no live pathogen.

*mRNA vaccines:* mRNA instructs cells to produce a protein (e.g., spike protein), which stimulates immune response. The mRNA degrades quickly and does not integrate into DNA. Examples: COVID-19 mRNA vaccines.

*Conjugate vaccines:* Polysaccharide antigens attached to carrier proteins to enhance immune response. Examples: Prevnar (pneumococcal), Hib.

## Chapter 3: Key Concepts

**Adult vaccination schedule (CDC/ACIP 2024):**

*Annual vaccines:*
- *Influenza (flu):* Every fall; everyone ≥ 6 months; high-dose or adjuvanted preferred for adults ≥ 65

*Vaccines by age/risk:*

*All adults (if not completed childhood series):*
- *Tdap:* One-time dose for Td-naive adults; then Td booster every 10 years; Tdap during each pregnancy (27-36 weeks)
- *MMR:* Adults born after 1957 who lack evidence of immunity: 1-2 doses
- *Varicella:* If no prior chickenpox or vaccination: 2 doses

*Age 19-26 (or shared decision-making to 45):*
- *HPV:* 2-dose series (if series begun before 15) or 3-dose series; prevents HPV-related cancers

*Age 50+:*
- *Shingrix (zoster vaccine):* 2 doses (separated by 2-6 months); ≥97% effective at preventing shingles; strongly recommended

*Age 60+:*
- *RSV vaccine:* Abrysvo or Mresvia; one-time dose; CDC recommends for adults 75+ and 60-74 at high risk

*Age 65+:*
- *Pneumococcal vaccines:* PCV21 (Capvaxive) or PCV20; one dose if not previously vaccinated with PCV20/21; prevents bacterial pneumonia, meningitis, bacteremia

*For travelers:*
- Hepatitis A, hepatitis B, typhoid, yellow fever, meningococcal, and others depending on destination

*For high-risk groups:*
- *Hepatitis B:* All adults 18-59; adults ≥ 60 if at risk; series of 2-3 doses (Heplisav-B is a 2-dose option)
- *Hepatitis A:* Adults at risk or travelers

## Chapter 4: Signs and Symptoms

**Common vaccine reactions (expected; not adverse effects):**

- Injection site soreness, redness, swelling (1-3 days)
- Low-grade fever (particularly Shingrix — common and indicates immune response)
- Fatigue, mild headache, muscle aches for 1-2 days (particularly after Shingrix 2nd dose)

These reactions reflect immune system activation and are expected — they are not evidence of vaccine harm.

**True adverse reactions (rare):**

*Anaphylaxis:* Severe allergic reaction; occurs within 15-30 minutes of injection; reason for post-vaccination observation period; treatable with epinephrine

*Guillain-Barré Syndrome (GBS):* Very rare risk with some vaccines (influenza vaccine: 1-2 per million vaccinations); also occurs with influenza disease itself (at higher rates than vaccine)

*Vaccine-associated enhanced disease:* Rare; more theoretical; various vaccines have been studied for this risk

## Chapter 5: Practical Interventions

**Getting vaccinated:**

*Where to get vaccines:*
- Primary care office
- Pharmacies (CVS, Walgreens, Walmart) — convenient for many adult vaccines
- Health department clinics
- Travel clinics (for travel-related vaccines)
- Workplace vaccination programs (many employers offer flu vaccines)

*Cost:*
- Under the ACA, recommended preventive vaccines are covered at no cost for most insured adults
- Medicare Part D covers most adult vaccines
- VFC (Vaccines for Children) program for pediatric vaccines; BVAP and similar programs exist for underinsured adults in some states

## Chapter 6: Environmental Factors

**Herd immunity and community protection:**

Vaccine-preventable diseases spread through communities. When a sufficient proportion of the population is immune, transmission chains are interrupted and even non-immune individuals are partially protected. This "herd immunity" threshold varies by disease:
- Measles: ~95% vaccination required (extremely contagious)
- Polio: ~80-85%
- COVID-19: variable with variants; harder to achieve with evolving immunology

Adults who are vaccinated protect not only themselves but also those who cannot be vaccinated (infants too young for certain vaccines, immunocompromised individuals for whom vaccines may be contraindicated or less effective).

## Chapter 7: Behavioral Approaches

**Addressing vaccine hesitancy in adults:**

Vaccine hesitancy in adults typically reflects one or more of:
- Concerns about side effects (often based on misinformation)
- Concerns about ingredients
- Distrust of pharmaceutical industry or government recommendations
- Belief that natural immunity is superior
- Perceived low personal risk

Effective approaches (from health communication research):
- Motivational interviewing over direct confrontation
- Acknowledging concerns as understandable
- Providing accurate information about specific concerns (not generic "vaccines are safe")
- Discussing the expected vs. unexpected reaction difference
- Physician recommendation: the single most powerful predictor of vaccine acceptance is physician recommendation

**Elena and HPV:**

Elena had not received HPV vaccination (it wasn't available when she was a teenager and she had never received it as an adult). At 33, she learned she was still within the shared decision-making window to 45. Her physician discussed the remaining benefit (HPV vaccination is less effective after exposure, but most people haven't been exposed to all vaccine-covered strains) and she completed the series.

## Chapter 8: Cognitive Approaches

**The "I already had it" vaccine logic:**

Some adults believe prior disease confers sufficient immunity without vaccination:
- *Prior chickenpox:* Does not prevent shingles (shingles is reactivation of dormant VZV; Shingrix significantly reduces this risk regardless of prior chicken pox)
- *Prior COVID-19:* Natural immunity varies; vaccination after COVID-19 infection provides enhanced, more durable protection ("hybrid immunity")
- *Prior influenza:* Each year's influenza strains differ; annual vaccination is necessary

**The cognitive bias of vaccine timing:**

Vaccines work by prevention — their effect is invisible (the disease that didn't happen). This makes vaccine benefit cognitively invisible compared to vaccine side effects (soreness from the injection is immediate and concrete). This asymmetry in cognitive salience contributes to vaccine hesitancy even when the risk-benefit calculation is clear.

## Chapter 9: When to Seek Help

**Vaccines and immunocompromised adults:**

Immunocompromised individuals (from cancer treatment, HIV, immunosuppressive medications including high-dose steroids, biologics, transplant medications) have specific vaccine considerations:
- Live vaccines (MMR, varicella, yellow fever, live nasal influenza) generally contraindicated during immunosuppression
- Inactivated vaccines generally safe but may be less effective due to blunted immune response
- Timing relative to immunosuppressive therapy is important
- These patients often need additional doses and specialist guidance

**Pregnancy and vaccination:**

Certain vaccines are recommended during pregnancy:
- *Influenza:* Inactivated flu vaccine recommended for all trimesters
- *Tdap:* Every pregnancy, ideally 27-36 weeks (protects newborn before they can be vaccinated)
- *COVID-19:* Recommended during pregnancy; updated bivalent boosters during pregnancy
- *RSV:* Abrysvo approved for maternal vaccination at 32-36 weeks to protect newborns

Live vaccines (MMR, varicella) are contraindicated during pregnancy — vaccinate post-partum if needed.

## Chapter 10: Your Protocol

**Isabel's vaccine assessment:**

Isabel, 52, had her vaccine history reviewed:
- Influenza: vaccinated annually (current)
- Tdap: last dose unknown; received Tdap booster at this visit
- Shingrix: had not received; scheduled for Dose 1 (Dose 2 in 2-6 months)
- Pneumococcal: received PCV20 at this visit (age 52, high-risk due to immunosuppressive therapy for RA)
- Hepatitis B: completed childhood series; confirmed

She experienced expected Shingrix side effects after Dose 2: fatigue and injection site pain for 2 days. She described this as "completely worth it" given her knowledge of the severity and frequency of shingles in older adults.

## Epilogue

Adult vaccination is one of the highest-value, lowest-effort preventive health interventions available. A single conversation with your primary care provider or pharmacist can identify your gaps and catch you up. The diseases that recommended vaccines prevent — shingles, pneumococcal pneumonia, influenza-related complications — cause substantial suffering that is substantially preventable.
