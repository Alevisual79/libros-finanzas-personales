## Prologue

Cancer screening is one of the most value-laden areas of medicine. The same test can save a life (by finding a cancer early enough to cure it) and harm a life (by finding an abnormality that would never have caused harm, leading to unnecessary treatment with real side effects). Understanding which screens are recommended, for whom, at what ages, and why requires engaging with this complexity.

The goal of this book is to explain the evidence behind standard cancer screening recommendations — not to create anxiety but to enable informed, proactive health decisions.

---

## Chapter 1: The Research

**Cancer screening principles:**

For a cancer screening test to be beneficial, several conditions must hold:
1. The cancer must have a detectable pre-clinical phase (screen-detectable before causing symptoms)
2. Earlier detection must improve outcomes (treatment of earlier stage is more effective)
3. The screening test must have adequate sensitivity and specificity
4. The benefits of screening must outweigh the harms (false positives, unnecessary biopsies/treatment, overdiagnosis)

**Overdiagnosis:**

A fundamental concept in cancer screening: some screen-detected cancers would never have caused symptoms or death if left undetected. "Overdiagnosis" — finding and treating a cancer that would never have harmed the patient — is the primary harm in some screening programs.

Examples: thyroid cancer screening produces significant overdiagnosis (high detection of indolent microcarcinomas); neuroblastoma screening in infants (abandoned after studies showed no mortality benefit and significant overdiagnosis); breast cancer screening is associated with some level of overdiagnosis, the extent of which is debated.

This doesn't mean screening is bad — it means the benefits and harms must be weighed honestly.

## Chapter 2: The Mechanism

**How cancers develop:**

Cancers develop through:
1. DNA damage (from carcinogen exposure, radiation, viral infection, random replication errors)
2. Failure of normal cellular repair and checkpoint mechanisms
3. Selection for cells with increased proliferation, survival advantage
4. Progression through precancerous stages (in many cancers) to invasive cancer
5. Metastasis to other organs

**Why early detection helps:**

*Localized vs. metastatic cancer survival:*
- Colorectal cancer: 5-year survival 91% (localized) vs. 14% (distant metastasis)
- Breast cancer: 99% (localized) vs. 28% (distant)
- Lung cancer: 63% (localized) vs. 7% (distant)

Catching cancers before metastasis dramatically changes outcomes for most cancer types. Screening aims to shift detection toward localized disease.

## Chapter 3: Key Concepts

**USPSTF recommendations:**

The U.S. Preventive Services Task Force (USPSTF) provides independent, evidence-based recommendations for clinical preventive services, including cancer screening. Grade A and B recommendations reflect sufficient evidence for benefit.

**Sensitivity and specificity:**

*Sensitivity:* The proportion of true cancer cases that the test detects ("true positive rate")
*Specificity:* The proportion of non-cancer cases that the test correctly identifies as negative ("true negative rate")

All screening tests have imperfect sensitivity and specificity — false positives (positive test in someone without cancer) and false negatives (missed cancer in someone who has it) occur with any test.

## Chapter 4: Signs and Symptoms

**Recommended cancer screens by type:**

**Colorectal Cancer:**

USPSTF Grade A recommendation for screening adults age 45-75.

Options (all are acceptable):
- *Colonoscopy:* Every 10 years; examines entire colon; removes polyps directly; single test can be diagnostic and therapeutic; requires bowel prep and sedation
- *Stool-based tests:*
  - FIT (Fecal Immunochemical Test): annually; detects blood in stool; no prep; abnormal result requires colonoscopy
  - Stool DNA (Cologuard): every 1-3 years; detects blood + DNA markers; no prep; higher sensitivity, some overdiagnosis
- *CT Colonography (virtual colonoscopy):* Every 5 years; radiation exposure; abnormal results require colonoscopy

**Breast Cancer:**

Recommendations vary by organization (a source of public confusion):
- *USPSTF (2024):* Biennial mammography for women 40-74
- *American Cancer Society:* Annual mammography from 45; option to start at 40; continue past 74 if good health
- *ACR:* Annual mammography from 40; high-risk women: MRI + mammography

High-risk women (BRCA1/2 mutation carriers, family history of first-degree relative with breast cancer under 50) may benefit from MRI in addition to mammography and should discuss personalized screening with their physician.

**Cervical Cancer:**

- Pap smear every 3 years (age 21-65) OR
- Pap + HPV co-test every 5 years (age 30-65)
- HPV vaccination has substantially reduced cervical cancer incidence; vaccinated individuals may eventually have modified screening recommendations

**Lung Cancer:**

USPSTF Grade B: annual low-dose CT (LDCT) for adults age 50-80 with:
- ≥ 20 pack-year smoking history AND
- Currently smoking or quit within past 15 years

The NLST trial found LDCT reduced lung cancer mortality by 20% compared to chest X-ray in this high-risk population. False positive rate is significant (96% of positive screens are not cancer in lung screening trials) — follow-up evaluation is required.

**Prostate Cancer (PSA):**

USPSTF Grade C: individual decision-making for men 55-69. PSA screening:
- Reduces prostate cancer mortality modestly
- Significant overdiagnosis and overtreatment risk (many screen-detected prostate cancers are low-grade and would never cause symptoms)
- Active surveillance (vs. immediate treatment) for low-risk screen-detected prostate cancer is now the standard recommendation, reducing treatment-related harms
- Not recommended routinely for men ≥ 70

## Chapter 5: Practical Interventions

**Action by age:**

*Age 21:* Begin cervical cancer screening (if female)

*Age 40:* Discuss breast cancer screening with your physician; begin or continue mammography; HPV vaccination if not completed

*Age 45:* Begin colorectal cancer screening

*Age 50-80 (if qualifying history):* Annual lung cancer LDCT

*Age 55-69 (men):* Discuss PSA testing and individual benefits/risks with your physician

**Scheduling overdue screens:**

If you have delayed recommended screenings, prioritize by risk:
- Family history of a cancer significantly elevates screening importance and often shifts the recommended starting age earlier
- Colorectal cancer screening is frequently delayed — the most commonly cited reason is discomfort with the procedure (stool tests are equally recommended and avoid this)

## Chapter 6: Environmental Factors

**Cancer risk factors:**

Understanding modifiable risk factors is as important as screening:

*Tobacco:* Causes 30% of cancer deaths overall; causally associated with lung, bladder, cervical, oral, esophageal, kidney, pancreatic, and other cancers

*Obesity:* Associated with 13 cancer types including endometrial, colorectal, and postmenopausal breast cancer; the second most significant modifiable cancer risk factor

*Alcohol:* Causally associated with 7 cancer types including breast, colorectal, liver, esophageal; no "safe" level for cancer risk

*Physical inactivity:* Associated with colorectal, breast, endometrial, and other cancers through inflammatory and hormonal mechanisms

*UV radiation:* Skin cancer; melanoma; squamous and basal cell carcinoma

*HPV infection:* Causally associated with cervical, anal, oropharyngeal, and other cancers; prevented by HPV vaccination (now recommended through age 26; shared decision-making to age 45)

## Chapter 7: Behavioral Approaches

**Reducing barriers to screening:**

Common barriers:
- *Fear of finding something:* Addressing this requires the reframe — finding cancer early is better than finding it late; what you don't know does hurt you in this context
- *Discomfort/embarrassment:* Stool tests, FIT test for colorectal cancer, and HPV co-tests reduce the procedural barriers
- *Cost:* Recommended screening tests are covered by insurance (including the ACA Marketplace) without cost-sharing when performed on schedule; Medicaid covers most recommended screens

**Isabel's cancer screening:**

Isabel, at 52, had:
- Annual mammography (breast cancer — performed; result: BI-RADS 1, no findings)
- Cervical: Pap + HPV co-test every 5 years (last: 3 years ago; normal)
- Colorectal: no screening to date — deferred due to colonoscopy aversion
  *Action taken:* Completed FIT test (stool test) — negative; scheduled for repeat in 1 year

## Chapter 8: Cognitive Approaches

**Managing cancer screening anxiety:**

Cancer screening produces anxiety for many people — the period between testing and results can be particularly distressing. Cognitive approaches:

*Grounding in base rates:* The majority of cancer screens return normal or benign results. A positive screening test is the beginning of investigation, not a diagnosis. Understanding the expected false positive rate for your specific test (e.g., approximately 10% of mammograms annually in the US result in recall for additional imaging; the vast majority are ultimately negative) provides accurate context.

*Process focus:* The decision to be screened is within your control; the result is not. Focusing on having made a health-positive decision, rather than catastrophizing about potential results, manages waiting period anxiety.

**Elena's avoidance pattern:**

Elena had avoided discussing HPV vaccination history and cervical screening with her physician, partly from an undefined anxiety about medical appointments. When she reframed the appointment as information-gathering rather than threat-seeking, she scheduled and attended — with a normal result. The anxiety about the appointment had been greater than the appointment itself.

## Chapter 9: When to Seek Help

**Family history and genetic risk:**

People with significant family histories may need different screening protocols:
- First-degree relative with colorectal cancer: begin colonoscopy at 40 or 10 years before the relative's diagnosis
- BRCA1/2 mutation: high-risk breast and ovarian cancer protocol; referral to genetic counselor
- Lynch syndrome: high-risk colorectal and endometrial cancer protocol
- First-degree relative with breast cancer under 50: discuss earlier or more frequent screening with physician

**Genetic testing:**

BRCA1/2 genetic testing is recommended for:
- Personal or family history of breast, ovarian, pancreatic, or prostate cancer suggesting hereditary risk
- Ashkenazi Jewish ancestry with family history of breast/ovarian cancer

## Chapter 10: Your Protocol

**Your cancer screening checklist:**

*Cervical cancer (women):*
- Age 21-65: Pap every 3 years, or Pap + HPV every 5 years (if ≥ 30)
- Are you current?

*Colorectal cancer:*
- Age 45-75: Colonoscopy every 10 years, or FIT every year, or Cologuard every 1-3 years
- Are you current? If not due to colonoscopy aversion: request FIT test at your next visit

*Breast cancer (women 40+):*
- Annual or biennial mammography
- When was your last mammogram?

*Lung cancer:*
- If ≥ 50 years old with 20+ pack-year smoking history: request annual LDCT

*Prostate cancer (men 55-69):*
- Discuss PSA testing with your physician

Record your screening dates and next due dates. Bring this list to every primary care appointment.

## Epilogue

Cancer screening is not perfect — no screening test is. But for the conditions where evidence supports it, the alternative to screening is detecting cancer at a more advanced stage when treatment is less effective. The conversation with your physician about which screens are appropriate for your age, sex, and family history is one of the most impactful preventive health conversations available.
