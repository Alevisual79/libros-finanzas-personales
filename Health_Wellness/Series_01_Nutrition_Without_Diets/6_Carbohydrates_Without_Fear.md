### Prologue
After the fall of dietary fat as public health villain #1, carbohydrates claimed the position. Low-carbohydrate diets became the dominant weight loss approach, with carbohydrates blamed for obesity, insulin resistance, metabolic disease, inflammation, and cognitive decline.

The truth is more complicated, and more useful. Carbohydrates are not a single thing. "Carbohydrates" encompasses whole grains and white bread, lentils and candy, broccoli and Skittles. Understanding the distinctions within this category is the beginning of a rational approach.

---

## Chapter 1: The Carbohydrate Rehabilitation

The anti-carbohydrate movement rests on a specific hypothesis: carbohydrates raise insulin; high insulin drives fat storage; therefore carbohydrates cause obesity and metabolic disease. This "carbohydrate-insulin model" of obesity has been studied intensively over the past two decades.

**The evidence:** Multiple controlled trials (where participants are randomized to low-carbohydrate vs. low-fat diets under controlled conditions) have found that caloric intake, not macronutrient ratio, is the primary driver of weight change. When calories are equated, low-fat and low-carbohydrate diets produce equivalent weight loss. Insulin levels are somewhat lower on low-carbohydrate diets, but the additional fat loss predicted by the model doesn't materialize at equivalent calories.

**What is real:** Carbohydrate quality matters enormously. The evidence that refined, processed carbohydrates (white bread, sweetened beverages, ultra-processed snack foods) contribute to metabolic disease is strong. The evidence that unrefined carbohydrates (legumes, whole grains, vegetables, fruit) cause metabolic disease is absent. In fact, the dietary patterns with the best evidence for longevity and chronic disease prevention (Mediterranean, traditional Japanese, Blue Zone diets) all include substantial carbohydrate from whole food sources.

The carbohydrate to be concerned about is the refined, low-fiber, high-glycemic variety — not carbohydrates as a category.

## Chapter 2: Simple vs. Complex Carbohydrates

**Simple carbohydrates (simple sugars):** Short-chain structures (1-2 sugar molecules). Glucose, fructose, sucrose, lactose. Rapidly digested and absorbed. Examples: table sugar, honey, fruit (which also contains fiber), milk, soft drinks.

**Complex carbohydrates (polysaccharides):** Long chains of sugar molecules. Starch and fiber. Found in whole grains, legumes, vegetables. Starch is digested to glucose; fiber is not digested by human enzymes (though gut bacteria ferment it).

**The practical relevance:** The simple/complex distinction is less important than the food matrix distinction. Whole fruit is primarily simple sugars but comes with fiber, water, and phytonutrients that moderate its glycemic effect. White bread is complex carbohydrate (starch) but is rapidly digested to glucose with minimal fiber to slow absorption.

**What actually matters:**
- Fiber content (higher fiber → slower digestion → more moderate glycemic response)
- Processing level (whole grain vs. refined grain)
- Food matrix (is the carbohydrate surrounded by fiber, protein, and fat, or consumed in isolation?)
- Liquid vs. solid form (liquid carbohydrates — juices, sodas — bypass satiety mechanisms)

## Chapter 3: The Glycemic Index in Practice

The glycemic index (GI) ranks foods by how quickly they raise blood glucose relative to pure glucose (score: 100).

- High GI (>70): white bread, white rice, watermelon, cornflakes
- Medium GI (55-70): whole wheat bread, oatmeal, banana, orange juice
- Low GI (<55): legumes, most vegetables, whole fruits, dairy, nuts

**The GI's limitations in real-world eating:**

*Glycemic load matters more than GI:* Glycemic load = GI × grams of carbohydrate per serving ÷ 100. Watermelon has a high GI but a low glycemic load because a serving contains little carbohydrate.

*Combination effects:* GI is tested on single foods consumed alone. In real meals, carbohydrates are eaten with fat, protein, and fiber, all of which slow glucose absorption significantly. A high-GI food consumed with protein and fat may produce a moderate glycemic response.

*Individual variation:* Blood glucose responses to the same food vary substantially between individuals. Studies using continuous glucose monitors find that "high GI" foods may not spike some people's blood glucose significantly, while "low GI" foods may spike others'. Personalization matters.

**The practical use of GI:** Rather than following GI rankings precisely, use it as a rough guide: minimize foods that are both high GI and high glycemic load (white bread and refined starches in large amounts, sweetened beverages). Don't worry about GI for whole foods consumed in mixed meals.

## Chapter 4: Carbohydrates and Energy

Carbohydrates are the body's preferred fuel source — not because fat and protein can't provide energy, but because carbohydrate (glucose) is more efficient:

- Produces ATP (cellular energy) faster than fat
- Doesn't require metabolic conversion before use
- Preferred by the brain (which uses approximately 130g glucose/day)
- Preferred by red blood cells (which can only use glucose)

**Carbohydrate and cognitive function:** Moderate glucose availability supports optimal cognitive performance. Very low carbohydrate intake produces ketosis (the brain adapts to use ketones), which many people find cognitively adequate. But the "brain fog" many people experience during low-carbohydrate adaptation is real — it typically resolves after 2-4 weeks as the brain adapts.

**Carbohydrate and mood:** Carbohydrate intake influences serotonin production (carbohydrate-driven insulin facilitates tryptophan entry into the brain, where it's converted to serotonin). This is part of why carbohydrate cravings often accompany low mood or stress — the body is seeking neurochemical support.

**Glycogen storage:** The body stores carbohydrate as glycogen in the liver (approximately 100g) and muscle (approximately 300-500g). This glycogen fuels activity between meals and during exercise. Low glycogen states produce fatigue, reduced physical performance, and sometimes mood effects.

## Chapter 5: Carbohydrates and Athletic Performance

For most forms of physical exercise — particularly moderate to high intensity activity — carbohydrates are the primary fuel:

**Why athletes need carbohydrates:** At moderate to high exercise intensities (>60-70% VO2max), the body increasingly relies on carbohydrate over fat for fuel. Glycogen depletion ("hitting the wall" in marathon running) produces marked decrements in performance.

**Carbohydrate periodization:** Advanced athletes intentionally vary carbohydrate intake based on training demands:
- High carbohydrate on high-intensity training days (to fuel glycogen stores)
- Lower carbohydrate on rest or low-intensity days (to promote fat adaptation)

For recreational exercisers (which most people are), this level of periodization is unnecessary. General adequacy of carbohydrate intake — from whole food sources — supports performance.

**Ruben** had adopted a low-carbohydrate eating pattern based on popular media, then found his evening gym sessions consistently exhausting. Adding back whole grains and fruit around his training sessions dramatically improved his workout performance and recovery. He had eliminated an essential performance fuel in pursuit of dietary purity.

## Chapter 6: Low-Carb Diets: For Whom and When

Low-carbohydrate diets (restricting carbohydrates to <100-150g/day) and ketogenic diets (<20-50g/day) are useful for specific circumstances:

**Strong evidence:**
- Type 2 diabetes management: low-carbohydrate diets significantly reduce blood glucose and HbA1c, often allowing medication reduction under medical supervision
- Epilepsy: the ketogenic diet is an established medical treatment for drug-resistant epilepsy, particularly in children
- Non-alcoholic fatty liver disease: low-carbohydrate diets reduce liver fat accumulation

**Moderate evidence:**
- Weight loss: low-carbohydrate diets produce slightly greater short-term weight loss than low-fat diets in the first 6 months; the difference narrows at 12+ months
- Metabolic syndrome: may improve insulin sensitivity, triglycerides, and HDL

**Not supported by evidence for most people:**
- Superior long-term weight maintenance (no advantage over other approaches)
- Cognitive enhancement in healthy people (anecdotal; trial evidence is mixed)
- Athletic performance in high-intensity sports (evidence favors carbohydrate adequacy)

Low-carbohydrate diets are a valid approach for specific circumstances. They are not universally superior and are not appropriate for people who feel better with adequate carbohydrate intake.

## Chapter 7: Fiber: The Forgotten Carbohydrate

Fiber is a carbohydrate that human digestive enzymes cannot break down. It passes through the small intestine intact and is fermented by bacteria in the large intestine.

**Types of fiber:**
- *Soluble fiber:* Dissolves in water; forms a gel in the gut; slows glucose absorption; lowers LDL cholesterol. Sources: oats, beans, lentils, peas, apples, psyllium.
- *Insoluble fiber:* Does not dissolve; adds bulk; speeds transit time; prevents constipation. Sources: whole wheat, vegetables, nuts.

**Fiber's health benefits:**
- Gut microbiome health (fermented fiber produces short-chain fatty acids — primary fuel for colon cells and systemic anti-inflammatory compounds)
- Cardiovascular: soluble fiber reduces LDL by approximately 5-10%
- Blood glucose regulation: slows glucose absorption, reducing glycemic spikes
- Satiety: high-fiber foods slow gastric emptying and increase meal satisfaction
- Colorectal cancer risk reduction: strongly supported by evidence
- All-cause mortality: higher fiber intake is among the most consistent predictors of longevity in observational data

**The fiber deficit:** The recommended fiber intake is 25g/day (women) and 38g/day (men). The average American consumes approximately 15g/day. Most people are substantially fiber-deficient.

**Practical fiber:** Eating legumes several times a week, choosing whole grains over refined grains, and including vegetables at most meals typically brings fiber intake into the adequate range without supplementation or elaborate planning.

## Chapter 8: Resistant Starch

Resistant starch (RS) is a type of starch that resists digestion in the small intestine and reaches the large intestine intact, where it functions similarly to dietary fiber.

**How resistant starch forms:**
- RS2: in raw starch (raw potatoes, green bananas, raw oats)
- RS3: formed when cooked starchy foods are cooled (cooked-then-cooled rice, potatoes, pasta)

**Health effects of resistant starch:**
- Prebiotic (feeds beneficial gut bacteria, producing butyrate — the primary fuel for colon cells)
- Improves insulin sensitivity
- Reduces glycemic response to the food containing it (cooked-then-cooled pasta has a lower glycemic response than freshly cooked pasta)
- Increases satiety

**Practical implication:** Making rice, potatoes, and pasta ahead, refrigerating, and eating cold or reheated (which preserves most RS3) is a simple way to increase resistant starch intake without changing food choices significantly. Potato salad, cold rice dishes, sushi rice, and overnight oats are naturally higher in resistant starch than their freshly cooked equivalents.

## Chapter 9: Carbohydrates and Mental Health

The brain's relationship with carbohydrates extends beyond energy provision to mood regulation and cognitive function.

**The serotonin connection:** Tryptophan (the amino acid precursor to serotonin) competes with other amino acids for transport into the brain. Carbohydrate-driven insulin removes competing amino acids from the blood, improving tryptophan's relative access to the brain. This mechanism partially explains why carbohydrate intake tends to improve mood and why carbohydrate cravings increase during stress and low mood.

**The glycemic volatility effect:** High-glycemic eating patterns that produce sharp blood glucose spikes and crashes are associated with increased irritability, anxiety, and fatigue. Stabilizing blood glucose through lower-glycemic carbohydrate choices and balanced meals (with protein and fat) smooths out these mood fluctuations.

**Very low carbohydrate diets and mood:** Some people report mood improvements on ketogenic diets; others report irritability and low mood that persist beyond the initial adaptation period. The evidence suggests substantial individual variation. People with a history of depression who find their mood worsens on low-carbohydrate diets should not push through the mood effects in pursuit of metabolic benefits.

Isabel, managing perimenopausal mood changes, found that low-glycemic carbohydrate sources (oats in the morning, legumes at lunch, sweet potato at dinner) stabilized her energy and mood more effectively than the low-carbohydrate approach she had briefly tried.

## Chapter 10: Your Carbohydrate Strategy

An evidence-based carbohydrate approach doesn't require counting grams or eliminating food groups:

**Prioritize:**
- Legumes (lentils, beans, chickpeas): fiber-rich, protein-containing, low-glycemic — arguably the best carbohydrate food category for health
- Vegetables: unlimited, all types
- Whole grains (oats, brown rice, quinoa, barley, whole wheat): higher fiber and micronutrient content than refined equivalents
- Whole fruit: fiber, vitamins, polyphenols — not a concern for most people
- Resistant starch foods (cooled cooked potatoes, rice, legumes)

**Limit:**
- Refined grains (white bread, white rice, pasta) as the primary carbohydrate source
- Sugar-sweetened beverages (most important dietary change for metabolic health)
- Ultra-processed snack foods (high GI, low fiber, engineered for overconsumption)

**Don't fear:**
- Potatoes (nutritious whole food; the problem is preparation — frying, loading with butter/sour cream — not the potato)
- White rice in moderation within an otherwise nutritious diet (traditional Asian diets include abundant white rice alongside other nutritious foods and have historically low metabolic disease rates)
- Any whole food carbohydrate source

## Epilogue

Carbohydrates are not the enemy. Refined, low-fiber carbohydrates consumed in excess contribute to metabolic disease. Whole food carbohydrates — vegetables, legumes, whole grains, fruit — are associated with better health outcomes across decades of research.

Choose the food, not the macronutrient. The food matrix matters far more than the carbohydrate content.
