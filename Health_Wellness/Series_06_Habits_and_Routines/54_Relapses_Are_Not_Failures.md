# Relapses Are Not Failures
## How to Return After You Fall Off Track

**Series 6: Habits and Routines — Book 4 of 10**

---

## Prologue

Elena quit smoking at 29. She had a cigarette at her best friend's wedding at 31 and then smoked irregularly for three months before stopping again. She had not "relapsed" — she had encountered a normal part of behavior change that is so common and so predictable that researchers build it into their models.

The central cognitive error in most people's relationship with behavior change is treating a lapse as evidence of failure rather than as a data point in a process. This cognitive error — not the lapse itself — is what causes relapse to become abandonment.

---

## Chapter 1: The Research

**The transtheoretical model and relapse:**

Prochaska and DiClemente's Transtheoretical Model (TTM) of behavior change explicitly includes relapse as a stage in the normal change process:
- Precontemplation → Contemplation → Preparation → Action → Maintenance → (Relapse) → [Return to Contemplation or Action]

Relapse — returning to the previous behavior pattern after a period of change — is not an indication that the person has failed the model. It is a predicted stage through which most people cycle multiple times before achieving stable maintenance.

**Cessation research:**

Research on smoking cessation, the most studied behavior change, finds:
- Most people who successfully quit smoking do so after 8-11 attempts
- Each prior attempt increases the probability of success in subsequent attempts
- People who have relapsed and returned have better long-term outcomes than people who have had fewer attempts (because each attempt builds skills and self-knowledge)

This finding applies across behavior change domains: dietary change, alcohol reduction, exercise, medication adherence.

## Chapter 2: The Mechanism

**The abstinence violation effect (AVE):**

The most significant mechanism by which lapse becomes relapse is the Abstinence Violation Effect, described by Marlatt and Gordon:

1. Person experiences a lapse (missed workout, one cigarette, one overeating episode)
2. Person experiences the lapse as a violation of a self-imposed rule
3. This violation triggers guilt, shame, and self-attribution ("I'm weak," "I can't do this")
4. The emotional distress from the attribution is uncomfortable
5. Continued behavior provides temporary relief from the distress
6. The original lapse becomes a full relapse

The critical point: it is not the lapse that causes relapse. It is the cognitive and emotional response to the lapse.

**The false dichotomy:**

Most people approach behavior change with an implicit all-or-nothing framework:
- "I'm on my diet" or "I'm off my diet"
- "I'm exercising" or "I've quit"
- "I'm in recovery" or "I've relapsed"

This binary framing makes a single deviation feel catastrophic (the whole effort has been "failed") and removes any middle ground between perfect adherence and complete abandonment.

## Chapter 3: Key Concepts

**Lapse vs. relapse:**

A *lapse* is a single deviation from the intended behavior — one missed workout, one unplanned high-calorie meal, one evening of scrolling instead of reading.

A *relapse* is a sustained return to the previous behavioral pattern — not exercising for weeks, returning to old eating patterns for months.

Lapses are normal and universal. Relapses are the result of how lapses are responded to.

**High-risk situations:**

Marlatt's research identified consistent categories of high-risk situations for relapse:
1. *Negative emotional states:* Stress, anger, frustration, boredom, anxiety triggering previous coping behaviors
2. *Social pressure:* Situations where peers or environment facilitate the old behavior
3. *Interpersonal conflict:* Arguments and relationship tension triggering emotional coping
4. *Positive emotional states:* Celebrations and reward situations where old behaviors are social context
5. *Testing personal control:* "Just once won't hurt" experimentation

Identifying your personal high-risk situations enables preparation rather than reactive response.

## Chapter 4: Signs and Symptoms

**Signs you're in relapse territory:**

*Early warning signs (lapse beginning to become relapse):*
- Thinking "I'll start again on Monday" (indefinite future restart)
- Using the lapse as justification for continued deviation ("since I've already blown it...")
- Feelings of shame that create avoidance of the topic
- Reducing contact with accountability supports (friends, apps, coaches)
- All-or-nothing thinking escalating

*Signs of healthy lapse management:*
- Noticing the lapse without catastrophizing
- Returning to the behavior at the next opportunity (not "next Monday")
- Treating the lapse as information ("what triggered it? How can I prepare better?")
- Maintaining identity ("I'm someone who [behavior]") through the lapse

## Chapter 5: Practical Interventions

**The immediate restart protocol:**

The most effective intervention for preventing lapse-to-relapse escalation: a pre-committed immediate restart plan.

Create in advance:
- "If I miss [behavior], my plan is to [return behavior] at [specific next opportunity]"
- "If I'm away from my routine for more than 3 days, my immediate return protocol is [specific reduced version of the habit]"

This plan is created when motivation is high, so it can be activated when motivation is low.

**Shrinking the return:**

After a lapse period, attempting to immediately return to the full pre-lapse behavior is often too demanding and sets up another failure. A shrunk return — a smaller, easier version of the behavior — restores momentum without demanding perfection.

If usual exercise is 45 minutes 4×/week and you haven't exercised in 3 weeks: return with 15-minute walks 3×/week, not 45 minutes 4 times immediately.

**The compassion buffer:**

Research on self-compassion and behavior change (Kristin Neff, Kelly McGonigal) consistently finds that self-compassion after a lapse produces better subsequent behavior than self-criticism. Self-criticism activates shame, which either drives avoidance or emotional eating/drinking/etc. as soothing. Self-compassion maintains motivation without shame-driven disruption.

## Chapter 6: Environmental Factors

**Environmental contributions to relapse:**

Most lapses are triggered by environmental changes:
- Travel (removes familiar cues and routines)
- Illness (valid reason to rest; risks becoming extended avoidance)
- Stressful work periods (time constraints + emotional state)
- Social environments that facilitate old behaviors (bar environments for alcohol, social eating occasions for dietary change)
- Seasonal changes (winter reduces outdoor activity, holiday periods disrupt food patterns)

Planning for these known environmental disruptions in advance:
- "When I travel for work, my modified protocol is..."
- "During the December holiday period, my expectations are..."
- "When I'm ill, I'll return to exercise within [X] days of feeling better"

## Chapter 7: Behavioral Approaches

**Relapse prevention planning:**

A proactive relapse prevention plan includes:
1. *Identify your high-risk situations:* When, where, and with whom are you most likely to lapse?
2. *Develop coping responses:* What will you do differently in those situations?
3. *Identify warning signs:* What thoughts, feelings, or behaviors signal you're moving toward a lapse?
4. *Create the return plan:* If a lapse occurs, what specifically will you do to return?

This planning, done when motivation is stable, provides a resource to draw on when motivation dips.

**Isabel's experience with behavior interruption:**

Isabel's RA diagnosis and subsequent medical treatment period produced a 6-week interruption in her exercise routine. She felt reluctant to return — the gap had created avoidance.

Her return strategy:
- Week 1: 15-minute walks only (not the strength routine she'd established)
- Week 2: Walks + 2 strength sessions (shorter than pre-interruption)
- Week 3: Return to full routine

She described the "shrunk return" as essential — attempting to return immediately to full intensity after the gap would have created another failure experience.

## Chapter 8: Cognitive Approaches

**Reframing the setback:**

Common unhelpful interpretations of lapses:
- "I can't do this" → "I'm learning what makes this difficult"
- "I've failed" → "I've had a lapse in an ongoing process"
- "I've undone all my progress" → "One interruption doesn't erase weeks of gains"
- "I'm not the kind of person who can change" → "This is difficult for everyone; difficulty is normal, not permanent"

**The curiosity response:**

Instead of judgment about the lapse: curiosity about it.
- What triggered it?
- What was I feeling or thinking just before?
- What environmental factors made it more likely?
- What can I adjust to make the same situation less likely to produce the same lapse?

This inquiry treats the lapse as useful data rather than shameful failure.

**The long view:**

Research on long-term health behavior shows that most successful maintainers have had multiple interruptions. The question is not whether interruptions occur but how quickly they're recovered from. A person who lapses and returns in 2 days outperforms a person who never lapses but also never sustains — simply because sustained imperfect behavior compounds differently than intermittent perfect behavior.

## Chapter 9: When to Seek Help

**When relapse patterns indicate underlying issues:**

Repeated relapse in specific behavioral domains can indicate:
- *Substance use:* Repeated relapse with alcohol, tobacco, or other substances may reflect physical dependence, not just willpower. Addiction medicine and dependency-specific behavioral treatments are appropriate.
- *Food and eating:* Repeated binge-restrict cycles with significant emotional component may indicate binge eating disorder or other eating pathology. Eating disorder treatment approaches differ from general behavior change approaches.
- *Gambling or screen use:* Behavioral addictions with relapse patterns may benefit from structured addiction-focused treatment.

**When the underlying driver needs treatment:**

If behavioral lapses are consistently driven by depression (low mood reducing motivation for any health behavior) or anxiety (avoidance patterns), treating the mental health condition is prerequisite to sustainable behavior change.

## Chapter 10: Your Protocol

**Your relapse prevention toolkit:**

*Pre-lapse preparation:*
- Written list of your top 3 high-risk situations for your key health habit
- Pre-committed response for each: "When [situation], I will [specific action]"
- Written immediate return protocol: "If I stop for [X days], I return by doing [reduced version]"

*Lapse response:*
1. Notice without catastrophizing ("This is a lapse, not a failure")
2. Identify the trigger (information, not judgment)
3. Activate the return protocol at the next available opportunity
4. Bring self-compassion to the process ("This is difficult; difficulty is normal")

*Long-term perspective:*
Keep a record not of lapses but of return days: the day you came back. These return days, accumulated over time, are the actual evidence of your behavior change capacity.

Elena's record after 2 years of health behavior work: 23 identified lapse periods; 23 returns. The lapses don't define her trajectory — the returns do.

---

## Epilogue

Behavior change is not a linear process. It is a spiral — returning to previous patterns, returning to change, advancing further, encountering challenges, returning again. Each cycle adds skill, self-knowledge, and capacity. The destination is not perfect behavior but durable behavior — behavior that persists through the inevitable interruptions, setbacks, and imperfections of an actual human life.
