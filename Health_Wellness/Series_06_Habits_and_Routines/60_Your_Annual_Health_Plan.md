# Your Annual Health Plan
## Building a Year of Intentional Health

**Series 6: Habits and Routines — Book 10 of 10**

## Prologue

Most people approach health reactively: they see a doctor when sick, think about sleep when exhausted, consider diet after a weight gain, and address exercise when they feel guilty. This reactive approach means that health receives attention only when it is already failing.

An annual health plan — a simple, deliberate review of where you are, where you want to be, and how you'll get there over the next 12 months — transforms health from reactive to intentional. It is not a rigid plan that must be followed perfectly. It is a framework for making deliberate choices and ensuring that the most impactful health priorities don't get indefinitely deferred.

## Chapter 1: The Research

**Goal setting and health behavior:**

The research on goal-setting theory (Locke and Latham) applied to health behavior finds:
- Specific goals produce better outcomes than general goals ("walk 30 minutes 4× per week" vs. "exercise more")
- Challenging but attainable goals produce better outcomes than easy goals
- Goals with scheduled review checkpoints are achieved at higher rates than goals without
- Written goals are achieved at higher rates than unwritten goals

**Planning for health behaviors:**

Implementation intentions — specific if-then plans for when and how behavior will occur — significantly improve health behavior follow-through. Annual planning that generates specific implementation intentions for the year's priorities outperforms general intention-setting.

## Chapter 2: The Mechanism

**Why health gets deprioritized:**

Health is chronically de-prioritized because most health behaviors:
- Produce benefits that are distant and uncertain (reduced disease risk decades from now)
- Compete with immediate demands that are pressing and certain (work, family, social obligations)
- Require upfront effort with delayed return

Without deliberate planning, health loses these competitions consistently. Annual planning creates a protected space where health priorities are set in advance — when motivation is available — so they don't have to be re-decided under pressure each day.

**The review cycle:**

Annual health reviews work because:
- One year is long enough for meaningful health change to occur
- Annual cadence aligns with natural planning cycles (new year, birthday, annual physical)
- Regular review allows course correction when behavior has drifted

## Chapter 3: Key Concepts

**The four domains of health to review:**

1. *Physical health:* Screenings current? Chronic conditions managed? Labs reviewed? Primary care relationship active?

2. *Movement and body:* Exercise habit established? Strength training included? Non-exercise movement adequate? Any pain or mobility limitations to address?

3. *Recovery and mental health:* Sleep quality adequate? Stress management in place? Mental health supported? Meaningful relationships active?

4. *Habits and nutrition:* Diet quality generally supportive of health goals? Major habit targets for the year? One habit to build, one to reduce?

**The annual health timeline:**

Some health behaviors have natural annual rhythms:
- Annual physical exam (if available)
- Annual flu vaccination (fall)
- Annual labs review
- Dental cleanings (typically biannual)
- Specialist follow-ups as scheduled
- Cancer screening schedules (by age/recommendation)

An annual plan maps these known recurrences and adds specific behavior targets for the year.

## Chapter 4: Signs and Symptoms

**Signs you need an annual health review:**

- You're not sure whether your routine screenings are up to date
- You don't have a primary care physician relationship
- You have a health goal you've been "meaning to address" for more than 6 months
- Your key health habit (exercise, sleep, diet) has eroded without a plan to restore it
- You're managing a chronic condition but haven't reviewed your management approach in a year

**Signs of effective annual health planning:**

- You know which screenings are current and when the next ones are due
- You have 1-3 specific health behavior targets for the year
- Your health behaviors have maintained reasonably through life events (illness, travel, work pressure) because you have recovery protocols
- You feel a sense of deliberate agency over your health rather than reactive management

## Chapter 5: Practical Interventions

**The annual health review — a practical template:**

*Part 1: Where am I? (Current state assessment)*

Medical:
- [ ] Annual physical exam completed or scheduled?
- [ ] Labs reviewed in the past year?
- [ ] Screenings current (cervical, colorectal, mammography, other)?
- [ ] Vaccinations current (flu, Tdap, Shingrix if ≥50)?
- [ ] Chronic conditions: management optimized? Medications reviewed?
- [ ] Specialist follow-ups completed?
- [ ] Dental: cleanings current?

Physical:
- [ ] Exercise habit: current frequency and type?
- [ ] Sleep: average hours, quality, and major disruptions?
- [ ] Diet quality: typical day's eating pattern?
- [ ] Weight/body composition: significant change from last year?

Mental and social:
- [ ] Stress levels: manageable, concerning, or high?
- [ ] Mental health: anxiety, depression, or burnout addressed?
- [ ] Social connection: adequate quality and frequency?

*Part 2: Where do I want to be? (Priority-setting)*

Choose 1-3 health priorities for the year. Resist the temptation to list 10 — 3 is a maximum; 1-2 is optimal.

For each priority, write:
- What does success look like at year end?
- What is my first step?
- What is my most likely obstacle?
- What is my obstacle response?

*Part 3: What do I need to schedule? (Implementation)*

- Schedule the medical appointments that are overdue or coming due
- Write the implementation intentions for your habit targets
- Identify the quarter or month for each major health action

## Chapter 6: Environmental Factors

**Health support infrastructure:**

An annual plan is only executable if the support infrastructure exists:
- Primary care physician relationship (if you don't have one, establishing this is often the most impactful priority for a year)
- Insurance understanding (what is covered? What are the deductibles and out-of-pocket?)
- Access to exercise (gym membership, home equipment, outdoor access)
- Workplace health benefits (some employers offer health coaching, gym subsidies, mental health benefits that are underused)

Annual review is the right time to assess whether your health support infrastructure is adequate.

## Chapter 7: Behavioral Approaches

**Quarterly check-ins:**

Annual plans benefit from quarterly review — short check-ins (15-20 minutes) that assess:
- What's working? (Continue and reinforce)
- What's not? (Adjust — not abandon)
- What needs attention this quarter? (Upcoming medical appointments, health behaviors that have drifted)

The quarterly check-in converts an annual intention into a living document that is actually used.

**The behavior maintenance question:**

For health behaviors already established: the review question is not "what should I start?" but "what do I need to do to maintain what's working?"

Maintenance is under-planned. Most annual health planning focuses on new behaviors; existing behaviors drift without review. Explicitly including maintenance strategies in the annual plan significantly improves durable health behavior.

## Chapter 8: Cognitive Approaches

**The planning fallacy:**

The planning fallacy (Kahneman and Tversky) describes the consistent human tendency to underestimate how much time tasks will take and overestimate motivation availability. Annual health planning is subject to this bias — people typically plan to change more than is realistic.

Realistic annual health planning:
- Choose fewer goals (the planning fallacy leads people to choose too many)
- Be explicit about obstacles and responses
- Plan for imperfect adherence rather than perfect adherence
- Build in recovery plans for lapses

**Health as identity project:**

The most durable health change over a year is typically the shift in health identity — from "someone working on their health" to "someone who takes care of their health as a normal part of life." This identity shift happens incrementally through the accumulation of small, consistent behaviors over the year.

Annual planning supports identity development by creating the consistent, recurring behaviors that gradually redefine how you see yourself in relation to health.

## Chapter 9: When to Seek Help

**When planning isn't sufficient:**

Annual planning is a self-management tool. It is not appropriate as the primary tool when:
- Mental health conditions (depression, anxiety, eating disorders) are significantly impairing health behavior — treat first, plan second
- Chronic disease management requires active medical supervision — the plan supports medical management, not replaces it
- Substance use disorders are present — addiction-specific treatment is the priority

**Health coaching:**

For people who benefit from structure and accountability, health coaching (distinct from personal training) provides:
- Regular check-ins on health goals
- Evidence-based behavior change support
- Accountability structures
- Support navigating the healthcare system

Evidence on health coaching finds modest but consistent improvement in health behaviors, chronic disease management, and patient satisfaction.

## Chapter 10: Your Protocol

**The three characters' annual plans:**

**Elena's Year 2 health plan:**

*Where am I:* Sleep excellent (7.5-8hrs). Exercise routine stable (30-min walks + 3×/week strength). Anxiety managed (CBT, no medication). Screenings current. Blood pressure 124/76.

*Priorities this year:*
1. Increase strength training to 4×/week (current 3× is good; upgrading)
2. Establish meal prep Sunday routine (diet quality is good but ad hoc)
3. Complete CPR certification (pragmatic prevention goal)

*Implementation:* Strength training added Thursday; meal prep anchored to Sunday 6pm; CPR class scheduled for March.

**Ruben's Year 2 health plan:**

*Where am I:* Pre-diabetes reversed (HbA1c 5.4%). BP normalized (124/78). Exercise established (daily walk + 3×/week strength). Sleep regular. Social connection improved.

*Priorities this year:*
1. Increase social connection quality (has acquaintances; wants closer friendships)
2. Annual labs in April (glucose follow-up, full panel)
3. Maintain exercise through upcoming busy project period (October-November risk)

*Implementation:* Join a recreational basketball league (social + physical); labs scheduled April 15; October exercise plan: reduce to 15-min minimum protocol but maintain the habit.

**Isabel's Year 2 health plan:**

*Where am I:* RA stable. Depression managed (PHQ-9 score 4). Sleep apnea treated. Exercise routine (swimming + walking + yoga). All screenings current. DEXA T-score -1.8 (monitoring).

*Priorities this year:*
1. Bone density protection: increase calcium and weight-bearing exercise component
2. Annual ophthalmology (hydroxychloroquine eye monitoring — scheduled)
3. Advance directive review (update beneficiary information)

*Implementation:* Added 3rd weight-bearing strength session; calcium supplementation reviewed with physician; ophthalmology appointment March; advance directive review scheduled with attorney April.

**Your annual health review:**

Set aside 1 hour. Use the template above. Write your answers. Identify 1-2 priorities. Schedule the appointments. Review quarterly.

This hour is among the highest-ROI health investments you'll make all year.

## Epilogue

Health does not happen by accident. It is the product of a thousand decisions, made daily over years, some large and most small. An annual health plan does not guarantee health — nothing can. But it converts health from something that happens to you into something you actively shape. The plan doesn't need to be perfect. It needs to be done.

Here's to the year ahead.
