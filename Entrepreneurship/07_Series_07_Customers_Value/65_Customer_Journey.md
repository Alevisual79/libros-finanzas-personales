## Prologue

When Christina analyzed the eighteen months of customer data from her online learning platform for graphic designers, she found something initially puzzling: her most satisfied customers — measured by survey scores, completion rates, and the enthusiastic testimonials they provided — were not the customers who were most likely to renew their annual subscription. The renewal rate for customers who gave her platform a nine or ten out of ten satisfaction score was only sixty-eight percent. The renewal rate for customers who gave eight or nine satisfaction scores was seventy-two percent.

The seemingly paradoxical finding — that the most enthusiastic customers were not the most likely to renew — pointed to something in the customer journey that satisfaction surveys couldn't capture. The customers who were most satisfied with the learning experience were the ones who had made the most progress. They had completed the courses, developed the skills, and achieved the design capability goals that had motivated their original subscription. And having achieved those goals, they no longer felt they needed the subscription.

The customers who were slightly less satisfied — who had made significant but incomplete progress — still had reasons to stay. The customer journey toward skill mastery had not ended for them; it had paused.

## Chapter 1: The Customer Journey Concept

The customer journey — the complete sequence of experiences a customer has with a business, from initial awareness through consideration, purchase, use, and (in the ideal case) loyal advocacy — is the framework that reveals how the business appears and feels from the customer's perspective across every touchpoint, not only the interactions the business most deliberately manages.

The customer journey matters for business design because: the quality of any individual touchpoint is perceived in the context of all other touchpoints (a great product experience can be undermined by a difficult purchase experience; a poor initial experience may prevent a prospective customer from ever discovering that the product is excellent); the gaps between touchpoints — the moments when the customer is between interactions with the business — create anxiety or confidence depending on what is communicated (or not) during those gaps; and the cumulative experience of the journey, not any single interaction, determines whether the customer becomes an advocate who drives new customer acquisition through referral.

Research by Forrester and McKinsey on customer journey management finds that the businesses that systematically measure and improve the customer journey outperform those that optimize individual touchpoints in isolation on both customer satisfaction and revenue growth. The insight: no touchpoint exists in isolation, and the journey connecting them is the experience that ultimately determines customer loyalty.

## Chapter 2: Mapping the Customer Journey

Customer journey mapping — the visual representation of the complete customer experience across all stages and touchpoints — is the research and design exercise that reveals the journey the customer actually experiences, as distinct from the journey the business intends to provide.

The customer journey map structure: organized by stages (the major phases of the customer's experience — awareness, consideration, purchase, onboarding, use, renewal or advocacy); for each stage, the map captures the customer's actions (what they do), thoughts (what they're thinking), feelings (how they feel), and touchpoints (the specific interactions with the business that occur at each stage). The map also typically captures the "moments of truth" — the highest-stakes interactions where the customer's experience most powerfully shapes their overall perception.

The customer journey map most valuable for business improvement: the one built from actual customer research (interviews, surveys, and behavioral data) rather than from the business's assumptions about the customer experience. The journey the business believes it provides and the journey the customer actually experiences are often significantly different, and the most important improvements emerge from the gap between the two.

## Chapter 3: The Awareness Stage

The awareness stage — the period in which the prospective customer first becomes aware that the business exists — is the stage over which the business has the least control and the least direct interaction, because the customer's awareness is formed through channels that the business does not manage: word of mouth, search results, social media, industry publications, and the informal recommendations of peers and colleagues.

The awareness stage experience from the customer's perspective: the customer is not yet thinking about the business; they are thinking about their own problem and searching for potential solutions. The business's goal in the awareness stage is to be visible in the places where the customer searches for solutions and to present itself in terms that immediately signal relevance.

The journey map insight for awareness stage optimization: understanding where the target customer's awareness of solutions is typically formed (which search terms they use, which communities they participate in, which publications they read, which peers they trust for recommendations) and ensuring that the business is visible and recognizable in those specific contexts — not in every context, but in the specific contexts that the target customer's awareness journey actually traverses.

## Chapter 4: The Consideration Stage

The consideration stage — the period in which the prospective customer is actively evaluating the business against alternatives — is the stage where the quality of the information experience is most critical. The customer is trying to make a decision and needs specific information to make it well: whether the business is right for their situation, what the engagement experience will be like, what results they can realistically expect, and whether the price is justified.

The consideration stage experience from the customer's perspective: the customer is doing research, and the quality of that research experience determines whether they arrive at the sales conversation with confidence and intent or with confusion and hesitance. The business that provides clear, specific, and honest information that addresses the actual questions the customer is asking in the consideration stage — rather than providing marketing messaging that doesn't answer those questions — makes the consideration stage easier to navigate and more likely to result in a purchase decision.

The journey map insight for consideration stage optimization: understanding the specific questions that prospective customers in the consideration stage are trying to answer, and ensuring that those questions are answered clearly in the business's marketing and sales materials — on the website, in the proposal, in case studies, and in the sales conversation. The consideration stage question most commonly unanswered: "Is this the right fit for my specific situation?" The business that answers this question specifically and honestly — including by saying when it is not the right fit — builds more trust in the consideration stage than the business that answers only with positive claims.

---

## Chapter 5: The Purchase Stage

The purchase stage — the moment when the prospective customer makes the decision to buy and executes the transaction — should be designed to be as frictionless as possible, because friction at the purchase moment is the highest-cost friction in the customer journey: the customer has already decided to buy and any obstacle between that decision and its execution risks losing a customer who was already committed.

The purchase stage experience from the customer's perspective: the customer wants the process to be quick, clear, and easy. They want to know exactly what they're buying, what it will cost, what happens next, and how to proceed. Any ambiguity, any unexpected complication, or any delay in the process creates doubt that can reverse a decision that had already been made.

The purchase stage friction sources most commonly overlooked by small businesses: a proposal process that takes longer to produce than the customer expected (creating anxiety about the business's responsiveness); a contract or agreement that is more complex or one-sided than the customer expected (creating anxiety about the relationship); and a payment process that is inconvenient or confusing (creating practical obstacles to completing the transaction). Each of these frictions reduces the conversion rate from decision to purchase and undermines the customer's initial experience of the business.

## Chapter 6: The Onboarding Stage

The onboarding stage — the period immediately following the purchase when the customer is beginning to engage with the business's product or service for the first time — is the highest-stakes stage of the customer journey for retention, because research consistently finds that customers form the perceptions and habits during the onboarding period that determine whether they become long-term users or early churners.

The onboarding stage experience from the customer's perspective: the customer has made a commitment based on expectations formed in the consideration stage, and the onboarding experience either confirms or disconfirms those expectations. The customer who finds the onboarding experience to be exactly as expected — or better than expected — is on the path to retention and advocacy. The customer who finds the onboarding experience to be different from, more difficult than, or less valuable than expected is on the path to churn.

The onboarding design investment with the highest retention return: identifying the "aha moment" — the specific experience that most powerfully confirms the customer's decision to purchase — and designing the onboarding to deliver that moment as quickly as possible. For Christina's learning platform, the aha moment was completing a first design project that used a newly learned technique. The redesigned onboarding guided new subscribers to complete a specific project within the first two weeks; the completion of that project was the experience that converted uncertain new subscribers into committed users.

## Chapter 7: The Ongoing Experience Stage

The ongoing experience stage — the extended period during which the customer uses the product or service regularly — is the stage that determines whether the initial satisfaction persists, deepens, or erodes over time. The customer's experience in this stage is shaped by the cumulative quality of the product or service, the rhythm of the business's engagement with the customer, and the evolution of the customer's needs relative to the product's or service's ability to serve those evolving needs.

The ongoing experience journey risks: the plateau effect (the customer's experience becomes routine and loses the sense of discovery and progress that generated early enthusiasm); the evolving needs gap (the customer's needs evolve and the business's offering doesn't keep pace); and the competing attention problem (other priorities and demands crowd out the engagement that would maintain the customer's progress and commitment).

Christina's insight from her puzzling renewal data: the customers who churned despite high satisfaction scores had plateaued — they had achieved the specific goals that had motivated their subscription and had no new goals clear enough to justify continued engagement. The solution was to redesign the ongoing experience to proactively surface new skill development opportunities to customers who had completed specific learning milestones — turning every completion into the beginning of a new learning goal rather than an endpoint.

## Chapter 8: The Renewal Decision Stage

For subscription businesses, the renewal decision stage — the period when the customer is deciding whether to continue their subscription — is the moment where the entirety of the customer's journey experience is summarized in a single decision. The customer who has had a consistently valuable, progressively deepening experience is naturally inclined to renew; the customer who feels they have extracted the primary value and doesn't see continuing value ahead is naturally inclined to discontinue.

The renewal decision influence factors: the customer's assessment of the value they received in the prior period (have they gotten what they expected?); the customer's anticipation of the value they expect in the coming period (is there something ahead that justifies continued commitment?); the renewal friction (how easy or difficult is the renewal process, and are there renewal incentives that tip the decision?); and the customer's perception of the alternatives (is there a competitor that seems to offer better value for their current needs?).

The proactive renewal management practice: identify the customers who are approaching renewal with lower engagement levels — lower usage frequency, fewer completed projects, fewer feature engagements — and reach out specifically to understand whether their needs are being met and to surface the value they might not have discovered. The customer who churns is almost always visibly disengaging before the renewal date; the business that monitors engagement and responds to disengagement with proactive outreach retains a meaningful proportion of the customers who would otherwise churn.

---

## Chapter 9: The Advocacy Stage

The advocacy stage — when the customer moves beyond satisfaction to active, voluntary promotion of the business to their network — is the highest-value stage of the customer journey, because the customer who advocates generates referrals that produce new customers who are better quality and less expensive to acquire than customers from any paid marketing channel.

The advocacy stage experience from the customer's perspective: the customer advocates when they feel genuinely grateful for the value they have received, when they feel confident that the business will provide equivalent value to the people they refer, and when the act of advocating is easy (they know what to say and who might benefit from the same experience). Advocacy is not primarily a function of how much the customer likes the business; it is a function of how naturally and confidently they can recommend it to the right people.

The advocacy enablement investment: making it easy for satisfied customers to refer (a specific, simple referral ask that makes the potential referral's situation clear), providing the language they need (a brief description of who benefits most from the business that the customer can use in referral conversations), and following up when a referral has been made (to thank the customer and report on the outcome, which reinforces the advocacy behavior).

## Chapter 10: The Journey as System

The customer journey is not a linear sequence but a system: each stage influences the next, earlier experiences shape the interpretation of later ones, and the cumulative quality of the full journey determines the outcome (retention and advocacy) more than any single stage's experience. The business that designs and manages the journey as a system — ensuring that each stage reinforces the others and that the transitions between stages are deliberately designed rather than incidentally experienced — produces better customer outcomes than the business that optimizes stages in isolation.

Christina's journey system redesign: she mapped the complete journey of a new subscriber from discovery through renewal, identified the three highest-impact friction points (the unclear onboarding path, the plateau that high-completing subscribers hit at month four, and the passive renewal process that didn't proactively address churn risk), and redesigned each. Two years after the redesign, her annual renewal rate was eighty-four percent (up from sixty-eight percent for her most satisfied subscribers), her advocacy rate was forty-six percent (up from thirty-one percent), and her new subscriber acquisition through customer referrals had increased by sixty percent.

The customer journey is the business experienced from the outside. Designing it well — with the customer's experience at every stage as the primary design criterion — is one of the highest-return investments a business makes, because it determines whether the customers the business works so hard to acquire stay, grow, and bring their peers.

## Epilogue

Christina's puzzling renewal data turned out to be the most valuable information her business had ever produced, because it revealed the gap in her journey design that was preventing her most engaged customers from remaining her most loyal customers. The customers who had gotten the most value from the platform were the ones leaving it — because the journey had no pathway beyond achievement. When she designed that pathway, they had a reason to stay. The customer journey is not something that happens to customers; it is something designed for them. The design determines the destination.
