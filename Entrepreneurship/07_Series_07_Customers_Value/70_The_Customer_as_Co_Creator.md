# The Customer as Co-Creator
## Involving your customers in building what they need

**Series 07: Customers and Value — Book 10 of 10**

---

## Prologue

After four years of building his supply chain analytics platform, Daniel had developed a clear philosophy about how product decisions should be made: gather customer feedback, filter it through the team's understanding of the market, and build what the product team believed would be most valuable. Customers provided the inputs; the product team made the decisions. It was a reasonable philosophy, and it produced reasonable results.

Then he hired a new head of product who introduced a practice he initially resisted: monthly customer co-design sessions in which a small group of selected customers worked directly with the product team on specific design problems — not describing what they wanted, but actively participating in the design of the solutions. The sessions were uncomfortable at first. Customers brought assumptions the team didn't share; the team brought constraints the customers didn't know about. The friction was productive.

Over eighteen months, the co-design sessions produced three major platform features that became the primary reasons new customers chose Daniel's software over competitors. None of the three would have been built — or built in the form they took — without the co-design process. The customers who participated in the sessions had not only contributed ideas; they had shaped the design in ways that reflected the actual workflow of supply chain professionals in ways that the product team, without that firsthand expertise, could not have achieved alone.

## Chapter 1: The Co-Creation Concept

Customer co-creation — the involvement of customers in the design, development, or improvement of the products or services they consume — is a practice with deep roots in academic research (C.K. Prahalad and Venkat Ramaswamy's 2000 Harvard Business Review paper "Co-opting Customer Competence" established its theoretical foundation) and increasingly widespread application in businesses from enterprise software companies to small craft producers.

The co-creation premise: the customer is not only the consumer of value but a potential contributor to its creation, because the customer possesses expertise in their own experience — in how the product is actually used, what frictions arise in real-world conditions, and what outcomes would be genuinely valuable — that the business cannot fully access through observation or research alone. Involving the customer in the creation process converts that expertise from an external input to an internal design resource.

The distinction between customer co-creation and customer feedback: feedback is retrospective (what do you think of what we've built?) and typically produces incremental input; co-creation is prospective (help us design what we should build) and produces genuinely different outcomes because the customer's expertise shapes the design rather than only evaluating it. The product built with deep customer co-creation involvement is qualitatively different from the product built from customer feedback.

## Chapter 2: The Forms of Customer Co-Creation

Customer co-creation takes different forms depending on the nature of the business, the depth of customer involvement, and the specific value the business is trying to create. The major forms range from the lightest (open feedback and ideation) to the deepest (formal product development partnerships).

Open innovation and crowdsourced ideation: structured programs that invite the customer base to contribute ideas, vote on proposed features or products, and identify the most important needs and opportunities. Platforms like UserVoice and ProductBoard formalize this process; the result is a ranked understanding of what the customer base most wants that provides both input for prioritization and evidence that specific requests reflect genuine broad demand rather than individual preferences.

Customer advisory boards: a structured group of selected customers who meet regularly with the business's leadership and product team to provide strategic guidance, react to product direction decisions, and share their experience and expertise. The customer advisory board is the most common form of deep customer co-creation in professional service and software businesses; its value comes from the sustained relationship and the trusted candor that develops when the same customers engage repeatedly over time.

Beta and pilot programs: involving customers in the testing and refinement of new products or features before they are released to the broader customer base. Beta participants provide real-world usage data and qualitative feedback in conditions more realistic than internal testing can produce; the relationship created in the beta process also tends to produce the most enthusiastic advocates when the product launches.

Co-design sessions: direct collaborative design work with customers, in which business and customer work together on specific design problems — not with the customer describing what they want but with both parties actively engaged in the design process. This form produces the deepest customer influence on the product and the strongest relationship between the business and the participating customers.

## Chapter 3: Selecting Co-Creation Partners

The quality of co-creation outcomes depends substantially on the selection of which customers participate. The co-creation participants who generate the most valuable insight and contribution are not necessarily the most vocal customers, the largest customers, or the most satisfied customers — they are the customers who most accurately represent the business's ideal customer profile, who have the domain expertise and the engagement level to contribute substantively, and who are genuinely motivated to help the business improve rather than primarily to influence it toward their own preferences.

The co-creation partner selection criteria: representative of the target customer (the participant pool should reflect the diversity of the ideal customer population, not only the most accessible or most enthusiastic customers, so that the co-creation outcomes serve the full customer base rather than a specific subgroup); sufficient engagement and expertise (participants must use the product or service enough and with enough sophistication to have genuine insight about how it functions in their context); and willingness to contribute honestly (the most valuable co-creation participants are those who will candidly identify problems and limitations, not only affirm what is already being built well).

Daniel's co-design session participant selection: he chose customers at different company sizes (to represent the range of his target market), in different industries (to capture the contextual variation in how supply chain analytics is used), and at different stages of product adoption (novice users reveal onboarding friction that expert users have long since forgotten; expert users reveal the advanced use cases that represent the product's highest-value applications). The diversity of participant profiles prevented the co-design sessions from optimizing for any single customer type.

## Chapter 4: Facilitation and Structure

The co-creation session or process that is poorly facilitated — where the customer's contribution is constrained to reactive responses, where the business's existing assumptions dominate the discussion, or where the power dynamics between business and customer prevent candid challenge — produces less valuable outcomes than well-facilitated sessions that create genuine collaborative conditions.

The facilitation principles that produce the most valuable co-creation outcomes: structured open-endedness (providing enough structure that the conversation is productive and specific, while allowing enough openness that the customer's authentic perspective can surface); assumption transparency (explicitly surfacing the business's current assumptions about the design problem, which allows the customer to challenge them rather than unknowingly working within their constraints); and productive divergence (designing the session to explore the full range of possibilities before converging on solutions, rather than immediately evaluating the business's existing proposals).

The common facilitation failure in customer co-creation: the session that looks like co-creation but is actually customer validation — the business has already decided what it's going to build and uses the session to build support for that decision rather than to genuinely explore alternatives. Customers in these sessions learn quickly that their input is not genuinely valued, and the relationship damage from that experience typically outweighs the validation benefit.

## Chapter 5: Co-Creating Services

While most co-creation discussion focuses on product development, service co-creation — the collaborative design of how a service is delivered — is often more accessible and more impactful for small service businesses, because service design is inherently less capital-intensive to change than product development.

The service co-creation opportunities most available to small businesses: onboarding design (involving new clients in the design of their own onboarding experience reveals the specific elements most important to them and creates co-ownership of the onboarding success); service delivery protocol (asking clients to participate in designing the rhythm, format, and content of the ongoing service relationship produces service design that fits the client's actual work context rather than the business's operational convenience); and service recovery design (asking clients who experienced service failures to participate in designing the recovery process creates solutions that address the actual experience of the failure rather than the business's assumptions about it).

The service co-creation dynamic: service co-creation is the most direct form of co-creation because the customer's participation in the design of their own service experience is immediately visible and tangible — they are not contributing to an abstract product that will be built later; they are designing the experience they will have beginning immediately. This immediacy makes the outcome more personal and the engagement more genuine.

## Chapter 6: Co-Creation and Customer Loyalty

The research on co-creation and customer loyalty consistently finds that customers who participate meaningfully in the design or development of a product or service are significantly more loyal than non-participating customers: they have higher retention rates, higher NPS scores, and higher rates of expansion and referral.

The psychological mechanism underlying this loyalty effect: the endowment effect (people value things more when they have contributed to their creation — Daniel Kahneman's research on loss aversion includes evidence that investment in creation, not only ownership, generates attachment); social identity (customers who have participated in co-creation identify with the business's purpose and success in a way that non-participating customers don't, which makes the customer's relationship with the business partly an expression of their own identity); and the reciprocity norm (customers who feel that the business has genuinely valued their input and incorporated it into the product feel an obligation of reciprocal engagement that passive customers don't).

The loyalty effect of co-creation in Daniel's business: the thirty-two customers who participated in co-design sessions over the first eighteen months had a one hundred percent annual retention rate, compared to seventy-nine percent for the non-participating customer base. They also referred new customers at four times the rate of non-participants and expanded their product usage at two point three times the rate. Co-creation had produced a cohort of customers who were not only more satisfied but more invested in the product's success.

## Chapter 7: Managing Co-Creation Expectations

The co-creation process creates an implicit commitment: the customer who invests their time and expertise in the design of a product or service reasonably expects that their contribution will be reflected in the outcome. The business that solicits co-creation input but doesn't act on it — or doesn't communicate clearly about what was incorporated and what wasn't, and why — damages the relationship and makes future co-creation less likely.

The co-creation communication obligations: after each co-creation session or program, communicate specifically about what was learned, which insights will be incorporated into the product or service, and what the timeline is for that incorporation. For insights that won't be incorporated, explain honestly why — whether because of technical constraints, business priorities, or the conflict with other customers' needs. The customer who understands why their suggestion wasn't implemented respects the decision; the customer who never receives any response assumes their input wasn't valued.

The co-creation feedback loop: creating a systematic process for reporting back to co-creation participants when their contributions have been incorporated into the product — a "you suggested this, we built it" communication — closes the loop in a way that reinforces participation in future sessions and generates the advocacy that makes co-creation participants the business's most enthusiastic promoters.

## Chapter 8: Co-Creation and Innovation

The most significant commercial benefit of deep customer co-creation is often the innovation it produces: the genuinely novel product or service design that wouldn't have been developed without the customer's authentic domain expertise and the collaborative design process that makes that expertise usable.

The innovation dynamic of co-creation: the customer who participates in the design process contributes expertise about the domain (how supply chain management actually works in a real organization, with all its complexity and political reality) that the business's internal team cannot fully replicate. The product team contributes expertise about the technical possibilities and constraints of the platform. The intersection of these two knowledge sets — domain expertise and technical capability, in genuine collaborative design dialogue — produces solutions that neither party would have developed alone.

Daniel's co-design innovation outcomes: the three features produced by the co-design sessions that became the primary competitive differentiators were all built around the intersection of domain knowledge and technical capability that neither the customers nor the product team could have contributed alone. The most impactful feature — a workflow integration that embedded the analytics platform directly into the supply chain team's daily planning routine — emerged from a co-design session in which a customer described in detail how their team actually used data in their daily planning process. The product team had never observed this workflow before; the customer had never imagined that the technical integration was possible.

## Chapter 9: Scaling Co-Creation

The co-creation practices most effective at small scale — personal sessions with individual customers — require adaptation as the business and customer base grow, because the bandwidth of the business's leadership and product team limits the number of direct co-creation engagements possible.

The co-creation scaling approaches: tiered co-creation programs (intensive co-design sessions with a small number of strategic customers; lighter-touch advisory board engagement with a larger group; open ideation programs for the full customer base — different depth for different segments); digital co-creation tools (platforms that enable asynchronous co-creation at scale — online communities where customers discuss and vote on design ideas, collaborative prototyping tools that allow customers to interact with designs before they're built); and the superuser program (a selected group of highly engaged customers who serve as ongoing co-creation partners, receiving early access to new features in exchange for systematic feedback and design collaboration).

The co-creation culture that sustains scaling: the business that builds customer co-creation into its operating culture — making customer involvement in product decisions the norm rather than the exception — maintains the co-creation benefit as it grows. The business that treats co-creation as a series of discrete events rather than an ongoing cultural practice loses the benefit when the events conclude.

## Chapter 10: The Co-Creator Relationship

The relationship between a business and its most engaged co-creation partners is qualitatively different from the typical vendor-customer relationship: it is genuinely collaborative, mutually beneficial, and characterized by the mutual respect and trust that characterize genuine partnerships rather than commercial transactions.

The co-creator relationship produces outcomes for both parties that neither could achieve alone: the business benefits from the customer's domain expertise and the product validation that genuine co-creation provides; the customer benefits from the influence they exercise over a tool they depend on, the relationship with the people building it, and the satisfaction of seeing their ideas become reality in ways that serve their colleagues and peers.

Daniel's co-creator relationships three years after the program's launch: the thirty-two original co-design session participants remained among his most engaged customers, his most active community contributors, and his most enthusiastic referral sources. Several had become publicly associated with his product's success — speaking at his annual conference, writing case studies about how the features they helped design had transformed their team's performance. The relationship was not a vendor-customer relationship in any normal sense; it was a genuine partnership in which the customers were invested in the product's success almost as personally as Daniel was.

The customer as co-creator is the customer at their highest state of engagement: not only consuming the value the business provides but contributing to the creation of it, invested in its success because they have invested themselves in its development. Building those relationships — deliberately, sustainably, and with genuine respect for the customer's expertise and investment — is among the highest-value activities a customer-focused business can engage in. It is the full realization of what customer value means: not only the value the customer receives, but the value the customer helps create.

---

## Epilogue

Daniel's initial resistance to co-design sessions was understandable: they felt inefficient, uncomfortable, and uncertain in their outcomes. What he discovered was that the discomfort was the feature, not the bug — the discomfort came from the genuine collision of different perspectives, different expertise, and different assumptions that produced genuinely different outcomes. The product built in comfortable internal sessions, where everyone shared the same assumptions, was the product his customers adapted to. The product built in uncomfortable co-design sessions, where the customers' expertise disrupted the team's assumptions, was the product his customers felt they had made. That distinction — adapting to versus making — is the difference between a product customers use and a product they champion.
