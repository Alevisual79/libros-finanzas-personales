## Seeing the world through your customer's eyes

### Prologue
For two years, Natalie had run her legal technology software company for small law firms with a deep conviction that her customers' primary struggle was time: that the documentation, billing, and scheduling tasks that consumed so much of small firm lawyers' hours were the problem she was solving. Her product was built around time savings, and her marketing led with time savings. "Save twelve hours per week" was her primary value proposition.

Her conversion rates were mediocre. Her trial-to-paid conversion was eleven percent, and her renewal rate after the first year was sixty-two percent. The product worked; the lawyers who used it said so. But something in how she was communicating its value — or in what problem she was positioning it to solve — was clearly not resonating with the majority of the lawyers who tried it.

She hired a UX researcher to conduct a series of customer interviews, not about the product but about the experience of running a small law practice. What the researcher found reshaped Natalie's understanding of her customer. The primary pain that small firm lawyers experienced was not time scarcity — it was anxiety: specifically, the persistent worry that something important would fall through the cracks. A missed deadline. A billing error that a client would find and question. A case management detail overlooked under the pressure of managing too many matters simultaneously. Time was a surface symptom. Anxiety about professional consequences was the underlying emotion.

## Chapter 1: What an Empathy Map Is

An empathy map is a visualization tool that organizes what is known about a specific type of customer's experience — their thoughts, feelings, behaviors, and statements — into a structured format that enables the business to understand and communicate the customer's perspective rather than only the business's own perspective about the customer.

The original empathy map format, developed by Dave Gray at XPLANE and popularized in design thinking practice, organizes the customer's experience into four quadrants: what they Say (the words and phrases they use to describe their experience, their problems, and their goals — their actual language, not translated or interpreted); what they Think (the thoughts they have, including the ones they might not say aloud — their internal narrative, their doubts, their assumptions); what they Do (the specific behaviors and actions that characterize their situation — the workarounds they use, the habits they've formed, the decisions they make); and what they Feel (the emotional state that underlies all of the above — the anxieties, frustrations, aspirations, and satisfactions that color their experience).

The empathy map's value: it forces the business to articulate the customer's experience in the customer's terms, from the inside, rather than from the business's outside perspective. The business that has completed a genuine empathy map exercise for its target customer has access to a qualitatively different kind of understanding than the business that has only described the customer's demographics and needs from an external vantage point.

## Chapter 2: What Customers Say

The "Say" quadrant captures the customer's actual language: the specific words and phrases they use when describing their situation, their problems, and what they're looking for. This language is both a research artifact (revealing what the customer is experiencing) and a marketing resource (the customer's own words are more credible and resonant in marketing materials than the business's invented descriptions of the customer's experience).

Collecting the "Say" data requires listening to customers in contexts where they're talking about their experiences authentically: customer interviews, community forums and social media discussions, review sites, customer support conversations, and the comments customers make in sales conversations. The researcher's job is to record the customer's actual language without editing or interpreting it — to preserve the specific words that capture the customer's experience.

Natalie's "Say" data from her UX researcher's interviews: lawyers consistently used phrases like "I'm always worried I missed something," "I never feel like I have a handle on everything," "there's always a ball in the air," and "I can't turn my brain off at the end of the day because I'm not sure I've done everything I needed to." These phrases didn't contain the word "time" at all. They were about vigilance, anxiety, and the feeling of carrying too many things in working memory simultaneously.

## Chapter 3: What Customers Think

The "Think" quadrant captures the customer's internal narrative — the thoughts they have but don't necessarily express aloud, including doubts, concerns, assumptions, and aspirations that may be too vulnerable, too uncertain, or too complex to share in casual conversation.

Accessing what customers think requires research methods that create the conditions for more candid sharing than normal social interaction allows: one-on-one interviews with an empathetic, non-judgmental researcher; anonymous surveys that remove the social pressure to present positively; and the ethnographic observation of customers in their natural working environment, where their unguarded behavior reveals internal states that they wouldn't articulate if asked directly.

The internal thoughts most commonly hidden in customer research: concerns about professional competence or status (the customer who is anxious about whether they are good enough at their work rarely volunteers this anxiety); financial concerns that feel shameful to acknowledge; skepticism about new solutions based on past disappointments; and the gap between what the customer values and what they think they're supposed to value (the lawyer who values client relationship quality above billing efficiency but feels they should be more focused on profitability).

Natalie's "Think" data: the lawyers her researcher interviewed thought about the professional consequences of mistakes in ways that they almost never verbalized — the fear of a bar complaint, the anxiety about a client who was already unhappy, the worry about a deadline that was approaching faster than they were ready for. These thoughts were operating constantly in the background of their workday, creating the anxiety that surfaced in their "Say" data.

## Chapter 4: What Customers Do

The "Do" quadrant captures the customer's actual behaviors — the specific actions they take, the workarounds they've developed, and the habits that characterize their daily experience of the problem the business addresses. Behavioral data is particularly valuable because behavior reveals priorities more reliably than stated preferences do: what customers say they value and what they actually do often diverge.

Behavioral research methods: direct observation (watching the customer perform relevant tasks in their actual environment — the most accurate source of behavioral data because it eliminates the retrospective distortion that memory introduces); usage data analysis (what customers do within a product or service, revealed by analytics); and structured diaries (asking customers to record specific behaviors in real time as they occur, which reduces memory distortion while accessing a wider range of contexts than direct observation can reach).

The behavioral pattern most commonly revealed through behavioral research that is not revealed through interviews: the workarounds that customers have developed to manage the problem. The workaround is the most direct evidence of the problem's real nature — it shows what the customer is actually trying to accomplish, in their actual behavior, in a way that survives the gap between stated preference and actual practice.

Natalie's "Do" data: the lawyers' actual behaviors revealed a specific workaround pattern that explained the anxiety. They maintained multiple parallel tracking systems (a software-based task list, a physical notepad, a whiteboard, a series of flagged emails) — not because they were disorganized, but because no single system was comprehensive enough that they trusted it completely. They were effectively hedging against any single system's failure by maintaining multiple redundant tracking mechanisms. The behavior was rational given the anxiety; it was also exhausting and inefficient.

## Chapter 5: What Customers Feel

The "Feel" quadrant captures the emotional substrate of the customer's experience — the feelings that motivate their behavior, color their perceptions, and determine what they value in a solution. Emotional understanding is the dimension most commonly missing from business customer analysis, and its absence produces products and marketing that are technically accurate but emotionally irrelevant.

Accessing what customers feel requires the research methods that create conditions for emotional honesty: one-on-one interviews that allow the customer to share without social performance pressure; projective research techniques that allow customers to attribute their emotional experience to others (making it less personally threatening to acknowledge); and the empathetic observation of customer behavior that reveals emotional states through nonverbal cues.

The emotional dimensions most common in professional service and software customer experience: the anxiety of responsibility (the fear of making a mistake with professional consequences); the frustration of effort (the feeling of expending significant energy without proportional progress); the satisfaction of competence (the feeling of being good at the work, when systems and tools support rather than impede it); and the aspiration for control (the desire to feel in command of one's responsibilities rather than at their mercy).

Natalie's "Feel" data: the dominant emotion in her customers' professional experience was anxiety — specifically, the anxiety of incompleteness. Not the anxiety of incompetence (they believed in their legal capabilities), but the anxiety of not having full visibility into whether everything that needed to be done was being managed. The feeling of having things under control — comprehensive visibility into all obligations, all deadlines, all client needs — was the emotional relief they were seeking. Time savings was a means to that relief; the feeling of control was the actual goal.

## Chapter 6: Synthesizing the Empathy Map

The value of the empathy map lies not in its individual quadrants but in the synthesis: the insight that emerges when the Say, Think, Do, and Feel data are considered together, revealing the patterns and tensions that constitute the customer's actual experience.

The synthesis questions that generate the most valuable insight: What tensions exist between what the customer Says and what they Think? (The gap between public statements and private thoughts often reveals the most important emotional dynamics.) What is the gap between what the customer Does and what they Say they want to do? (This gap reveals the friction that prevents the customer from achieving their stated goals.) What emotions are most consistently present across the quadrants, and what does that consistency reveal about what the customer most needs?

Natalie's empathy map synthesis: the consistent emotional pattern across all four quadrants was anxiety about incompleteness — the worry that something was being overlooked. The customers said they felt overwhelmed; they thought about the consequences of missing things; they behaved as if no single system could be fully trusted; and they felt anxious about whether they were on top of everything. The product's actual value proposition, as revealed by the empathy map, was not time savings but peace of mind: the feeling of having comprehensive, reliable visibility into all obligations.

## Chapter 7: From Empathy Map to Value Proposition

The empathy map translates directly into a more powerful value proposition by revealing what the customer actually values — the emotional outcomes they're seeking — as distinct from the functional features the business provides.

Natalie's value proposition before the empathy map: "Save twelve hours per week on documentation, billing, and scheduling." This proposition described functional features and time savings, which was accurate but emotionally irrelevant to the anxiety that actually drove her customers' behavior.

Natalie's value proposition after the empathy map: "Never wonder if you're on top of everything — one place for every matter, every deadline, and every client commitment." This proposition addressed the emotional job (the feeling of comprehensive visibility) in the language her customers actually used (the worry about not being on top of things) rather than in the language of feature descriptions.

The conversion impact of the repositioned value proposition: her trial-to-paid conversion rate improved from eleven percent to twenty-eight percent. The product hadn't changed. The price hadn't changed. The customer's experience of the value proposition had changed — because the positioning spoke to the emotion that drove the purchase decision rather than the feature that enabled it.

## Chapter 8: Using the Empathy Map in Product Development

The empathy map is as valuable a product development tool as it is a marketing tool, because it reveals what the product needs to feel like to the customer — the emotional experience of using it — as well as what it needs to do.

The product design decisions most informed by empathy map insight: the information architecture (what is visible, and in what order — determined by what the customer most anxiously needs to see at any moment); the language used throughout the product (which should match the customer's own vocabulary, not the product developer's technical vocabulary); and the "moments of relief" designed into the product (the specific points in the user experience where the product explicitly addresses the customer's dominant anxiety).

Natalie's product development response to the empathy map: she redesigned the product's home screen around what she now called "the anxiety list" — a real-time summary of every upcoming deadline, every client without a recent update, and every document awaiting review. The redesigned home screen addressed the customer's dominant anxiety (worry about incompleteness) at the first moment they opened the product each day. User engagement with the redesigned product increased significantly; the qualitative feedback consistently mentioned the specific emotional relief the home screen provided.

## Chapter 9: Team-Based Empathy Map Exercises

The empathy map is most powerful when built and discussed collaboratively by a cross-functional team — product, marketing, sales, and customer success — because each function has access to different aspects of the customer's experience and the synthesis of multiple perspectives produces richer insight than any single function's view.

The facilitated empathy map exercise: gather the team, provide the research data (interview transcripts, survey results, usage analytics, support conversation logs), and work through each quadrant together — first independently (each team member fills their quadrant based on the evidence they have), then collectively (comparing and reconciling different perspectives). The discussion that arises when perspectives differ reveals the gaps in understanding that require additional research.

The team empathy map exercise has a secondary benefit beyond the insight it produces: it creates shared understanding of the customer's experience across functions that normally operate in silos. The product developer who has participated in an empathy map exercise based on actual customer research has a different — and more accurate — internal model of the customer than the developer who has only read a product requirements document.

## Chapter 10: The Living Empathy Map

The empathy map that is built once and treated as a permanent artifact quickly becomes inaccurate, because customers' situations, problems, and emotional experiences evolve as their context changes, as new alternatives emerge, and as their relationship with the business deepens.

The practice that keeps the empathy map current: quarterly updates based on fresh customer research — new interviews, new survey data, new analysis of support conversations and product usage patterns. The quarterly review should ask not "is our empathy map still accurate?" (a question that confirmation bias makes difficult to answer honestly) but "what has changed in our customers' experience in the past three months, and how does our empathy map need to be updated to reflect that change?"

Natalie's empathy map updates: eighteen months after her initial empathy map exercise, she conducted a follow-up research round and discovered that the emotional experience of her customers who had been using the product for more than a year had shifted. The dominant emotion among long-term users was no longer anxiety (which the product had successfully addressed) but aspiration — they now felt in control of their practice and were thinking about growing it. The empathy map for long-term users pointed to a different value proposition than the one for new users, which informed her product roadmap for features that would serve the ambitions of growing practices rather than only the anxiety of overwhelmed ones.

---

## Epilogue

Natalie's company grew from a business with mediocre conversion rates and a disconnected value proposition to one with strong retention and genuine customer affinity — not because she built a better product, but because she understood the emotional experience of using it more accurately. The empathy map gave her permission to see her customers as people rather than users, to understand their anxiety as the context for their decisions rather than an obstacle to selling them a product, and to build a value proposition that spoke to what they actually felt rather than what she had assumed they would care about. The empathy map is a research artifact. Its product is a human understanding of the people who need what the business provides. That understanding is the foundation of everything.
