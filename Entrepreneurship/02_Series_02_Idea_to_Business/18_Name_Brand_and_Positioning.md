# Name, Brand and Positioning
## The invisible assets that make people choose you

**Series 02: From Idea to Business — Book 8 of 10**

---

## Prologue

Two management consulting practices launched in the same city in the same year, serving the same market (mid-sized manufacturing companies) with the same core service (operational efficiency consulting). Three years later, one had twelve clients and $1.8 million in revenue. The other had three clients and $280,000 in revenue. The services were comparable in quality; the founders had similar experience levels. The difference was almost entirely attributable to the clarity and coherence of how each business presented itself: its name, its visual identity, its articulation of what it was for and for whom, and the consistency with which that articulation was expressed across every interaction.

Brand is not a logo. It is not a color palette. It is not a carefully crafted tagline. Brand is the sum total of what people think and feel when they encounter your business — the expectations they bring, the meaning they attach, the trust they extend or withhold. Every interaction your business has with the world — its name, its visual presentation, its communication, its product quality, its customer service — contributes to the brand that people experience. This book is about building that experience deliberately.

## Chapter 1: What Brand Actually Is

Research on brand perception by Jennifer Aaker, one of the preeminent researchers in brand psychology, documents that brands — like people — are perceived through a constellation of personality dimensions: sincerity (down-to-earth, honest, wholesome, cheerful), excitement (daring, spirited, imaginative, up-to-date), competence (reliable, intelligent, successful), sophistication (upper class, charming), and ruggedness (outdoorsy, tough). These dimensions are not chosen by the brand's owner; they emerge from the totality of impressions the brand makes on its audience.

What the brand owner can control is the inputs to those impressions: the deliberate choices of name, visual identity, communication style, product quality, and customer experience that contribute to the brand's overall personality. The most strategically valuable brands are those whose personality is coherent — where all of the inputs point in the same direction, reinforcing a consistent set of impressions — and distinctive — where the personality is meaningfully different from the alternatives the customer is comparing.

The most common brand failure in early-stage businesses is incoherence: the name suggests one brand personality (formal, corporate), the visual identity suggests another (playful, approachable), the communication style suggests a third (casual, technical), and the customer experience doesn't match any of them. Each inconsistency creates cognitive friction that reduces memorability, trust, and the clarity of the promise the brand is making. Building a coherent brand requires choosing the personality dimensions that are most authentic to the business and most relevant to the target customer, then ensuring that every brand expression reinforces those dimensions consistently.

## Chapter 2: Choosing a Name That Works

The business name is the most permanent and most public expression of the brand, and it should be chosen with care that is often not given to it. The most common naming failures are names that are too generic (Consulting Solutions, Inc. — memorable to no one and differentiating from nothing), too clever (Hexagonal Holdings — evocative of nothing and opaque to everyone), or too literal (Bob's Chicago Pizza — tells you what and where but gives you no reason to prefer it over alternatives).

Research on brand naming by Allen Adamson and others identifies several characteristics that make names effective: memorability (can people recall and recognize the name after limited exposure?); distinctiveness (is it different from competitors' names in ways that make it stand out in competitive contexts?); relevance (does it create positive associations with the business's core value proposition?); and availability (is the name available as a trademark and as a domain name, and do the social media handles it would require exist?). Not every effective name satisfies all four criteria equally, but the most valuable names satisfy at least three.

The categories of names that most effectively satisfy these criteria include: invented words (Kodak, Google, Xerox — completely distinctive because they existed nowhere before the brand created them, though they require significant marketing investment to convey meaning); founder names (McKinsey, Goldman Sachs, Mars — convey authenticity and accountability, though they become limiting when the business extends beyond its original scope); and evocative words (Apple — doesn't describe computers but evokes accessibility and creativity, satisfying relevance and memorability without the limitation of a literal description).

## Chapter 3: Visual Identity That Communicates

The visual identity of a business — its logo, color palette, typography, and overall design aesthetic — is not decoration. It is a communication system that conveys brand personality at a glance, creates recognition across repeated exposures, and signals the quality and professionalism of the business in ways that words cannot efficiently convey.

Research on color psychology and brand perception documents consistent associations between specific colors and brand personality dimensions. Blue conveys competence, trust, and reliability — which is why it dominates in banking, healthcare, and technology brands seeking to convey trustworthiness. Red conveys energy, urgency, and excitement — which is why it dominates in food, entertainment, and retail brands seeking to stimulate appetite and action. Green conveys sustainability, health, and naturalness. Orange conveys creativity and friendliness. These are not rules; they are tendencies that can be deliberately used or deliberately subverted, depending on the brand's positioning strategy.

The visual identity decision that matters most for early-stage businesses is not which color to choose but how much to invest in professional visual identity development before the business model is validated. A business that spends $15,000 on a full brand identity before confirming product-market fit has invested in a signal of quality before confirming that the substance the signal promises is actually present. A better sequencing is: invest in minimum viable visual identity (a clean, professional but not elaborate logo and color selection) sufficient to make a credible first impression, then invest in more sophisticated visual identity development as the business model confirms and the investment can be grounded in an accurate understanding of the audience and positioning.

## Chapter 4: Positioning — The Most Strategic Brand Decision

Positioning is the answer to the question: "In the mind of the target customer, what is this business for, and what makes it different from the alternatives?" It is the strategic core of brand building because it determines which customers find the business relevant, how the business is evaluated against competitors, and what price premium (if any) the brand can sustain.

Al Ries and Jack Trout's "Positioning: The Battle for Your Mind" (1981) remains the foundational text on positioning strategy. Its core insight — that positioning is not what you do to a product but what you do to the mind of the prospective customer — remains accurate and important. You do not position your product by adding a feature or changing a price; you position it by changing how people categorize and evaluate it relative to the alternatives they are aware of. The product that is positioned as "the premium option for enterprise customers who value reliability over price" will be evaluated differently by the same customer than the product with identical features positioned as "the best value for growing companies."

Effective positioning requires three elements: a specific target customer (not "small businesses" but "professional services firms with five to fifty employees who bill by the hour"); a clear frame of reference (the category within which the customer will evaluate the business — not "business software" but "practice management software for professional services firms"); and a compelling point of difference (what the business does better than or differently from alternatives within the frame of reference, from the target customer's perspective).

## Chapter 5: Brand Promise and Delivery

A brand is, at its most fundamental level, a promise. The name, the visual identity, and the positioning together constitute a set of expectations that the target customer develops: expectations about the quality of the product, the nature of the customer experience, and the values the business embodies. Brand value is built when the product and experience consistently deliver on those expectations; brand value is destroyed when the product and experience fall short of them.

Research on customer expectations and satisfaction by Oliver on "what is customer satisfaction" documents the "disconfirmation paradigm": customer satisfaction is a function of the gap between expected performance and actual performance, not of actual performance alone. A product that performs at a high absolute level but below the expectations the brand has set produces dissatisfied customers. A product that performs at a modest absolute level but exceeds the (modest) expectations the brand has set produces satisfied customers. This asymmetry has a specific implication for brand strategy: the promises made through positioning and marketing must be ones the product and experience can reliably deliver, not the ones that would be most attractive if delivered.

The "brand promise gap" — the distance between what the brand communicates and what the product and experience actually deliver — is the most destructive force in brand building and one of the most common sources of poor customer retention and negative word of mouth. Early-stage businesses are particularly vulnerable to this gap because the temptation to position the business at its aspirational level (what it will be when fully developed) rather than its current level (what it actually delivers today) is strong and the gap between aspiration and reality is wide.

## Chapter 6: Building Brand in a Digital World

The digital environment has fundamentally changed the mechanics of brand building for early-stage businesses, primarily by reducing the cost of brand communication while increasing the volume of brand signals that potential customers can access and assess. A potential customer who encountered a small consulting firm in 1995 had access to a brochure, possibly a website, and perhaps a referral from a mutual connection. The same potential customer today can access the firm's website, all of its social media accounts, reviews on multiple platforms, the founder's personal LinkedIn profile, the employees' profiles, the content the firm has published, and the comments others have made about it — all before any direct interaction.

This information abundance changes the calculus of brand building in two important ways. First, it makes consistency more important than ever: in 1995, a potential customer was unlikely to encounter enough brand signals to identify inconsistencies; today, inconsistencies are immediately visible across multiple channels. Second, it makes authenticity more important than ever: the ability to present a polished, curated image is no longer a differentiator because most businesses have it; the ability to present a genuine, consistent, trustworthy personality is still relatively rare and highly valued.

The most effective digital brand building strategies for early-stage businesses concentrate on consistency across all digital touchpoints (the same personality, voice, and visual identity on the website, on social media, in email communications, and in any content published), quality over quantity in content production (a few high-quality pieces of content that genuinely help the target customer are worth far more than a high volume of low-quality content), and genuine engagement in the communities where the target customer participates (which builds brand association with the community's values rather than merely advertising within it).

## Chapter 7: Competitive Positioning in Practice

Competitive positioning — the specific location in the competitive landscape that a business occupies relative to the alternatives available to its target customer — is not a fixed decision but an evolving one that must be revisited as the competitive landscape changes and as the business's own capabilities develop.

The positioning tool most useful for early-stage competitive analysis is the "perceptual map": a two-dimensional diagram that plots competitors against the two attributes most important to the target customer's purchase decision. The business that understands which positions in its competitive landscape are occupied (by strong, well-resourced competitors) and which are unoccupied or weakly defended can identify the positioning that provides the most defensible competitive opportunity.

The two consulting practices in this book's prologue occupied different positions on a perceptual map defined by "specialization in manufacturing" and "size of engagement." The more successful firm positioned itself in the "deep manufacturing specialization, mid-size engagement" quadrant — a position that was underserved by the larger consulting firms (which focused on larger engagements) and the generalist local firms (which had less deep manufacturing expertise). This position was defensible because the combination of attributes it required (genuine manufacturing expertise and the ability to serve mid-market companies efficiently) was difficult for either adjacent competitor type to replicate without significant investment.

## Chapter 8: Personal Brand and Business Brand

For many early-stage businesses — particularly professional services firms, solopreneurs, and businesses whose differentiation rests significantly on the founder's specific expertise and relationships — the founder's personal brand is as important as the business brand, and sometimes more so.

Research on personal brand and business outcomes in professional services finds that advisors, consultants, and service providers whose personal brand is strong and well-aligned with their firm's brand generate more referrals, close more business at higher prices, and retain clients longer than those whose personal brand is absent or misaligned. The personal brand operates as a trust signal that bridges the information gap between a potential client's knowledge of the firm's capabilities and their confidence in engaging it.

The personal brand is built through the same mechanisms as the business brand: consistent, authentic communication of a clear point of view on the topics most relevant to the target audience; genuine expertise demonstrated through original thinking rather than generic content; and network investment that places the person in the contexts where their target audience is present. The LinkedIn publishing strategy, the conference speaking engagement, the industry podcast appearance, and the thoughtful commentary in professional community forums are all personal brand investments that compound over time into a reputation that generates business opportunities.

## Chapter 9: Brand Mistakes That Kill Early Businesses

The brand mistakes most common in early-stage businesses are predictable enough to be worth naming explicitly, because awareness of the pattern is the first defense against it.

The most damaging is the premium brand promise with the commodity product delivery: positioning the business as a high-quality, high-care option and delivering an average product with minimal differentiation. This positioning creates the worst of both worlds — the pricing constraint of a premium position without the retention and referral benefits that genuine premium delivery produces.

The second most damaging is the unclear or confusing positioning that tries to be all things to all potential customers: "We serve businesses of all sizes, in all industries, with all types of marketing support." This positioning is true of almost every marketing firm and therefore differentiates none of them. The potential customer who reads it has no basis for preferring this firm over any other, because the promise contains no specific relevance to their situation.

The third is brand inconsistency across touchpoints: a sophisticated website with generic email communications, a professional logo with a founder's social media presence that contradicts the brand's personality, a formal brand promise with an informal customer experience. Each inconsistency creates cognitive dissonance that reduces the clarity of the brand's meaning and the strength of the customer's trust response.

## Chapter 10: Brand as Long-Term Asset

Brand is the most durable asset a business builds, because it exists in the minds of customers and potential customers rather than on a balance sheet that can be liquidated. The company's products can be copied, its pricing can be undercut, its processes can be replicated — but the brand's position in the minds of the people who know it cannot be replicated by any competitor, because it represents the accumulated memory of every interaction that audience has had with the business.

The business that invests consistently in brand building — delivering reliably on its brand promise, communicating that promise coherently, and building genuine relationships with the communities its customers inhabit — builds a durable competitive advantage that is qualitatively different from the advantages of superior product, lower price, or better distribution. Those advantages can be eroded by competitors with sufficient resources; the brand advantage of being the trusted, recognized, preferred option in the minds of a specific audience is resistant to competitive erosion in a way that other advantages are not.

The two consulting practices in this book's prologue are both still operating, years after the chapter in their story described here. The successful one has seventeen clients and $2.4 million in revenue. It has turned down acquisition offers from two larger firms because the founder values the independence the brand's position gives her. The other has remained small, serving the same three clients with limited growth. The brand work done in the first three years is not the only explanation for the difference. But it is the explanation that compounded most reliably over time.

---

## Epilogue

A name is just a name. A logo is just a logo. A positioning statement is just a sentence. By themselves, these things build nothing. But in the context of a business that delivers genuinely on what they promise, that communicates them consistently across every touchpoint, and that builds relationships with the people they are intended to attract — they become the infrastructure of trust that makes everything else in the business more effective. Build the brand. Deliver on it. Let the trust compound.
