# The Entrepreneur You Want to Be
## Designing your entrepreneurial life with intention

**Series 09: Entrepreneur as Person — Book 10 of 10**

---

## Prologue

Twelve years into building her HR consulting firm, Claire sat down to do something she had never done before: write down who she wanted to be as an entrepreneur — not what she wanted the business to achieve, but who she wanted to be in the process of building it.

The exercise had been suggested by a peer in her Entrepreneurs' Organization forum, who had done it herself after a period of business success that had left her strangely dissatisfied. The peer's insight: "I had achieved everything I had planned for the business and discovered that I had never planned for the person doing the building. The business got what I intended; I got whatever was left over."

Claire's list was revealing. She wanted to be known as someone who led with genuine care for her team. She wanted to be intellectually engaged by the problems she worked on, not just commercially engaged. She wanted to be honest with clients even when honesty cost her short-term business. She wanted to have enough capacity for her marriage and her children that neither would feel they were competing with the business for her attention. And she wanted, by the time she was done, to be able to look back on not just a successful business but a life she had actually chosen.

The question was: did the business she had built align with who she had just described?

## Chapter 1: The Intentional Entrepreneurial Life

The most common approach to entrepreneurial life design is implicit: the founder makes business decisions primarily based on commercial logic and deals with the personal consequences of those decisions as they arise. The business needs grow rapidly so the founder works more; the business needs a new capability so the founder learns it; the business hits difficulty so the founder sacrifices the personal domains that have the least immediate consequence — rest, relationships, health, personal development — and recovers them when the difficulty passes.

The result of implicit life design: a life that reflects the business's requirements more than the founder's intentions. The founder who has never explicitly designed what they want their entrepreneurial life to look like has, by default, delegated that design to the business — which is indifferent to the founder's wellbeing, relationships, and personal fulfillment. The business will consume whatever the founder makes available to it; the question is whether the founder has decided what is available.

The explicit life design alternative: the deliberate examination, in advance and periodically throughout the entrepreneurial journey, of what the founder wants the non-commercial dimensions of their entrepreneurial life to look like — how they want to lead, what kind of relationships they want to maintain with their team, what personal domains they want to protect, and what kind of person they want to become in the process of building the business. This design doesn't guarantee that the business will accommodate the intention; it does ensure that the intention is known and that decisions that violate it are recognized as such rather than made blindly.

## Chapter 2: Defining Success for Yourself

The entrepreneurial culture offers a default definition of success: revenue growth, market share, capital raised, valuation achieved, eventual exit multiple. These definitions are legible, comparable, and externally validated — they tell the story that investors and the business press and the entrepreneurship community most readily understand and celebrate. They may or may not align with what the individual founder actually most values in their entrepreneurial experience.

The personal success definition exercise: articulating, explicitly and independently of external definitions, what success would look like in one's own entrepreneurial life — across all domains, not only the commercial ones. The questions that most effectively surface personal success definition: What would I want to be true about my business at its peak? What would I want to be true about my leadership at my best? What would I want to be true about my personal life during the business years? And when I am old enough to look back on this period, what would make me feel that it was well-spent?

The success definition misalignment problem: many entrepreneurs discover, often at the point of achieving the externally defined success they pursued, that it doesn't feel like success in the way they expected — because the external definition was never fully aligned with their personal values. Richard (from Book 86 in this series) discovered this at business exit; Nina (from Book 87) discovered it mid-journey when the business success was present but the personal meaning wasn't. The discovery, whenever it comes, opens the same opportunity: the chance to explicitly define success on personal rather than external terms.

Claire's success definition: her list of who she wanted to be was, implicitly, a definition of success. The business success she pursued needed to be achieved in a way that expressed those values rather than violating them — or it wouldn't be success in the terms that actually mattered to her.

## Chapter 3: Leading the Way You Want to Be Led

The leadership approach that founders most naturally adopt is typically the one modeled by the leaders they observed before becoming entrepreneurs — the management style of the best and worst bosses they worked for, the organizational culture of the companies they came from, and the received wisdom of the management books and podcasts that formed their leadership education.

The intentional leadership alternative: explicitly examining the leader one wants to be — what values should govern one's leadership, what kind of organizational environment one wants to create, what one's relationships with team members should be like — rather than defaulting to the approaches absorbed from prior experience.

The leadership intention dimensions: the values-in-use (the specific principles that should govern difficult leadership decisions — whether to prioritize short-term performance or long-term development when they conflict, how to handle underperformance, when to be direct vs. when to be supportive); the relationship style (the kind of relationship one wants to have with team members — more collegial or more formal, more disclosing or more boundaried, more coach-like or more directive); and the culture intention (the specific cultural characteristics one wants the organization to develop — what it should feel like to work there, what behaviors should be rewarded, what norms should govern how people treat each other).

Claire's leadership gap: the audit of whether her actual leadership aligned with who she wanted to be revealed a significant gap. She wanted to be known as someone who led with genuine care for her team; her 360 feedback, which she requested as part of this process, revealed that her team experienced her as caring but distant — present in crises but not consistently available for the kind of ongoing relationship that "genuine care" implied. The gap between intention and experience was addressable. But it required knowing the gap existed.

## Chapter 4: Building a Business That Reflects Your Values

The business's values — the specific principles that govern how it operates, what it offers, who it serves, and how it treats its people — can either be expressions of the founder's deepest professional values or can drift, through the commercial pressures of growth and competition, away from those values toward whatever produces the most immediate commercial result.

The values-business alignment audit: examining whether the business's actual practices — the clients it takes, the work it does, the treatment of its team, the commitments it makes and keeps — reflect the values the founder most deeply holds. The gap between stated values and operational practices is one of the most reliable sources of the founder's sense of inauthenticity and dissatisfaction, because it means the business is contradicting the values the founder is trying to live.

The hard values choices: the business that is genuinely values-aligned must, at some points, make decisions that sacrifice commercial opportunity in favor of values coherence — declining the client whose project would be commercially attractive but whose values or approach violate the firm's commitments; choosing the slower growth path that maintains quality and culture over the faster path that would compromise both; and holding to compensation and treatment standards even when the cost creates financial pressure.

Claire's values-business alignment: the audit revealed that her firm had taken several clients, primarily for revenue reasons, whose work she found neither interesting nor meaningful — and whose organizational cultures conflicted with the practices she was trying to develop in her own firm. The revenue was welcome; the contradiction was corrosive. She made the decision to develop a more selective client acquisition process that would screen for the alignment she wanted to maintain.

## Chapter 5: Protecting What Matters Outside the Business

The domains of life outside the business — relationships, health, personal interests, community connection, and personal development — require protection because they don't self-advocate with the same urgency that the business does. The business presents its demands continuously and consequentially; the relationship, the health practice, and the personal interest wait patiently, losing their place in the schedule without complaint, accumulating deficits without visible crisis until the deficit becomes severe enough to produce one.

The protection mechanism: explicit, calendar-based, non-negotiable commitments to the personal domains that matter. Not aspiration ("I want to spend more time with my children") but structure ("Tuesday and Thursday evenings are blocked from 6 PM for family dinner and presence, and I do not schedule client calls during those times"). The difference between aspiration and structure is the difference between a value and a practice — the former is a preference that yields to competing demands; the latter is a commitment that is maintained even when competing demands are present.

The protection failure: the most common reason that personal domain protection fails is the accumulation of exceptions. The one call that can't be rescheduled, the crisis that requires an unscheduled evening, the client deadline that falls on a protected morning — each exception is individually justified and collectively corrosive. The protection that doesn't survive exceptions isn't protection; it's preference. The founder who has genuinely decided that the personal domain matters treats the protection as a constraint, like a tax or a law of physics, that exceptions can't override except in genuine emergencies.

Claire's non-negotiable structure: she blocked Tuesday and Thursday dinner time, Saturday morning for exercise, and Sunday afternoon for family time. For eighteen months, she protected these blocks with the same priority she gave client commitments. Her marriage improved measurably; her children commented on the difference; and her own wellbeing — which she measured by a simple daily satisfaction rating — showed a sustained improvement over the period.

## Chapter 6: The Continuous Development of the Founder

The entrepreneur who stops developing — who stops learning, who stops examining their assumptions, who stops seeking the challenges that reveal their limitations — is not maintaining their current level; they are declining relative to the complexity and the competition that are always increasing around them.

The development investment as professional necessity: the founders who most consistently sustain excellent business performance over long periods are those who invest most consistently in their own development — through structured learning (executive education, professional programs, and reading in areas where their capability is weakest), reflective practice (the deliberate examination of their own decisions and their outcomes, which is the mechanism by which experience produces learning rather than just repetition), and the relationships (mentors, coaches, peers) that provide the external perspective that self-reflection alone cannot.

The specific development priorities for founders at different stages: early-stage founders most commonly need development in leadership (managing the first employees), sales and communication (articulating the value proposition to customers and investors), and financial management (understanding the numbers that govern the business). Mid-stage founders most commonly need development in organizational design (building the team and structure that enables growth), strategic thinking (moving from operational to strategic orientation), and personal sustainability (the practices that prevent burnout in the growth years). Later-stage founders most commonly need development in leadership team development, board management, and the personal clarity about what they want the next chapter of their professional life to be.

## Chapter 7: The Entrepreneur in the Community

The entrepreneur who participates actively in their professional and local communities — who contributes knowledge, mentorship, and resources to the community beyond the business — is doing something that is simultaneously personally meaningful, professionally beneficial, and socially important.

The community participation dimensions: the professional community (the industry associations, entrepreneurship organizations, and peer networks in which the founder participates as contributor, not only consumer — sharing knowledge, mentoring others, participating in the collective knowledge-building of the field); the local community (the geographic community in which the business operates and in which the founder and employees live — the involvement through civic participation, charitable activity, and local investment that makes the business a genuine community asset rather than only a commercial entity); and the entrepreneurship ecosystem (the broader network of founders, investors, educators, and support organizations that constitute the ecosystem in which the business exists — the founder who contributes to this ecosystem's health is simultaneously building the environment in which their own business operates).

The community contribution as meaning source: the founders who most consistently report high life satisfaction are those whose sense of purpose extends beyond their own business to include genuine contribution to something larger. The community investment — the time given to mentoring a newer founder, the knowledge shared at an industry event, the philanthropic contribution to a local organization — is not a distraction from the entrepreneurial mission; for founders who engage in it with genuine intention, it is an expression of it.

## Chapter 8: The Entrepreneurial Life as Intentional Narrative

The entrepreneurial journey, viewed from any significant distance, is a narrative: a story of what was attempted, what was built, what was learned, how the person changed in the process, and what the whole thing ultimately meant. The narrative can be designed, to a meaningful degree, by the intentions with which it is lived.

The narrative design questions: What story do I want to be able to tell about how I led? What story do I want to be able to tell about how I treated the people who worked for me? What story do I want to be able to tell about my priorities — what I was willing to sacrifice for the business and what I protected as non-negotiable? And what story do I want to be able to tell about what the business was for — what it contributed beyond its commercial results?

The narrative design practice: the periodic review — quarterly, annually, at significant transitions — of whether the story being lived is the one intended. The founder who examines their narrative periodically, not only at the end, has the opportunity to redirect it when it has drifted from the intended direction. The founder who examines it only at the end may find that the story that was lived was not the one that was intended — but that the absence of periodic examination allowed the drift to continue uncorrected for too long.

## Chapter 9: Acceptance and Imperfection

The intentional entrepreneurial life is not a perfect one. The founder who sets the intention to lead with genuine care will have days when they lead with impatience; the founder who commits to protecting family time will have crises that break through the protection; the founder who articulates clear values will make decisions that violate them under pressure. The imperfection is not the failure of intention; it is the human condition in which every intention operates.

The acceptance practice: engaging with the imperfection of the lived entrepreneurial life with the same compassion and practical orientation that one would bring to a business setback. The business that misses a milestone doesn't abandon the goal; it examines what happened and adjusts the approach. The founder who has violated an intention doesn't abandon the intention; they examine what produced the violation and adjusts the approach.

The self-compassion research: Kristin Neff's work on self-compassion finds that the founders who treat themselves with self-compassion in the wake of failures and imperfections demonstrate significantly better subsequent performance, more sustained motivation, and higher psychological wellbeing than those who respond to imperfection with harsh self-criticism. The counter-intuitive finding is that self-compassion is not self-indulgence; it is the emotional regulation practice that enables the clear-eyed engagement with failure and limitation that actually produces learning and improvement.

## Chapter 10: The Entrepreneur You Want to Be

Claire's business ten years after the intentional design exercise: she has revised the design three times as her circumstances, values, and priorities evolved. She has not always lived it perfectly; she has consistently returned to it when she recognized she had drifted from it. The business is successful by both commercial and personal measures. More importantly, the way she is running it is the way she intended — the leadership is what she intended it to be, the culture is the one she designed, and the personal domains she committed to protecting have been protected far more consistently than before the design existed.

The entrepreneur she has become is not the one that the implicit drift of business demands would have produced. It is the one she designed: imperfectly executed, continuously revised, and ultimately more fully herself than the default would have allowed. The business she built reflects who she is and what she values. That, she believes, is the best thing a business can do.

The entrepreneurial life is not simply what happens while you are building a business. It is a life that you can design, or not design, with consequences in either case. The founder who designs it — who explicitly commits to who they want to be, what they want to protect, and what they want to contribute — builds both a better business and a more fully inhabited life. That is the entrepreneur worth becoming. That is the entrepreneur worth being.

---

## Epilogue

Claire's forum peer had asked a deceptively simple question: who do you want to be while you're building this? The question was not about the business. It was about the person. The business would be what it was going to be — affected by markets and teams and capital and timing and the thousand variables that the founder does not fully control. The person was more deterministic: not entirely in the founder's control, but more in it than the business. The person was the one thing the founder actually got to design. Claire had designed hers. The design was imperfect and continuously revised. It was also the most important work she had ever done.
