## The art of deciding when you don't have enough information

### Prologue
Thomas had been in the same decision for eleven weeks. He needed to choose between two strategic paths for his e-commerce platform: deepen into the luxury goods vertical where he had traction, or expand into the home goods market where a large competitor appeared to be struggling. Both paths had compelling arguments. Both had genuine risks. He had more data than he had started with, but not certainty — because certainty was not available. The information needed to know which path was right would only exist after the path had been chosen.

This is the defining condition of entrepreneurial decision-making: the most important decisions are precisely the ones that cannot be made with the information that would make them easy. The information required to know whether a market is large enough comes from being in the market. The information required to know whether a product will find customers comes from building it. The information required to know whether a team member will perform at the required level comes from hiring them. Waiting for the certainty that would make the decision comfortable is waiting for something that will not arrive. The skill of entrepreneurial decision-making is not the skill of getting more information before deciding; it is the skill of deciding well with less information than you would prefer.

## Chapter 1: The Uncertainty Landscape

Not all uncertainty is the same, and distinguishing between types of uncertainty is the first step toward managing it effectively. Research on uncertainty in decision-making distinguishes between risk (situations in which the probabilities of different outcomes are known or estimable, even if the outcomes themselves are uncertain), uncertainty (situations in which the probabilities themselves are not known), and ambiguity (situations in which even the relevant outcomes have not been identified).

Most entrepreneurial decisions involve the second and third types far more than the first. When a new product is launched, the entrepreneur typically does not know the probability that customers will adopt it, because the relevant reference class — products solving this specific problem, for this specific customer, at this time — does not contain enough cases to generate reliable probability estimates. This is what Frank Knight called "Knightian uncertainty" in his 1921 distinction between risk and uncertainty: conditions in which decision-relevant probabilities are not computable from past frequency data.

The implication is that the tools developed for rational decision-making under risk — expected value calculations, probability-weighted scenario analysis — have limited applicability to the most important entrepreneurial decisions. They are useful for decisions where the probability landscape can be reasonably estimated, including many operational decisions. For the fundamental strategic decisions of entrepreneurship — what to build, who to build it for, which opportunity to pursue — different decision frameworks are required.

## Chapter 2: How Expert Entrepreneurs Actually Decide

Saras Sarasvathy's research on expert entrepreneurial decision-making, comparing the decision processes of novice entrepreneurs and expert entrepreneurs with at least ten years of experience and one successful exit, produced findings that fundamentally challenged the standard model of entrepreneurial decision-making. The standard model assumes that entrepreneurs begin with a clear goal and work backward to identify the optimal means of achieving it — the "causal" or "predictive" logic of MBAs and business plans. What Sarasvathy found was that expert entrepreneurs predominantly use a different logic she called "effectuation."

Effectuation begins not with a goal but with an inventory of available means: who you are (your values, skills, knowledge, and personality), what you know (your domain expertise, industry knowledge, and procedural capabilities), and who you know (your network of relationships). From this inventory, the effectual entrepreneur asks not "how do I achieve this goal?" but "what can I do with what I have, and what goals become possible from what I can do?" The strategies that emerge from effectuation are often different from those that emerge from causal analysis, and they tend to be more robust in high-uncertainty conditions because they are grounded in what is actually available rather than in predictions about what will be needed.

The most practically important element of effectuation for decision-making under uncertainty is the "affordable loss" principle: rather than trying to calculate the expected return on a decision, the effectual entrepreneur asks "what is the most I could afford to lose on this decision, and is the downside within that range?" This reframe replaces the paralysis-inducing expected value calculation (which requires probability estimates that don't exist) with the manageable question of loss tolerance (which is knowable from one's actual financial and psychological situation).

## Chapter 3: Speed as a Strategic Variable

In conditions of high uncertainty, the speed of decision-making is itself a strategic variable — not always a virtue, but often more valuable than its alternative. The research on decision timing in dynamic environments documents a consistent finding: in rapidly changing environments, the cost of a slow good decision frequently exceeds the cost of a fast adequate decision, because the delay enables the environment to change in ways that make the good decision obsolete.

Jeff Bezos's "two-door" decision framework — distinguishing between "two-way door" decisions (reversible, where you can walk back through if the decision is wrong) and "one-way door" decisions (irreversible or very costly to reverse) — provides a practical heuristic for calibrating decision speed to decision reversibility. Two-way door decisions should be made quickly, at the lowest organizational level capable of making them, by people with the most relevant specific knowledge. One-way door decisions deserve the most careful deliberation available. The distinction matters because many decisions that feel weighty and are made slowly are actually two-way doors — meaning the cost of the careful deliberation (time and the alternatives foregone while deciding) exceeds the risk of the faster, more experimental approach.

Thomas's strategic choice — luxury goods deepening versus home goods expansion — felt like a one-way door but was actually closer to a two-way door. He could invest moderately in the home goods test without fully committing to the expansion, gather three months of performance data, and make the final allocation decision with substantially more information and at acceptable cost. The insight that a decision he was treating as binary was actually a sequence of smaller, more reversible decisions reduced his eleven-week paralysis to a one-week planning process for the initial test.

## Chapter 4: Cognitive Biases and the Entrepreneurial Brain

The psychology of decision-making under uncertainty has produced an extensive catalog of systematic cognitive biases — predictable ways in which human judgment deviates from rational inference — that are especially consequential in entrepreneurial contexts.

Optimism bias — the tendency to overestimate the probability of positive outcomes and underestimate the probability of negative ones — is arguably the most important for entrepreneurial decision-making. Research by Tali Sharot on the neuroscience of optimism documents that approximately eighty percent of people show some degree of optimism bias, and that the bias is stronger for events in the more distant future and for outcomes that are personally important. These are precisely the conditions of early entrepreneurial decisions: distant outcomes, high personal stakes, and therefore the highest optimism bias. Research on business failure rates documents this directly: the distribution of entrepreneurs' expected survival rates for their own businesses is significantly higher than the actual survival rate, a systematic bias that produces underestimation of risk and overinvestment in failing courses of action.

Confirmation bias — the tendency to seek, interpret, and remember information that confirms existing beliefs while discounting information that challenges them — is equally consequential. The entrepreneur who has committed to a business idea searches for evidence that the idea will work and finds it; the evidence that would suggest the idea needs significant revision tends to register as less credible or less representative. Research on entrepreneur perception of market data finds that founders show systematic confirmation bias in interpreting the same ambiguous customer feedback, consistently arriving at conclusions that support their pre-existing product assumptions more strongly than the data warrants.

## Chapter 5: The Pre-Mortem

One of the most research-supported tools for improving decisions under uncertainty is the pre-mortem, developed by psychologist Gary Klein and researched extensively in organizational decision contexts. The pre-mortem asks decision-makers to imagine, before implementing a decision, that the decision has been made and six months have passed and the outcome has been a clear failure. The task is then to explain why it failed — to generate, in advance, the most plausible causal stories about how the decision could go wrong.

The pre-mortem improves decision quality through several mechanisms: it temporarily removes the psychological commitment to the chosen course of action that makes post-hoc rationalization likely; it creates explicit permission to articulate concerns and risks that might otherwise remain unspoken due to social pressure toward consensus; and it systematically generates the alternative outcomes that optimism bias tends to suppress. Research by Klein and others documents that pre-mortems increase the identification of potential decision risks by fifteen to thirty percent compared to standard decision review processes.

The pre-mortem is most useful for high-stakes, relatively irreversible decisions — the one-way doors that deserve careful deliberation. Thomas applied the pre-mortem to his eventual choice (the home goods test), asking himself to specify the most plausible reasons the test would fail to produce useful information within the three-month period. He identified three: insufficient marketing spend to generate traffic, pricing the home goods products at the wrong premium, and failing to adequately support the test with customer service capacity. Each risk had a specific mitigation he could plan in advance. The pre-mortem did not eliminate the uncertainty; it converted it from ambient anxiety into specific, addressable risks.

## Chapter 6: Seeking Disconfirmation

The default information-gathering behavior of most people facing an important decision is to seek information that supports the option they are leaning toward — confirming evidence for the hypothesis they want to be true. Research on hypothesis testing by psychologist Peter Wason documents this tendency in simple experimental settings (the "2-4-6" task) and it scales up powerfully to real-world decisions where the stakes increase the motivation to find confirming evidence.

The antidote is the deliberate practice of seeking disconfirming information: actively looking for the evidence that would suggest your preferred hypothesis is wrong. For the entrepreneur evaluating a market opportunity, this means not only finding the data that supports the opportunity but actively searching for data that would suggest the market is smaller than hoped, the customer need less acute than imagined, or the competitive position less favorable than apparent. The search for disconfirming evidence does not guarantee better decisions; it guarantees more accurate information inputs to decisions, which is the best available condition for good decisions under uncertainty.

A specific implementation of disconfirmation-seeking is the "kill criteria" practice: before launching an initiative or making a significant investment, specifying in advance the evidence that would lead you to stop or significantly change direction. "We will stop this test if we do not reach X conversion rate by Y date" or "we will revise the hypothesis if Z happens" converts the post-hoc rationalization problem — the tendency to find reasons why disappointing results don't really mean what they appear to mean — into a pre-committed standard that makes the stop decision easier to execute when the evidence warrants it.

## Chapter 7: Decision Quality vs. Decision Outcomes

One of the most important and most consistently violated principles of decision-making is the distinction between decision quality and decision outcomes. A good decision process can produce a bad outcome; a bad decision process can produce a good outcome. These combinations are not equally frequent — good process produces good outcomes more often than bad process does — but the outcome of any individual decision is too noisy, too influenced by factors outside the decision-maker's control, to serve as reliable feedback about the quality of the decision process that produced it.

Research on decision quality assessment documents that most people evaluate decisions primarily by their outcomes — a cognitive error sometimes called "outcome bias": concluding that the decision was good because the outcome was good, or bad because the outcome was bad, rather than evaluating whether the decision process was appropriate given the information available at the time of the decision. This bias prevents learning from good processes that had bad outcomes (which deserve continuation) and produces overconfidence from bad processes that had good outcomes (which deserve revision).

The practical implication for entrepreneurs is the development of a process-focused rather than outcome-focused decision review practice: after each significant decision, examining not only whether the outcome was good or bad but whether the process was appropriate. Did you gather the information that was reasonably available? Did you consider the alternative options seriously? Did you identify the key assumptions and test them where feasible? Did you set kill criteria in advance? A "yes" to these questions describes a good decision process even when the outcome turns out poorly. A "no" to these questions describes a bad decision process even when the outcome turns out well.

## Chapter 8: The Role of Intuition

Research on expert intuition, most extensively summarized by Daniel Kahneman and Gary Klein's joint analysis of intuitive expertise, documents a nuanced picture of when intuition is and is not a reliable guide to decision-making under uncertainty. Intuition — the rapid, pattern-recognition-based judgment that doesn't feel like deliberate reasoning — is reliable when it is trained in a domain with sufficient regularities to support pattern learning and with rapid, clear feedback that has allowed the pattern recognition to be calibrated against reality. Chess grandmasters have reliable intuition about board positions because chess has clear regularities and immediate, unambiguous feedback. Stock pickers have unreliable intuition about market movements because the relevant patterns are too complex and the feedback too delayed and ambiguous for reliable pattern learning.

Entrepreneurial intuition occupies a mixed position. In domains where the entrepreneur has extensive experience — a product leader's intuition about usability, a salesperson's intuition about customer readiness, a founder's intuition about their specific industry dynamics — expert intuition can be a genuinely valuable and rapid input to decision-making. In domains without this experience base — a first-time founder's intuition about market size, or an engineer's intuition about customer willingness to pay — intuition is not more reliable than chance and substantially less reliable than systematic inquiry.

The practical guidance is not to dismiss entrepreneurial intuition but to calibrate its deployment: trust intuition in domains of genuine experience; treat it with skepticism in new domains; and always subject strong intuitions to at least minimal reality testing before acting on them as if they were analyzed conclusions.

## Chapter 9: Building a Decision-Making Practice

The research on decision quality improvement consistently finds that decision-making practices — deliberate structures for making decisions that compensate for known cognitive biases and facilitate systematic consideration of relevant information — produce meaningfully better outcomes than unstructured deliberation, particularly under conditions of uncertainty and time pressure.

The minimum viable decision-making practice for an entrepreneur consists of four elements: decision journaling (recording significant decisions with the information available at the time, the reasoning behind the choice, the expected outcome, and the criteria for evaluating the outcome — which enables honest post-hoc review uncontaminated by hindsight); a forcing function for identifying the type of decision (reversible or not, which determines the appropriate deliberation level); a standardized consideration of alternatives (for significant decisions, deliberately generating and examining at least two alternatives to the initially preferred option); and a kill criteria specification for any initiative that represents a significant resource commitment.

Thomas eventually installed a decision journal after his eleven-week paralysis showed him how much time he was spending in deliberation that could be reduced by a clearer decision framework. Six months later, reviewing the journal, he found the most useful information not in individual decision entries but in the aggregate patterns: the domains in which his initial intuitions had been right, the domains in which they had consistently been wrong, and the specific biases — primarily optimism about timeline and confirmation bias about his target customer's willingness to pay — that had affected his most costly mistakes. The journal did not eliminate uncertainty. It made it legible.

## Chapter 10: Accepting the Limits of Certainty

The deepest skill in entrepreneurial decision-making is not analytical — it is psychological: the genuine acceptance that certainty is not available, that good process is the closest approximation to certainty, and that the discomfort of acting on adequate information will be present throughout the entrepreneurial journey and must be habituated to rather than resolved.

Research on tolerance for ambiguity — the capacity to remain effective in the presence of incomplete, unclear, or contradictory information — finds that it is one of the strongest differentiators between people who thrive in high-uncertainty environments and those who do not. Crucially, tolerance for ambiguity is not a fixed trait; it develops with deliberate exposure to uncertain conditions and with the accumulation of evidence that one can act effectively and survive outcomes that are different from what was intended. Every decision made under uncertainty, followed by learning from the outcome, builds the experiential evidence base that allows subsequent uncertain decisions to be made with less anxiety and more equanimity.

Thomas launched his home goods test in week twelve. The test produced results that surprised him: the home goods market showed substantially lower conversion rates than luxury goods, confirming the deeper opportunity in his original vertical. He had his answer — and it had arrived through a three-month test rather than through eleven weeks of contemplation. The decision under uncertainty was not less valid for having been made before full information was available. It was the appropriate decision for the context: good enough process, reasonable loss exposure, specific kill criteria, and rapid enough to preserve the strategic options that excessive deliberation would have foreclosed.

## Epilogue

Uncertainty is not an obstacle to entrepreneurial decision-making. It is the condition within which all entrepreneurial decisions occur and must be made. The entrepreneur who waits for certainty does not make better decisions; they make fewer decisions and fall further behind the competitive frontier that is advancing without them. The skills described in this book — distinguishing uncertainty types, applying effectual logic, moving quickly on reversible decisions, seeking disconfirmation, building decision practices — do not eliminate the uncertainty. They allow you to navigate it well enough, consistently enough, to build something worth building. That is all any entrepreneur can do. It turns out to be enough.
