## The transition from solo to team — how to do it right the first time

### Prologue
For two and a half years, Nina had run her brand strategy consultancy alone. Every client was hers. Every project was managed by her. Every piece of work that left the business passed through her hands. She had built something she was proud of: $280,000 in annual revenue, a roster of clients who referred her to other clients, and a business that reflected her standards precisely because she controlled every aspect of it. She was also, by any honest measure, at capacity. She was turning down work she wanted. She was maintaining client relationships inadequately because she had no time. She was not taking vacations. She was working every weekend.

The decision to hire her first employee — a junior brand strategist — felt simultaneously necessary and terrifying. Necessary because the alternative was stagnation; terrifying because she had never managed anyone, never had to depend on someone else's work for her clients' satisfaction, never been responsible for another person's professional development. The hire she made, three months after making the decision, changed her business and her working life in ways she hadn't fully anticipated. This book is about making that first hire well.

## Chapter 1: The Decision to Hire

The decision to hire should be made when the cost of not hiring — in turned-down revenue, degraded client relationships, founder burnout, and growth constraint — exceeds the cost and risk of hiring. For most founders, this point arrives before they hire, because the natural tendency is to wait until the situation is unambiguously unsustainable before taking the risk of adding the complexity and cost of an employee.

The specific signals that indicate the hire decision is overdue: consistently working more hours than the business demands of the founder's actual role (which means a significant portion of the founder's time is being consumed by work that someone less expensive could do); regularly declining work that the business could handle with additional capacity; making promises to clients that are being kept only through unsustainable personal effort; and experiencing the degradation in quality or responsiveness that comes from being stretched beyond productive capacity.

The financial test for the hire decision: the business should be able to sustain the fully-loaded cost of the hire (salary + payroll taxes + benefits, typically 1.25x to 1.4x base salary) while maintaining the founder's compensation and operating reserve. A hire that requires the founder to reduce their own compensation is not yet financially justified; the revenue growth that the hire enables should produce the margin to fund both the hire and maintain the founder's financial position.

## Chapter 2: Defining the Role

The most common mistake in first hires is insufficient role definition: posting a job description before having thought through exactly what work the new person will do, what decisions they will have authority to make, what success looks like in the role, and how their contribution connects to the business's priorities.

The role definition process before writing a job description: list every significant task currently being done by the founder, and assess each one: Is this a task that requires the founder's specific judgment and expertise? Or is it a task that a skilled, trained person could handle with appropriate guidance? The tasks in the second category are the natural candidates for delegation to the first hire. The role should be built around a coherent set of these delegatable tasks, organized into a function (operations, business development, client service, production) that makes sense as a job.

The role design document that produces the best hiring outcomes: a clear statement of the role's primary purpose (the one-sentence summary of what this role exists to do), the specific responsibilities (the recurring tasks and decisions the role is accountable for), the success criteria (how the business will know in six months whether this person is doing the role well), the skills and experience required (what the person must have to do the job, distinguished from what would be nice to have but isn't essential), and the reporting relationship and decision-making authority.

## Chapter 3: Where to Find Candidates

The candidate sources for the first hire are meaningfully different in quality for most small businesses: candidates who come through the founder's professional and personal network are significantly more likely to be a good fit — in terms of values, work style, and realistic expectations about the job — than candidates who respond to a public job posting without any prior connection to the business.

The network sourcing process: notify everyone in the professional network that the business is hiring — specifically, that you are looking for someone with defined characteristics for a defined role. Frame the message as informational (seeking recommendations) rather than transactional (asking for referrals). Most quality candidates come from someone in the network who knows someone who is a good fit, not from the network member themselves being interested.

Public job postings (LinkedIn, Indeed, industry-specific job boards) reach a wider candidate pool but generate more unqualified applications that require time to screen, and the candidates who respond have less information about the business and the role than network candidates who have been warmly referred. The filtering required to identify strong candidates from a public posting is a meaningful time investment; for the first hire, the network-first approach typically produces better candidates with less effort.

## Chapter 4: The Hiring Process

The hiring process for a first employee should be thorough enough to give the founder genuine confidence in the decision — which typically requires more than one conversation and more than an assessment of skills — without being so extended and elaborate that strong candidates drop out or the process consumes weeks of the founder's time.

The hiring process that most reliably produces a good first hire: an initial screening conversation (thirty minutes, primarily to assess communication, cultural alignment, and basic fit with the role requirements); a more substantive interview focused on the candidate's approach to their work, their most significant professional challenges and what they learned from them, and the specific competencies most important for the role; a work sample or skills assessment (asking the candidate to complete a relevant sample task, which reveals capability more reliably than interview performance); reference conversations (speaking with two to three former managers or colleagues about the candidate's performance, work style, and growth trajectory); and a final conversation that allows the candidate to ask questions and that clarifies expectations before an offer is made.

The reference check is the step most commonly skipped in small business hiring and most reliably predictive of whether a hire will work out: former managers who have observed the candidate's actual performance over an extended period have information that no interview can produce. A reference who is hesitant or guarded, who cannot name specific accomplishments with confidence, or who offers praise that is conspicuously vague is providing information through that hesitance that the candidate's confident self-presentation in interviews cannot reveal.

## Chapter 5: Compensation and the Offer

The first hire compensation decision should be made before the hiring process begins — not improvised based on what the leading candidate expects or requests. Establishing a salary range based on market data for the role, the candidate's experience level, and the business's financial capacity prevents the embarrassing situations of either making an offer that embarrasses the founder (too low relative to market) or committing to compensation the business can't sustain.

The offer should include: base salary (reflecting the market range research and the candidate's experience positioning within that range); any variable pay components (bonus eligibility, criteria for the bonus, and the target amount); benefits (health insurance, retirement, paid time off, and any other benefits the business provides); and practical details (start date, onboarding plan, and any conditions of the offer like background check).

The salary negotiation that typically accompanies an offer: the founder who has done market research and has set a salary range based on that research can negotiate with confidence. The appropriate response to a counter-proposal is not reflexive capitulation (which suggests the initial offer wasn't genuine) or reflexive refusal (which may lose a strong candidate over a modest increment). The appropriate response is honest assessment of whether the requested compensation is within the range that is both market-appropriate and financially sustainable for the business.

## Chapter 6: Onboarding for Success

The quality of the first employee's onboarding — the first weeks and months during which they learn the business, establish their place in it, and begin contributing — is the primary determinant of whether a good hire becomes a good employee within a reasonable time. Poor onboarding is the most common reason that technically good hires fail to perform at the expected level.

The first hire's specific onboarding challenges: there is no team whose existing practices and culture they can observe and align with; the founder, who is their manager, is the same person they are supposed to be relieving of work; and the processes and systems of the business may be entirely in the founder's head rather than documented anywhere. Onboarding the first employee requires the founder to externalize knowledge that has never needed to be externalized before.

The onboarding investment that most reliably produces rapid contribution: a detailed first week schedule (leaving nothing to chance — every hour is planned with specific activities, conversations, and context-building exercises); written documentation of the key processes the employee will handle (created during the weeks before the start date, not improvisedly during the first week); daily check-in conversations in the first two weeks; and explicit, specific feedback at the end of each of the first four weeks (what is going well, what needs adjustment, what the founder's expectations are for the following week).

## Chapter 7: The Manager Transition

The founder who becomes a manager for the first time — who is responsible for another person's performance, development, and day-to-day experience of work — is making a role transition that many founders underestimate. Managing is a skill that is different from the skills that built the business, and it requires deliberate development rather than mere willingness.

The most important managerial skills for first-time managers: clear expectation setting (being specific about what quality looks like, what processes to follow, and what decisions the employee can make versus which require the manager's involvement); feedback delivery (the specific, behavioral, timely feedback that helps employees improve and that most founder-managers find uncomfortable to deliver, particularly when the feedback is critical); and delegation without micromanagement (providing clear direction, appropriate resources, and genuine autonomy to complete delegated work, rather than hovering over the employee's every decision).

Nina's most significant manager transition challenge: she had difficulty releasing control of client work. Her instinct was to review everything before it went to clients, to rewrite anything that didn't match her voice exactly, and to handle any client communication that felt sensitive. This behavior prevented her new hire from developing client-facing skills and from genuinely relieving Nina's workload — the primary purpose of the hire. The coach she worked with helped her design a graduated responsibility model: the junior strategist handled specific types of client communication independently from month one; by month four, she managed several client relationships with Nina's oversight; by month six, she was fully responsible for two client accounts.

## Chapter 8: Legal and Administrative Requirements

Hiring an employee generates a set of legal and administrative obligations that the solo founder has not previously encountered: payroll tax registration, workers' compensation insurance, mandatory employment law compliance, and the documentation requirements that protect the business in the event of employment disputes.

The essential compliance checklist for first hires in the United States: obtaining an Employer Identification Number (EIN) from the IRS; registering with the state for payroll tax withholding; verifying the employee's work authorization (Form I-9); completing federal and state new hire reporting (required within a specific window after hire); obtaining workers' compensation insurance (required in most states); setting up payroll processing (either through payroll software like Gusto or ADP, or through a payroll service provider); and having the employee complete tax withholding forms (W-4 for federal, state equivalent for state taxes).

The employment documentation most important for protecting the business: an offer letter that clearly describes the position, compensation, benefits, and at-will employment status; a confidentiality and intellectual property assignment agreement (ensuring that work product created by the employee is owned by the business and that confidential business information is protected); and an employee handbook that communicates the business's policies, expectations, and complaint procedures.

## Chapter 9: Evaluating the Hire

The performance evaluation of the first employee — the assessment of whether the hire is working as expected — should begin informally from week one and move to a structured format at the three-month and six-month marks. The three-month review is particularly important because it provides the first comprehensive performance feedback while there is still adequate time for the employee to adjust before the relationship becomes too entrenched to redirect without difficulty.

The three-month review framework: assess performance against the success criteria defined before the hire; identify the specific competencies that are strongest and those that need development; communicate clearly about any performance gaps and the expectations for addressing them; and get the employee's assessment of their own experience — what is going well, what is challenging, what they need from the manager to be more effective.

The performance issue that is identified at three months and addressed directly is manageable; the performance issue that is avoided at three months, tolerated at six months, and finally addressed at twelve months has typically become entrenched enough to require a much more difficult conversation and may not be resolvable without the separation that could have been avoided with earlier intervention.

## Chapter 10: Building Toward a Team

The first hire is not only a practical necessity for a growing business; it is the first step in the founder's evolution from individual contributor to leader. The skills, habits, and self-understanding developed through the first hire — how to define a role clearly, how to assess candidates accurately, how to onboard effectively, how to delegate with genuine authority, how to give feedback that improves rather than discourages — are the foundation on which every subsequent hire is built.

Nina's first hire, three years later: the junior brand strategist she hired is now an account director who manages six client relationships independently and who has been instrumental in the hiring and development of two subsequent team members. The business now has four people and $680,000 in annual revenue. Nina's role has evolved from individual contributor to creative director and business leader — a transition that the first hire forced and that the subsequent hires continued. She describes the eighteen months after her first hire as the period of most significant professional development in her career. It was not easy. It was the right choice.

---

## Epilogue

The first hire is the founder's first deliberate choice to build something larger than themselves — to invest in the possibility that the business can exist beyond the limits of the founder's personal capacity and time. It is uncomfortable because it requires trusting someone else with work that has always been yours; it is necessary because the alternative is a ceiling that is permanently set by your personal capacity. Nina's ceiling has been removed. The business she is building is larger than any one person, which means it is the kind of business that has a future beyond her personally. The first hire was not just an HR decision. It was the beginning of a different kind of entrepreneurship.
